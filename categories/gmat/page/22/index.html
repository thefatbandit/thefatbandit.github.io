3.5 Weaken Does Not Mean Destroy.

Lesson Content When we hear the phrase “weaken the argument,” we may feel that our job is to destroy the argument. In fact, much of the time, the correct answer to a Weaken question, especially a more difficult one, will not provide an earth-shattering attack. Thus, we need to be adept at finding subtle ways to weaken an argument. 💡MUST KNOW: Often, the correct answer to a Weaken the Argument question will not totally destroy the argument but instead weaken it in a subtle yet important way.

Lesson Content Students often get the impression that, in answering Weaken the Argument questions, they should eliminate any answer choice that seems to bring up a new topic. 3.7.1 Weakening Answer Type 1: Exposes a False Dichotomy by Offering a Third Alternative

Lesson Content One way in which authors of arguments fail to consider other possibilities is by relying on false dichotomies in coming to conclusions. The term “false dichotomy” may sound complex, but what it means is actually rather simple. 💡MUST KNOW: A false dichotomy occurs when an argument assumes that, just because one conclusion is impossible, another conclusion must be true. In other words, a false dichotomy occurs when an author incorrectly limits the number of possible conclusions to two.

Lesson Content We’ve learned a bit about cause-and-effect arguments in the Assumptions chapter. We must be careful whenever a speaker makes a cause-and-effect argument, because determining whether one thing actually causes another can be difficult. 💡MUST KNOW: We must be careful whenever a speaker makes a cause-and-effect argument, because determining whether one thing actually causes another can be very difficult. In fact, making such determinations is so difficult that, for decades, courts of law did not acknowledge that cigarette smoking causes lung cancer.

3.7.3 Weakening Answer Type 3: Shows that a Generalization that Supports an Argument is Faulty

Lesson Content When an author uses a sample of a population as evidence in his argument, he assumes that that sample provides solid support for his conclusion. 💡MUST KNOW: When an author uses a sample of a population as evidence in his argument, he assumes that that sample provides solid support for his conclusion. In Weaken the Argument questions, we’ll see that that assumption can be a poor one. Authors sometimes make generalizations, drawing conclusions about populations by considering small samples of those populations.

3.7.4 Weakening Answer Type 4: Shows that a Comparison Used to Support a Conclusion is Faulty

Lesson Content When an author uses a comparison as evidence in an argument, she assumes that that comparison provides solid support for her conclusion. 💡MUST KNOW: When an author uses a comparison as evidence in an argument, she assumes that that comparison provides solid support for her conclusion. Often, in Weaken the Argument questions, we’ll see that that assumption is a poor one, because authors often make faulty comparisons. When an author of an argument makes a faulty comparison, she attempts to support the argument by comparing two things that are fundamentally different in an important way such that the comparison does not actually support the conclusion.

3.7.7 Weakening Answer Type 7: Indicates that a Plan that the Argument Concludes will not Work Actually will Work

Lesson Content Plans are common topics in GMAT Critical Reasoning questions, and Weaken questions can involve conclusions that plans will not work or don’t make sense. 💡MUST KNOW: When authors claim that plans will not work or don’t make sense, support for such conclusions can be weakened by correct answers that provide new information that indicates that actually the plans will work or do make sense. The correct answers to these questions generally weaken the arguments by providing new information that offsets or makes up for the information used to support the arguments’ conclusions that the plans will not work.
