7.3 Recognizing Inference Questions

Lesson Content Every Inference question on the GMAT begins with a passage or other stimulus, which is followed by a question that asks, in one way or another, which of the answer choices states something that must be true given what is said in the stimulus. While Inference questions can be worded in various ways, GMAT Critical Reasoning questions tend to utilize predictable language, a fact that we can exploit by familiarizing ourselves with the types of questions we will see.

7.5 Common Inference Question Trap Answer Types

Lesson Content While we know that there is only one correct answer to each Inference question, in most Inference questions, as we have already seen, some of the incorrect answers may somehow rather convincingly seem to be correct and, thus, may tempt test takers for a variety of reasons. In order to mentally prepare you to handle these trap answers, and thus reduce the likelihood of your falling for them, let’s go over some of the types of trap answers that can appear in Inference questions.

7.5.1 An Answer That Could be True or is Likely to be True

Lesson Content Remember that an Inference question requires us to find an answer that must be true. If we select an answer that only could be true or is only likely to be true, we are by definition selecting an incorrect answer. 💡MUST KNOW: In answering an Inference question, if we select an answer that only could be true or is only likely to be true, we are by definition selecting an incorrect answer.

7.5.2 An Answer that Presents Information from the Stimulus in a Distorted Fashion

Lesson Content Unless we are paying very close attention when answering a Critical Reasoning question, it’s easy to forget and or twist some of what we read. 💡MUST KNOW: Unless we are paying very close attention when answering a Critical Reasoning question, it’s easy to forget and or twist some of what we read. Thus, we may create a distorted picture of what we read. Such a distorted picture makes it a lot easier for a test-taker to fall for trap answers that include unsupported information.

7.5.3 An Answer that is out of Proportion to the Information that is Presented in the Stimulus

Lesson Content An “out of proportion” answer is one that exaggerates information presented in the stimulus. For example, if a passage were to suggest that “some people in Group A like basketball,” while an answer choice implies that “all people in Group A are basketball fans,” the choice would be out of proportion and incorrect. Let’s say there are ten people in Group A, then “some” could be three people, four people, or even ten people.

7.5.4 An Answer That Seems to Be Supported by the Stimulus but Actually Depends on Other Information

Lesson Content In answering Inference questions, we must be extremely careful with answer choices that bring in new information. 💡MUST KNOW: In an Inference question, an answer choice that depends on support from information beyond what’s presented in the stimulus can’t be correct. Consider the following example: While surgery to treat cataracts restores vision clarity quickly, it is somewhat expensive. At the same time, there are relatively inexpensive holistic methods that some people who experience cataracts could use to clear their vision within a reasonable timeframe, just as effectively though not as quickly as surgery does.

7.5.5 An Answer that is True in the Real World but is not Supported by the Stimulus

Lesson Content In reading Inference questions, you may encounter incorrect answer choices that say things that are true in the real world but are not completely supported by the information presented to us in the stimulus. Consider the following example: The cost of solar panels that can be used by homeowners to generate electricity has been decreasing. Over the past seven years, the cost per watt of power generation capacity has decreased by 50 percent, and this trend is expected to continue.

7.6 Correct Answer Types in Inference Questions

Lesson Content Just as knowing how incorrect answers may work can be helpful, a solid understanding of how correct answers may look can help us to efficiently and correctly answer Inference questions.

7.6.1 Answers that are Conclusions Based on the Entirety of What is Said in the Passage

Lesson Content The first type of Inference correct answer that we’ll discuss is an answer that is a conclusion based on the entirety of the information presented in the passage. Often, what is conveyed by the statements in the passage in an Inference question adds up logically to a conclusion. Consider the following example: The presence on Mars of certain clay minerals believed to form only in water gave scientists reason to believe that life may have once existed on Mars, in the water in which the clay minerals were formed.

7.6.2 Answers that are Fully Supported by Small Portions of the Passages

Lesson Content Another type of correct answer that often appears in Inference questions is an answer that is fully supported by a small portion of the stimulus. 💡MUST KNOW: In some cases, the correct answers to Inference questions are fully supported by small portions of or even single sentences of the passages. Understand that a correct answer to an Inference question need not be supported by the entire stimulus and that an answer will be correct as long as some portion of the stimulus fully supports the answer.
