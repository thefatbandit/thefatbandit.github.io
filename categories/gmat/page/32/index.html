10.2.9 Logical Flaw Five: Confusing Necessary or Significant Information with Sufficient Information

Lesson Content Necessary information, significant information, and sufficient information differ from each other in key ways. 💡MUST KNOW: Necessary information, significant information, and sufficient information differ from each other in key ways. At the same time, since they are somewhat similar, these types of information can be misused in arguments, in which cases the arguments are flawed. A key thing to understand about these types of information is that information that is necessary or significant is not necessarily the same as information that is sufficient for supporting a conclusion.

Lesson Content The most obviously wrong answer choices in Logical Flaw questions are those that describe things that authors or arguments don’t actually do. Consider the following example: Unlike other major automobile companies, all of which produce mostly automobiles that are fossil fuel powered, The Blitz Automobile Company makes only electric automobiles. 10.3.2 Incorrect Choices that Bring Up Things that the Argument Does but are not Flaws

Lesson Content In order for a Logical Flaw answer choice to be correct, it has to have two characteristics. 💡MUST KNOW: In order for a Logical Flaw answer choice to be correct, it has to have two characteristics. It has to describe something that actually goes on in the argument, and what it describes has to be a flaw in the argument. Many Logical Flaw incorrect answer choices satisfy the first criterion, as they describe things that the authors or arguments actually do, but do not satisfy the second one, as what they describe are not flaws.
