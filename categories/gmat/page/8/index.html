5.2 Adjectives and Adverbs

Lesson Content Adjectives are modifiers that can modify only nouns or pronouns, while adverbs are modifiers that can modify verbs, adjectives, and other adverbs. 💡MUST KNOW: Adjectives can modify only nouns or pronouns. Adverbs can modify verbs, adjectives, and other adverbs. Here are some examples of common adjectives: hard, bright, pretty, thoughtless, spurious, enthusiastic, yellow, tentative, sonorous, new, good. The new book is now in stores. 5.3 Misplaced Modifiers

Lesson Content As the name implies, a misplaced modifier is a modifier that is in the wrong place in a sentence. 💡MUST KNOW: The misplacement of modifiers results in errors in meaning, and misplaced modifiers are frequently used in incorrect answer choices in Sentence Correction questions. Misplaced modifier questions can be particularly challenging because even native English speakers often misplace modifiers in their speech and writing. Let’s see how misplacement can affect the meaning of a sentence by considering the effects of misplacing adjectives.

5.3.1 Misplaced Adjectives

Lesson Content When an adjective is incorrectly separated from the noun it modifies, the meaning of the sentence can be distorted. Incorrect: On the tables at the event were nutritious trays of fruits and vegetables. Hmm – those nutritious trays sure sound delicious, don’t they? Better have a good dentist on call, right? Of course, the adjective “nutritious” is misplaced. On the tables at the event were trays of nutritious fruits and vegetables.

5.3.5 Pay Special Attention to Prepositional Phrases of Time

Lesson Content Prepositional phrases of time include structures such as “by 1920,” “after the Cold War ended,” and “in 2018.” When we encounter such phrases, we must take extra care to ensure that their placement does not result in ambiguity, confusion, or an illogical meaning. 5.3.6 Misplaced Introductory Modifiers

Lesson Content We often encounter sentences that open with introductory (opening) modifiers. Consider the following example: Built to last, the lighthouse has weathered many storms. We can see that the sentence opens with an introductory noun modifier, “built to last.” The noun that the introductory modifier is meant to modify, “the lighthouse,” appears right after the introductory modifier. 💡MUST KNOW: When an introductory modifier is a noun modifier, the noun that it modifies must appear right after the introductory modifier.

5.4 Dangling Modifiers

Lesson Content Dangling modifiers are similar to misplaced modifiers, except in that, in a case of a dangling modifier, the noun that the modifier is supposed to describe is completely absent from the sentence. 💡MUST KNOW: In a case of a dangling modifier, the noun that the modifier is supposed to describe is completely absent from the sentence. Such a sentence is always incorrect. Dangling modifiers are common in Sentence Correction questions.
