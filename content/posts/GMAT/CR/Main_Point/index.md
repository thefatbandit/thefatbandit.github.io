Main Point questions ask you to summarize the author’s point of view.

## Fact Test + Main Point

As with Must Be True, Main point answer choice must pass the fact test. But apart from that it should also capture the author’s point.

Applying the primary objectives, we’ll most likely have selected the conclusion of the stimulus already. So we have the main point with us already.

**<mark>Identify Primary Conclusion → Find paraphrase of conclusion in answer choices</mark>**

{{< alert type="danger" >}}

🚨 Many Main Point problems will place the ***conclusion*** either ***at the beginning*** or ***at the end.*** Its not necessary to have them at the end of the stimulus.
{{< /alert >}}

## Question Stem Format

The format is fairly consistent with the primary feature being a request to identify the conclusion or point of the argument.

- “Which of the following most accurately expresses the main conclusion of the argument?”
- “Which of the following most accurately expresses the conclusion of the argument?”
- “Which of the following most accurately restates the main point of the passage?”
- “The main point of the argument is that”

## 🚨Incorrect Answer Choices🚨

There are 2 types of incorrect answer choices that often appear in GMAT: 

1. True but don’t encapsulate the author’s point
2. Repeat premises of the argument

## Fill In the Blanks

Relatively rare. In these instances, the blank is preceded by a conclusion indicator (as opposed to the usual premise indicator)

- “Therefore ______”
- “Hence in the coming years, the rate of economic growth will likely ___________”
- “Thus, from the politician’s statements, we can conclude ________________”

### Difference from other question types

1. The indicator modifying the blank is often a premise indicator (such as “because” or “since”)
2. The question stem contains language indicating you are completing a different task, such as strengthening the argument. 

### Formula

Read the stimulus for clues revealing the direction of the argument and the author’s intent.
