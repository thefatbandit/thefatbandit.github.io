## Must Be True

Must by True questions require you to select an answer choice that is proven by the stimulus information.

The correct answer choice maybe:

- Paraphrase of the stimulus
- Logical consequence of one or more parts of the stimulus

## Fact Test

The correct answer choice can always be proven by referring to the facts stated in the stimulus. Any answer choice we select **should not** be selected on the basis that it “sounds good”.

{{< alert type="danger" >}}
🚨 Do not bring in information from outside the stimulus (however true it may be in your experience) aside from commonsense assumptions.
{{< /alert >}}

## Question Stem types

1. The stem often indicates the information in the stimulus should be taken as true which indicates towards the 1st family of Qs:
    - “If the statements above are true...”
    - “The statements above, if true...”
    - “If the information above is correct...”
2. Following this, the stem asks you to identify a single answer choice that is proven or supported:
    - “...which of the following must be true?”
    - “...which of the following conclusions can be properly drawn on the basis of it?”
    - “...most strongly support which of the following?”
    - “Which of the following can be properly inferred...”

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡 ***“infer”*** means ***“must be true”*** only in the GMAT context
{{< /alert >}}
    
##### ***E.g.***
- “If the statements above are true, which of the following must be true?”
- “Which of the following conclusions are best supported by the statements above?”
- “The statements above, if true, best support which of the following assertions?”
- “Which of the following can be correctly inferred from the statements above?”
- “Which of the following is most strongly supported by the information above?”

## The Correct Answer Choice

### Stimulus Information

There are essentially 4 classifications of *information* in the stimulus that can be used to prove an answer choice correct:

1. **<u>Actual statements of the Stimulus</u>**
2. **<u>Commonsense Assumptions</u>**
3. **<u>Consequences of the statements presented in the stimulus:</u>** Combining the statements from stimulus to produce a technically new statement, but 1 that is supported clearly & directly by the stimulus
4. **<u>Info under the “umbrella” of the stimulus:</u>** Elements that are not explicitly mentioned in the stimulus but pertain to its broader topic of discussion. 

### Scope of Answer Statements

- **Broad Scope:** some, could, many
- **Narrow Scope:** only, none

### -ve & Double -ve

- Let’s go the the store→ +ve
- Let’s *not* go to the store→ -ve
- Let’s *never not* go to the store→ 2 -ve = 1 +ve
- Mary is *not* *un*-happy→ 2 -ve = 1 +ve

## Incorrect Answers (Attractive but not Correct)

1. **<u>Could be/Likely to be True:</u>** These are particularly tricky since there’s nothing wrong with the answers. But these answer *choices fail the Fact Test*
2. **<u>Exaggerated Answers:</u>** These answers take the statements from stimulus and stretch the info beyond its scope 
3. **<u>“New” Information Answers:</u>** They may seem an extension of the stimulus info and are logical too, but *are not explicitly mentioned* in the stimulus. 
They can be checked by:
    1. Examining the scope of the argument to make sure the “new” info is within the range of a term or concept in stimulus
    2. Examining the answer to make sure it is not the consequence of combining stimulus elements
4. **<mark><u>The Shell Game:</u></mark>** Idea similar to the one raised in stimulus but is incorrect
5. **<u>The Opposite Answer:</u>** They are opposite to what the stimulus says. These are very attractive to the *fast/careless reader* 
6. **<mark><u>The Reverse Answer:</u></mark>** Using familiar elements from the stimulus but rearranging the elements to make it incorrect. Again a trap for fast readers.
    - **E.G.:** “*Many* people have *some* type of security system in their home”
        
        <u>Reversed:</u> “*Some* people have *many* types of security systems in their home”
        

## Stimulus Opinions Vs. Assertions

When a stimulus contains only the opinions of others, then in a Must Be True question, we can eliminate any answer choice that makes a flat assertion without reference to those opinions. ***No factual statement can be concluded in such a case***

Similarly if a ***stimulus contains only facts***, then ***answer choices presenting an opinion can also be eliminated***

## Conditional Reasoning

These are logical relationships composed of sufficient and necessary conditions

- **Sufficient Condition (*SC*):** An event or circumstance whose occurrence indicates that a necessary condition must also occur.
- **Necessary Condition (*NC*):** An event whose occurrence is required in order for a sufficient condition to occur.
- ***Indicator Words for conditions***
    
    
    | Sufficient Condition | Necessary Condition |
    | --- | --- |
    | If | Then |
    | When | Only |
    | Whenever | Only if |
    | Every | Must |
    | All | Requried |
    | Any | Unless |
    | Each | Except |
    | In order to | Until |
    | People who | Without |

- ***Eg.*** “If someone gets an A+ on a test, then they must have studied for the test”

  - **Necessary:** Must have studied
  - **Sufficient:** get an A+

### Logical Features of Conditional Reasoning

1. **SC -/> NC:** SC doesn’t *make* the NC happen
2. **Order of SC & NC not fixed**
3. **<mark style="color: white; background: #e06666!important">The conditional relationship DOES NOT *have to reflect reality.</mark>*** Our job is not to figure out what’s reasonable and what’s not. We just have to capture the meaning of author’s sentence.

### Valid/Invalid Statements

#### Simple Formula To Remember

**Original Statement:** A→B

{{< split 6 6>}}

**<mark style="color: white; background: #00ced1!important">Valid Statements</mark>**

1. **A → B (*Valid Repeat*)**
2. **~~B~~ → ~~A~~ (*Contrapositive*)**

---

**<mark style="color: white; background: #e06666!important">Invalid Statements</mark>**

1. **B → A (*Mistaken Reversal*)**
2. **~~A~~ → ~~B~~ (*Mistaken Negation*)**

{{< /split >}}
  
<details><summary><b><i><u>Explanation & Example</u></i></b></summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

1. **Valid Repeat:** A paraphrasing of the original statement
- John received an A+ on the test, so he must have studied for the test.

2. **Mistaken Reversal:** Switches the elements in the sufficient and necessary conditions
- John studied for the test, so he must have received an A+ on the test.

3. **Mistaken Negation:** It negates both conditions, creating a statement that doesn’t have to be true
- John did not receive an A+ on the test, so he must no have studied for the test.

4. **Contrapositive:** It both reverses & negates the original statement. Hence by being a combination of 2 -ve, the statement becomes a +ve of sorts & hence ***is valid***
  - John didn’t study for the test, so he must not have received an A+ on the test

{{< /alert >}}

Since such headaches are often symptomatic of more serious maladies, sufferers are strongly advised instead to consult their physicians.

## Explanations

<details><summary><b><i><u>Q1- Explnanation</u></i></b></summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

#### (B)

The idea in this stimulus is that while headaches can often be relieved by certain types of medication, headaches that are recurring or long-lasting can
indicate a more serious condition, and doctors recommend that the sufferer
seek medical attention instead of relief from over-the-counter medicine. As we consider what would be proven true from these statements, it is important to not go beyond the information and facts presented, or make unwarranted assumptions beyond what the stimulus directly supports.

Answer choice (A): There is no information in the stimulus that correlates the severity of headache pain with the seriousness of the cause of the headache.

<mark>Answer choice (B):</mark> This is the correct answer. The stimulus indicates that
while a person may be able to find relief from a recurring or long-lasting
headache through the use of medication, physicians recommend that in
these situations (hence the “in some cases”) the sufferer should instead seek
medical attention and not self-medicate (i.e. not seek immediate relief from
the pain). Note that this answer can be easy to overlook because it goes
against some commonly-held notions of physicians being obligated to
attempt to quickly alleviate pain.

Answer choice (C): All that we know from the information presented is that “headaches can often be relieved by over the-counter medication” (italics added for emphasis). There is nothing that suggests then that some headaches cannot be relieved by these same medications. Be careful not to assume that “some cannot” just because you are not explicitly told “all can.”

Answer choice (D): This answer choice is simply much too broad to be completely supported by the information in the stimulus. In the specific instance of a recurring/long-lasting headache, physicians may be focused more on the underlying cause than on immediate pain relief, but that does not necessarily mean that physicians in general focus less on pain relief (in all instances) than on other physical symptoms.

Answer choice (E): This answer choice contradicts the information given,
so it is an Opposite answer and is correct. The stimulus states that over-thecounter medications could potentially provide relief for a headache caused
by something more serious (“even if such medication can provide relief”);
the point is that physicians suggest sufferers forego immediate relief and
seek to instead establish the underlying cause of the headache.

<details><summary><b><i><u>Q2- Explanation</u></i></b></summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}
    
#### (A)

This stimulus presents a fairly straightforward scenario: the tariffs imposed on steel imports by the government of country A have helped domestic steel producers become more competitive and make record profits, because in most cases foreign producers were unable to compete under the new tariffs. Note that there is no conclusion present, and so this is a fact set, not an
argument.

<mark>Answer choice (A):</mark> This is the correct answer. Since we know that domestic steel producers benefited positively from the tariffs, and foreign steel producers were affected negatively, it must be true that not all steel producers were unaffected. Worded another way, answer choice A states “some steel producers were affected by…[the] tariffs.”

As an aside, if, upon reading (A), you determined this was a strong answer,
should you have simply selected it and skipped reading the remaining
answers? No, because perhaps you misread (A), or perhaps there is a better
answer among the remaining choices. The only reason not to read the remaining choices would be if you were just about out of time.

Answer choice (B): The stimulus tells us that “foreign steel producers were in many cases unable to compete effectively under the burden of the newly imposed tariffs.” This does not imply that some foreign steel producers were able to compete effectively. Be careful not to assume that “some could compete” just because you are not explicitly told “all could not compete.” Although this answer choice Could Be True, it does not have to be true.

Answer choice (C): Again, we know that in many cases foreign steel producers could not compete effectively, but we cannot know anything about the percentage of foreign steel producers that could not compete effectively. Again, this answer choice Could Be True, but it does not have to be true.

Answer choice (D): The stimulus states that “many” domestic steel producers enjoyed record profits, but “many” does not necessarily equal “most” (or “majority”). Logically speaking, “many” simply means “some,” unless you have further information about the overall number under discussion (more on this in Chapter Twelve).

Answer choice (E): This answer choice is much too broad in scope to be
completely supported by the information in the stimulus. We know that in
country A these particular tariffs helped to aid the domestic steel industry,
but that is not sufficient evidence to prove that tariffs are “generally an
effective approach” to protecting any domestic industry in any country

<details><summary><b><i><u>Q3- Explanation</u></i></b></summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

#### (C)

This question presents a fact set that begins by telling us that blood tests
are, at times, unable to conclusively determine if a woman is pregnant or
not. Then we are told that some doctors refuse to perform certain surgeries
due to an inconclusive pregnancy test result. These facts indicate that
definitive knowledge of whether or not a patient is pregnant must have a
potential impact on some surgical procedures. Hence, doctors will not
operate on that patient without conclusive pregnancy tests results. Note too
that we cannot know the number or type of surgical procedures that could
potentially be affected by pregnancy; we simply know that some doctors
will not perform further surgeries following an inconclusive test result.

Answer choice (A): This answer choice is far too broad in scope to be
proven by the stimulus. Just because some surgical procedures may be
potentially impacted by a patient’s pregnancy does not mean that a
pregnancy test should precede all surgeries. Further, by the logic presented
in answer choice (A), all patients—men and women—should be given
pregnancy tests prior to any surgery, and clearly the stimulus does not
support that conclusion.

Answer choice (B): There is no information in the stimulus to indicate the
frequency with which inconclusive tests turn out to ultimately be positive or
negative.

<mark>Answer choice (C):</mark> This is the correct answer. As stated above, the facts
of the stimulus support the notion that whether or not a patient is pregnant
could potentially affect a surgical procedure to be performed on that patient.

Answer choice (D): This answer choice has very strong language, as it
states that some doctors require a pregnancy test prior to “any surgery.”
However we cannot know that some doctors require a pregnancy test before
every surgery they perform on a female candidate; we only know that some
doctors refuse to perform certain surgeries following an inconclusive test
result. Always be wary of extreme or absolute language in answer choice
for Must Be True questions, since it takes similarly strong language in a
stimulus to prove a strongly worded answer choice correct.

Answer choice (E): The stimulus is exclusively about inconclusive (nonresult) pregnancy tests, so an answer choice that addresses tests producing a
“negative result” goes beyond the facts provided in the stimulus.
