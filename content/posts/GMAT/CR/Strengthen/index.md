These questions ask you to identify the answer choice that best supports the argument. The correct answer choice does not necessarily justify the arguments, nor is the correct answer choice necessarily an assumption of the argument.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡 The correct answer choice simply helps the argument in some way.
{{< /alert >}}

## Rules to Solve (*Same as Weaken*)

1. The ***stimulus will always contain an argument*** since you are asked to weaken the author’s reasoning.
2. ***Focus on the conclusion***. The more we know about its specifics, the better armed we will be to shore up the argument.
3. ***Information in the stimulus is suspect***. Read the arguments carefully.
4. 🚨***Pre-phase possible answers***🚨 before proceeding to the answer choices.
5. 💡***Answer choices are accepted as given***💡 even if they include “new information”.

## Identifying Question Type

1. The stem uses words synonymous to “strenghten”.
- ***<u>“Strengthen” synonyms:</u>*** Strengthen, support, helps, ***most justifies***
    
2. The stem usually indicates that you should accept the answer choices as true
- ##### Examples
  - “Which of the following if true, most strengthen the argument above?”
  - “Which of the following, if true, most strongly supports the statement above?”
  - “Which of the following, if true, does most to justify the conclusion above?”
  - Which of the following, if true, supports the claim above EXCEPT?

## How to Strengthen an Argument

1. **<u>Identify the conclusion:</u>** Ask yourself “Would this answer choice assist the author in some way?”
2. **<u>Personalize the argument:</u>** Personalizing allows you to see the argument from a very involved perspective and helps assess the strength of each answer.
3. **<u>Look for weakness in the argument:</u>** A weak spot in an argument is tailor-made for an answer that eliminates that weakness. Bringing the premise-conclusion assumptions to light strengthens the argument because it validates part of the author’s thinking.
4. **<u>Validating surveys/analogies:</u>** Arguments using these rely upon their validity. So answer choices validating them are usually correct.
5. **<u>Scale of strengthen doesn’t matter</u>**

## Answer Traps

1. **<u>Opposite Answers:</u>** Because of their direct relation to the conclusion, these are attractive answer choices.
2. **<u>Shell Game Answers:</u>** An idea similar to a concept raised in stimulus but that is changed just enough to be incorrect.  
3. **<u>Out Of Scope Answers:</u>** These simply miss the point of the argument and support unrelated/tangential issues to the argument.

## Causality & Strengthen Questions

To strengthen a causal conclusion, you take the exact opposite approach that you would in a Weaken question:

1. ***<u>Cause_2 -/> Effect</u>*** Eliminate alternate causes for the stated effect
2. ***Cause → Effect*** Show that when the cause occurs, the effect does occur
3. ***<u>~~Cause~~ → ~~Effect~~</u>*** Show that the effect did not occur since the cause did not
4. ***<u>Effect -/> Cause</u>*** Show that the stated relationship can not be reversed
5. ***<u>Statistical Accuracy:</u>*** Show that the data used to make the causal statement is accurate

## Practice

1. Consumer Advocate: Many household cleaners contain ingredients which are highly toxic when ingested by children or pets. Because of this significant risk, I propose a law prohibiting the use of such toxic ingredients in household cleaners.
    
    Which of the following, if true, provides the most support for the argument above?
    
    (A) Most toxic household cleaners have labels which clearly warn of their toxicity.
    
    (B) There are many different types of household cleaners, and some are more effective than others.
    
    (C) When the use of household cleaners is discontinued, harmful bacteria are more likely to propagate in areas where children and pets are commonly found.
    
    (D) The toxic ingredients in most household cleaners could be replaced by comparably priced, non-toxic ingredients of equal or better quality. 
    
    (E) The amount of toxic ingredients found in most household cleaners is much less than the amount contained in most types of common gasoline
    
<details><summary><b><i><u>Solution</u></i></b></summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

#### (D)

**Premise:** Many household cleaners contain ingredients that are toxic when ingested.

**Conclusion:** There should be law prohibiting the use of such toxic ingredients in household cleaners.

(A)- Clear warning labels would reduce the need for an across-the-board prohibition of the cleaners. So this statement in fact, weakens the argument

(B)- It just says that all household cleaners are not equal. But since it doesn’t talk about the toxic ingredients, the statement is irrelevant.

(C)- The statement, if relevant at all, would weaken the assertion that household cleaners should be discontinued

<mark>(D)</mark>- This is the correct answer. It provides information to show that the advocate’s plan is practicable. If the toxic ingredients could be removed with no added cost or decrease in effectiveness, then this preempts any cost/effectiveness-based objections.

(E)- The comparison between the amount of toxic chemicals in the cleaners is irrelevant to the question of outlawing them.
