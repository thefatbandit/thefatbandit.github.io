# Steps
- Decide upon the broad topic
- Read it VERY carefully to capture all the details
- Identify the problems between each part of the stimulus
- Don't panic if you don't understand a terminology. Focus on understanding the how the ideas connect with each other
- <details><summary><b>Speaker Identifiers</b></summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

- Pay attention to the speaker. Often it will help in identifying the broad topic addressed in the stimulus
    - <u>Business:</u> Manager, Executive, Business Plan
    - <u>Politics:</u> Politician, Editorial, Lawmaker, Activist
    - <u>Science/Medicine:</u> Doctor, Physician, Scientist, Researcher
- Some identifiers are confusing or too broad. For example a columnist can write about a lot of topics. But they will almost always be opinionated.
- Some identifiers setup a direct expectation:
    - An "*advertisement*" in front of the stimulus almost always means presence of flawed reasoning.
    - Same goes for arguments by politicians

{{< /alert >}}
</details>

- <details><summary><b>Challenging GMAT Vocabulary</b></summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

- <u>Advanced Words:</u> No need to study list of words separately. It's a rare occurrence & even then it's decipherable from the context.
- <u>Scientific Jargon:</u> Definition/usage information will be supplied in the stimulus, often in the vicinity of the word. No need to guess the meaning.
- Focus on the entire message and use the most relevant parts
{{< /alert >}}
</details>

- <details><summary><b>Stimuli Categories</b></summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}
- **Facts:** Lack of a strong reaction on reading a statement generally points to it being a fact
- **Arguments (*IMP*):** Set of statements that are derived from the others.
{{< /alert >}}
</details>

## **Argument Vs. Facts in Stimulus**

To know this we must recognize whether a conclusion exists. For this we must identify the parts of a sentence, namely 2:

- **Conclusion:** The point the author tries to prove by using another statement
    <details><summary><b>Questions to ask for identifying conclusion</b></summary>
    {{< alert type="secondary" >}} 
    - What is the author driving at?
    - What does the author want me to believe?
    - What point follows from the others?
    {{< /alert >}} 
    </details>
- **Premise:** A fact, proposition or statement from which a conclusion is made. It gives a reason for believing something
    <details><summary><b>Questions to ask for identifying premise</b></summary> 
    {{< alert type="secondary" >}} 
        - What reasons has the author used to persuade me?
        - Why should I believe this argument?
        - What evidence exists?
    {{< /alert >}} 
    </details>

### **Exercise**

1. Wine is made by crushing grapes and eventually separating the juice from the grape skins. However, the separated juice contains impurities and many wineries do not filter the juice. These wineries claim the unfiltered juice ultimately produces a more flavorful and intense wine. Since these wine makers are experts, we should trust their judgment and not shy away from unfiltered wine.

<details><summary><b><i><u>Solution</u></i></b></summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

Features the counter-premise indicator “however” and the premise indicator “since.”
  
  - <b><u>Premise:</b></u> “Wine is made by crushing grapes and eventually separating the juice from the grape skins.”
  - <b><u>Counter-Premise:</b></u> “However, the separated juice contains impurities and many wineries do not filter the juice.”
  - <b><u>Premise:</b></u> “These wineries claim the unfiltered juice ultimately produces a more flavorful and intense wine.”
  - <b><u>Premise:</b></u> “Since these wine makers are experts,”
  - <b><u>Conclusion:</b></u> “we should trust their judgment and not shy away from unfiltered wine.”
{{< /alert >}}
</details>

{{< vs 1>}}

2. Phenylketonurics are people who cannot metabolize the amino acid phenylalanine. There are dangers associated with phenylketonuria, and products containing phenylalanine must carry a warning label that states, “Phenylketonurics: contains phenylalanine.” In addition, all children in developed societies receive a phenylketonuria test at birth. Hence, at the moment, we are doing as much as possible to protect against this condition.

<details><summary><b><i><u>Solution</u></i></b></summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

   - <b><u>Premise:</b></u> “Phenylketonurics are people who cannot metabolize the amino acid phenylalanine.”
   - <b><u>Premise:</b></u> “There are dangers associated with phenylketonuria, and products containing phenylalanine must carry a warning label that states, ‘Phenylketonurics: contains phenylalanine.’”
   - <b><u>Additional Premise:</b></u> “In addition, all children in developed societies received a phenylketonuria test at birth.”
   - <b><u>Conclusion:</b></u> “Hence, at the moment, we are doing as much as
   possible to protect against this condition.”

{{< /alert >}}
</details>

{{< vs 1 >}}
{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡 **Recognizing Conclusions Without Indicators**
Force the parts of the argument to be the premise & conclusion using indicator words & see if it's making logical sense. If not, reverse the premise-conclusion & check again

{{< /alert >}}

**E.g.-** The best way of eliminating traffic congestion will not be easily found. There are so many competing possibilities that it will take millions of dollars to study every option, and implementation of most options carries an exorbitant price tag.

<details><summary><b><i><u>Methodology</u></i></b></summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

Let the first sentence be the premise & the 2nd one be the conclusion.
    
  ***Because*** the best way of eliminating traffic congestion will not be easily found, ***we can conclude that*** there are so many competing possibilities that it will take millions of dollars to study every option, and implementation of most options carries an exorbitant
  price tag.
  
  This is not making sense so let's reverse it
  
  ***Because*** there are so many competing possibilities that it will take millions of dollars to study every option, and implementation of most options carries an exorbitant price tag, ***we can conclude that*** the best way of eliminating traffic congestion will not be easily
  found.
  
  This is making sense & is the solution 

{{< /alert >}}
</details>   
    

## Complex Arguments

Contain more than one conclusion. 1 main & other sub-conclusions. The author uses 1 conclusion as base for other conclusion.

**E.g.-** Because Germany has the best goalkeeper in soccer, they therefore have the best defense in soccer. Because they have the best defense in football, they will win the next World Cup.

<details><summary><b><i><u>Solution</u></i></b></summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

- <b><u>Premise:</b></u> “Because Germany has the
best goalkeeper in soccer,”
- <b><u>Sub-Conclusion (conclusion of the
previous premise/Premise for the
following conclusion):</b></u> “they
therefore have the best defense in
soccer.”
- <b><u>Main Conclusion:</b></u> “they will win the next
World Cup.”

{{< /alert >}}
</details>  

## Decoding Text

### Killer Sentences

Basically kuch nahi chamkega ki kaun kya bol raha hain.

**E.g-** The survival strategies of various classes of vertebrates demonstrate that, contrary to the claims of early zoologists, physical similarity does not produce strategic similarity: for example, the strategies that an electric eel uses to defend itself from attack are significantly different than those used by the hagfish, an eel-like fish also known as a slime eel, but both manifest the same basic morphological appearance to predators.

**Solutions Steps:**

- Slow Down
- Break the sentence into smaller components. **USE PUNCTUATION MARKS FOR THIS**
    - **Comma [,]:** Separate Ideas
    - **Colon [:]:** Precedes explanation/list
    - **Semi-colon [;]:** Independent ideas that could stand on their own too. ***Comma on steroids.***
    - **Parentheses [()]:** Additional info to the sentence
    - **Dash [—]:** Extremely versatile. Typically sets apart ideas.

### Reference Words

Refer back/forward to ideas in the stimulus without explicitly naming the idea. Makes the writing more compact.

**Examples:**

- Opponents of solar power argue that the high cost of technology makes solar a non-feasible solution on a large scale basis, especially in rural areas where the power grid is very widely distributed. But ***this*** is not true, because in Germany—one of the world’s leaders in solar energy production—some of the largest solar arrays are built in rural areas.
  
<details><summary><b><i><u>Solution</u></i></b></summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

*This* refers to “the high cost of technology makes solar a non-feasible solution on a large scale basis.”

{{< /alert >}}
</details>  
        
- ***Those*** students who have exceeded the academic standards in our school will be honored at a ceremony next month.
  
<details><summary><b><i><u>Solution</u></i></b></summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

*Those students* refers to the students who have have “exceeded the academic standards in our school” and who “will be honored at a ceremony next month,”

{{< /alert >}}
</details> 
        

# Truth Vs Validity

**Validity (IMP):** Logical Relationship of the pieces of argument.

**Truthfulness:** Absolute truth about whether the stuff happens in the real world.

{{< alert type="danger">}}
🚨 Just focus on whether the conclusion is true **RELATIVE** to the premises.
{{< /alert >}}

## Inferences & Assumptions

Different from the general sense. Have to see in the GMAT argumentation context.

### Inference

An item that ***must be true*** based on the information in the argument.

### Assumptions

A premise that must be true in order for the argument to be true.

# Exercise

For the given stimulus, answer the following questions:

- What is the conclusion of the argument if any?
- What premises are given in support of this conclusion?
- Is the argument strong or weak? If you think that the argument is weak, please explain why.

1. Admittedly, the practice of allowing students to retake a class they previously failed and receive a new grade is controversial. But the mission of any school or university is to educate their students, and allowing students to retake courses supports this mission. Therefore, for the time
being, our school should continue to allow students to retake previously failed courses and receive a new grade.

<details><summary><b><i><u>Solution</u></i></b></summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

- <u>Conclusion:</u> Therefore, for the time being, our school should continue to allow students to retake previously failed courses and receive a new grade.
- <u>Premise:</u> Admittedly, the practice of allowing students to retake a class they previously failed and receive a new grade is controversial.
- <u>Premise:</u> But the mission of any school or university is to educate their students, and allowing students to retake courses supports this mission.
        
The arguement is reasonably strong. A practice is stated as being controversial, but then a reasonable statement is made in support of the practice. The conclusion then advocates continuing an already-existing practice. As no viable reason has been presented against the practice, and a viable reason has been given for the practice, it is not unreasonable to conclude that the practice should continue for the time being.

{{< /alert >}}
</details> 
{{< vs 1>}}

2. 🚨 While it was once believed that the health of the human body was dependent on a balance between four substances, or “humors,” the advent of medical research in the nineteenth century led to the understanding that this view was both simplistic and inaccurate. Thereafter, physicians— especially those in Europe, such as Edward Jenner—began formulating theories of treatment that are now the foundation of modern medicine.

<details><summary><b><i><u>Solution</u></i></b></summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

- <u>Premise:</u> While it was once believed that the health of the human body was dependent on a balance between four substances, or “humors,” the advent of medical research in the nineteenth century led to the understanding that this view was both simplistic and inaccurate.
- <u>Premise:</u> Thereafter, physicians—especially those in Europe, such as Edward Jenner—began formulating theories of treatment that are now the foundation of modern medicine.

The stimulus is only a fact set and does not contain a conclusion. Therefore, there is no argument present and no evaluation of argument validity can be made.

{{< /alert >}}
</details>

3. 💡Cookiecutter sharks feed on a variety of fishes and mammals by gouging round plugs of flesh out of larger animals. Although attacks on humans are documented, they are rare, and thus these sharks are rightly classified as only a minor threat to people. As many fishes that are not a threat to humans are not endangered, there should be no objection to the new ocean exploration and drilling project, which threatens a cookiecutter shark breeding ground.

<details><summary><b><i><u>Solution</u></i></b></summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

- <u>Conclusion:</u> There should be no objection to the new ocean
exploration and drilling project.
- <u>Premise:</u> Cookiecutter sharks feed on a variety of fishes and
mammals by gouging round plugs of flesh out of larger
animals.
- <u>Premise:</u> Although attacks on humans are documented, they are
rare.
- <u>Premise:</u> Thus these sharks are rightly classified as only a minor
threat to people.
- <u>Premise:</u> As many fishes that are not a threat to humans are not
endangered.
- <u>Premise:</u> [The project] threatens a cookiecutter shark breeding
ground.

The argument is weak. The author simply notes that many fishes that are not threats are not endangered, but no information is given that establishes whether the cookiecutter shark is endangered. Without that information, the author cannot conclude that there should be no objection to the new drilling project.

{{< /alert >}}
</details>
