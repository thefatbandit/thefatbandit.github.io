Every sentence must have a ***noun (subject) that performs the action (verb)***.

## Subject-Verb Errors

### Subject/Verb Missing

A sentence missing the subject/verb is just a fragment. In GMAT, *the correct answer must be a complete sentence or independent clause*.

**E.g:**  **The cat sitting by the stairs.** ❌

{{< alert type="danger" >}}
🚨 Verbs ≠ Complete Sentence
{{< /alert >}}

Here although sitting looks like a verb, its a ***working verb*** that can run a sentence by itself.

1. **The cat sitting by the stairs WATCHED the mouse** ✅ | *<u>watched</u>* is a working verb here.
2. **The cat WAS SITTING by the stairs.** ✅ **|** *<u>sitting</u>* is called a ***present participle***

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡 "*-ing*" word by itself is never a working verb.
{{< /alert >}}

### Subject-Verb Number Disagreement

**Singular Subject ⇒ Singular Verb | Plural Subject ⇒ Plural Verb**

The GMAT test-maker sets the paper in a way that masks the subject.

**E.g.**

<details><summary>The discovery of new medicines (was/were) vital to the company's growth.</summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

*“The **discovery** of new medicines **was** vital to the company's growth.”*
    
Here both *discovery* & *new medicines* can be confused as the subjects. But the noun *medicines* is part of the prepositional phrase of *new medicines*.

{{< /alert >}}
</details>
{{< vs 1 >}}

<details><summary>Lin and Guy *drive* to work. ✅ | 
Lin, as well as Guy, *drive* to work every day. ❌</summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

In the 2nd sentence, *as well as Guy* become the modifier and leaves only *Lin* as the subject. 

So the correct sentence would be: “*Lin, as well as Guy, drives to work everyday”*

{{< /alert >}}
</details>
{{< vs 1 >}}   

{{< alert type="danger" >}}
🚨 X ***and*** Y ⇒ Plural (Subject= X and Y)
X ***as well as*** Y ⇒ Singular (Subject = X)
{{< /alert >}}

<details><summary>A complicated right answer:<br>
<mark style="background: #e06666!important">Lin</mark> <mark style="background: #00ced1!important">and</mark> <mark style="background: #e06666!important">Guy</mark> <mark>drive</mark> to work together every morning <mark style="background: #00ced1!important">and</mark> <mark>greet</mark> their co-workers cheerfully.</summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

<mark style="background: #e06666!important">Red: Subjects</mark> | <mark>Yellow: Verbs</mark> | <mark style="background: #00ced1!important">Green: Conjunctions</mark>

{{< /alert >}}
</details>

## Mitigation Strategies

Inserting ***middlemen*** ***words*** between the subject & the verb is the most common way to hide a subject. ***Remove the middlemen words***.

Furthermore, a lot of ***warmup*** words can be used in front of the subject to obscure it.

There are a few common types of middlemen and warmups.

## Dependent Clauses

*Clauses that begin with **who/because*** and cannot stand alone as sentences. 

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡 Stripping away the dependent clause still leaves out a complete sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

<details><summary><b><i><u>Eg:</u></i></b> Because she studied hard, she earned a good score on the test</summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

**Dependent:** because she studied hard

**Independent:** She earned a good score on the test.

{{< /alert >}}
</details>

## Complex Sentences

### That

The word “that” in a sentence signals that more information is coming. 

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡 ***The word “THAT” acts as a reset in a sentence.*** A new subject-verb-object structure will follow.
{{< /alert >}}

The <mark style="background: #e06666!important">teacher</mark> <mark>is</mark> confident that | her <mark style="background: #e06666!important">students</mark> <mark>mastered</mark> the <mark style="background: #00ced1!important">lesson</mark>.

<mark style="background: #e06666!important">Red: Subjects</mark> | <mark>Yellow: Verbs</mark> | <mark style="background: #00ced1!important">Green: object</mark>

***Sentences without “that”*** might seem fine in the real world, they don’t follow grammar conventions. These are called ***run-off sentences***.

**<u>Eg:</u>** 

&emsp;❌ The teacher is confident her students mastered the lesson.

&emsp;✅ The teacher is confident that her students mastered the lesson.

Without “*that*”, the sentence has an ambiguous meaning.

&emsp;❌ I know Meryl Streep is an actor.

Do we know Meryl Streep herself? Or something about her?

&emsp;✅ I know that Meryl Streep is an actor.

### Conjunctions

***Conjunctions*** can be used to connect 2 complete sentences.

Lin drove to work, but Guy rode his bike 
**Yellow:** Independent Clause | **Green:** conjunction

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡 Ind. Clause ↔ Comma-Conjunction ↔ Ind. Clause
{{< /alert >}}

But just a comma to join the sentences is not enough. This is called a run-on sentence.

&emsp;❌ Lin drove to work, Guy rode his bike 

{{< alert type="danger" >}}
🚨 Despite a conjunction being used, we need to ensure that the 2 parts being joined are in fact independent clauses.
{{< /alert >}}

**<u>Eg:</u>** 

&emsp;Andrew & Lisa are inseparable; doing everything together ❌

&emsp;Andrew & Lisa are inseparable; they do everything together ✅

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡 Commonly used 7 conjunctions for this purpose:
**<u>F</u>**or | **<u>A</u>**nd | **<u>N</u>**or | **<u>B</u>**ut | **<u>O</u>**r | **<u>Y</u>**et | **<u>S</u>**o (FANBOYS)
{{< /alert >}}

In summary take care:

- Both sides of the comma-FANBOY ⇒ Ind. Clause
- Don’t connect 2 Ind. Clauses using ONLY a comma.

### Connecting Punctuation

#### Semicolon (;) {*Ind. Clause ↔ (;) ↔ Ind. Clause*}

***Semicolon*** → Transition Expression (*however, therefore, in addition*)

**<mark style="background: #e06666!important">NOTE:</mark>** Conjunctions ⇒ comma (,) | Transitional Elements ⇒ semicolon (;)

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡 Commonly used together with “however”, “”therefore”, or “in addition” to establish relationship beyond the (***;***)
{{< /alert >}}

##### Lists

A rare but correct use is to separate items that themselves contain commas:

&emsp;❌ I listen to Earth, Wind & Fire, Wow, Owls, and Blood, Sweat & Tears.

&emsp;✅ I listen to Earth, Wind & Fire; Wow, Owls; and Blood, Sweat & Tears.

#### Colon (:) {*Ind. Clause ↔ (;) ↔ Dep./Ind. Clause*}

Provides further explanation for what comes before it.

- The ***sentence before it must be a stand alone***.
    
  &emsp;❌ I love listening to: classical, rock, rap, and pop music.
  
  &emsp;✅ I love listening to many kinds of music: classical, rock, rap, and pop.
    
- “Namely” or “that is” can be inserted after the colon.
- Whatever needs the explanation should be kept close to the colon.
- We can put a main clause after a colon as well. But it should explain the preceding clause.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡 **Semicolon:** Connects 2 related independent clauses
**Colon:** Connects a sentence with examples/explanation
{{< /alert >}}

#### Dash (—)

- A strong version of a comma, semicolon, colon.
- Sometimes it helps in retaining the meaning. You can think of it as brackets too.
    
    &emsp;❌ My three best friends, Danny, Enrico, and Joey, and I went skiing. (*maybe 7 people are going*)
    
    &emsp;✅ My three best friends — Danny, Enrico, and Joey — and I went skiing.
    
- It can also be used to restate/explain an earlier part of the sentence.
    
    &emsp;✅  Post-MBA compensation for investment bankers tends to surge far ahead that for management consultants — by tens, if not hundreds, of thousand dollars a year.
    

## Practice 1

In each of the following sentences:

(a) <mark>highlight</mark> the verb and (b) underline the subject.

(c) determine whether the subject and the verb make sense together 

(d) determine whether the subject agrees in number with the verb. If the subject is singular, the verb form must be singular. If the subject is plural, the verb form must be plural.

If the sentence is a fragment, or if the subject and verb do not make sense together, or if the
subject and verb do not agree, (e) rewrite the sentence correcting the mistake. If the sentence is correct as it is, mark it with the word CORRECT.

1. A venomous snake designated the emblem of the rebellion by the insurgency.
    - <details><summary><b><i><u>Solution</u></i></b></summary>
      {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

A venomous snake <mark>WAS designated</mark> the emblem of the rebellion by the insurgency.
        
The original is a fragment, with designated as a past participle. A simple fix is to add a form of the verb to be, such as was.

      {{< /alert >}}
      </details>
        
2. A number of players on the team have improved since the last season.
    - <details><summary><b><i><u>Solution</u></i></b></summary>
      {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

A number of players on the team <mark>have improved</mark> since the last season.

CORRECT since “A number of” is plural

      {{< /alert >}}
      </details>
        
3. The decision to place the beautiful artefacts in out-of-the-way nooks around the mansion's various rooms was inspiring.
    - <details><summary><b><i><u>Solution</u></i></b></summary>
      {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

The decision to place the beautiful artefacts in out-of-the-way nooks around the mansion's various rooms <mark>was inspiring</mark>.
  
  The various middlemen phrases beginning with to, in, and around are to be omitted.

      {{< /alert >}}
      </details>
        
4. Just around the corner is a fantastic bakery and a small supermarket.
    - <details><summary><b><i><u>Solution</u></i></b></summary>
      {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

Just around the corner <mark>ARE</mark> a fantastic bakery and a small supermarket.
        
The word and has created a compound subject which now requires a plural verb. 

      {{< /alert >}}
      </details>        

## Practice 2

Fix the following sentences if necessary:

1. The petroleum distillates were so viscous, the engineers had to heat the pipe by nearly 30 degrees.
    <details><summary><b><i><u>Solution</u></i></b></summary>
      {{< alert type="secondary" >}}
        
1. The petroleum distillates were so viscous <mark>THAT</mark> the engineers had to heat the pipe by nearly 30 degrees. (run-on Sentence)

      {{< /alert >}}
      </details>  

2. The municipality's back-to-work program has had notable success, nevertheless, it is not suitable for a state-wide rollout for several reasons.
    <details><summary><b><i><u>Solution</u></i></b></summary>
      {{< alert type="secondary" >}}
        
The municipality's back-to-work program has had notable success **<mark>;</mark>** nevertheless, it is not suitable for a state-wide rollout for several reasons.

      {{< /alert >}}
      </details>  
        
3. The Bentley trench, situated more than a mile and a half below sea level and completely covered by Antarctic glaciers, and it is the lowest point on the planet not under the oceans.
    <details><summary><b><i><u>Solution</u></i></b></summary>
      {{< alert type="secondary" >}}
        
The Bentley trench, situated more than a mile and a half below sea level and completely covered by Antarctic glaciers, **IS** the lowest point on the planet not under the oceans.

<u>Explanation:</u> The original sentence has an independent clause linked to a sentence fragment by the use of and. In the corrected version, one main clause combines all of the information given.

      {{< /alert >}}
      </details>
