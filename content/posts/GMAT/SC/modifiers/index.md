A modifier describes/provides extra information about something else in the sentence. Incorrectly used modifiers lead to ambiguity or illogical meaning.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡 Many modifiers answer the questions ***who***, ***what***, ***when***, ***where***, or ***why***
{{< /alert >}}

## Adjectives & Adverbs

These are *one-word modifiers*. 

- ***<u>Adjective</u> →*** **ONLY** Noun/Pronoun 

- ***<u>Adverb</u> →*** Almost anything EXCEPT Noun/Pronoun

  - Verbs, Adjectives
  - Prepositional Phrases
  - Entire Clauses

Many adverbs are formed by adding “*-ly”* to the adjective

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡 Often Adjective ↔ Adverb use will be interchanged as the sentence error.
{{< /alert >}}

These errors are often grammatically correct but can be found out on logical basis.

<details><summary><b><i><u>Eg:</u></i></b> Max's grandmother is his supposed Irish ancestor. ☑️ | 
Max's grandmother is his supposedly Irish ancestor. ✅✅</summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

1. Max’s grandmother is not actually his ancestor. (*Doesn’t make sense*)

2. Max’s grandmother is not actually Irish (*plausible*). 

{{< /alert >}}
</details>

### Essential Vs. Non-Essential Modifiers

- Removing ***essential modifiers*** from a sentence may compromise its meaning.
- The meaning is still retained on removing ***non-essential modifiers***.
    - “*comma-which*” is a non-essential modifier.

### Position of Noun Modifiers

The reader must know exactly which noun is the modifier modifying. 

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
🚨 ***Noun Modifiers & Nouns should be very close to each other.***
{{< /alert >}}

**<u>Eg:</u>** 

&emsp;❌ A hard worker and loyal team player, the new project was managed by Sue.

&emsp;✅ A hard worker and loyal team player, Sue managed the new project.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡 In case of multiple modifiers, the ***essential modifier is kept closer to the noun***.

&emsp;☑️ Jim biked along an **old dirt road** to get to his house, *which cut through the woods*.
<br>&emsp;✅✅ To get to his house, Jim biked along an **old dirt road**, *which cut through the woods*.
{{< /alert >}}

### Possessive Nouns ≠ Nouns

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡 Possessive Nouns can never be the subject of a modifier.
{{< /alert >}}

***Possessive Nouns = Adjectives***

<details><summary><b><i><u>Eg:</u></i></b></summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

&emsp;❌ Happy about his raise, Bill's celebration included taking his friends out to dinner.
<br>&emsp;✅ Happy about his raise, Bill celebrated by taking his friends to dinner.

{{< /alert >}}
</details>
{{< vs 1 >}}   

In the above sentence the modifier “*happy about his raise”* should be referring to Bill. But it seems like it’s modifying “*Bill’s Celebration”*.

- ***That/Whose:*** People or Things.

## Adverbial Modifiers

These modify anything that isn’t just a plain noun. (Everything ≠ Noun)

![Untitled](https://s3-us-west-2.amazonaws.com/secure.notion-static.com/bf0ba3c7-36c6-42d1-9bac-fe3cebbb4c87/Untitled.png)

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡 They answer questions like *how*, *where*, *when*, or *why* an action occurred.
{{< /alert >}}

In general adverbial modifiers can be placed anywhere in the sentence and don’t have the positional bound like noun modifiers.

It is possible to place adverbial modifiers poorly, though.

&emsp;❌ He walked and caught up with his sister more rapidly.<br>&emsp;✅ He walked more rapidly and caught with his sister.

### Sequence in Participle Modifiers

Adverbial modifiers that use participles. So the the cause-effect relationship of these should be clearly visible.

&emsp;✅ Slipping on the ice, she broke her ankle.
<br>&emsp;❌ Breaking her ankle, she slipped on the ice.
<br>&emsp;✅ She slipped on the ice, breaking her ankle.

Here *Slipping on Ice→Breaking her ankle & not the other way round.*

## Subordinators

These clauses modify the main clause to which they are attached. 

**<u>Eg:</u>** *<mark>Although the economy is strong</mark>, the RETAIL INDUSTRY IS STRUGGLING.*

It is almost like a complete sentence but has a subordinator (*although*) at the beginning.

| although | before | unless | because | that | so that |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| if | yet | after | while | since | when |

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡 The subordinator and the clause-subbordinator relationship should be consistent.

*Although the economy is strong, the retail industry is doing well.* ❌
{{< /alert >}}

#### Errors

- ***Multiple Connecting words***
    
    *ALTHOUGH I need to relax, YET I have so many things to do! ❌*
    
- ***Connecting with the right meaning***
    
    If the sentence parts are an opposition we should expect contrasting connecting words between them.
    
    &emsp;She is not interested in playing sports, AND she likes watching them on TV. ❌<br>&emsp;She is not interested in playing sports, BUT she likes watching them on TV. ✅<br>&emsp;ALTHOUGH she is not interested in playing sports, she likes watching them on TV.✅
    
- 💡 ***Main Clause + (Not a Clause)***
    
    *Citizens of many countries are expressing concern about the environmental damage cause by the widespread release of greenhouse gases may be impossible to reverse.* ❌
    
    Here there is no subject for the clause “may be impossible to reverse”. You may want to think that environmental damage is the subject. But its part of a prepositional phrase & can never be the subject of a clause.
    
    So we use either *<u>remove the prepositional phrase</u>*:
    
    *Citizens of many countries are expressing concern <u>THAT</u> the environmental damage cause by the widespread release of greenhouse gases may be impossible to reverse*
    
    Or make the 2nd clause an additional clause:
    
    *Citizens of many countries are expressing concern about the environmental damage cause by the widespread release of greenhouse gases, <u>DAMAGE THAT may be impossible to reverse</u>.*
    

## 🚨Which🚨 (Difficult to trap)

WHICH always refers to the closest preceding noun.

**Eg:** 

&emsp;❌ Crime has recently decreased in <u>our neighborhood</u>, <u>WHICH has led to a rise in property values</u>.

But here it is being used to refer to an entire clause.

{{< alert type="danger" >}}
🚨 Use WHICH *only* to refer to nouns — never to refer to an entire clause.
{{< /alert >}}
### Corrections

1. Turn the first thought into a noun phrase and make this phrase the subject of the verb, eliminating WHICH altogether:
    
    *The recent <u>decrease</u> in crime in our neighbourhood <u>has led</u> to a rise in property values.*
    
2. Use an adverbial modifier to refer to the whole clause:
    
    *<u>Crime has recently decreased</u> in our neighbourhood, <u>leading</u> to a rise in property values.*
    

## Modifiers Vs. Core

2 long non-essential modifiers in a row.

**<u>Eg:</u>**

&emsp;❌ George Carlin, <u>both shocking and entertaining audiences across the nation</u>, <u>who also struggled publicly with drug abuse</u>, influenced and inspired a generation of comedians.

We can place 1 modifier before & 1 after the noun: (Modifier ↔ Noun ↔ Modifier)

&emsp;✅ Both shocking and entertaining audiences across the nation, George Carlin, who also struggled publicly with drug abuse, influenced and inspired a generation of comedians.

On the tougher questions, ***one modifier can be made part of the sentence***:

&emsp;✅ Both shocking and entertaining audiences across the nation, George Carlin influenced and inspired a generation of comedians yet struggled publicly with drug abuse.

##### **<u>Money & Volume</u>**

Dollars and gallons (unit nouns) by nature, are countable. But these nouns represent uncountable quantities like money and volume: one money, two moneys,... (?)

#### Comparative Vs. Superlative (*Words Relating 2 Things Vs. > 2 Things*)

- Comparative form of adjectives/adverbs (better, worse, more, less) for 2 things.
- Superlative form of adjectives/adverbs (best, worst, most, least) for > 2 things.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡 2 Things ⇒ BETWEEN | >2 Things ⇒ AMONG

&emsp;✅ I mediated a dispute BETWEEN Maya and Kalen.
<br>&emsp;✅ I mediated a dispute AMONG Maya, Logan, and Kalen.
{{< /alert >}}

#### The Word “*<u>Numbers</u>*” (*COUNTABLE*)

Use Greater Than Vs. ~~More than~~

&emsp;✅ The rare Montauk beaked griffin is not extinct; its NUMBERS are now suspected to be much GREATER than before.

#### Increase/Decrease Vs. Greater/Less

- **Increase/Decrease -** Signal change of a thing over time.
- **Greater/Less -** Signals comparison between 2 things.

Watch out for *redundancies* with increase/decrease:

&emsp;❌ The price of silver FELL by a more than 35% DECREASE.
<br>&emsp;✅ The price of silver DECREASED by more than 35%.
<br>&emsp;✅ The price of silver FELL by more than 35%.

## “THAT” in a sentence

#### “That” is a modifier

{{< alert type="danger" >}}
🚨 “***That”***, “***Which”***, “***Who”***, & “***Where”*** modify the immediately preceding noun.
{{< /alert >}}

#### “That of” is used for comparison

&emsp;❌ The debt-ratio of Greece is greater than Japan. (Greece’s Debt Ratio ↔ Japan)
<br>&emsp;✅ The debt-ratio of Greece is greater than ***THAT OF*** Japan. (Debt-Ratios of 2 countries compared)
    

#### Parallelism in “that”

Care must be taken to use that in the parallel clauses.

<details><summary>❌ I believe that Santa Clause is real and the Knicks will win the NBA title.
<br>&emsp;✅ I believe that Santa Clause is real and <b><i>THAT</i></b> the Knicks will win the NBA title.</summary>
{{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### **Reason**

Here in the 1st portion, the “Santa Clause is real” is part of the belief but that Knicks will be treated as an absolute truth. While in the 2nd sentence, the “that” creates a correct parallel and the Knicks winning is also part of the belief now.

{{< /alert >}}
</details> 

#### Exception of *“that”* modifier rule (Idiomatic Phrases)

Although the general rule to “that” as a modifier holds, there are cases where it should not be blindly applied. 

**Eg:** Here we can’t simply use “gun” as the subject of our modifier.

&emsp;❌ The son of a *gun* **THAT** stole my lunch deserves to die.<br>&emsp;✅ The *son of a gun* **THAT** stole my lunch deserves to die.

## Practice-2

The following four sentences contain <u>**highlighted**</u> sections. Use the rules in this chapter to correct any errors that you can find in them. Do not change anything that is not highlighted.

1. Jim is trying to reduce the <u>**number**</u> of soda that he drinks <u>**,**</u> at last night’s party, however, his resolve to drink <u>**fewer**</u> soda was sorely tested <u>**,**</u> he found himself quaffing <u>**many**</u> of the sodas.

   <details><summary><b><u>Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

  Jim is trying to reduce the <u>**AMOUNT**</u> of soda that he drinks <u>**;**</u> at last night’s party, however, his resolve to drink <u>**LESS**</u> soda was sorely tested <u>**, AND**</u>he found himself quaffing <u>**A NUMBER**</u> of the sodas.
        
   1. Number should be amount or quantity. Here soda is an uncountable substance-otherwise, soda would be sodas.
   2. The comma after drinks should be a semicolon, which would appropriately separate two independent clauses: Jim is trying ... that he drinks and at last night's party ... sorely tested.
   3. Fewer should be Jess. Once again, regard soda as an uncountable substance.
   4. An and should be inserted after the comma after tested. This placement of and appropriately separates two main clauses: his resolve ... was sorely tested and he found ... sodas.
   5. Many should be a number. Since there is an -son the end of sodas, you know that sodas are now thought of as countable things-presumably servings of soda. A number of is an appropriate modifier for countable things.

{{< /alert >}}
</details>
{{< vs 1 >}}
        
2. **<u>Between</u>** 1998 and 2003, there was heavy fighting in Parthia <u>**between**</u> numerous armed factions <u>**yet**</u> this conflict, so much more complicated than a conventional war between two states, involved no <u>**less**</u> that eight countries and twenty-five militias.

   <details><summary><b><u>Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

   **<u>Between</u>** 1998 and 2003, there was heavy fighting in Parthia <u>**AMONG**</u> numerous armed factions <u>**;**</u> this conflict, so much more complicated than a conventional war between two states, involved no <u>**FEWER**</u> that eight countries and twenty-five militias.

   1. The first between is correct, since only two dates are mentioned.
   The second between should be among, since the fighting involved more than two factions.
   2. Yet should be a semicolon. Yet is illogical because the action in the second clause (this conflict ... militias) did not happen despite the action in the first clause (Between .. factions), as a word such as yet suggests.
   3. The third between is correct, since only two states are mentioned.
   4. Less should be fewer, because countries and militias are countable entities.

{{< /alert >}}
</details>
{{< vs 1 >}}

3. Most legislators—including <u>**MUCH**</u> in the governor's own party—realise that the governor's budget would imperil the state's finances <u>**, nonetheless**</u>, the budget is likely to be approved <u>**, because**</u> few legislators want to anger voters by cutting spending or raising taxes.

   <details><summary><b><u>Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

Most legislators—including **<u>MANY</u>** in the governor's own party—realise that the governor's budget would imperil the state's finances <u>**; nonetheless**</u>, the budget is likely to
be approved, because few legislators want to anger voters by cutting spending or raising taxes.

1. Much should be many, because legislators are countable.
2. Nonetheless should be preceded by a semicolon. The clauses it connects are Most legislators ... state's finances and the budget is likely to be approved.
3. The comma before because is correct. Because is a subordinating conjunction; therefore, it can be separated from a main clause by a comma.
