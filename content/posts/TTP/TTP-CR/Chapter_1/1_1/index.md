## Lesson Content
In the verbal reasoning section of the GMAT, between 9 and 13 of the 36 questions that appear will be Critical Reasoning questions.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In the verbal reasoning section of the GMAT, between 9 and 13 of the 36 questions that appear will be Critical Reasoning questions.
{{< /alert >}}

Critical Reasoning questions fall into the following eleven major question types:

(1) Identify the Assumption

These questions ask us to identify an assumption upon which an argument depends.

> <em>Which of the following is an assumption logically necessary for coming to the conclusion of the above argument?</em>

(2) Weaken the Argument

These questions ask us to find a statement of fact that would weaken or hurt an argument.

> <em>Which of the following, if true, most seriously weakens the argument above?</em>

(3) Strengthen the Argument

These questions ask us to find a statement of fact that would help an argument or support a conclusion.

> <em>Which of the following, if true, most strongly supports the hypothesis?</em>

(4) Resolve a Paradox

These questions ask us to find a statement of fact that would resolve what appears to be (but is not actually) a paradox.

> <em>Which of the following statements, if true, would best resolve the apparent contradiction described above?</em>

(5) Inference

These questions ask us to find an unwritten conclusion that must be true given what a passage says.

> <em>If the statements above are true, which of the following can be properly inferred from them?</em>

(6) Find the Conclusion

These questions ask us to find a conclusion that is supported by a passage.

> <em>If the statements above are true, which of the following is most strongly supported by them?</em>

(7) Evaluate the Argument

These questions ask us to find an answer choice that helps us to determine whether an argument is well constructed.

> <em>In evaluating the argument, it would be most useful to establish which of the following?</em>

(8) Logical Flaw

These questions ask us to find a flaw in the reasoning of an argument.

> <em>Which of the following best describes the error of reasoning contained in the argument above?</em>

(9) Complete the Passage

These questions ask us to find an answer that best completes an uncompleted passage.

> <em>Which of the following best completes the passage?</em>

(10) Method of Reasoning

These questions ask us to identify the way in which a passage is logically organized or structured.

> <em>The author develops the argument by</em>

(11) Boldface

These questions ask us to determine what functions portions of passages play in arguments.

> <em>In the argument given, the two boldfaced portions play which of the following roles?</em>

Don’t worry if any (or all) of these question types seem new to you.
