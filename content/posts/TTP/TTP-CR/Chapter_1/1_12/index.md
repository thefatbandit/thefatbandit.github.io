## Lesson Content
To do well in Critical Reasoning, we must see the logical implications of statements in Critical Reasoning passages and answer choices.

A logical implication is simply the logical consequence of a set of facts or conditions.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A logical implication is simply the logical consequence of a set of facts or conditions.
{{< /alert >}}

We can present a logical implication with an “if/then” statement.

In the above “if/then” statement, Y is the logical implication of X.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

We can present a logical implication with an “if/then” statement.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s consider some examples:

> - If the service at a restaurant improves, then a smaller percentage of patrons will complain to management.

It makes sense that, the better the service at a restaurant is, the smaller will be the percentage of its patrons complaining to management.

> - If it is easier to get to a store, then more people will shop there.

We’d expect that, all else equal, as the ease of getting to a store goes up, the number of people who shop there will increase.

> - If the price of fuel for cars increases, then demand for more fuel-efficient cars will increase, and demand for less-fuel efficient cars will decrease.

Increased demand for more fuel-efficient cars and decreased demand for less fuel-efficient cars are logical implications of an increase in price for fuel for cars.

> - If people can make something more efficiently, then they can produce more of that thing in the same amount of time.

We can see that it makes perfect sense that, when people can make something more efficiently, they can produce more of it in the same amount of time.

> - If the number of vehicles a city owns increases, then its vehicle maintenance expenses will increase.

We’d expect that, as the number of vehicles owned by a city increases, the cost to maintain those vehicles also increases.

In GMAT Critical Reasoning, we can, and are expected to, use common sense to determine what the logical implications of statements in the passages and answer choices are.

💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In GMAT Critical Reasoning, we can, and are expected to, use common sense to determine what the logical implications of statements in the passages and answer choices are.
{{< /alert >}}

For example, let’s say a Critical Reasoning answer choice says the following:

> <em>It has become more difficult to park close to the restaurant.</em>

We can use common sense to determine what the logical implications of that answer choice are.

Let’s consider another example:

> <em>It is more profitable for manufacturing companies to use method A than to use method B.</em>

It’s common knowledge that, generally, companies seek to be profitable.

Let’s consider one more example of a statement we could see in a Critical Reasoning passage:

> <em>Cenospheres are microscopic spheres that form only when hydrocarbons burn.</em>

In this case, the logical implication is that, if cenospheres form only when hydrocarbons burn, then if no hydrocarbons burn, no cenospheres will form.

Country X gets all its sugar from two sugar cane farms. One of these farms is destroyed during a hurricane, with the result that there is 50 percent drop in the number of kilograms of sugar produced per month in Country X.

1. [x] (A) The cost of sugar in Country X will increase.
2. [ ] (B) The cost of sugar in Country X will decrease.
    > <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) The cost of sugar in Country X will not change.
    > <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

3. [ ] (C) There will be no change in the number of people dining at Restaurant R.

##  Example 3 

In the United States, from 1920 to 1930, the number of automobiles in use increased significantly.

Which of the following, if true about the United States between 1920 and 1930, most helps to explain why the price of crude oil decreased despite the increase in demand for gasoline?

1. [ ] (A) In the United States, the average automobile burned approximately the same amount of fuel per mile in 1930 as the average automobile burned in 1920.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) Between 1920 and 1930, discoveries of new oil fields in the United States allowed producers to increase production of crude oil by over 100 percent.
3. [ ] (C) United States oil producers exported significantly more crude oil in 1930 than they did in 1920.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) From 1920 to 1930, the number of refineries that could produce gasoline from crude oil in the United States increased substantially.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) In the United States, from 1920 to 1930, it cost less per barrel for a large oil company to produce crude oil than it did for a small oil company to produce crude oil.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: N/A



We see that the passage in this question presents a scenario in which demand for gasoline increased, but the price of crude oil, from which gasoline is derived, decreased.


Then, the question stem asks us to find a choice that “most helps to explain why the price of crude oil decreased despite the increase in demand for gasoline.”


If we think about this question in terms of logical implications, we see that the correct answer must have the following logical implication:


If the correct answer is true, a likely result is that the price of oil decreased.


So, let’s go through the choices to find one that has that implication.


> <b>(A) In the United States, the average automobile burned approximately the same amount of fuel per mile in 1930 as the average automobile burned in 1920.
</b>


 Notice that the implication of this choice is what we already know from the passage. After all, if the average automobile burned approximately the same amount of fuel per mile in 1930 as the average automobile burned in 1920, then we would expect that, as the number of automobiles increased, the amount of gasoline demanded would also increase. And in fact, the passage says, “the number of automobiles in use increased significantly. As a result, the demand for gasoline also increased.”


Since we don’t need to explain why the demand for gasoline increased as the number of automobiles increased, this choice doesn’t do what the correct answer must do.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B) Between 1920 and 1930, discoveries of new oil fields in the United States allowed producers to increase production of crude oil by over 100 percent.
</b>


 Let’s think about the implications of this choice.


What would be the logical result of the fact that discoveries of new oil fields in the United States allowed producers to increase production of crude oil by over 100 percent? All else equal, the result would be a reduction in the price of crude oil. Right?


After all, if producers were able to increase production of crude oil by over 100 percent, then the supply of crude oil grew significantly, and it’s common knowledge that, when the supply of something increases, all else equal, the price of that thing decreases.


Now, it’s true that all else was not equal, since demand for gasoline, which is derived from crude oil, increased. All the same, since an increase in the supply of crude  oil would have had the effect of pushing the price of crude oil lower, this choice helps to explain why, even though demand for gasoline increased, the price of crude oil decreased.


> <b>(C) United States oil producers exported significantly more crude oil in 1930 than they did in 1920.
</b>


 Notice that an implication of this choice is that demand for crude oil was even stronger than the information in the passage indicated. After all, if United States oil producers exported significantly more crude oil in 1930 than they did in 1920, then not only did demand for gasoline, which is derived from crude oil, increase in the United States, but also the amount of United States crude oil used outside the United States increased.


We can see that the fact that there was demand for United States crude oil outside the United States would not explain why the price of crude oil decreased. The fact that there was an additional source of demand would be expected to cause the price to increase, not decrease.


> <b>(D) From 1920 to 1930, the number of refineries that could produce gasoline from crude oil in the United States increased substantially.
</b>


 The fact that the number of refineries that could produce gasoline from crude oil in the United States increased substantially doesn’t have clear implications with regard to the price of crude oil. Supply of and demand for crude oil itself would affect the price, not the fact that refineries could produce gasoline from crude oil.


That said, if anything, the increase in the number of refineries could perhaps have explained an increase in the price of crude oil. After all, an increase in the number of refineries could have resulted in an increase in the rate at which crude oil was used to make gasoline and thus resulted in an increase in demand for crude oil. So, if anything, this choice does the opposite of what the correct answer must do.


> <b>(E) In the United States, from 1920 to 1930, it cost less per barrel for a large oil company to produce crude oil than it did for a small oil company to produce crude oil.
</b>


 The fact that it cost less per barrel for a large oil company to produce crude oil than it did for a small oil company to produce crude oil doesn’t have any clear implications with regard to the price of crude oil. After all, we don’t have information about the sizes of the companies that produced crude oil between 1920 and 1930. So, we have no reason to believe that the sizes of oil companies had anything to do with the decrease in the price of crude oil.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

##  Example 4 

The teachers at Wildwood High School plan to begin using a method of teaching math in the classroom that has been shown to be much more effective than the method the school uses currently.

Which of the following, if true, most strengthens the argument?

1. [ ] (A) Teachers at Wildwood High School have been teaching math using the current method for over five years.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) The teachers at Wildwood High School do not have plans to change the methods they use for teaching reading or science.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) Most of the students at Wildwood High School attend almost all of their math classes.
4. [ ] (D) Historically, the performance in math of Wildwood High School students has been higher than the national average.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) For some Wildwood High School students, doing homework thoroughly is much more important for their math performance than learning in the classroom.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: N/A



Let’s break down the argument presented by the passage:


<u>Premise</u>: The teachers at Wildwood High School plan to begin using a method of teaching math in the classroom that has been shown to be much more effective than the method the school uses currently.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Clearly, therefore, the performance in math of Wildwood High School students will improve.


We see that the argument uses the fact that the teachers at Wildwood High School plan to use a more effective method of teaching math in the classroom to support the conclusion that the performance in math of the students will improve.


Then, the question asks, “Which of the following, if true, most strengthens the argument?”


So, the correct answer to this question must strengthen the argument. In other words, it must provide additional support for the conclusion.


We can also think about the correct answer in terms of logical implications. To strengthen the argument, the correct answer must have the following logical implication:


If the correct answer is true, the likely result is that the performance in math of Wildwood High School Students will improve.


So, let’s consider the answer choices with these ideas in mind.


> <b>(A) Teachers at Wildwood High School have been teaching math using the current method for over five years.
</b>


 The fact that teachers at Wildwood High School have been using the current method to teach math for over five years has no implications for the effect of switching to a more effective method of teaching math. After all, regardless of how long the teachers at Wildwood High School have been using the current method, switching to the new method may or may not result in an improvement in students’ performance in math.


> <b>(B) The teachers at Wildwood High School do not have plans to change the methods they use for teaching reading or science.
</b>


 The fact that teachers at Wildwood High School do not have plans to change the methods they use for teaching reading or science has no implications for the effect of switching to a more effective method of teaching math. After all, regardless of whether the teachers change the methods they use for teaching reading or science, switching to the new method of teaching math may or may not result in an improvement in students’ performance in math.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) Most of the students at Wildwood High School attend almost all of their math classes.
</b>


 Let’s think about the logical implications of this choice in the context of what we already know about Wildwood High School. The passage tells us that the teachers plan to begin using a more effective method of teaching math in the classroom.


Now, this choice tells us that students at Wildwood High attend most of their math classes. So, what are the implications of that fact?


Well, one of the implications is that the students <em>will be in class</em> when the teachers use the more effective method for teaching math. As a result, the teachers will be able to teach the students by using the new method in the classroom, and thus, there’s reason to believe that the student’s performance in math will indeed improve because of the teachers’ using the new method.


Thus, we can see that this choice strengthens the argument, because its implications give us more reason to believe that the students’ performance in math will improve.


> <b>(D) Historically, the performance in math of Wildwood High School students has been higher than the national average.
</b>


 The fact that the performance in math of Wildwood High School students has been higher than the national average has no clear implications for the effect of switching to a more effective method of teaching math. After all, regardless of whether the performance in math of the students has been higher than the national average, switching to the new method of teaching math may or may not result in an improvement in the students’ performance in math.


> <b>(E) For some Wildwood High School students, doing homework thoroughly is much more important for their math performance than learning in the classroom.
</b>


 Notice that, if anything, the implications of this choice are the opposite of what we need. After all, one implication of this choice is that changing the way math is taught in the classroom may not affect some students’ math performance. After all, if doing homework is much more important for these students’ math performance than learning in the classroom, then a change in what goes on in the classroom may not make much difference in how these students do.


In other words, an implication of this choice is that, at least in the cases of some students, the teachers’ using a more effective method may not result in improvements in math performance.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

There are some common Critical Reasoning mistakes that we see people make again and again.
