## Lesson Content
Often, because they want to save time, or simply because they don’t feel like reading every word, test-takers skip or gloss over words in Critical Reasoning passages, question stems, or answer choices or decide not to read the last several words of a passage, question stem, or answer choice.

In fact, what often happens is that a test-taker who has skipped words in a Critical Reasoning question flails around and repeatedly goes through the answer choices without finding the correct answer because, in skipping those words, the test-taker has failed to acquire the information necessary for answering the question.

Of course, a test-taker’s skipping words in a question can also result in the test-taker’s not finding the correct answer at all, choosing an incorrect choice, and missing the question.

So, we can see that carefully reading every word of a Critical Reasoning question can end up being not only the surest but also the shortest path to the correct answer.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Skipping words in reading Critical Reasoning passages, question stems, or answer choices can be devastating to Critical Reasoning performance. So, to maximize performance, when answering a Critical Reasoning question, carefully read every word.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s consider an example to see how our performance can be affected by whether we skip words or carefully read every word of a Critical Reasoning question.

##  Example 5 

In the past, most purchasers of automobiles prioritized performance over eco-friendliness when considering which automobile to purchase.

Which of the following, if true, would cast the most doubt on the conclusion of the argument above?

1. [ ] (A) Automobiles are, on average, more eco-friendly than they were in the past.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) In general, using public transportation is more eco-friendly than using even the most eco-friendly automobile.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) Currently, people are generally more aware of the ecological effects of automobile use than they were in the past.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) A large percentage of people consider durability the most important quality of an automobile.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) Currently, many of the most eco-friendly new automobiles are also the highest performing new automobiles.

##### Question Level: easy



The passage in this question states the following conclusion:


<u>Conclusion</u>: It must be the case that, now, more people than ever prioritize eco-friendliness over performance in choosing among automobiles.


The passage provides the following premise to support the conclusion:


<u>Premise</u>: In recent years, the percentage of people choosing to purchase the most eco-friendly automobiles available has increased significantly.


We see that the question stem is the following:


Which of the following, if true, would cast the most doubt on the conclusion of the argument above?


So, to correctly answer the question we have to find the choice that casts the most doubt on the conclusion.


Now, here’s the thing.


If we read the conclusion in its entirety, we’re likely to answer this question correctly, but if we gloss over or fail to read some words of the conclusion, we could easily end up choosing an incorrect answer.


For instance, in reading the conclusion, we could gloss over the words “over performance,” reading the conclusion in the following way:


It must be the case that, now, more people than ever prioritize eco-friendliness <s>over performance</s> in choosing among automobiles.


Notice what happens if we gloss over “over performance.” The conclusion could seem to be that eco-friendliness is people’s <em>top</em> or <em>main</em> priority in choosing an automobile. So, by glossing over “over performance,” we could get the impression that the conclusion is different from what it is.


Another thing we could do in reading the conclusion is fail to read the last few words, reading the conclusion in the following way:


It must be the case that, now, more people than ever prioritize eco-friendliness over performance <s>in choosing among automobiles</s>.


See what happens if we don’t read the last few words? If we fail to read the last few words, we could get the impression that the conclusion is that people prioritize eco-friendliness over performance <em>in general</em>, not just “in choosing among automobiles.”


OK, let’s now review the answer choices and see how glossing over or failing to read some words of the conclusion could affect how we answer this question.


> <b>(A) Automobiles are, on average, more eco-friendly than they were in the past.
</b>


 Regardless of how eco-friendly cars are, the fact that the percentage of people choosing to purchase the <em>most</em> eco-friendly automobiles available has increased significantly still supports the conclusion that, now, more people than ever prioritize eco-friendliness over performance in choosing among automobiles.


So, the fact that cars are, on average, more eco-friendly than they were in the past does not cast any doubt on the conclusion.


> <b>(B) In general, using public transportation is more eco-friendly than using even the most eco-friendly automobile.
</b>


 Eliminating this choice should be fairly straightforward since the conclusion of the argument is about people’s prioritizing eco-friendliness <em>in choosing among automobiles</em>, while this choice expresses a comparison of the eco-friendliness of public transportation and the eco-friendliness of automobiles. In other words, the fact that using public transportation is more eco-friendly than using an automobile does not indicate that, in choosing among automobiles, people are not prioritizing eco-friendliness over performance.


Notice, however, what could happen if we were to fail to read the last few words of the conclusion and misread it in the following way:


It must be the case that, now, more people than ever prioritize eco-friendliness over performance <s>in choosing among automobiles</s>.


By failing to read the last few words of the conclusion, we could perceive the conclusion to be that, now, more people than ever prioritize eco-friendliness over performance <em>in general</em>. In that case, we could get the impression that this choice casts doubt on the conclusion.


After all, if it is true that using public transportation is more eco-friendly than using even the most eco-friendly automobile, then we could make the case that people who are purchasing automobiles, rather than using public transportation, are not prioritizing eco-friendliness over performance in general. Thus, we could make the case that this choice casts doubt on our mistaken version of the conclusion.


You may think that you would never choose a choice on the basis of such shaky reasoning, but remember, when taking the GMAT, you will be operating under time constraints and will have to make relatively quick decisions regarding which choices are incorrect or correct, and any choice that seems to cast doubt on the conclusion could be very tempting.


The moral of the story is that reading every word of the question is important for Critical Reasoning success.


> <b>(C) Currently, people are generally more aware of the ecological effects of automobile use than they were in the past.
</b>


 What this choice says is in line with the conclusion that more people than ever prioritize eco-friendliness over performance in choosing among automobiles.


In fact, what this choice says could explain <em>why</em> more people than ever prioritize eco-friendliness over performance in choosing among automobiles. After all, people’s being more aware of the ecological effects of automobile use could cause them to prioritize eco-friendliness over performance in choosing an automobile.


So, this choice certainly does not cast doubt on the conclusion.


> <b>(D) A large percentage of people consider durability the most important quality of an automobile.
</b>


 Eliminating this choice should be fairly straightforward.


The conclusion of the argument is that more people than ever prioritize eco-friendliness over performance in choosing an automobile, and more people than ever could do so regardless of how many people consider durability the most important quality of an automobile. After all, even if people’s order of priorities is (1) durability, (2) eco-friendliness, and (3) performance, they are prioritizing eco-friendliness over performance, even though durability is their number one priority.


So, what this choice says cast no doubt whatsoever on the conclusion.


Notice, however, what could happen if we were to gloss over the words “over performance” and misread the conclusion in the following way:


It must be the case that, now, more people than ever prioritize eco-friendliness <s>over performance</s> in choosing among automobiles.


If we gloss over “over performance,” the conclusion could seem to be that eco-friendliness is people’s <em>top</em> or <em>main</em> priority in choosing an automobile. Of course, in that case, we could easily get the impression that what this choice says, that a large percentage of people consider durability the most important quality of an automobile, casts doubt on our mistaken version of the conclusion. After all, if a large percentage of people consider durability the most important quality of an automobile, then it could seem plausible that eco-friendliness is not the top or main priority of more people than ever.


You may think that you would never choose an answer choice on the basis of such shaky reasoning, but remember, when taking the GMAT, you will be operating under time constraints and will have to make relatively quick decisions regarding which choices are incorrect or correct, and any choice that seems to cast doubt on the conclusion could be very tempting.


The moral of the story is that reading every word of the question is important for Critical Reasoning success.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E) Currently, many of the most eco-friendly new automobiles are also the highest performing new automobiles.
</b>


 What this choice says casts doubt on the conclusion.


The conclusion that, now, more people than ever prioritize eco-friendliness over performance in choosing among automobiles is supported by the premise that the percentage of people choosing to purchase the most eco-friendly automobiles available has increased significantly.


However, what if, as this choice says, many of the most eco-friendly new automobiles are also the highest performing ones? In that case, it could very well be that people are not prioritizing eco-friendliness over performance and are purchasing the most eco-friendly automobiles only because the most eco-friendly automobiles are also the highest performing automobiles.


So, what this choice says is a reason to believe that, even though the percentage of people choosing to purchase the most eco-friendly automobiles available has increased, it could very well be that the conclusion is not correct.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

Let’s now discuss why we should not eliminate Critical Reasoning answer choices just because they bring in new information.
