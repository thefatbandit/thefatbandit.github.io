## Lesson Content
Many students lack sufficient understanding of what GMAT Critical Reasoning questions test; they don’t understand the skills required to perform well on these questions.

GMAT Critical Reasoning questions are designed to test our skill in making, analyzing, and evaluating arguments and plans.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

GMAT Critical Reasoning questions are designed to test our skill in making, analyzing, and evaluating arguments and plans.
{{< /alert >}}

Correctly answering these questions requires using what the makers of the GMAT call higher-order thinking skills to reason your way to the correct choice.

It’s helpful to know what Critical Reasoning is not.

What GMAT Critical Reasoning is not:

> - GMAT Critical Reasoning is not a word matching game.

> - GMAT Critical Reasoning is not really a test of how well we have memorized information, such as a list of Critical Reasoning question types.

> - GMAT Critical reasoning is not a test of how well we use simple strategies to find correct answers.

Now let’s discuss what GMAT Critical Reasoning is.

> - vision

> - attention to detail.

> - skill in the use of logic.

> - avoiding falling into mental traps and cognitive biases.

> - emphasizing careful, clear thinking and minimizing shallow thinking.

> - good judgment.

> - execution of best practices.

We can distil all the above into the same three main aspects that are involved in answering GMAT questions of any type.

Arriving at the correct answers to Critical Reasoning questions depends on:

> - What you know.

> - What you see.

> - What you do.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Arriving at the correct answers to GMAT Critical Reasoning questions generally involves three aspects, what we know, what we see, and what we do.
{{< /alert >}}

As this chapter progresses, and in the Critical Reasoning Chapters that follow, we’re going to help you to develop every skill you need to correctly and efficiently answer Critical Reasoning questions.
