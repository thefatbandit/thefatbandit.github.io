## Lesson Content
Evidence or premises are facts that support the conclusion and are used to build the argument.

> Let’s consider our simple example again:

<em>Critical Reasoning is like a game. Games are fun. Critical Reasoning must be fun.</em>

In the argument above, the premises that support the conclusion are “Critical Reasoning is like a game,” and “Games are fun.” Those statements support the conclusion "Critical Reasoning must be fun."

Evidence provided by authors of Critical Reasoning questions comes in many forms.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Evidence or premises are facts that support the conclusion and are used to build the argument.
{{< /alert >}}

Remember that a statement of fact is never an opinion.

> <em>The study found that people who eat at least five servings of fruit each day have significantly lower rates of tooth decay than are had by people who do not. </em>

> <em>The town of West Hills recently began adding chlorine to the public water supply.</em>

> <em>Starting tomorrow, park rangers will begin flying over the park aerial drones equipped with thermal cameras able to detect campfires.</em>

> <em>The Mars Company saw a 300 percent increase in customer complaints last month. </em>

> <em>None of the students in the class have taken a course in logic.</em>

> <em>Scientists found shark fossils in a dry lake located in what is now central Florida. </em>

> <em>The council of Gloucester passed a new law making it legal for police to stop and issue a traffic ticket to any driver seen not wearing a seat belt while driving in Gloucester.</em>

Notice that each of the statements above is factual; there is no opinion component to them.

It is important to know that we never argue with evidence presented to us in Critical Reasoning questions.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In answering Critical Reasoning questions, we never argue with evidence presented to us. If something is presented as a fact, we must consider it a fact.
{{< /alert >}}

We’ll have more to say about evidence as the course progresses.
