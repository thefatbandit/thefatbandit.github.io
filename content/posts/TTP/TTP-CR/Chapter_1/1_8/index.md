## Lesson Content
A vital part of any argument is its assumptions.

An assumption is an unstated piece of information that must be true if the logic of an argument is to work.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

An assumption is an unstated piece of information that must be true if the logic of the argument is to work.
{{< /alert >}}

It can be helpful to think of an argument as a math problem:

> (any background info)+<strong> Evidence + Assumptions = Conclusion</strong>

In other words, think of assumptions as the unstated glue, the missing information, that connects the evidence to the conclusion.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Think of assumptions as the unstated glue, the missing information, that connects the evidence to the conclusion. Without this glue, the argument will fall apart, because, without this glue, the evidence provided does not support the argument's conclusion.
{{< /alert >}}

Because the entire next chapter is devoted to assumptions, we won’t say anything further here about assumptions.

##  Example 2 

The Town of Seaford is projected to have a sizable budget deficit in the coming year.

Which of the following does the author assume in arriving at the above conclusion?

1. [ ] (A) Tax rates set by the Town of Seaford could be increased substantially.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) If the Town of Seaford does not cover its budget deficit, it will be forced to declare bankruptcy in the coming year.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) People will continue to use the North Bay Bridge to get to Seaford even if they must pay a toll to do so.
4. [ ] (D) The Town of Seaford has been spending money on wasteful projects.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) The number of cars that cross the North Bay Bridge every day will not increase in the near future.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



The passage in this question starts with some background information:


<u>Background</u>: The Town of Seaford is projected to have a sizable budget deficit in the coming year.


This background information provides context for the argument that follows.


The passage then provides a premise to support a conclusion:


<u>Premise</u>: Every day, over 20,000 cars cross the North Bay Bridge into Seaford.


Finally, the passage states the conclusion of the argument:


<u>Conclusion</u>: By charging a toll to cross the North Bay Bridge, the Town of Seaford can generate enough revenue to cover its budget deficit.


We can see that the background information and premise are facts, while the conclusion is an opinion supported by the premise, and that the background information, premise, and conclusion work together to present an understandable, logical argument.


Having read the passage, we come to the question stem:


<u>Question Stem</u>: Which of the following does the author assume in arriving at the above conclusion?


So, to correctly answer the question we have to find an assumption that the author has made in arriving at the conclusion.


> <b>(A) Tax rates set by the Town of Seaford could be increased substantially.
</b>


 The author does not have to assume that tax rates could be increased in order to conclude that the Town of Seaford could cover its budget deficit by charging a toll for crossing the bridge. After all, it’s toll revenue, not tax revenue, that is expected to cover the deficit.


> <b>(B) If the Town of Seaford does not cover its budget deficit, it will be forced to declare bankruptcy in the coming year.
</b>


 This choice does not have the effect we need. This choice describes a consequence of not covering the budget deficit: the town will have to declare bankruptcy.


The author does not have to assume anything about consequences of not covering the deficit to conclude that the town can cover the deficit by charging a toll to cross the bridge.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) People will continue to use the North Bay Bridge to get to Seaford even if they must pay a toll to do so.
</b>


 This choice states an assumption that the author must be making in arriving at the conclusion of the argument.


The author supports the conclusion that the Town of Seaford can cover its budget deficit by charging a toll for crossing the North Bay Bridge by using the premise that over 20,000 cars cross the bridge every day. Of course, the author is reasoning that, if the town were to receive a toll for each of 20,000 cars every day, the town would generate a significant amount of revenue over the coming year.


However, what if people use the North Bay Bridge only because there is no toll? In that case, it’s possible that, if the town were to charge a toll for crossing the bridge, people wouldn’t continue to use the bridge, in which case the town would not be able to cover its budget deficit by charging a toll to cross the bridge.


So, we can see that, in arriving at the argument’s conclusion, the author must be assuming that people <em>will</em> continue to use the North Bay Bridge even if they must pay a toll in order to do so.


> <b>(D) The Town of Seaford has been spending money on wasteful projects.
</b>


 This choice has the wrong effect. The correct answer will state something that the author is assuming in arriving at the conclusion, but what this choice does is provide a possible explanation for why the Town of Seaford is projected to have a sizable budget deficit: it has been spending money on wasteful projects.


Notice that the author does not have to assume that the Town of Seaford has been spending money on wasteful projects in order to conclude that the town can cover its budget deficit by charging a toll to cross the North Bay Bridge. After all, the town could cover the deficit by charging a toll regardless of what the town has spent money on.


> <b>(E) The number of cars that cross the North Bay Bridge every day will not increase in the near future.
</b>


 Since, presumably, the more cars cross the bridge, the more the Town of Seaford could collect in tolls, the author certainly does not have to assume that the number of cars that cross the bridge every day will not increase. In fact, if the number of cars that cross the bridge daily were to increase, it would be even more likely that the town could cover its deficit by charging a toll. So, the author is not assuming what this choice says.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)
