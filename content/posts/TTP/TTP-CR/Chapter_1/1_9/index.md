## Lesson Content
In this chapter, we’re helping you build a basic toolkit.

“A few” means “not many, but more than one.”

“Several” means “three or more but not many.”

“Some” means “more than one, but possibly all.”

“Many” means “a large number of.” Notice that “many” is a version of “some.” Notice also that “many” is not necessarily “most.”

“Most” means “more than 50 percent.”

“A majority” means “more than 50 percent.”

“A minority” means “less than 50 percent.”

“Solely” = “only” e.g., “John is <strong>solely</strong> responsible.” = “<strong>Only</strong> John is responsible.”

“At least one” = “one or more” e.g., “<strong>At least one</strong> pig can fly.” = “<strong>One or more</strong> pigs can fly.”

“Exclusively” = “only” e.g., “Bats are <strong>exclusively</strong> nocturnal.” = “Bats are <strong>only</strong> nocturnal.”

“Uniquely” = “in a way connected to only one person or thing” e.g., “The facility is <strong>uniquely</strong> equipped to recycle such materials.” = “<strong>Only</strong> that facility is equipped in that way to recycle such materials.”

“Always” = “all” e.g., “Radiation leaks are <strong>always</strong> dangerous.” = “<strong>All</strong> radiation leaks are dangerous.” “Martha is <strong>always</strong> late.” = “On <strong>all</strong> occasions, Martha is late.”

You’ll see these words come up often.
