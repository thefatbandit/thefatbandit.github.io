## Lesson Content
In our review of GMAT Critical Reasoning questions, we have seen many arguments that are flawed.

Logical Flaw questions also present us with stimuli containing flawed arguments, but the focus of Logical Flaw questions is somewhat different from the foci of the other types of questions, in that Logical Flaw questions are more directly focused on the flaws in the arguments.

> - choose an answer that best describes a flaw found in the argument (in the case of a pure Logical Flaw question).

or

> - choose an answer that, if true, would weaken the flawed argument (in the case of a hybrid Logical Flaw/Weaken question).

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In answering a Logical Flaw question, our job is to do one of two things: (1) choose an answer that best describes a flaw found in the argument (in the case of a pure Logical Flaw question), or (2) choose an answer that, if true, would weaken the flawed argument (in the case of a hybrid Logical Flaw/Weaken question).
{{< /alert >}}

The good news is that many, if not most, Logical Flaw questions are just advanced Assumption questions, and, in many of the arguments we’ll encounter, the flaws we’ll find center squarely on assumptions made by the authors.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Many Logical Flaw questions are just advanced Assumption questions.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s continue by discussing the close connection between assumptions and logical flaws.
