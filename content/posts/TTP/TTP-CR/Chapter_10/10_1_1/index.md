## Lesson Content
Remember – an assumption is a piece of missing information that must be true for the logic of an argument to be sound.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

An assumption is a piece of missing information that must be true for the support for an argument to be sound.
{{< /alert >}}

As you know well by this point, authors make assumptions when making arguments.

Let’s look at a few examples:

> <em>Health insurance for those living in major cities costs significantly more than does health insurance for those living in small towns. However, empirical data shows that people who live in large cities are no less healthy than people who live in small towns. It follows that health insurance companies profit more from people who live in major cities than from those who live in small towns. </em>

> <em>Which of the following best describes the error of reasoning contained in the argument above?</em>

> (A) <em>The argument assumes without justification that the costs associated with providing health care are not significantly higher in major cities than in small towns.</em>

Is there an important, and questionable, assumption being made by the author in this argument? For sure, there is.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Remember, in solving Logical Flaw questions, it’s very helpful to seek out the assumptions made by authors.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at another example:

> <em>Unlike other major automobile companies, all of which produce mostly automobiles that are fossil fuel powered, The Blitz Automobile Company makes only electric automobiles. Because electric automobiles are easier to maintain, less costly to power, and more environmentally friendly, they are superior to fossil fuel powered vehicles. Thus, soon, the Blitz Automobile Company will put all of the other automobile companies out of business.</em>

> <em>Which of the following best describes the error of reasoning contained in the argument above?</em>

> <em>(A) It neglects to consider the possibility that the other automobile companies may shift to producing electric automobiles. </em>

Is that argument presented a good one? Has the author made any questionable assumptions? He has, right? An assumption in this argument is that the other automobile companies are going to continue producing the products that they currently produce.

Now, it’s important to see that we can’t say for certain that the other companies will make this change, but we don’t need to be certain.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In answering logical flaw questions, be alert for answer choices that point out questionable assumptions made by authors. Many times, the flaw in the argument results from an author’s questionable assumption.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 1 

Music Columnist: Over the past several years, there has been a huge upswing in the number of young people listening to modern jazz music.

The music columnist’s argument is most vulnerable to criticism on which of the following grounds?

1. [ ] (A) It fails to consider the possibility that the majority of young people will not gravitate toward listening to modern jazz music.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) It assumes without warrant that the cause of the ongoing decline in listenership of radio stations that play “pop” music is the trend toward listening to modern jazz music.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) It ignores the fact that, because of the cyclical nature of musical trends, increases in listenership of jazz music are typically followed by decreases.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) It presupposes that the increase in the population of modern jazz music listeners is not associated with an increase in total music listenership.
5. [ ] (E) It overlooks the fact that the modern jazz music of the current era is similar in many ways to “pop” music.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



The music columnist concludes the following:


<u>Conclusion</u>: Radio stations that play the more commercial, “pop” forms of music are going to experience a significant decline in listenership.


The music columnist’s support for that conclusion is the fact that the number of young people listening to modern jazz music has increased significantly.


How is this argument flawed? The correct answer to this question points to the fact that the music columnist has relied on a questionable assumption in arriving at the conclusion.


> <b>(A) It fails to consider the possibility that the majority of young people will not gravitate toward listening to modern jazz music.
</b>


 While it is true that the argument does not include consideration of the possibility that the majority of young people will not gravitate toward listening to modern jazz, that the argument does not include consideration of that possibility is not a flaw, because, even if it is the case that the majority of young people will not gravitate toward listening to modern jazz, the conclusion could still be valid.


The conclusion is simply that the radio stations will experience a significant decline in listenership, and the stations’ experiencing a significant decline in listenership does not require all young people’s or most young people’s gravitating toward listening to modern jazz and, therefore, ceasing to listen to pop music radio. So, the music columnist’s argument works even though it does not include consideration of the possibility that the majority of young people will not gravitate toward listening to modern jazz.


> <b>(B) It assumes without warrant that the cause of the ongoing decline in listenership of radio stations that play “pop” music is the trend toward listening to modern jazz music.
</b>


What this choice says misses the point of the argument. The point of the argument is not to explain why an <em>ongoing</em> decline in pop music radio listenership is occurring. It is to support a prediction about a <em>future</em> significant decline in pop music radio listenership. To avoid choosing a choice like this one, be careful to note exactly what the conclusion of the argument is.


> <b>(C) It ignores the fact that, because of the cyclical nature of musical trends, increases in listenership of jazz music are typically followed by decreases.
</b>


 While the argument does seem to ignore the fact that increases in listenership of jazz are typically followed by decreases, even if the argument does so, that the argument does so is not a flaw. The conclusion is not that the number of young people listening to modern jazz will remain relatively high forever, or that pop music radio listenership will remain relatively low forever. The conclusion is simply that pop music radio will experience a significant decline in listenership. Even if that decline were likely to be reversed at some point in the future, the conclusion could still be valid. So, the fact that jazz listenership is typically cyclical and may decrease at some point in the future does not affect the strength of the argument.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D) It presupposes that the increase in the population of modern jazz music listeners is not associated with an increase in total music listenership.
</b>


While it is true that modern jazz music listenership is increasing, the music columnist provides no evidence clearly indicating that this increase will result in a decrease in pop music radio listenership. It is entirely possible that an increase in modern jazz music listenership will involve a group of people mostly different from the group of people who listen to pop music radio. In such a case, the increase in modern jazz listenership may result not in a significant decrease in pop music radio listenership but, rather, in an increase in total music listenership.


So, the weakness in the music columnist’s argument is that it relies on the questionable assumption that the increase in the population of modern jazz listeners is not associated with an increase in total music listenership.


> <b>(E) It overlooks the fact that the modern jazz music of the current era is similar in many ways to “pop” music.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, as one could come up with some convoluted reasoning that explains that, since modern jazz and pop music are similar, the increase in modern jazz listenership somehow won’t result in a decrease in pop music listenership. The truth is, however, that we have no clear reason to believe that overlooking the fact that these two forms of music are similar in some ways constitutes a flaw in the argument. The two forms of music could be quite different from each other, and listeners could gravitate from listening to one to listening to the other, even if the two are similar in some ways. So, the fact that the two forms of music are similar in some ways does not affect the strength of the support for the conclusion.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
