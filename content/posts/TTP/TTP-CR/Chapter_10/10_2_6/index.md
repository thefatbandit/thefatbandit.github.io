## Lesson Content
As we have already discussed in another chapter, when making a generalization, we base a broad claim about a larger population upon observations of a small sample of a population.

For example, let’s say that we know ten people who live in Dallas, and all vote Republican.

In answering Logical Flaw questions, be on the lookout for broad generalizations made from small samples; such generalizations will be suspect.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When we analyze data from a relatively small sample, we risk making incorrect conclusions. In answering Logical Flaw questions, be on the lookout for broad generalizations made from small samples; such generalizations will be suspect.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following simple example:

> <em>Help Desk Manager: We use independently administered customer satisfaction surveys to rate the performance of our technical help team, and we recently switched from using one survey provider to using another. After the switch, the average customer satisfaction rating of our technical team immediately increased. Since the new survey provider is using a survey that is longer and more detailed than the survey used by the previous provider, clearly the higher ratings from the new survey are more accurate than were the lower ratings from the previous survey.</em>

> <em>Which of the following statements, if true, provides the best evidence that the help desk manager’s reasoning is flawed?</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>The more positive a person’s relationship with a technical help team the more likely the person will take the time to respond to a long, detailed survey for the company.</em>

##  Example 7 

Bicycle Company Representative: It’s clear that, when we release our new model for sale to the general public, it will become one of the most popular bicycles in the marketplace, because, when we gave bicycles of our new model to ten professional cyclists to try for a month, ten out of ten liked the bicycles so much that they asked to keep them after the trial period ended.

Which of the following best describes the greatest weakness in the bicycle company representative’s argument?

1. [ ] (A) It assumes without warrant that professional cyclists are good judges of the quality of a bicycle.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) The evidence provided in support of the claim made is not consistent with that claim.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) The support for its conclusion is based in part on the presupposition that its conclusion is valid.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) The support for its conclusion is based on data unrelated to that conclusion.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) Its conclusion is based on a sample that may be unrepresentative of the set that the conclusion is about.

##### Question Level: easy



The bicycle company representative has arrived at the following conclusion:


<u>Conclusion</u>: When we release our new model for sale to the general public, it will become one of the most popular bicycles in the marketplace.


In arriving at that conclusion, the bicycle company representative has made the assumption that the opinions of a sample of people (ten professional cyclists) are representative of the opinions of people in general. Is this assumption a good one? Well, it may be correct, but also, it very well may not be – right? Notice how the bicycle company representative considers a sample that is both very small and not necessarily representative of the opinions of people in general yet draws a sweeping conclusion about popularity of the bicycle with the general public. Beware of Critical Reasoning arguments that base broad conclusions on very specific evidence.


> <b>(A) It assumes without warrant that professional cyclists are good judges of the quality of a bicycle.
</b>


This choice may seem to be correct, because the bicycle company representative’s argument does rely on the opinions of the ten professional cyclists without questioning whether those cyclists are good judges of the quality of a bicycle. The reason this choice is, nevertheless, incorrect is that the assumption that the professional cyclists are good judges of the quality of a bicycle is not “unwarranted.” Since it’s reasonable to presume that professional cyclists are knowledgeable about bicycles, assuming that they are good judges of the quality of a bicycle is not unwarranted but, rather, reasonable.


> <b>(B) The evidence provided in support of the claim made is not consistent with that claim.
</b>


While the sample used to support the argument may not be representative of the opinions of the general public, what this choice says about the evidence is subtly incorrect. The word “inconsistent” does not correctly describe the logical flaw here. The evidence that the professional cyclists like the bicycle isn’t inconsistent with the conclusion that the bicycle will become one of most popular bicycles in the marketplace.


> <b>(C) The support for its conclusion is based in part on the presupposition that its conclusion is valid.
</b>


This answer choice is tempting, because the fact that the conclusion, that the new bicycle will become one of the most popular bicycles in the marketplace, appears at the beginning of the passage could be perceived as indicating that argument uses the conclusion to support the conclusion. However, the truth is that the support for the conclusion is not the conclusion itself but, rather, the opinions of the professional cyclists. As is the case with choice (B), to avoid choosing this trap, we must read carefully.


> <b>(D) The support for its conclusion is based on data unrelated to that conclusion.
</b>


This choice uses words that seem to describe this argument, “employs data unrelated to the conclusion,” and, since a test-taker may sense that something is wrong with basing a conclusion about the popularity of a bicycle with the general public on professional cyclists’ opinions of that bicycle, this answer could seem to be correct.


However, some answer choices sound “smart” unless you really understand what they are saying. The GMAT often strings together words and phrases that sound very intelligent while saying practically nothing – or worse, something just a tiny bit incorrect but not incorrect enough that you would notice unless you look carefully. In this case, while the sample may not be representative of the opinions of the general public, the opinions of the professional cyclists are certainly related to the conclusion about the popularity of the bicycle. So, saying that the data is “unrelated to the conclusion” is incorrect.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E) Its conclusion is based on a sample that may be unrepresentative of the set that the conclusion is about.
</b>


This answer sums up the argument’s greatest weakness. A claim regarding the popularity of the new bicycle with the general public is based on a sample of opinions of professional cyclists, which may not be representative of the opinions of people in general. Since professional cyclists may want bicycles with characteristics quite different from the bicycle characteristics preferred by most members of the general public, it does not make sense to assume that the fact that ten professional cyclists like a bicycle of a certain model means that that bicycle will become one of the most popular bicycles in the marketplace. Notice that this correct answer highlights a flaw in an assumption made by the author.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
