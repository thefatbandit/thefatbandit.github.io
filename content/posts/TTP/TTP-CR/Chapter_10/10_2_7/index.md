## Lesson Content
As has been discussed in other chapters, authors commonly make errors in reasoning based on numbers and percentages.

Consider the following example:

> <em>During the past 25 years, the percentage of Greysoft employees who invest their retirement monies in the company mutual fund has decreased by fifteen percent. Since a certain number of investors withdraw their money from the company mutual fund each year, to function optimally, the mutual fund requires a consistent number of new investors. Therefore, this decrease will have a significant, deleterious effect on Greysoft’s mutual fund program.</em>

> <em>A serious flaw in the reasoning of the argument is that</em>

Is this a sound argument? Is the author making any questionable assumptions? Notice that an assumption in this argument is that the fact that there was a decrease in the percentage of Greysoft employees who invest in the company’s mutual fund necessarily means that there has been a decrease in the number of investors in the company’s mutual fund.

While the conclusion would be true if the number of Greysoft employees has remained the same, it is entirely possible that the company has expanded.

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) the author assumes without sufficient warrant that a percentage decrease represents a decrease in an absolute number.</em>

##  Example 8 

Campaign Director: In the last election, our candidate lost by just a few percentage points.

Which of the following best describes a weakness in the support for the prediction made?

1. [ ] (A) It neglects to explain why more people would vote for the candidate mentioned in this election than voted for this candidate in the previous election.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) It does not include consideration of whether voter approval for the candidate mentioned has increased or voter approval for the opposing candidate has decreased.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) It fails to establish exactly what number of voters would have had to vote differently in order for the candidate mentioned to have won the previous election.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) It fails to account for the possibility that the number of people planning to vote in the current election is significantly greater than the number who voted in the previous election.
5. [ ] (E) It does not include consideration of the possibility that many voters surveyed had only recently decided to vote for the candidate mentioned.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

##### Question Level: medium



<u>Conclusion</u>: Our candidate is going to win the current election by a comfortable margin.


The conclusion is based on what seems to be compelling mathematical information. The candidate mentioned lost the previous election by just a few percentage points, and voter surveys indicate that 9 percent more voters plan to vote for this candidate in the current election. So, it seems likely that this candidate will win by a comfortable margin.


What could be wrong with this reasoning?


> <b>(A) It neglects to explain why more people would vote for the candidate mentioned in this election than voted for this candidate in the previous election.
</b>


Does the support for the conclusion have to include an explanation for why more people will vote for the candidate mentioned in the current election than voted for this candidate in the previous election? Not at all. Regardless of why more people will vote for this candidate, if there is reason to believe that more people will vote for this candidate, a reason such as the survey results mentioned, the prediction is supported.


> <b>(B) It does not include consideration of whether voter approval for the candidate mentioned has increased or voter approval for the opposing candidate has decreased.
</b>


This choice is similar to choice (A), in that in order to support the conclusion that the candidate mentioned will win, we don’t need to know whether the number of votes for this candidate increased as a result of increased approval for this candidate. Regardless of what underlies the increase in votes for this candidate, as long as there is a clear reason to believe that this candidate will get enough votes to win, the prediction is supported.


> <b>(C) It fails to establish exactly what number of voters would have had to vote differently in order for the candidate mentioned to have won the previous election.
</b>


It ‘s true that the argument does not establish how many voters would have had to vote differently in the previous election in order for the candidate mentioned to have won. At the same time, in failing to do so, is the argument flawed? No. We don’t need that information to conclude that, since this candidate lost by only a few percentage points last time, a 9 percent increase in votes for this candidate will be enough to result in this candidate’s winning the election.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D) It fails to account for the possibility that the number of people planning to vote in the current election is significantly greater than the number who voted in the previous election.
</b>


If the number of voters who plan to vote in this election is significantly greater than the number who voted in the last election, it could be that a 9 percent increase in votes for the candidate mentioned will mean that this candidate will still lose the election, or that this candidate will not win by a “comfortable margin.” For instance, if the total number of people planning to vote in this election is 20 percent greater than the number who voted in the previous election, just to get the same percentage of all votes that this candidate got in the previous election, this candidate would have to get 20 percent more votes. In such a case, a 9 percent increase in the number of voters planning to vote for this candidate would mean a <em>decrease </em>in the percentage of all voters who are for this candidate. If the total number of people who plan to vote in the current election is 7 percent greater than the total number who voted in the previous election, with a 9 percent increase in votes, this candidate might just barely win. So, a weakness in the support for the prediction is that the support fails to account for the possibility that the total number of people planning to vote in this election is significantly greater than the number who voted in the previous election, in which scenario, a 9 percent increase in the number of votes for the candidate mentioned could still result in a loss for this candidate.


> <b>(E) It does not include consideration of the possibility that many voters surveyed had only recently decided to vote for the candidate mentioned.
</b>


This choice could be tempting if a test-taker perceives it as pointing out the possibility that, since many voters only recently decided to vote for the candidate mentioned, they could easily change their minds and vote for a different candidate. The truth is, however, that, even if many voters only recently decided to vote for this candidate, there is no clear reason to believe that they will change their minds and vote differently. So, failing to consider the possibility that many voters surveyed had only recently decided to vote for the candidate mentioned is not clearly a weakness in the support for the prediction.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
