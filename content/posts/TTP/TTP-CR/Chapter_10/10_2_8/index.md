## Lesson Content
Sometimes an author will draw a conclusion that’s simply not supported by the evidence provided.

For example, an author could seek to support the conclusion that a product is safe to use by going on about all the testing of the product that has been done.

Notice how the evidence in the following example doesn’t really support the conclusion.

> <em>Professor: Over the past few years, I have noticed that the students coming to our school are less motivated than students here were in previous years. It seems that the school has become more focused on admitting students who can pay full tuition than it is on attracting students who actually want to learn.</em>

> <em>Admissions Director: Your assessment is incorrect. Over the past few years we have been using the same application that we have always used, have received approximately the same number of applications that we have received in the past, and have continued to interview applicants as we always have.</em>

> <em>The admissions director’s response is flawed in that</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>the evidence provided does not conflict with the assertion that the response is meant to refute.</em>

The admissions director asserts that the professor’s assessment is incorrect, but then provides evidence that is only tangentially related to the issue.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Arguments can be flawed because their conclusions are not actually supported by the evidence provided.
{{< /alert >}}

The impact of the evidence in such a flawed argument is not such that it supports the conclusion.

##  Example 9 

Vehicle Safety Products CEO: Our car seats for infants have undergone thousands of hours of testing over the last five years.

The reasoning of the argument is most vulnerable to criticism that the argument

1. [ ] (A) fails to prove that the company’s car seats for infants will work well in trucks also.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) bases a general conclusion on characteristics of a small, unrepresentative sample.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) fails to consider the possibility that the most effective way to keep an infant in a car safe is to drive carefully.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) relies on the unwarranted assumption that all drivers of cars need seats for infants.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) presents as evidence a premise that is not clearly linked to its conclusion.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Breakdown of the argument:


<u>Premise</u>: Vehicle Safety Products CEO: Our car seats for infants have undergone thousands of hours of testing over the last five years.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Clearly, then, our car seats for infants must be the safest possible infant seats that drivers can purchase for their cars.


We must always take care not to be “blinded by the light. ”


Does the fact that a product has been extensively tested indicate that the product is well made or that the product will do exactly what it is intended to do? Of course not, right? In fact, the fact that a product has been rigorously tested over many years could even indicate that the creators of the product are having a hard time getting to the product to do what they want it to do.


We should be skeptical when the CEO says that, because her car seats for infants have undergone thousands of hours of testing over the last five years, her car seats for infants must be the safest possible infant seats that drivers can purchase for their cars. There is not a clear connection between the evidence that she presents and her conclusion.


> <b>(A) fails to prove that the company’s car seats for infants will work well in trucks also.
</b>


 The CEO’s conclusion is that her company’s car seats for infants must be the safest possible infant seats that drivers can purchase for their cars.


Notice that she does not make any claims regarding trucks. Her claim is about cars. So, she need not prove that the company’s car seats for infants will work well in trucks also.


So, while it is true that the CEO does not prove that the company’s car seats for infants will work well in trucks also, the fact that she does not do so is not a flaw in the argument.


> <b>(B) bases a general conclusion on characteristics of a small, unrepresentative sample.
</b>


 The CEO says that her car seats for infants have undergone thousands of hours of testing over the last five years. “Thousands of hours of testing over five years” certainly would not be considered “a small, unrepresentative sample. ” In fact, that testing seems extensive.


So, what this answer choice says is not a flaw in the CEO’s argument, because this choice does not match what the CEO does in making her argument.


> <b>(C) fails to consider the possibility that the most effective way to keep an infant in a car safe is to drive carefully.
</b>


 The CEO’s conclusion is that her company’s car seats for infants must be the safest possible infant seats that drivers can purchase for their cars.


So, she certainly need not consider the possibility that the most effective way to keep an infant in a car safe is to drive carefully. Her conclusion is not about what the best way to keep an infant safe is. Her conclusion is about which car seats for infants are the safest.


So, while it may be true that the CEO has not considered the possibility that the most effective way to keep an infant in a car safe is to drive carefully, the fact that she has not done so is not a flaw in her argument.


> <b>(D) relies on the unwarranted assumption that all drivers of cars need seats for infants.
</b>


 The CEO does not assume that all drivers of cars need seats for infants. She’s simply making the claim that her company’s car seats for infants must be the safest possible infant seats that drivers can purchase for their cars. This claim could be true regardless of whether all drivers of cars need seats for infants.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E) presents as evidence a premise that is not clearly linked to its conclusion.
</b>


 Notice that there is a big disconnect in the CEO’s argument.


Think about the following.


Does the fact that her car seats for infants have undergone thousands of hours of testing over the last five years allow her to conclude that her car seats for infants must be the safest possible infant seats that drivers can purchase for their cars? No, right? All that testing could have shown anything, including that the car seats are utterly unsafe.


In words, the fact that something has been heavily tested does not allow us to conclude that that thing works as it should. Perhaps the thing is being tested so much because whoever is making it can’t quite get it right.


Whatever the case may be, the fact that something has been tested tells us nothing about that thing other than that it has been tested.


So, what this answer choice says, that the argument presents as evidence a premise that is not clearly linked to its conclusion, captures the issue that the fact that the car seats have been tested does not provide any support for the conclusion that the seats are the safest possible infant seats that drivers can purchase for their cars.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Example 10 

Companies that sell food products made with ingredients derived from genetically modified organisms (GMOs) oppose enactment of laws requiring that such products be labeled as containing GMOs.

The reasoning of the argument is most vulnerable to criticism that the argument

1. [ ] (A) provides no evidence to support the assertion that there is nothing wrong with eating foods that contain ingredients derived from GMOs.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) presents as evidence an assertion that is not clearly linked to its conclusion.
3. [ ] (C) includes no consideration of the fact that companies that sell food products made with ingredients derived from GMOs have a vested interest in maintaining positive consumer perceptions of such foods.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) simply contradicts the assertion made by the companies without providing evidence that supports its point of view.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) makes a distinction between companies that sell food products made with GMO derived ingredients and companies that do not when, in reality, companies may sell both types of products.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



The passage presents a scenario in which companies that make foods containing GMO ingredients oppose enactment of laws requiring that the foods be labeled in such a way as to indicate that they contain such ingredients. Then, it presents a premise and a conclusion.


<u>Premise</u>: The companies assert that there is nothing wrong with eating foods that contain ingredients derived from GMOs.


<u>Conclusion</u>: The companies have no reason to believe that the labeling will discourage consumers from purchasing products that contain GMO derived ingredients.


Does the conclusion follow from the premise? Not really. Even though the companies assert that there is nothing wrong with eating foods that contain ingredients derived from GMOs, and even if there were in fact nothing wrong with eating such foods, the companies could have many reasons to believe that the labeling will discourage consumers from purchasing their products. For example, consumers could take the presence of the labels as an indication that there is in fact something wrong with eating such foods.


> <b>(A) provides no evidence to support the assertion that there is nothing wrong with eating foods that contain ingredients derived from GMOs.
</b>


While the assertion that there is nothing wrong with eating foods that contain ingredients derived from GMOs appears in the argument, the argument does not depend on that assertion’s being valid. The argument is not making that assertion. Rather, the case being made is that the companies making that assertion have nothing to be concerned about.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B) presents as evidence an assertion that is not clearly linked to its conclusion.
</b>


As discussed in the introduction to this explanation, the argument jumps from saying that the companies have asserted that there is nothing wrong with eating foods made with GMO derived ingredients to concluding that the companies have no reason to believe that consumers will be discouraged from buying such foods by GMO labeling. The evidence and the conclusion are essentially statements about two different topics, and there is no clear link via which the evidence, the companies’ assertion, supports the conclusion.


> <b>(C) includes no consideration of the fact that companies that sell food products made with ingredients derived from GMOs have a vested interest in maintaining positive consumer perceptions of such foods.
</b>


The conclusion of the argument is not about the motivations of the companies or about the validity of any assertions made by the companies. So, the fact that the companies have a vested interest in maintaining positive consumer perceptions of such foods would not undermine the support for the conclusion. Therefore, the fact that the argument includes no consideration of the fact that the companies have this vested interest is not a flaw in the argument.


> <b>(D) simply contradicts the assertion made by the companies without providing evidence that supports its point of view.
</b>


This choice could be temping, because the argument seems to be in opposition to the position of the companies. However, the truth is that, if we look carefully, we see that the argument does not contradict the <em>assertion </em>that it says has been made by the companies.


> <b>(E) makes a distinction between companies that sell food products made with GMO derived ingredients and companies that do not when, in reality, companies may sell both types of products.
</b>


This choice is blatantly incorrect. The argument makes no such distinction. Companies that sell both type of products are in fact companies that sell products made with GMO derived ingredients, and the argument does not say anything that is inconsistent with this fact.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
