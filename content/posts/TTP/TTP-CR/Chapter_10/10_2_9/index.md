## Lesson Content
Necessary information, significant information, and sufficient information differ from each other in key ways.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Necessary information, significant information, and sufficient information differ from each other in key ways.
{{< /alert >}}

At the same time, since they are somewhat similar, these types of information can be misused in arguments, in which cases the arguments are flawed.

A key thing to understand about these types of information is that information that is necessary or significant is not necessarily the same as information that is sufficient for supporting a conclusion.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Necessary information or significant information is not necessarily the same as information that is sufficient for supporting a conclusion.
{{< /alert >}}

To clearly see how these types of information differ, let’s consider the difference between conditions that are necessary and conditions that are sufficient.

A necessary condition is one that has to be satisfied in order for a conclusion to be correct or an outcome to occur.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A necessary condition is one that has to be satisfied in order for a conclusion to be correct or an outcome to occur.
{{< /alert >}}

Here are two examples of necessary conditions:

> - Water is necessary for the growth of plants.

> - All mammals are animals.

A sufficient condition, on the other hand, is one that is sufficient to ensure that a conclusion is correct or an outcome will occur.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A sufficient condition, on the other hand, is one that is sufficient to ensure that a conclusion is correct or an outcome will occur.
{{< /alert >}}

Are our examples of necessary conditions also examples of sufficient conditions? Let’s consider them again.

> - Is the presence of water sufficient for concluding that a plant will grow? No.

> - Is lack of evidence indicating that something is not an animal sufficient for concluding that it is a mammal? No.

Here are two examples of sufficient conditions:

> - If we know that an animal is a shark, then we have evidence sufficient for concluding that it is a fish.

> - If we were to know for sure that a person will refuse to read any biography, then we would have evidence sufficient for concluding that a plan involving having that person read the biography of Max Weber will not work.

When an argument that requires sufficient information for arriving at its conclusion uses necessary information or significant information instead, that argument will be flawed.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When an argument that requires sufficient information for arriving at its conclusion uses necessary information or significant information instead, that argument will be flawed.
{{< /alert >}}

For instance, an argument that says that, since a company has a great product, that company must be profitable is likely flawed.

We saw an issue involving the difference between a necessary condition and a sufficient condition earlier in this chapter in the Creos minimum wage question.

> <em>In Creos, even people employed full time at the previous minimum wage would not earn incomes above the official poverty level. At the same time, while the income of a person working full time at the new, higher minimum wage would exceed the official poverty level, some economists felt that employers would be reluctant to hire people at the new wage level. Fortunately, the increase in the minimum wage has not deterred employers from hiring. Therefore, there will not be anyone living in poverty in Creos.</em>

> <em>Which of the following, if true, would serve to call attention to a flaw in the above argument? </em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) Some people in Creos are experiencing chronic illnesses of severity such that the people experiencing the illnesses feel that they are unable to work.</em>

A minimum wage that is above poverty level is <em>significant</em>, and it’s even fairly accurate to say that having the opportunity to earn an above poverty level wage is a condition <em>necessary</em> for people’s avoiding living in poverty.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Arguments that appear in Logical Flaw questions can be flawed as a result of their using significant or necessary information where sufficient information is required for arriving at the arguments’ conclusions.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 11 

Guatama Elementary School Principal: Our students’ reading scores have tended to be below the statewide average for many years.

Which of the following best describes the error of reasoning made by the principal in the argument above?

1. [ ] (A) He fails to establish that the surfaces of the schoolyards of many other schools in the state do not contain toxic heavy metals.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) He overlooks the fact some students at Guatama Elementary School already score higher than the statewide average on reading tests.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) He fails to consider that, as a result of any increases in the reading scores of students at Guatama Elementary School, the statewide average will increase.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) He assumes without warrant that the impact of remedying the issue involving toxic heavy metals will be sufficient to eliminate the difference in test scores.
5. [ ] (E) He fails to specify how the issue involving toxic heavy metals in the surface of the schoolyard will be remedied.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

##### Question Level: easy



The principal states the following conclusion:


<u>Conclusion</u>: Our students’ reading scores will increase to the statewide average.


Is the conclusion well supported?


> <b>(A) He fails to establish that the surfaces of the schoolyards of many other schools in the state do not contain toxic heavy metals.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, as it could be perceived as implying that the heavy metals may not be the cause of the relatively low test scores, since many other schools in the state may also have heavy metals in the surfaces of their schoolyards. One could reason that, if students at many other schools may also be exposed to heavy metals, and yet their scores are higher, then maybe heavy metals aren’t the issue.


The truth is, however, that that story is rather convoluted. For one thing, it could be that students at other schools whose schoolyard surfaces contain toxic heavy metals are also scoring below the statewide average on tests. Also, even if there are heavy metals in the surfaces of schoolyards of many other schools, reducing Gautama Elementary students’ exposure to heavy metals could result in their test scores increasing to match the statewide average.


> <b>(B) He overlooks the fact some students at Guatama Elementary School already score higher than the statewide average on reading tests.
</b>


 What if the principal had considered this fact? Would his doing so make any difference in the support for the conclusion? Even if some students at Guatama Elementary School already score higher than the statewide average on reading tests, it would still be the case that reading scores at Guatama Elementary have tended to be lower than the statewide average and that remedying the issue involving toxic metals could result in Guatama Elementary students’ reading scores increasing to match the statewide average.


> <b>(C) He fails to consider that, as a result of any increases in the reading scores of students at Guatama Elementary School, the statewide average will increase.
</b>


This answer could be tempting, as one could take it as indicating that, even if reading scores of Guatama Elementary students increase, they may not increase enough to match the statewide average. However, this choice does not call attention to any real flaw in the argument. Even though the statewide average would increase in this way, it could still be that, because of the toxic metals issue’s being remedied, the difference between Guatama Elementary students’ scores and the statewide average will be eliminated.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D) He assumes without warrant that the impact of remedying the issue involving toxic heavy metals will be sufficient to eliminate the difference in test scores.
</b>


While it may be that Gautama Elementary students’ reading scores will increase as a result of remedying the toxic metals issue, concluding that remedying the issue will result in the elimination of the difference between their scores and the statewide average is going too far given the limited information available.


While it is true that toxic heavy metals can interfere with cognition and that such heavy metals have been discovered in the surface of the schoolyard, there could be multiple factors contributing to the existence of the difference between the test scores of students at Guatama Elementary School and the statewide average. So, while remedying the heavy metals issue may cause Guatama Elementary’s students test scores to increase, the principal’s argument is flawed, in that he assumes that the impact of remedying the issue will be sufficient for eliminating the difference in test scores without evidence indicating that it will be sufficient and that there are not additional factors that would have to be dealt with in order for the difference in test scores to be eliminated.


Remedying the toxic metals issue may be significant, but, in concluding that doing so is sufficient for eliminating the difference in test scores, the principal is making an unwarranted assumption.


> <b>(E) He fails to specify how the issue involving toxic heavy metals in the surface of the schoolyard will be remedied.
</b>


Concluding that remedying the toxic metals issue will result in Guatama Elementary students’ test scores increasing to match the statewide average does not require specifying exactly how the remedying will be accomplished.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
