## Lesson Content
The argument in a Logical Flaw question can have a conclusion that is math related, such as a conclusion based on population data, a conclusion based on research results, or a conclusion about company profits.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Logical Flaw questions involving mathematical arguments present an opportunity for a question writer to create answer choices that are tempting but incorrect because they bring up mathematical information that doesn’t matter for the support of the conclusion presented.
{{< /alert >}}

For instance, in a case in which the conclusion of an argument is about the average earnings of a person in a country, an answer choice that makes a point about the rate of unemployment in that country might seem to highlight a flaw in the argument and yet actually have nothing to do with the support for the conclusion presented.

Consider the following example:

> <em>Education Secretary: The results of a recent study show that, the more years of high school education a person has, the less likely it is that that person will experience drug addiction. Therefore, if we can get a greater percentage of students to complete all of high school, we will succeed in reducing the incidence of drug addiction in young people in our country.</em>

> <em>The argument is most vulnerable to which of the following criticisms?</em>

> <em>(<u>Choice that Brings Up Mathematical Information that Doesn’</u></em><em><u>t Matter</u></em><em>) It overlooks the possibility that young people represent a smaller proportion of the country’s total population than they represented in the past.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) It overlooks the possibility that drug use is the cause of rather than the effect of students’ ceasing to attend high school before completing all of it.</em>

The conclusion is about reducing the incidence of drug addiction in young people.

So how could a choice that brings up mathematical information that doesn’t matter be tempting? If a test-taker loses track of exactly what has to be supported, information that doesn’t matter could seem to matter.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In answering Logical Flaw questions, beware of incorrect answer choices that bring up mathematical information that seems to matter but doesn’t really matter.
{{< /alert >}}

The keys to avoiding choosing such a choice are:

> - to be very clear about what the argument is seeking to support.

and

> - to go beyond choosing a choice that merely “sounds right” to carefully considering the logical relationships between any mathematical information presented in answer choices and what the argument is seeking to support.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In answering Logical Flaw questions, to avoid incorrect answer choices that bring up mathematical information that seems to matter but doesn’t really matter, we must (1) be very clear about what the argument is seeking to support, and (2) go beyond choosing a choice that merely “sounds right” to carefully considering the logical relationships between any mathematical information presented in answer choices and what the argument is seeking to support.
{{< /alert >}}

As you go through the following example, pay attention to the ways in which answer choices can seem to be correct even though the mathematical information that they bring up does not matter.

##  Example 16 

A few decades ago, when only around 10 percent of people in England would go through life without having children, the growth in the country’s population was minimal.

The argument is most vulnerable to which of the following criticisms?

1. [ ] (A) It overlooks the possibility that the percentage of recent immigrants into England who are choosing to have children is greater than the percentage of all people in England who are.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) It overlooks the possibility that the number of children per parent in England has increased significantly over the past few decades.
3. [ ] (C) It presupposes that a decrease in the population of England would be considered negative rather than positive for the country.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) It neglects to consider the fact that England’s population is higher currently than it was a few decades ago.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) It assumes without sufficient warrant that, at its current pace, immigration into England will cause the population to remain at the same level.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

##### Question Level: medium



<u>Conclusion</u>: The population of England would decrease significantly without immigration.


The conclusion is supported via a comparison of the percentage of people in England who go through life without having children currently with the percentage of people in England who would go through life without having children a few decades ago. Since, when the percentage of people who would have children was much lower, population growth was minimal, the argument concludes that, currently, the population of England would decrease significantly without immigration.


So, what’s wrong with this argument?


> <b>(A) It overlooks the possibility that the percentage of recent immigrants into England who are choosing to have children is greater than the percentage of all people in England who are.
</b>


 This choice could be tempting, as it could be perceived as indicating that immigrants are increasing the birth rate in England. However, the truth is that, regardless of what percentage of recent immigrants are choosing to have children, the percentage of <em>all people in England</em> who are choosing to have children remains exactly what the argument says it is. So, the mathematical information brought up by this choice doesn’t actually matter.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B) It overlooks the possibility that the number of children per parent in England has increased significantly over the past few decades.
</b>


 The support for the conclusion centers on one fact, that the percentage of people in England who do not have children has increased. In doing so, the argument overlooks the fact that the birth rate in England is the result of, not just the percentage of people who have children, but also other factors, such as the number of children each parent has. It could very well be that the number of children per parent has increased significantly, in which case the population of England could increase even without immigration. By overlooking this possibility, the author of the argument may have arrived at an incorrect conclusion.


> <b>(C) It presupposes that a decrease in the population of England would be considered negative rather than positive for the country.
</b>


 While the argument could be perceived as implying that a decrease in population would be negative for England, the truth is that the argument does not actually include such a position. What this choice describes is something that the argument does not do at all.


> <b>(D) It neglects to consider the fact that England’s population is higher currently than it was a few decades ago.
</b>


 While it is true that the argument does not mention the fact that England’s population is higher currently than it was a few decades ago, that it does not do so is not a weakness. Making the case that the population will decrease as a result of an increase in the percentage of people who go through life without having children does not require taking into account the current size of the population. Regardless of whether the population of England is greater than, smaller than, or the same size as it was a few decades ago, an increase in the percentage of people who don’t have children will have the same effect on the current population, whatever that population may be. So, the mathematical information that this choice brings up does not matter for the support for the conclusion.


> <b>(E) It assumes without sufficient warrant that, at its current pace, immigration into England will cause the population to remain at the same level.
</b>


 This choice is blatantly incorrect, as what this choice describes is something that the argument does not do. The argument does not include any assumption that, at its current pace, the immigration into England will cause the population to remain the same. All the argument says is that, without immigration, the population would decrease significantly. Saying that is not the same as assuming that the population will remain the same.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
