## Lesson Content
Remember, in answering a Complete the Passage Question, our goal is to find the answer that best completes the passage.

Consider the following example:

> <em>Miracle Walnut Company sells two products – bags of walnuts in their shells, and bags of walnuts unshelled. The machinery that the company uses to remove the shells of walnuts has broken down, and will be replaced with new, more efficient machinery rather than repaired. Since delivery and installation of new machinery will take about a week, Miracle Walnut Company will not produce unshelled walnuts for a week while the replacement of the shell removing machinery is in progress. Nevertheless, during that week, it is expected that all of Miracle Walnut Company’s orders will be filled, because __________.</em>

> <em>(<u>Choice that Follows from What the Passage Says</u>) Miracle Walnut Company will, after that week, be producing unshelled walnuts with increased efficiency </em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) the company has, in inventory, bags of unshelled walnuts sufficient to cover at least a week’s worth of orders</em>

Notice how the choice that follows from what the passage says could be tempting.

Of course, this choice doesn’t really work, because the fact that the company will be producing unshelled walnuts with increased efficiency<em> </em>after a week of having no machinery does not explain why it is that the company’s orders will be filled during that week.

The correct answer, in contrast, does not merely follow from what the passage says, but rather, provides additional information that supports the conclusion that the company will fill its orders and, thus, effectively completes the passage.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Some trap answers in Complete the Passage questions will sound plausible or inferable given what is stated in the passages, and yet these answers don’t logically complete the passages. We must be careful not to choose such answers.
{{< /alert >}}

To avoid choosing such an incorrect answer, keep in mind what you are seeking to do in answering a Complete the Passage question, which, unless the passage is set up to be completed with an inference or a conclusion, is choosing a choice that provides new information that completes the logic of the passage, not one that merely states a conclusion based on the information that the passage already includes.

##  Example 8 

Which of the following best completes the discussion?

The number of years that a person spends in school is negatively correlated with the likelihood of that person’s becoming involved in criminal activity.

1. [ ] (A) people who complete high school are less likely to become involved in crime than those who quit before they graduate
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) some people who have spent many years in school are no more educated than others who have spent relatively few years in school
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) the majority of people who do not complete high school do not become involved in any significant criminal activity
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) states that increased the number of years people are legally required to attend school saw reduced levels of criminal activity from the people who therefore attended school for more years
5. [ ] (E) the percentage of college graduates who become involved in crime is even lower than the percentage of high school graduates who become involved in crime
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

##### Question Level: hard



To correctly answer this question, we need a choice that answers the following:


What has been found that is a reason to discount the possibility that the reason for the observed correlation is not that, in general, education reduces a person’s likelihood of becoming involved in crime, but, rather, that people who are relatively more motivated to pursue education are relatively less motivated to become involved in crime?


Since we are seeking information that is a reason to discount a possibility mentioned in the passage, and since the passage mentions nothing that would constitute a reason to discount that possibility, the correct answer to this question has to provide new information rather than simply state a conclusion that is supported by information that the passage already includes.


> <b>(A) people who complete high school are less likely to become involved in crime than those who quit before they graduate
</b>


This choice simply states a conclusion already supported by the information in the passage. If, as the passage says, the more years of education a person has the less likely it is that that person will engage in crime, then it makes sense to conclude that people who complete high school are less likely to become involved in crime than those who quit before they graduate. So, this choice does not really add new information to the passage and do what the information that logically completes the passage has to do, provide a reason to discount the possibility mentioned.


> <b>(B) some people who have spent many years in school are no more educated than others who have spent relatively few years in school
</b>


Because this choice is about the results of spending years in school, which topic is a key component of the what the passage is about, this choice may seem relevant and therefore correct. The truth is, however, that the fact that some people who have spent many years in school are no more educated than others who have spent relatively few years in school has little to no effect on the support for the theory that education is the factor underlying the observed negative correlation or on the support for the alternative theory that motivation is the factor underlying the observed negative correlation. Presumably, people other than the “some” mentioned by this choice become more educated when they spend more years in school, in which case, we are still left wondering whether education or motivation is what underlies the observed negative correlation between years spent in school and likelihood of becoming involved in criminal activity.


Further, even if we were to decide that what this choice says could indicate that few people who attend many years of school are more educated than people who attend relatively few years of school, we would not have the answer we need. If it were the case that few people who attend many years of school are more educated than people who attend relatively few years of school, then a person’s attending school for more years generally would not result in that person’s becoming more educated, in which case, clearly, education would not be the factor underlying the negative correlation between years spent in school and likelihood of becoming involved in crime, and so, the factor underlying that negative correlation could be motivation. We need a choice that indicates that motivation is not the factor underlying the negative correlation, not one that indicates that it could very well be that factor.


> <b>(C) the majority of people who do not complete high school do not become involved in any significant criminal activity
</b>


This choice provides reason to discount the wrong possibility. It provides reason to discount the possibility that most people who do not complete high school will become involved in significant criminal activity, and it doesn’t provide reason to discount the possibility mentioned in the passage. The fact that the majority of people who don’t complete high school, in other words, the majority of people who do not complete as many years of school as many others complete, do not become involved in any significant criminal activity does not help us to decide whether the factor underlying the negative correlation between years spent in school and likelihood of becoming involved in crime is education or motivation. The fact that the majority of people who do not complete high school do not become involved in any significant criminal activity is just a data point, a data point characterized by people’s completing a certain number of years of school and having a certain likelihood of becoming involved in criminal activity. Of course, from the information in the passage, we already knew that many such data points, data points involving certain amounts of time spent in school and certain likelihoods of becoming involved in crime, exist. The fact that this one exists changes nothing.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D) states that increased the number of years people are legally required to attend school saw reduced levels of criminal activity from the people who therefore attended school for more years
</b>


People who attend school for an increased number of years only because they are legally required to do so are likely not very motivated to become educated. Therefore, the fact that states saw “reduced levels of criminal activity” from these people indicates that education itself, without motivation, can lead to decreased levels of crime. Therefore, what this choice says is a reason to discount the possibility that motivation is the factor underlying the negative correlation between years spent in school and likelihood of becoming involved in criminal activity.


> <b>(E) the percentage of college graduates who become involved in crime is even lower than the percentage of high school graduates who become involved in crime
</b>


If the number of years a person attends school is negatively correlated with the likelihood of that person’s becoming involved in criminal activity, then what this choice says about college graduates in comparison with high school graduates is a conclusion that follows from what the passage says. So, this choice does not really add new information to the passage and does not do what the information that logically completes the passage has to do, serve as a reason to discount the possibility mentioned.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
