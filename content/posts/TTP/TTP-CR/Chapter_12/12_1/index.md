## Lesson Content
Unlike many other types of Critical Reasoning questions, Method of Reasoning questions focus on how an argument is structured, not on the merits of the argument.

In other words, when we consider the stimulus of a Method of Reasoning question, we don’t need to focus on whether the argument is sound or convincing or weak.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When solving a Method of Reasoning question, our job is to focus on how the argument is organized and analyze the argument’s structure.
{{< /alert >}}

A simple analogy is as follows:<em> </em>in answering Strengthen or Weaken questions, we act as construction workers; we repair buildings or tear them down.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In answering Strengthen or Weaken questions, for example, we act as construction workers; we repair buildings or tear them down. To answer Method of Reasoning questions, we act more as inspectors; inspecting the arguments, we figure out how the parts fit together.
{{< /alert >}}

For example, imagine that we were given the following question:

> <em>Letter to the Editor: A recent article in your newspaper stated that coffee is the most popular hot beverage in the United States. However, every American I know drinks tea rather than coffee. Therefore, this article must be false and should be corrected.</em>

> <em>The argument above employs which of the following reasoning techniques?</em>

> <em>(A) A claim is introduced, evidence that calls that claim into question is offered, and a contrary claim is made.</em>

> <em>(B) A claim is introduced, evidence that supports that claim is offered, and a similar claim is made.</em>

This is clearly a flawed argument.

However, determining how the argument is flawed isn’t our concern right now.

So, what does this argument say?

This argument has three sentences.

> <em>A recent article in your newspaper stated that coffee is the most popular hot beverage in the United States.</em>

The second sentence provides evidence that seeks (not necessarily successfully) to undermine that claim:

> <em>However, every American I know drinks tea rather than coffee.</em>

The third sentence offers the author’s conclusion:

> <em>Therefore, this article must be false and should be corrected.</em>

Considering the two choices, we can clearly see that choice (A) is correct.

> <em>(A) A claim is introduced, evidence that calls that claim into question is offered, and a contrary claim is made.</em>

In answering Method of Reasoning questions, it’s important to keep in mind that these questions contain arguments.

Let’s now discuss a simple yet effective strategy that we can deploy to help us correctly answer Method of Reasoning questions.
