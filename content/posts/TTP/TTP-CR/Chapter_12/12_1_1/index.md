## Lesson Content
It’s obvious that, for an answer choice to be correct, that answer choice must be completely correct.

We can leverage this fact to help us solve Method of Reasoning questions.

The answers to Method of Reasoning questions describe the logical structures of authors’ arguments.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Since the answers to Method of Reasoning questions describe the logical structures of arguments, a correct answer must describe exactly how a given argument is structured. If even one part of an answer choice says something untrue about the argument, that answer is incorrect.
{{< /alert >}}

A key thing to realize is that, sometimes, a Method of Reasoning answer choice will start off sounding correct, even though it isn’t really.

Notice the key differences between the answer choices in the following example:

> <em>Surprisingly, the results of a recent study showed that, on average, those who regularly drink moderate amounts of alcohol are in better health and live longer than those who abstain. However, these results should not necessarily be taken as indicating that consuming alcohol in moderation is beneficial to health, as many people who abstain from consuming alcohol do so because they are already not healthy.</em>

> <em>The passage above proceeds by</em>

> <em>(A) making the case that a conclusion should be rejected because it is completely unsupported.</em>

> <em>(B) weakening the link between evidence and a cause-and-effect conclusion that seems to be supported by that evidence by bringing up an alternate reason for the observed pattern.</em>

The passage definitely recommends against concluding that drinking alcohol in moderation is beneficial to health, but how exactly does it proceed? Let’s consider choice (A) first.

Choice (A) starts off seeming to be correct, as it says that the passage proceeds by making the case that a conclusion should be rejected, and rejecting a conclusion sounds something like what the passage does when it says that the results “should not necessarily be taken as indicating that consuming alcohol in moderation is beneficial to health.” As we continue to read choice (A), however, it becomes apparent that it is incorrect, because choice (A) describes the passage as saying that the conclusion is “completely unsupported.” The truth is that the passage does mention some support for the conclusion that consuming alcohol in moderation is beneficial to health, when it says, “those who regularly drink moderate amounts of alcohol are in better health and live longer than those who abstain.” Also, if we reconsider the beginning of choice (A), we can see that the passage does not even really make the case that the conclusion that consuming alcohol is beneficial for health should be rejected.

So, unlike what (A) says, the passage doesn’t really recommend rejecting the conclusion that consuming alcohol in moderation is beneficial for health and does mention support for that conclusion.

Choice (B), on the other hand, exactly matches how the passage proceeds.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

By reading Method of Reasoning answer choices completely and by carefully comparing them with how the stimuli proceed, you can consistently eliminate incorrect answers.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 1 

Prison sentences are meant to reduce crime, by serving as disincentives to engage in crime.

The author develops the argument by

1. [ ] (A) using key differences between the actions of two groups of people categorized by age to illuminate a phenomenon.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) demonstrating the lack of the effect of a factor by comparing situations that include and do not include that factor.
3. [ ] (C) undermining the force of the evidence supporting a common belief by providing a reasonable alternative interpretation of the same information.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) outlining a theory and then providing evidence to support that theory.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) questioning the support for a hypothesis by showing that the data used to support the hypothesis is skewed.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



In order to answer this question correctly, we have to find the choice that correctly describes how the author develops the argument. A choice that only partly matches how the argument is developed will be incorrect.


Let’s review how the argument proceeds:


- It describes how prison sentences are meant to work.


- It presents a theory that seems to follow from the information on how prison sentences are meant to work.


- It presents evidence that conflicts with that theory.


- It concludes that the theory is incorrect.


> <b>(A) using key differences between the actions of two groups of people categorized by age to illuminate a phenomenon.
</b>


What this choice describes is nearly the opposite of how the author develops the argument. The conclusion is supported by a similarity between rather than by differences between the actions of people in two different age groups.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B) demonstrating the lack of the effect of a factor by comparing situations that include and do not include that factor.
</b>


This choice describes exactly how the passage proceeds. It demonstrates that longer prison sentences do not have a material crime reducing effect by showing that a situation in which the factor, threat of longer prison sentences, is present is not significantly different from a situation in which that factor is not present. In other words, since young people aged eighteen and older are no less likely to commit crimes than people younger than eighteen, even though the people aged eighteen and older face longer prison sentences if caught, the factor, the threat of longer prison sentences, has no effect.


> <b>(C) undermining the force of the evidence supporting a common belief by providing a reasonable alternative interpretation of the same information.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, because its first word, “undermining,” could be perceived as describing what the argument does when it presents information conflicting with the theory that the threat of longer prison sentences serves to reduce crime more than shorter sentences do. However, since the passage proceeds by providing new information rather than by providing an alternative interpretation of already presented information, this choice does not accurately describe how the author develops the argument.


> <b>(D) outlining a theory and then providing evidence to support that theory.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, because it mentions a theory and the passage does start off with an idea that might be true in “theory.” However, by reading the choice completely, we can see that it does not accurately describe how the author develops the argument, as the author does not provide evidence to support a theory, but rather provides evidence indicating that what seems true in theory is not actually true.


> <b>(E) questioning the support for a hypothesis by showing that the data used to support the hypothesis is skewed.
</b>


This choice could seem correct initially, as it mentions “questioning the support for a hypothesis,” which seems at least somewhat like what the passage does when it provides information indicating that something that might be true “in theory” is not actually true. However, since the passage does not bring up any skewed data, this choice turns out to be incorrect.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
