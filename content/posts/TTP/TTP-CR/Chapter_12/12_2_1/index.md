## Lesson Content
In answering two-part questions, we must determine what type of response the second speaker makes to the first speaker’s argument.

<em>The journalist responds to the editor’s criticism by</em>

> <em>(A) <strong>rejecting </strong>the editor’s conclusion while proposing …</em>

> <em>(B) <strong>providing </strong>evidence that the editor’s argument has significantly …</em>

> <em>(C) <strong>objecting to </strong>the editor’s introducing into the discussion …</em>

> <em>(D) <strong>pointing out </strong>a benefit of maintaining the board, which …</em>

> <em>(E) <strong>shifting </strong>the discussion from the argument at hand to an attack on …</em>

Notice the five introducing verbs in these answer choices, and remember that, in answering a Two-Part Method of Reasoning question, we always can refer to the stimulus and determine whether the speakers do what answer choices say they do.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The verbs used by the speakers in Two-Part Method of Reasoning questions can help us arrive at correct answers. However, as always, we must analyze these questions carefully and completely.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at another example:

> <em>Film School President: It is essential that students in our Screenwriting program have exposure to all aspects of the film business, from location scouting to editing to still photography, so that they understand how their work fits into the film industry at large. This knowledge is vital to being able both to network and to work successfully alongside other industry professionals.</em>

> <em>Screenwriting Instructor: That statement is false. Every hour spent learning other crafts is an hour not spent writing, and it is well known that a screenwriter must spend hundreds, if not thousands, of hours writing before his or her work reaches a professional standard. Therefore, any time spent on subjects other than writing will put my screenwriting students at a disadvantage in their careers.</em>

> <em>The Screenwriting Instructor responds to the Film School President’s statement by</em>

> <em>(A) pointing out a flaw in the Film School President’s argument while conceding that its main conclusion is valid.</em>

> <em>(B) rejecting the Film School President’s conclusion and presenting evidence to support a contrary conclusion.</em>

> (C) <em>rejecting the Film School President’s conclusion and providing statistical evidence that supports a contrary conclusion.</em>

Notice that the two speakers’ claims are not necessarily incompatible; it is possible that a screenwriter must spend hundreds (or thousands) of hours writing before his/her work reaches a professional standard, and yet that it is still important that a screenwriting student understand various non-writing parts of the film industry.

Nevertheless, put this issue aside.

First, we see the Film School President’s statements indicating that a broad education in film is essential for screenwriting students to network and to understand their place in the industry.

Since the stem asks us how the Screenwriting Instructor responds to the President, our correct answer must correctly describe the relationship between the two arguments.

> <em>The Screenwriting Instructor responds to the Film School President’s statement by <strong>pointing out</strong> a flaw in the Film School President’s argument while <strong>conceding that</strong> its main conclusion is valid.</em>

Does the Screenwriting Instructor take these actions? If we refer back to the stimulus, we see that the Screenwriting Instructor does neither of these two things.

Now, let’s look at the verbs in option (B).

> <em>The Screenwriting Instructor responds by <strong>rejecting</strong> the Film School President’s conclusion and </em><strong><em>presenting</em></strong><em> evidence to support a contrary conclusion.</em>

Does the Screenwriting Instructor take both actions? Yes, for sure he does.

Now, let’s look at the two verbs in option (C).

> <em>The Screenwriting Instructor responds to the Film School President’s statement by <strong>rejecting </strong>the Film School President’s conclusion and <strong>providing </strong>statistical evidence that supports a contrary conclusion.</em>

If you were not reading carefully, you may say that option (C) could also be correct.

##  Example 4 

Marco: Many people claim that, within a decade, the majority of automobiles on the road will be powered by alternative energy sources such as electricity, natural gas, or hydrogen.

Ted: As alternative energy powered vehicles become more common, their prices will significantly decrease as used vehicles enter the resale market and manufacturers take advantage of economies of scale.

Ted responds to Marco by

1. [ ] (A) arguing that Marco has based his claim on an unrepresentative sample.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) suggesting that Marco has insufficiently defined the term “alternative.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) rejecting a premise upon which Marco’s argument depends.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) questioning Marco’s motives for opposing alternative energy powered vehicles.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) pointing out additional factors that Marco may not have considered.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



Marco claims that it is unlikely that, in the future, the majority of automobiles on the road will be powered by alternative energy sources, basing his conclusion on the fact that such vehicles are more expensive than comparable gasoline powered vehicles. Notice that Ted does not say that Marco is wrong. Rather, he presents reasons that the prices of these vehicles are likely to decrease. The correct answer will certainly focus on his pointing out these additional considerations.


> <b>(A) arguing that Marco has based his claim on an unrepresentative sample.
</b>


Marco does not base his claim on a sample. Furthermore, Ted never questions Marco’s basic claim. Ted only states that there is additional information to be considered. Remember, go back and check. Use the verbs.


> <b>(B) suggesting that Marco has insufficiently defined the term “alternative. ”
</b>


Note that Ted never questions Marco’s use of the word “alternative.” Even though Marco never explicitly defines “alternative,” he gives examples of it that Ted appears to accept.


> <b>(C) rejecting a premise upon which Marco’s argument depends.
</b>


This answer is tempting: Ted does in a sense claim that Marco’s evidence is incomplete. However, Ted never states that Marco’s premise that alternative-energy vehicles are more expensive is incorrect. Rather, he simply states that their prices will decrease over time. Keep in mind that, even if Ted is correct and the prices will decrease, Marco’s claim that alternative-energy cars are more expensive may be valid at this point in time.


> <b>(D) questioning Marco’s motives for opposing alternative energy powered vehicles.
</b>


Ted never addresses Marco’s motivations, nor does he say that Marco is wrong. Ted merely offers additional information that may be pertinent to the argument.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E) pointing out additional factors that Marco may not have considered.
</b>


Ted claims that the prices of alternative energy powered vehicles will decline and supports that claim with evidence. That is, he points out additional information that might undermine the support for conclusion of the argument.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
