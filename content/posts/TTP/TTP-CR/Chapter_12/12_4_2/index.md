## Lesson Content
Partially correct answer choices are always tempting because they typically begin by accurately, or somewhat accurately, describing part of the argument.

Notice how the partially correct answer in the following example could be tempting:

> <em>It is common knowledge that overconsumption of salt can result in excessively high blood pressure. What is not as well known is that heavy consumption of sugar can have similar effects. Results of a 2010 study indicate that consuming a diet high in sugar can lead to an increase in blood pressure greater than that typically resulting from consumption of salt. This effect can be attributed in part to the body’s responding to the sugar consumption by producing large amounts of insulin, the presence of which, in turn, causes an increase in blood pressure.</em>

> <em>In which of the following ways does the author of the above passage proceed?</em>

> <em>(<u>Partially Correct Answer</u>) The author presents a claim supported by scientific evidence and explains how that claim conflicts with common knowledge. </em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) The author uses supporting information to back up a claim that supplements common knowledge.</em>

In this example, the partially correct answer starts off correctly, as the author does present a claim supported by scientific evidence.

The correct answer, on the other hand, accurately describes how the argument proceeds.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Every part of an answer must be correct, otherwise it is wrong.
{{< /alert >}}

To avoid choosing a partially correct answer, be sure to compare all parts of Method of Reasoning answer choices with what the passage says.

##  Example 8 

Eugene: The highways around our city are chronically congested with traffic.

Edda: When Houston, Texas responded to traffic congestion by widening a main highway, the availability of new lanes induced more drivers to use the highway.

Edda responds to Eugene’s argument in which of the following ways?

1. [ ] (A) She agrees with the conclusion but shows that there is reason to question the choice of evidence used.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) She demonstrates that there is reason to question the final claim made by bringing up a specific case.
3. [ ] (C) She challenges the assumption that what has worked elsewhere will work in the situation mentioned.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) She refutes the conclusion by establishing that two disparate situations are analogous.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) She uses the example of a particular case to illustrate the inadvisability of assuming that all such cases are the same.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

##### Question Level: easy



Eugene’s conclusion is that that “our city” must commit to widening its highways. The conclusion is supported by the fact that highways around “our city” are congested and the fact that, if the highways had more lanes, there would be more highway space per car.


Edda responds to Eugene’s argument by bringing up a specific case in which the approach that Eugene suggests the city must commit to, widening highways, resulted in an increase, rather than a reduction, in congestion.


Think about Edda’s response. While some of the answer choices are entirely incorrect, some of the answer choices start off saying things that could describe how Edda responded. Here’s how the choices start off:


<em>A. </em><em>She agrees with the conclusion …</em>


<em>B. </em><em>She demonstrates that there is reason to question the final claim made …</em>


<em>C. </em><em>She challenges the assumption …</em>


<em>D. </em><em>She refutes the conclusion …</em>


<em>E. </em><em>She uses the example of a particular case …</em>


Choice (A) is clearly incorrect, because she does not indicate that she agrees with the conclusion.


Choice (D) is more subtly incorrect, because, while she does provide a reason to question the conclusion, she does not directly refute the conclusion.


At the same time, choices (B), (C), and (E) all start off in ways that match what Edda does.


She does demonstrate that there is reason to question the final claim made, as (B) says.


What she does could be seen as challenging an assumption, as (C) says she does.


She does use an example of a particular case, as (E) says.


So, to answer this question correctly, we have to be sure to carefully consider all of what each choice says, so that we don’t end up choosing an answer that starts off by matching what Edda does but ends up saying something that does not match what she does.


> <b>(A) She agrees with the conclusion but shows that there is reason to question the choice of evidence used.
</b>


Edda does not indicate that she agrees with Eugene’s conclusion. Rather, she brings up an example the existence of which is a reason to question Eugene’s conclusion. This choice is blatantly incorrect.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B) She demonstrates that there is reason to question the final claim made by bringing up a specific case.
</b>


Edda does exactly what this choice says she does, demonstrates that there is reason to question the final claim made by Eugene, “Our city must commit to widening its highways,” by bringing up an example of a situation in which the use of the approach that Eugene says “our city” must commit to resulted in an increase in congestion.


> <b>(C) She challenges the assumption that what has worked elsewhere will work in the situation mentioned.
</b>


This answer choice starts off seeming to be correct, since the case that Edda brings up is a reason to question assumptions implicit in Eugene’s argument, such as the assumption that widening the highways will not result in an increase in the number of cars on the highways. However, this choice turns out to be incorrect, as the assumption that it mentions would not be an assumption that Edda challenges.


Eugene’s argument does not depend on the assumption that what worked elsewhere will work in this situation. His argument depends on the simple math he mentioned, and on assumptions that connect the implications of that math to his conclusion, none of which assumptions involves the fact that the approach of widening highways worked elsewhere. Widening existing highways could work in the situation described by Eugene even if it has never been done anywhere else.


Further, nothing that either Eugene or Edda says indicates that either believes that the approach worked elsewhere. So, it does not make sense to describe Edda’s response as challenging any assumption involving the approach’s having worked elsewhere.


> <b>(D) She refutes the conclusion by establishing that two disparate situations are analogous.
</b>


While Edda’s response does indicate that there is reason to question Eugene’s conclusion, she does not refute his conclusion.


Also, while Edda does mention a situation that is similar in some ways to the situation discussed by Eugene, she does not establish that the two situations are analogous. Rather she merely mentions the situation in Houston, Texas without establishing that it is analogous to the one discussed by Eugene.


> <b>(E) She uses the example of a particular case to illustrate the inadvisability of assuming that all such cases are the same.
</b>


The beginning of this choice matches exactly what Edda does; she does use the example of a particular case. However, while Edda does not directly say why she has brought up this example – she merely brings up the example without saying why – what Edda says is, presumably, a response to what Eugene says. So, since Eugene’s argument involves only the specifics of one case and clearly does not require assuming that all cases like the one he discusses are the same, we safely presume that Edda did not bring up the example in order to illustrate the inadvisability of assuming that all cases like the one Eugene discusses are the same, because there is no logical reason why she would have done so.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
