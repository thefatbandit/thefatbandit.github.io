## Lesson Content
Another tricky type of incorrect answer that you could see in a Method of Reasoning question is one that describes a method that the argument could use but doesn’t use.

Consider the following example:

> <em>Commercial landlords in Mennerton are excited about the completion of the new high speed rail line between Mennerton and Boselton, because they believe that, since the new line will make Mennerton more easily accessible, large businesses that would have located in Boselton will now locate instead in Mennerton, where rents are lower. They may be disappointed, as plans are now being made to extend the new line from Boselton to Verdantville. Once that extension is complete, Boselton will be an even better location for large businesses than it is now, and thus Mennerton may lose commercial tenants rather than gain them.</em>

> <em>The passage above proceeds by</em>

> <em>(<u>Incorrect Answer</u>) attacking the assumption that a change in the accessibility of Mennerton would be important to large businesses considering whether to locate there.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) providing additional information that serves as a reason to doubt the validity of a conclusion made.</em>

The landlords have concluded that, because the high speed rail line will make Mennerton more accessible, more businesses will want to locate there.

One way to weaken the support for the conclusion would be, as the incorrect answer says, attacking the assumption that a change in the accessibility of Mennerton would be important to large businesses considering whether to locate there.

The correct answer, on the other hand, describes exactly how the argument proceeds.

Here’s an example of this type of answer choice in the context of a Two-Part Method of Reasoning Question:

> <em>School Principal: Recently we have discovered that there is lead in the surface of the main schoolyard. Clearly, this lead is to blame for the lower than average reading scores of students at our school.</em>

> <em>Reading Specialist: While lead can impact cognitive ability, it seems unlikely that the lead in that schoolyard is to blame for the low reading scores, as even students who haven’t ever used that schoolyard are scoring lower than average in reading.</em>

> <em>The reading specialist indicates that there is reason to doubt the validity of the principal’s conclusion by doing which of the following?</em>

> <em>(<u>Incorrect Answer</u>) Bringing up another factor that could be the cause of the effect that the principal mentions.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) Showing that the effect that the principal mentions is still present even when the cause is not.</em>

In this example, the principal arrives at a cause-and-effect conclusion, that the lead in the schoolyard is the reason for the students’ scoring lower than average in reading.

The reading specialist could have taken various paths in indicating that there is reason to doubt the validity of the principal’s conclusion.

The method that the reading specialist used is correctly described in the correct answer.

In answering Method of Reasoning questions, don’t fall for incorrect answers that describe methods that the arguments could have used but didn’t.

##  Example 9 

Editorial: The red-legged beetle burrows its way into hardwood trees, damaging the wood in such a way as to reduce its value should it be harvested.

The editorialist’s argument proceeds by

1. [ ] (A) undermining the case for the use of a potential solution by refuting the claim that the red-legged beetles are present in the state.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) offering reason to believe that the red-legged beetle will not cause any damage in the editorialist’s state.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) pointing out that a particular course of action may be unnecessary since the conditions that would call for it are not present.
4. [ ] (D) contending that the destruction caused by insecticides would be worse than the damage to trees caused by beetles.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) arguing that, if a particular course of action is not appropriate in all cases, it is not appropriate in this particular case.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

##### Question Level: easy



The editorialist begins by providing background information about the beetle and indicating that some forestry officials wish to use insecticides because the beetle has no natural predators. The editorialist then states that the use of insecticides is somewhat destructive and, furthermore, may not be needed, since virtually no hardwood is harvested in that state.


The editorialist could have responded to the forestry officials’ assertion by questioning whether the beetles are present in the state, whether the beetles would cause damage, or whether the use of insecticides is worth its drawbacks. However, the editorialist followed none of these paths. So, we have to be careful to avoid choosing a choice that says that the editorialist took one of these paths that he could have taken but didn’t.


> <b>(A) undermining the case for the use of a potential solution by refuting the claim that the red-legged beetles are present in the state.
</b>


While the editorialist could have taken the path of making the case that the use of insecticides may be unwarranted by questioning whether the beetles are present in the state, he didn’t.


> <b>(B) offering reason to believe that the red-legged beetle will not cause any damage in the editorialist’s state.
</b>


While the editorialist could have taken the path of making the case that the use of insecticides may be unwarranted by offering a reason to believe that the beetles will cause no damage in the state, he didn’t.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(C) pointing out that a particular course of action may be unnecessary since the conditions that would call for it are not present.
</b>


This answer describes how the editorialist responds. He points out that a “particular course of action” (the use of insecticides) “may be unnecessary” (may be unwarranted) since “the conditions that would call for it” (harvesting of hardwood trees) “are not present” in the state.


> <b>(D) contending that the destruction caused by insecticides would be worse than the damage to trees caused by beetles.
</b>


While the editorialist could have taken the path of making the case that the use of insecticides may be unwarranted by contending that the destruction caused by insecticides would be worse than the damage to trees caused by beetles, he didn’t.


> <b>(E) arguing that, if a particular course of action is not appropriate in all cases, it is not appropriate in this particular case.
</b>


While the editorialist could have taken the path of making the case that the use of insecticides may be unwarranted by claiming that, if a particular course of action is not appropriate in all cases, it is not appropriate in this particular case, he didn’t.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

Let’s now discuss one more type of incorrect answer – an answer choice that describes a method that resembles what the argument does but is not what the argument does.
