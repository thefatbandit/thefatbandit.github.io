## Lesson Content
Perhaps the trickiest incorrect answers in Method of Reasoning questions are the ones that describe methods that somehow resemble methods used in the arguments although the methods described are not actually the methods used in the arguments.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Perhaps the trickiest incorrect answers in Method of Reasoning questions are the ones that describe methods that somehow resemble methods used in the arguments although the methods described are not actually the methods used in the arguments.
{{< /alert >}}

For example, the method that is actually used in an argument could be to bring up additional evidence to show that implementing a plan is unlikely to have the desired effect.

Let’s consider such a choice in context:

> <em>Reintroducing wolves to the Yellowstone area of the United States resulted in a restoration of the area’s ecosystem by restoring key, previously longstanding relationships among various animal and plant species. Some ecologists have theorized that similar results could be achieved by reintroducing dingoes, which are wild dogs somewhat similar to wolves, to areas of Australia from which they have been missing. However, the types of longstanding relationships between species that have existed in the Yellowstone area are not as common in Australia. Therefore, reintroducing dingoes would not necessarily have effects similar to those achieved via reintroducing wolves.</em>

> <em>In making the given argument, the author proceeds by</em>

> <em>(<u>Incorrect Choice</u>) demonstrating the effect of a factor by using a comparison of situations that include and do not include that factor.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) bringing up evidence that indicates that a comparison upon which a theory is based may not take into account a key difference.</em>

Notice the resemblance between the incorrect choice and the correct answer.

However, the exact truth is that the author does not <em>demonstrate the effect</em> of any factor.

The correct answer describes exactly how the argument proceeds.

To choose a choice like the correct answer rather than one like the incorrect choice, you have to be careful not to go for a choice that sounds like what is going on in the argument but doesn’t really match.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In answering Method of Reasoning questions, don’t fall for incorrect answers that are worded to sound much like what the arguments do but don’t actually describe the methods used by the arguments.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 10 

University President: If we loan Dr.

Admissions Official: Dr.

The admissions official responds to university president’s argument in which of the following ways?

1. [ ] (A) She agrees with the president on the extent of the benefits that would result from engaging in the proposed deal but brings up potential drawbacks.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) She introduces considerations that serve to call into question the validity of the president’s conclusion.
3. [ ] (C) She disagrees on principle with engaging in the proposed deal, as students who have already enrolled in the school expect to work with Dr.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) She challenges the judgment that the benefits of engaging in the deal are outweighed by the negative effects of engaging in the deal.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) She disagrees with the president’s conclusion that master classes taught by famous alumni of the New Actors Group would attract students to the school.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

##### Question Level: medium



The university president’s conclusion is that giving away the drama department’s star professor for a year will actually increase enrollment in the drama department, because the organization to which they are lending the professor will send famous alumni to give master classes.


The admissions official counters the university president’s argument by introducing the consideration that the president’s plan will eliminate one of the major reasons that students enroll in the drama department – Dr. Spencer, that is – and the consideration that nine master classes may not be sufficiently attractive to offset any decrease in the attractiveness of the school that would result from Dr. Spencer’s absence.


To correctly answer this question, we have to be careful to choose a choice that accurately describes what the admissions official does and to avoid choosing a choice that seems to describe what she does but doesn’t really do so.


> <b>(A) She agrees with the president on the extent of the benefits that would result from engaging in the proposed deal but brings up potential drawbacks.
</b>


This choice could be tempting because it to a degree resembles what the admissions official does. It seems reasonable to believe that she at least agrees that students would be attracted by the master classes, and she does bring up considerations that could conceivably be described as drawbacks. However, she does not actually agree with the president on the extent of the benefits that would result from engaging in the proposed deal. The president believes that trading the professor for the once a month master classes would cause enrollment to increase, while the admissions official is not so sure that it would.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B) She introduces considerations that serve to call into question the validity of the president’s conclusion.
</b>


As discussed in the introduction to this explanation, the admissions official counters the university president’s argument by introducing the consideration that the president’s plan will eliminate one of the major reasons that students enroll in the drama department – Dr. Spencer, that is – and the consideration that nine master classes may not be sufficiently attractive to offset any decrease in the attractiveness of the school that would result from Dr. Spencer’s absence.


If more students fail to enroll because of Dr. Spencer’s absence than do enroll because of the master classes, there will be a net decrease. Thus, given what the admissions official says, the president’s conclusion could be invalid. All the same, whether the president’s conclusion is actually invalid is irrelevant; we must be concerned with only how the admissions official’s argument addresses the president’s argument <em>structurally.</em>


> <b>(C) She disagrees on principle with engaging in the proposed deal, as students who have already enrolled in the school expect to work with Dr. Spencer.
</b>


While bringing up the fact that students may be expecting to work with Dr. Spencer could be a valid way undermine the case for engaging in the deal, bringing up that fact is not what the admissions official does.


> <b>(D) She challenges the judgment that the benefits of engaging in the deal are outweighed by the negative effects of engaging in the deal.
</b>


What this choice says certainly sounds like what the Admissions Official does: she challenges a judgment about benefits, drawbacks, and the degree to which one outweighs the other. So, you can see how, in the heat of taking the GMAT, a test-taker might either choose this answer or stare at it wondering whether it is correct. However, there’s a major problem with this choice: what this choice describes is basically the opposite of what the Admissions Official does. She doesn’t challenge the judgment that the benefits of engaging in the deal are outweighed by the negative effects of engaging in the deal. She challenges the judgment that the costs involved are outweighed by the benefits.


> <b>(E) She disagrees with the president’s conclusion that master classes taught by famous alumni of the New Actors Group would attract students to the school.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, because it sounds somewhat like what the admissions official does. However, while she does disagree that there would be a net gain in the attractiveness of the school, she doesn’t disagree that the master classes themselves would be attractive.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
