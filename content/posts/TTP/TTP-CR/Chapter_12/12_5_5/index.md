## Lesson Content
In most of the Critical Reasoning arguments that we have seen, the conclusions have appeared at or near to the ends of the passages, and the fact that they are conclusions has often been signaled by words such as “Therefore” or “Clearly.” This pattern does not hold for most arguments seen in Boldface questions.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Often, in the arguments used in Boldface questions, main conclusions are not preceded by conclusion signaling words like “therefore,” and the conclusions are worded so as to be barely noticeable.
{{< /alert >}}

For instance, in an argument about saving coral reefs in a Strengthen question, the conclusion might be something like the following:

> <em>Therefore, actually, there is much that can be done to help coral reefs survive.</em>

In an argument about the same topic in a Boldface question the conclusion could be one more like the following.

> <em>That perception is incorrect.</em>

The above Boldface question conclusion is not preceded by a signaling word and barely says anything.

Often, another factor making the main conclusion in a Boldface question tricky to find will be the fact that the conclusion does not appear at the end of the argument.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Another factor making the main conclusion in a Boldface question tricky to find will be the fact that the conclusion does not appear at the end of the argument.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example:

> <em>There is a common perception that, because higher than normal water temperatures can kill off coral reefs, and because climate change related emissions are causing increases in ocean water temperatures, other than reducing climate change causing emissions, there is not much that can be done to save reefs. <strong>That perception is incorrect.</strong> Factors such as overfishing and pollution from farm fertilizer runoff result in much more damage to coral reefs than is being caused by high temperature water, and such factors can be addressed via local policy initiatives.</em>

> <em>In the argument given, the portion in boldface plays which of the following roles?</em>

> <em>(A) It states the argument’</em><em>s main conclusion.</em>

Notice how, in the example above, the conclusion, which is stated by the portion in boldface, is expressed in just a few words found in the middle of the passage, and is not preceded by a connecting word like “therefore.” Then, the next sentence is the support for the conclusion.

So, when you are looking for the main conclusion in the passage in a Boldface question, remember that it could be anywhere in the passage.

💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Often, in the passages in Boldface questions, the conclusions are obscured by their wording and positioning.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 14 

Supplementing fossil fuels with ethanol made from corn has been touted as a way to reduce the negative environmental impacts of internal combustion engines.

Of the following, which provides the most accurate description of the roles played by the portions of the passage in boldface?

1. [ ] (A) The first is the author’s main conclusion; the second is evidence in support of the main conclusion.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) The first is a consideration in support of a conclusion that the author takes a position against; the second is evidence that the author uses to support his position.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) The first states a conclusion that the author questions; the second presents proof that the conclusion that the author questions is irrefutable.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) The first is a position that the author supports; the second is evidence that the author uses to support his main conclusion.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) The first is a conclusion that the author argues against; the second is a consideration that weighs in favor of a conclusion that the author opposes.

##### Question Level: medium



Let’s find the author’s main conclusion, if there is one. In this case, there is one, and it’s very short and hidden at the end of a sentence.


<u>Conclusion</u>: it is not.


Yes, those three words found at the end of the second sentence are the main conclusion, and what they mean in full is that the approach outlined in the first sentence of the passage is not sound. OK, now for the portions in boldface.


Now that we know what the argument’s main conclusion is, we can see that the first portion in boldface is a conclusion that the author argues against or opposes.


The second portion in boldface is supportive of the conclusion stated by the first boldfaced portion that the main conclusion opposes, and if we read the second boldfaced portion in context, we can see that it states a concession that the author makes in the process of opposing the conclusion stated by the first portion in boldface.


> <b>(A) The first is the author’s main conclusion; the second is evidence in support of the main conclusion.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, as the second bolded portion does support the first. However, as discussed in the introduction to this explanation, the first bolded portion is not the main conclusion.


> <b>(B) The first is a consideration in support of a conclusion that the author takes a position against; the second is evidence that the author uses to support his position.
</b>


This first portion in boldface is not support for the conclusion that the author takes a position against but, rather, that conclusion itself. The second is not support for the author’s position but, rather, support for the conclusion stated by the first boldfaced portion that the author opposes.


> <b>(C) The first states a conclusion that the author questions; the second presents proof that the conclusion that the author questions is irrefutable.
</b>


This choice seems almost correct, but notice there are clear differences between what it says and what actually goes on in the passage.


The first part of this choice is a little off, because the author doesn’t just, as this choice says, question the conclusion presented by the first bolded portion. The author argues against it.


The second part of this choice is also off, because, while the second bolded portion does provide support for the conclusion that the author argues against, it does not provide proof that that conclusion is irrefutable.


> <b>(D) The first is a position that the author supports; the second is evidence that the author uses to support his main conclusion.
</b>


The first bolded portion does not state a position that the author supports. The first bolded portion states a conclusion that the author argues against. Then, the second bolded portion is not evidence that the author uses to support the main conclusion. Rather, it supports the conclusion stated in the first boldfaced portion, which conclusion the author argues against.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E) The first is a conclusion that the author argues against; the second is a consideration that weighs in favor of a conclusion that the author opposes.
</b>


Both parts of this choice describe exactly what goes on in the passage, which is discussed further in the introduction to this explanation.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

Let’s now consider how characteristics of the boldface portions can add to the trickiness of Boldface questions.
