## Lesson Content
Another way in which the creators of Boldface questions trick test-takers by taking advantage of common perceptions of how arguments work is by making the boldfaced portions look as if they play roles other than the roles that they play.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In the passages in Boldface questions, the portions in boldface can be worded and positioned to appear to play roles other than the roles that they play.
{{< /alert >}}

One obvious way in which this can be done is by bolding the last sentence in the passage.

Consider the following example:

> <em>There is a common perception that, because higher than normal water temperatures can kill off coral reefs, and<strong> because climate change related emissions are causing increases in ocean water temperatures,</strong> other than reducing climate change causing emissions, there is not much that can be done to save reefs. That perception is incorrect. Factors such as overfishing and pollution from farm fertilizer runoff result in much more damage to coral reefs than is being caused by high temperature water, and <strong>such factors can be addressed via local policy initiatives.</strong></em>

> <em>In the argument given, the two portions in boldface play which of the following roles?</em>

> <em>(<u>Trap Choice</u>) The first provides support for the main conclusion of the argument; the second is that main conclusion.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) The first states a consideration in support of a conclusion that the argument opposes; the second provides support for the argument’</em><em>s main conclusion.</em>

While the portions in boldface play the roles described in the correct answer above, notice what would happen if we were to just put the two together without considering the rest of the passage.

> <strong><em>because climate change related emissions are causing increases in ocean water temperatures,</em></strong><em> <strong>such factors can be addressed via local policy initiatives.</strong></em>

Now we have something that looks almost like an argument, one in which the two portions play the roles described in the trap choice: the first portion provides support for the second portion, and the second portion is the conclusion.

To avoid getting taken in by such a trap, remember that wording and placement of statements in boldface can be misleading.

Notice how the portions in boldface could be perceived as playing roles other than the roles that they play in the following example.

##  Example 15 

Since plant foods generally do not contain vitamin B12, the accepted wisdom is that, <strong>in order to maintain sufficient levels of vitamin B12, humans must either consume animal products, such as meat or dairy products, or take supplements.</strong> This view may be incorrect.

The two sections in boldface play which of the following roles in the argument above?

1. [x] (A) The first is a conclusion that the author questions; the second is evidence that the author uses to support the author’s position.
2. [ ] (B) The first is a position that the author argues against; the second presents evidence that supports that position.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) The first is a fact that the author uses to provide context; the second is an example presented by the author to illustrate the point made in the first.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) The first and second are both used by the author to support the author’s main conclusion.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) The first is a conclusion that is not the only conclusion present in the passage; the second is an example used to support the first.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



First, let’s find the main conclusion, if there is one. In this case, the main conclusion is:


<u>Conclusion</u>: This view may be incorrect.


Now, let’s consider the roles of the portions in boldface.


The first portion in boldface precedes the main conclusion and presents the “accepted wisdom” that the main conclusion calls a “view” and says may be incorrect.


The second portion is part of the evidence that the author uses to support the main conclusion, which is that the view that, in order to maintain sufficient levels of vitamin B12, humans must either consume animal products or take supplements may be incorrect.


This question is a little tricky though, as the fact that vegetarians experienced B12 deficiencies could be perceived as supporting the view that, in order to maintain sufficient levels of vitamin B12, humans must either consume animal products or take supplements. Only by carefully considering the second bolded portion in the context of the entire argument can we see that the fact about vegetarians is actually a key part of the support for questioning the view that, in order to maintain sufficient levels of vitamin B12, humans must either consume animal products or take supplements.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(A) The first is a conclusion that the author questions; the second is evidence that the author uses to support the author’s position.
</b>


As discussed above, the first portion in boldface is a conclusion that the author questions, and the second portion in boldface is evidence that supports the author’s questioning of the conclusion stated by the first portion in boldface.


> <b>(B) The first is a position that the author argues against; the second presents evidence that supports that position.
</b>


This first half of this choice is basically correct, as the first portion in boldface does present a conclusion that the author questions, and thus basically argues against. The second half of this choice is a trap, since, taken out of context, the second bolded portion could seem to support the first. However, the truth is that, rather than support the first bolded portion, the second bolded portion is part of the support for questioning the first bolded portion.


> <b>(C) The first is a fact that the author uses to provide context; the second is an example presented by the author to illustrate the point made in the first.
</b>


This first is not just a fact that the author uses to provide context. It is the conclusion that the argument is set up to question. Furthermore, the second is not an example used to illustrate the point made in the first; it is part of the evidence supporting the case for questioning the first.


> <b>(D) The first and second are both used by the author to support the author’s main conclusion.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, as, since the first bolded portion and the second bolded portion seem to be logically related, a test-taker could get the impression that they play similar roles, and that, thus, both support the author’s main conclusion. However, as discussed above, only the second bolded portion provides support for the author’s conclusion.


> <b>(E) The first is a conclusion that is not the only conclusion present in the passage; the second is an example used to support the first.
</b>


This choice starts off correctly, as the first bolded portion is indeed a conclusion. However, it is not the only conclusion presented in the passage. Meanwhile, while the second portion in boldface might seem to support the first, the truth is that it is actually evidence that supports the case for questioning the conclusion stated in the first portion in boldface.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)
