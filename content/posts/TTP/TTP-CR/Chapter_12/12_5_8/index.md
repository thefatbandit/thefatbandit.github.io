## Lesson Content
As we just discussed, sorting out the roles of the statements in Boldface question passages can be complicated by the fact that there can be multiple conclusions with premises that support each of them.

For instance, if a portion in boldface is a premise that supports a conclusion opposed by the main conclusion of the argument, an answer choice that says that that boldfaced portion is itself a conclusion opposed by the conclusion of the argument could be a tempting trap.

Consider the following example:

> <em>There is a common perception that, because<strong> higher than normal water temperatures can kill off coral reefs</strong>,and because climate change related emissions are causing increases in ocean water temperatures, other than reducing climate change causing emissions, there is not much that can be done to save reefs. <strong>That perception is incorrect.</strong> Factors such as overfishing and pollution from farm fertilizer runoff result in much more damage to coral reefs than is being caused by high temperature water, and such factors can be addressed via local policy initiatives.</em>

> <em>In the argument given, the two portions in boldface play which of the following roles?</em>

> <em>(</em><em><u>Trap Answer</u></em><em>) The first is a common perception with which the main conclusion of the argument is in disagreement; the second is the argument’</em><em>s main conclusion.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) The first states a consideration in support of a conclusion that the argument opposes; the second is the argument’</em><em>s main conclusion.</em>

In the above example, it would be easy to fall for the trap answer’s description of the first portion in boldface, because what the trap answer says is closely related to what is correct.

The correct answer, on the other hand, correctly describes the roles of the two portions in boldface.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Boldface questions can include incorrect answers that are tempting because they say things that are partly correct or are somehow closely related to what is correct.
{{< /alert >}}

To avoid choosing a trap answer that says something partly correct or closely related to what is correct, be careful to differentiate between parts of the arguments, such as conclusions and the premises that support those conclusions.

##  Example 17 

The city of Mantrose currently pays for the maintenance of its public rail system by charging tolls to use the circular bypass road that surrounds the city.

In the argument given, the two boldfaced portions play which of the following roles?

1. [ ] (A) The first presents a plan that the argument seeks to defend against a particular criticism; the second is that criticism.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) The first presents a plan whose consequences are at issue in the argument; the second is the argument’s conclusion about those consequences.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) The first presents a reason that a plan is being opposed; the second is evidence undermining the case for that opposition.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) The first outlines a plan to which there is opposition; the second presents a reason to discount the case for that opposition.
5. [ ] (E) The first describes certain consequences of a plan to which there is opposition; the second describes other consequences of the plan that will offset the consequences described in the first.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

##### Question Level: easy



First let’s identify the main conclusion of the argument, if there is one. In this case, there is one.


<u>Conclusion</u>: The proposed change is not likely to affect maintenance of Mantrose’s public rail system.


Now that we have a reference point, let’s identify the roles of the portions in boldface.


The first portion is clearly a statement of fact, because it provides clear and verifiable information. More specifically, the first presents a plan that the argument seeks to defend.


The prediction made in the second statement is part of the support for the main conclusion. The support for the main conclusion is developed from that prediction.


To choose the correct answer, we have to keep our facts straight, and not be fooled by choices that say things that are partially correct or are somehow closely related to what is correct.


> <b>(A) The first presents a plan that the argument seeks to defend against a particular criticism; the second is that criticism.
</b>


Notice that the first part of answer (A) is correct; the first statement does present a plan that the argument seeks to defend. So far, so good. But, we must keep focused and analyze the second part of answer (A). Is the second bolded statement criticism? No, it is not.


> <b>(B) The first presents a plan whose consequences are at issue in the argument; the second is the argument’s conclusion about those consequences.
</b>


This choice is somewhat tempting, for two reasons: the first half is correct, and the second half almost sounds correct. While the second bolded portion is not, as the second half of the choice says, the argument’s conclusion, the second bolded portion does support the conclusion. So, it would be pretty easy to lose track and choose this choice, which isn’t quite correct but reads almost as if it were correct.


> <b>(C) The first presents a reason that a plan is being opposed; the second is evidence undermining the case for that opposition.
</b>


This is another choice that could trick a test-taker. The second half of the choice does correctly describe the second bolded portion. Meanwhile, while the first bolded portion does not, as this choice says, present a reason why a plan is being opposed, it does present the plan that is being opposed. So, if you weren’t paying close attention, this partially correct choice could be tempting.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D) The first outlines a plan to which there is opposition; the second presents a reason to discount the case for that opposition.
</b>


As this choice says, the first portion in boldface is the plan to which there is opposition, and the second presents a reason to discount the case for that opposition.


> <b>(E) The first describes certain consequences of a plan to which there is opposition; the second describes other consequences of the plan that will offset the consequences described in the first.
</b>


A test-taker might get tricked by this choice for the following reasons. While the first portion does not, as this choice says, describe the consequences of the plan, it does describe the plan. In addition, while the second bolded portion does not, as this choice says, describe offsetting consequences of the plan, it does describe an offsetting factor. So, this choice sounds somewhat correct.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
