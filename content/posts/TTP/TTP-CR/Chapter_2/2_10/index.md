## Lesson Content
A key aspect of arriving at the correct answers to Assumption questions is noticing the difference between trap choices and correct answers.

> - It says something that is rather clearly not an assumption integral to the argument but is so obviously connected to the argument that a test-taker is tempted to choose it.

> - It says something that may seem to be an assumption integral to the argument but is not actually an assumption integral to the argument.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A trap choice in an Assumption question does one of two things:
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s now discuss how we can avoid choosing trap choices of the first type, the ones that are rather clearly not assumptions that the arguments depend on but are obviously connected to the arguments.

These choices may explain a part of the argument, may be conclusions that the argument supports, or may relate to the argument in some other way that makes them relevant to the discussion in the passage and, therefore, tempting.

Accordingly, key to avoiding choosing a trap choice that is obviously connected to the argument is to keep in mind exactly what we are looking for, an unstated piece of information necessary in order for the evidence to support the argument's conclusion (we’re seeking an assumption).

As long as we are clear about what we are looking for and follow the strategy of identifying the conclusion and how it is supported and then seeking an assumption necessary for connecting the support to the conclusion, we’ll be unlikely to be fooled into choosing such a trap choice.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When answering an Assumption question, key to avoiding choosing a trap choice that is obviously connected to the argument is to keep in mind exactly what we are looking for, an unstated piece of information necessary in order for the evidence to support the argument's conclusion. We'll see that, as connected to the argument as such a choice may be, it simply does not do what the correct answer has to do.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example:

> <em>The compensation of agents at the Home Squad real estate agency located in Verdantville comes only in the form of commissions that are based on the prices of the houses that they sell. This year the average selling price of a house in Verdantville is expected to be only half what it was last year. Therefore, this year, the average compensation of agents at the Home Squad agency will likely be around half what it was last year.</em>

> <em>Which of the following is an assumption upon which the above conclusion depends?</em>

> <em>(<u>Trap Choice</u>)</em> <em>Demand for houses in Verdantville plummeted because a large company relocated from Verdantville to a different city.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>This year, the number of houses sold per agent at the Home Squad agency will not be significantly higher than it was last year.</em>

If a test-taker were rushing through these choices without considering exactly what type of answer is needed, the trap choice could be tempting enough that the test-taker would choose it.

However, if we keep in mind what we need to find, an assumption, and ask ourselves what gap the trap choice fills in the argument, we’ll see that it doesn’t fill a gap and avoid being fooled into choosing it.

The conclusion of the argument is the following:

> <u>Conclusion</u>: <em>This year, the average compensation of agents at the Home Squad agency will likely be around half what it was last year.</em>

In order to conclude that the agents will make less, we don’t have to assume that, as the trap choice says, a company relocated.

The correct answer, on the other hand, really is integral to the support for the conclusion.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A test-taker can significantly positively affect performance in answering Assumption questions by simply keeping in mind that the correct answers have to be assumptions integral to support for the conclusions and, thus, avoid choosing trap choices that are temptingly connected to the arguments but clearly don’t do what’s required.
{{< /alert >}}

Now, let’s consider traps of the second type, the ones that can seem to be assumptions that arguments depend upon, even though they are not really.

Consider the following example:

> <em>It had long been believed that prehistoric cave paintings, which often depict animals, were created by men for reasons related to hunting animals for food. However, many of these paintings include stenciled images of hands, and, by analyzing the images, researchers have determined that most of the hands used in creating the stenciled images were women’s hands. So, to the surprise of many, it appears that most prehistoric cave paintings were created by women.</em>

> <em>Which of the following is an assumption upon which the above argument relies?</em>

> <em>(<u>Trap Choice</u>)</em> <em>The people who created the cave paintings did not participate in hunting the types of animals depicted in the paintings.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>The hands depicted in images in cave paintings were not the hands of people other than the artists who created the paintings.</em>

Notice the difference between the trap choice and the correct answer.

The trap choice says something that one could feel makes sense given what is said in the argument.

Notice, however, that while, after reading the argument, one might assume that the artists who created the cave paintings did not hunt, the argument does not depend on that assumption.

The correct answer, on the other hand, says something that must be true in order for the argument to work.

Notice also how the correct answer is worded to make it unappealing.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

An Assumption question can include trap choices that appear to be assumptions that the argument depends upon. In order to avoid picking a choice of this type, we have to look carefully to see which answer choice truly fits a gap in the argument and which does not.
{{< /alert >}}

Now, to get to the correct answers to the following examples, remember to:

> - read the passage carefully.

> - identify <em>exactly</em> what the conclusion is.

> - identify how the conclusion is supported.

> - knock out the choices that are temptingly related to the argument but are not assumptions that the argument requires.

> - choose the choice that actually fills a gap in the argument.

##  Example 28 

People taking Pro-Max, a new plant-based protein powder, after each gym workout for three months gained more lean muscle than was gained by people taking Pro-Lean, a competing protein powder made from milk, after each gym workout for three months.

Which of the following is an assumption upon which the above argument depends?

1. [x] (A) The people who used Pro-Max did not have gym workout routines significantly more effective than those had by the people who used Pro-Lean.
2. [ ] (B) A serving of Pro-Max protein powder does not cost more money than a serving Pro-Lean protein powder.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) Pro-Lean protein powder does not taste significantly better than Pro-Max protein powder.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) None of the people who used Pro-Lean protein powder were also personal trainers in gyms.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) Pro-Max protein powder is lower in calories than Pro-Lean protein powder.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A) The people who used Pro-Max did not have gym workout routines significantly more effective than those had by the people who used Pro-Lean.
</b>


Notice that the author draws a cause-and-effect link between using Pro-Max and gaining lean muscle.


To make this cause-and-effect claim, the author must be assuming that it is in fact the Pro-Max that is responsible for the gains in lean muscle. In other words, she is assuming that there is not another possible explanation for why the people taking Pro-Max gained more lean muscle than the people taking Pro-Lean gained.


What if the people who used Pro-Max <em>did</em> have gym workout routines significantly more effective than those had by the people who used Pro-Lean? In this case, wouldn’t we have a very plausible alternative explanation for why people taking Pro-Max gained more lean muscle than people taking Pro-Lean gained? Definitely! It could have been the more effective workout routines, rather than the Pro-Max, that resulted in greater lean muscle gains experienced by people who took Pro-Max.


In that case, the argument would fall apart.


Therefore, in drawing the conclusion that Pro-Max must be superior to Pro-Lean for building lean muscle after working out in a gym, the author assumes that the people who used Pro-Max did <em>not</em> have gym workout routines significantly more effective than those had by the people who used Pro-Lean.


> <b>(B) A serving of Pro-Max protein powder does not cost more money than a serving Pro-Lean protein powder.
</b>


Notice that the author never discusses the costs of Pro-Max or Pro-Lean. Her conclusion is simply that Pro-Max must be superior to Pro-Lean for building lean muscle after working out in a gym.


Since the costs of the products would not affect the lean muscle gains achieved by people using the products, the author need not assume anything about the cost of either product.


In you chose this answer as correct, you may have done so because what it says is probably pertinent to choosing between two competing products. Costs may matter, right? However, we’re seeking not something that merely seems connected to the argument but, rather, an assumption upon which the argument depends.


> <b>(C) Pro-Lean protein powder does not taste significantly better than Pro-Max protein powder.
</b>


Notice that the author never discusses the taste of Pro-Max or Pro-Lean. Her conclusion is simply that Pro-Max must be superior to Pro-Lean for building lean muscle after working out in a gym. One or both of them could taste like swamp water, and her argument could still be valid.


Be careful not to choose as correct things that sound good or things that jive well with reality. After all, you may know that some protein powders don’t taste very good. You might have considered the possibility that, if a protein powder does not taste very good, then perhaps people would not use it regularly. However, we know that, regardless of the taste, people used the protein powders “after each gym workout for three months.”


So, the argument does not rely on any assumptions about taste.


> <b>(D) None of the people who used Pro-Lean protein powder were also personal trainers in gyms.
</b>


Remember, it was the people taking Pro-Max for three months after each gym workout who gained more lean muscle than was gained by people taking Pro-Lean. From that fact, the author concludes that Pro-Max must be superior to Pro-Lean for building lean muscle after working out in a gym.


If anything, a person’s being a personal trainer in a gym would contribute to that person’s gaining lean muscle. So, if, contrary to what this choice says, SOME of the people using Pro-Lean were personal trainers, we’d be even more convinced that Pro-Max is the superior product, because it would be the case that, even with the advantage of being personal trainers, the people using Pro-Lean did not gain as much lean muscle as the people using Pro-Max gained.


Thus, the argument certainly does not depend on the assumption that none of the people who consumed Pro-Lean protein powder were also personal trainers in gyms.


> <b>(E) Pro-Max protein powder is lower in calories than Pro-Lean protein powder.
</b>


 This choice is a tempting trap that may seem to be an assumption upon which the argument depends.


It would be easy to read this choice and decide that what this choice says, that Pro-Max is lower in calories than Pro-Lean, is an assumption necessary for arriving at the argument’s conclusion.


Why would it be easy to decide that? Because the conclusion is that Pro-Max is “superior,” and we may think that a powder that is “lower in calories” is superior, and that one not “lower in calories” would not be superior.


Remember, though, what we are looking for “an assumption upon which the above argument depends.” Does what this choice says have to be assumed in order for the argument to work?


The argument’s conclusion is that Pro-Max is “superior” to Pro-Lean. To support that conclusion, the argument uses the evidence that people taking Pro-Max gained more lean muscle than people taking Pro-Lean gained. So, the argument is based on RESULTS, and here’s the thing:


We don’t have to assume anything about calories in order for those results to support the conclusion. Even if Pro-Max were NOT lower in calories than Pro-Lean is, the results would still support the conclusion that Pro-Max is superior for building lean muscle.


So, the argument does not depend on the assumption that Pro-Max is lower in calories than Pro-Lean because, even if Pro-Max were not lower in calories, the evidence provided would still support the argument’s conclusion.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

##  Example 29 

Homeownership, as opposed to renting a home, can have many benefits, both for individuals who own homes and for society as a whole.

Which of the following is an assumption on which the argument relies?

1. [ ] (A) Crime rates are lower in cities in which the ratio of homeowners to renters is relatively high because people who own homes tend to feel invested in their communities.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) People involved in government are interested in improving conditions for individuals and society at large.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) Without government encouragement to buy homes, most people in the country will choose to rent the homes in which they live.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) The post retirement housing expenses of people who own homes are often lower than those of people who rent.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) Some of the people who are currently renting homes or considering renting homes could afford to buy homes should they choose to do so.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



Breakdown of the Argument:


<u>Premise:</u> Homeownership can have many benefits, both for individuals and for society.


<u>Premise:</u> Fewer than 50 percent of the country’s citizens own homes.


<u>Premise:</u> The government does little to encourage homeownership.


<u>Conclusion:</u> By doing more to encourage homeownership by making people aware of the advantages homeownership has over renting, the government could improve conditions both for individuals and for society.


The reasoning of the argument is that, since homeownership can have benefits, and since fewer than 50 percent of the country’s citizens own homes, by encouraging people to buy homes via making them aware of the benefits of buying homes, the government can make everyone better off. Of course, there is an assumption in this argument, the assumption that people will buy homes if they are aware of the benefits of homeownership.


Remember, whenever a plan or course of action is presented, the author believes that that plan will work as she has proposed. She assumes that she has carefully and comprehensively analyzed the situation. She assumes that there are not any malicious variables that will spoil the plan. We’ll keep these ideas in mind as we look through the answer choices.


> <b>(A) Crime rates are lower in cities in which the ratio of homeowners to renters is relatively high because people who own homes tend to feel invested in their communities.
</b>


 This choice could be tempting, because what it says is related to the argument and because it could seem to support the argument’s premise that homeownership benefits society as a whole.


However, the effect of this choice is not the effect we need. This choice <em>explains </em>how homeownership benefits society, rather than states an assumption upon which the argument relies.


The argument is already supported by the fact that homeownership benefits society. So, making the argument does not require assuming that homeownership has any particular benefit to society, such as its resulting in reduced levels of crime.


> <b>(B) People involved in government are interested in improving conditions for individuals and society at large.
</b>


 While choices (A), (C), and (D) are clearly not assumptions upon which the argument relies, this answer choice could seem to be an assumption integral to the argument, as one could make the case that, in order for the benefits described in the argument to be generated, people in government would have to be interested in making that outcome happen.


However, if we look carefully, we can see that this choice supports the wrong conclusion.


This choice supports the conclusion that the government will encourage homeownership. After all, the fact that people in government are interested in improving conditions supports the conclusion that the government will encourage homeownership because of its benefits.


However, the conclusion of the argument is not that the government <em>will</em> encourage homeownership. The conclusion is that, by doing more to encourage homeownership, the government <em>could</em> improve conditions.


We can see that it’s not necessary to assume that people in government are interested in improving conditions in order to conclude that they could improve them by encouraging homeownership.


> <b>(C) Without government encouragement to buy homes, most people in the country will choose to rent the homes in which they live.
</b>


 This choice is tempting because it is related to what the passage says. In fact, it’s a <em>conclusion </em>supported by the passage.


By saying that fewer than 50 percent of people own homes and that the government has not encouraged homeownership, the passage supports the conclusion that, without government encouragement, most people will choose to rent.


However, this choice is not an assumption upon which the argument relies.


Notice that, even if, without government encouragement, most people would choose to buy rather than rent, it is still possible that, by encouraging homeownership, the government could get even more people to buy and thus benefit society.


So, even if what this choice says were not true, the argument could still work.


> <b>(D) The post retirement housing expenses of people who own homes are often lower than those of people who rent.
</b>


 This choice could be tempting since this choice may partially <em>explain </em>how homeownership benefits individuals who own homes: homeownership may help them to have lower post retirement housing expenses.


However, this choice is not an assumption upon which the argument relies, as the argument is already supported by the fact that homeownership benefits individuals who own homes, and that fact supports the argument without being explained.


In other words, arriving at the conclusion does not require assuming that homeownership has the particular benefit mentioned by this choice. Even if the post retirement housing expenses of people who own homes WERE NOT lower than those of people who rent, the argument could still work.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E) Some of the people who are currently renting homes or considering renting homes could afford to buy homes should they choose to do so.
</b>


 The support for the conclusion that conditions for everyone would improve if the government were to encourage homeownership by making people aware of the advantages homeownership has over renting depends on the assumption that some of the people who are renting or considering renting homes could afford to buy homes. After all, if nobody renting or considering renting could afford to buy a home, then making people aware that they would benefit from purchasing homes rather than renting would make no difference in the number who buy homes.


In other words, if NONE of the people who are currently renting homes or considering renting homes could afford to buy homes should they choose to do so, then we have a malicious variable, and the argument falls apart.


Notice that, in general, this answer choice helps underscore the author’s belief that there are no malicious variables that will enter the equation and spoil the plan.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

Let’s now discuss common incorrect answer choices in assumption questions.
