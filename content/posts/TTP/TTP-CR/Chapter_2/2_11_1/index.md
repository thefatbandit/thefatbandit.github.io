## Lesson Content
Sometimes, Critical Reasoning incorrect answer choices will take important words or concepts from the stimulus and swap these with seemingly similar stand-ins that do not actually mean the same thing.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Sometimes, Critical Reasoning incorrect answer choices will take important words or concepts from the stimulus and swap these with seemingly similar stand-ins that do not actually mean the same thing.
{{< /alert >}}

Of course, since what these incorrect choices discuss is similar to what the passage discusses, if we’re not paying attention, it’s easy to become ensnared by such a switch.

One way in which this type of swap can appear in an Assumption question is in an answer choice that helps to support a conclusion that is related to the conclusion of the argument but is not the conclusion of the argument.

Consider the following mini-example:

> <em>Zelda: I want to sell more of my watercolor paintings. Displaying some of them in the town library seems to be the best way to do so.</em>

> <em>Tom: Your work would be seen by more people at Snazzy’s Gallery downtown. So, you would be more likely to achieve your goal if you were to display your work there.</em>

> <em>Which of the following, if assumed, would allow Tom’s conclusion to be properly drawn?</em>

> <em>(<u>Choice that Supports the Wrong Conclusion</u>) Paintings sold at Snazzy’s Gallery generally sell for prices significantly higher than are received for similar paintings that have been displayed at the library.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) Some people who would see paintings at Snazzy’s Gallery would be interested in purchasing paintings of the type that Zelda wants to sell.</em>

This question is tricky, because Tom’s conclusion is about Zelda’s goal, and Zelda’s goal is mentioned long before Tom’s conclusion is stated.

> <u>Zelda’s Goal</u>: <em>I want to sell more of my watercolor paintings.</em>

Thus, Tom’s conclusion is:

> <u>Tom’s Conclusion</u>: <em>You would be more likely to achieve your goal (of selling more of your paintings) if you were to display them at Snazzy’s Gallery.</em>

Note that the choice that supports the wrong conclusion may <em>appear </em>at first glance to be necessary for supporting Tom’s conclusion, as it mentions something that we might consider important in this situation, the prices received for paintings sold at each of the two venues.

However, we are seeking an assumption necessary for arriving at a specific conclusion, Tom’s conclusion that Zelda could <em>sell more paintings</em> at the gallery than she would at the library.

What Tom’s conclusion <em>does</em> depend on is the assumption that people who would see Zelda’s paintings at Snazzy’s Gallery would purchase paintings like hers.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When answering Assumption questions, beware of trap choices that support conclusions other than the conclusion of the argument in the stimulus.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 30 

It is generally believed that, since plant matter generally does not contain in significant quantities vitamin B12, a nutrient found in many animal products, people following plant-based diets may become deficient in vitamin B12.

Which of the following is an assumption upon which the author relies in arriving at the conclusion presented?

1. [ ] (A) A person consuming plant-based food to which vitamin B12 has been added cannot detect the presence of the added B12 by simply tasting the food.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) No people have health issues that involve their having difficulty absorbing and utilizing the B12 in the food they eat.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) It will not turn out that that the environmental consequences of people’s choosing to follow plant-based diets are worse than those of people’s consuming some animal products.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) All people who follow plant-based diets are aware that, by following plant-based diets, they may become deficient in vitamin B12.
5. [ ] (E) There are no necessary nutrients that are not present in plant material and that cannot be added to plant-based foods or consumed in supplement form.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

##### Question Level: medium



Remember, when answering an Assumption question, we must find an assumption necessary in order for the evidence to support the conclusion of the argument presented, not a different conclusion. So, we have to be sure to correctly identify the exact conclusion of the argument presented.


The following is the conclusion of the argument presented:


<u>Conclusion</u>: There is no reason to be concerned that the ongoing trend toward following plant-based diets will result in anyone’s experiencing vitamin B12 deficiency.


The following is the support provided for that conclusion:


<u>Premise</u>: People following plant-based diets can easily add vitamin B12 to plant-based foods or take supplements that provide vitamin B12.


So, the argument jumps from the premise that people following plant-based diets can add vitamin B12 to foods or take supplements to the conclusion that there is NO REASON to be concerned that the ongoing trend toward following plant-based diets will result in ANYONE’S experiencing vitamin B12 deficiency.


That’s a pretty big jump involving a variety of assumptions.


> <b>(A) A person consuming plant-based food to which vitamin B12 has been added cannot detect the presence of the added B12 by simply tasting the food.
</b>


This choice supports the wrong conclusion. It supports a conclusion along the lines of that B12 added to plant-based foods is not detectable by someone eating those foods. Of course, that’s not the conclusion of the argument presented.


Meanwhile, regardless of whether what this choice says is true, the evidence provided supports the argument’s conclusion. After all, even if people CAN detect the presence of B12 added to food by simply tasting the food, the fact that people can add B12 to foods or take B12 supplements could support the conclusion that there is no reason to be concerned that the trend toward following plant-based diets will result in anyone’s experiencing B12 deficiency.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B) No people have health issues that involve their having difficulty absorbing and utilizing the B12 in the food they eat.
</b>


This choice could be tempting because it supports a conclusion similar to the argument’s conclusion.


This choice provides some support for the conclusion that no people will experience B12 deficiencies. After all, the information that no people have health issues that involve their having difficulty absorbing and utilizing the B12 in the food they eat makes us more confident that nobody will experience a B12 deficiency.


However, what this choice says is not an assumption upon which the support for the conclusion of <em>this</em> argument depends.


After all, health issues resulting in B12 deficiencies and the following of plant-based diets resulting in B12 deficiencies are two separate matters.


So, even if, contrary to what this choice says, SOME people DO HAVE health issues that involve their having difficulty absorbing and utilizing the B12 in the food they eat, the fact that people can add B12 to foods or take B12 supplements could support the conclusion that there is no reason to be concerned that the trend toward following plant-based diets will result in anyone’s experiencing B12 deficiency.


> <b>(C) It will not turn out that that the environmental consequences of people’s choosing to follow plant-based diets are worse than those of people’s consuming some animal products.
</b>


This choice supports the wrong conclusion. It supports a conclusion along the lines of that following plant-based diets is better for the environment than consuming animal products. Of course, that’s not the conclusion of the argument presented.


Meanwhile, regardless of whether what this choice says is true, the evidence provided supports the argument’s conclusion. After all, even if it WILL turn out that that the environmental consequences of people’s choosing to follow plant-based diets are worse than those of people’s consuming some animal products, the fact that people can add B12 to foods or take B12 supplements could support the conclusion that there is no reason to be concerned that the trend toward following plant-based diets will result in anyone’s experiencing B12 deficiency.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D) All people who follow plant-based diets are aware that, by following plant-based diets, they may become deficient in vitamin B12.
</b>


The conclusion that there is no reason to be concerned that the trend toward following plant-based diets will result in anyone’s experiencing vitamin B12 deficiency is supported by the premise that people following plant-based diets can easily add vitamin B12 to foods or take supplements.


Of course, in order for that premise to support the conclusion, the two have to be connected by the assumption that people are aware that, by following plant-based diets, they may become deficient in vitamin B12. After all, if people are NOT aware of that fact, then, even though they can add B12 to food or take supplements, some may not add B12 to food or take supplements. In that case, the conclusion that there is no reason to be concerned that the trend toward following plant-based diets will result in anyone’s experiencing vitamin B12 deficiency is not supported by the premise.


So, what this choice says is an assumption upon which the argument depends since, if NOT all people who follow plant-based diets are aware that, by following plant-based diets, they may become deficient in vitamin B12, the argument falls apart.


(Notice that, because this choice uses the word “all,” this choice may be considered an “extreme choice.” Nevertheless, it is the correct answer to this question. So, here we have a great example of a question that we would miss if we were to use the gimmicky method of eliminating all “extreme choices.”)


> <b>(E) There are no necessary nutrients that are not present in plant material and that cannot be added to plant-based foods or consumed in supplement form.
</b>


This choice supports the wrong conclusion. It supports a conclusion along the lines of that people following plant-based diets get all the nutrients they need. Of course, that’s not the conclusion of the argument presented.


Meanwhile, regardless of whether what this choice says is true, the evidence provided supports the argument’s conclusion. After all, even if there are SOME necessary nutrients that are not present in plant material and that cannot be added to plant-based foods or consumed in supplement form, the fact that people can add B12 to foods or take B12 supplements could support the conclusion that there is no reason to be concerned that the trend toward following plant-based diets will result in anyone’s experiencing B12 deficiency.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

In addition to looking out for answer choices that support the wrong conclusions, we need to be on the lookout for choices that do the reverse of what the correct answer does.
