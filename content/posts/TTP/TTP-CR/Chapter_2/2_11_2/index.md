## Lesson Content
Assumption questions often include answer choices that do basically the reverse of what a correct answer would do.

These “reverse” answer choices can be surprisingly seductive at first since they express something that is relevant to the argument in the passage and that has an effect on that argument.

Let’s return to Steven and his unfortunate experience with the polygraph test:

> <em>Steven was asked to take a polygraph, i.e., lie detector, test prior to being hired as a security guard at Company X. In the words of the polygraph examiner, Steven “absolutely failed” the section that asked whether the information on his resume was truthful. Company X determined that, since Steven failed the polygraph test, he must have lied on his resume.</em>

> <em>In drawing the conclusion, Company X assumed which of the following? </em>

Consider the following as a possible answer choice:

> <em>A polygraph can be failed for a number of reasons that may be unrelated to the truth of the subject’s statements.</em>

Notice that this answer choice weakens the support for the conclusion by calling into question the reliability of the polygraph test, thus doing the opposite of what a correct answer would.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When answering Assumption questions, look out for choices that have effects that are basically the reverse of the effect of an assumption that would help support an argument.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example.

##  Example 31 

State Governor: In order to avoid having taxpayers pay for the lifestyles of people who could be working but choose instead to use illicit drugs that render them dysfunctional and unemployable, I propose that we regularly test for illicit drug use everyone receiving welfare benefits and that any such recipient testing positive for illicit drug use not be eligible to receive welfare benefits for two years following the test.

Which of the following must the governor be assuming in coming to the above conclusion?

1. [x] (A) The savings achieved by making drug users ineligible to receive welfare benefits will be greater than the expense of testing all welfare benefit recipients for drug use.
2. [ ] (B) People who use illicit drugs and who know that they are going to be tested for drug use can take measures that result in their testing negative for drug use.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) Everyone who engages in the use of illicit drugs becomes dysfunctional and unemployable.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) Most people who become ineligible for welfare benefits because of their testing positive for use of illicit drugs will discontinue their drug use and find employment.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) The percentage of people receiving welfare benefits who use illicit drugs is greater than the percentage of people not receiving such benefits who use illicit drugs.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

##### Question Level: medium



The governor’s reasoning is that, since taxpayers pay for welfare benefits, taxpayer money can be saved via testing welfare benefit recipients for illicit drug use and denying welfare benefits to people whose test results indicate that they use illicit drugs.


<u>Conclusion:</u> By following this strategy we can save taxpayer dollars.


In order to choose the correct answer to this question, we have to keep the governor’s conclusion in mind. Otherwise, we could be tempted into choosing a trap choice that expresses something that is related to the argument but is not an assumption upon which the argument depends.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A) The savings achieved by making drug users ineligible to receive welfare benefits will be greater than the expense of testing all welfare benefit recipients for drug use.
</b>


 The point of using the strategy is to save taxpayer money, and denying welfare benefits would reduce the amount of money spent.


However, there’s another part to the plan, testing the recipients for illicit drug use. If the amount of money that would have to be spent to test all recipients for drug use exceeds the amount of money that would be saved by denying benefits to those who test positive, then the strategy will not be successful in saving taxpayer money.


In other words, if the savings achieved by making drug users ineligible to receive welfare benefits will NOT be greater than the expense of testing all welfare benefit recipients for drug use – a malicious variable for sure – then the argument falls apart.


So, the success of the plan depends on the assumption that the savings achieved through denying drug users benefits will be greater than the expense of testing.


> <b>(B) People who use illicit drugs and who know that they are going to be tested for drug use can take measures that result in their testing negative for drug use.
</b>


This answer choice is relevant to the argument, but its effect on the argument is the opposite of what we are looking for. Rather than provide additional support for the conclusion, this choice weakens the argument.


After all, if people who use drugs can hide their drug use by taking measures that result in their testing negative for drug use, then the likelihood of success of the proposed strategy, which depends on correctly identifying those who use drugs, is questionable.


In general, when answering an Assumption question, be careful not to choose an answer choice that says something that weakens the argument.


> <b>(C) Everyone who engages in the use of illicit drugs becomes dysfunctional and unemployable.
</b>


The governor’s conclusion is about a strategy for saving money, not about the effects of using illicit drugs.


The strategy of denying welfare benefits to people who test positive for illicit drugs could result in saving taxpayer dollars even if most people who use illicit drugs do NOT become dysfunctional and unemployable.


So, this choice does not state an assumption upon which the argument depends.


> <b>(D) Most people who become ineligible for welfare benefits because of their testing positive for use of illicit drugs will discontinue their drug use and find employment.
</b>


This choice is a trap.


Since the governor did mention something about “people who could be working” if they weren’t using drugs, one could get the impression that the point of the strategy is to get people to stop using drugs and start working.


However, that is not exactly the point of the strategy. The point of the strategy is simply to save taxpayer money, and it is possible that implementing the strategy could result in saving taxpayer money even if most people who become ineligible for welfare benefits because of their testing positive for use of illicit drugs will NOT discontinue their drug use and find employment.


So, what this choice says does not have to be true in order for the strategy to work.


> <b>(E) The percentage of people receiving welfare benefits who use illicit drugs is greater than the percentage of people not receiving such benefits who use illicit drugs.
</b>


While, in order for the strategy to work, at least some people receiving welfare benefits have to be using illicit drugs, it does not have to be the case that most people receiving welfare benefits are using illicit drugs.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

Let’s now talk about an answer choice that doesn’t help to support the argument but instead merely explains an aspect of the scenario presented in the passage.
