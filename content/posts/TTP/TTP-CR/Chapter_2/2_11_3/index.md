## Lesson Content
Sometimes an incorrect choice in an Assumption question will provide an explanation for something discussed in the passage.

Let’s consider again our example of Henrietta and her pie:

> <em>Henrietta bakes the best apple pies in Clay County. The best apple pie entries win the Clay County Southern Charm baking contest every year without fail. Therefore, Henrietta’s apple pie will win the Southern Charm baking contest this year.</em>

> <em>Which of the following is an assumption upon which the argument depends?</em>

> <em>(<u>Incorrect Choice that Explains</u>)</em> <em>Every time the Southern Charm baking contest has been held, the same judges have judged the pies entered into the contest.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> Henrietta will enter her apple pie into the Southern Charm baking contest.

The incorrect choice that explains could be tempting.

Of course, the fact that an answer choice provides an explanation of an aspect of the scenario discussed in the passage does not make it the correct answer.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The fact that an answer choice provides an explanation of an aspect of the scenario discussed in the passage does not make it the correct answer. If the question asks for an assumption upon which the argument depends, only an answer choice that provides such an assumption will be correct.
{{< /alert >}}

As we discussed previously, the correct answer in the Henrietta’s pie example is an assumption upon which the argument depends.

Be sure to differentiate any choices that are explanations from the choice that is an assumption as you consider the following example.

##  Example 32 

A study undertaken in Sweden found that, when people worked for six hours a day, they tended to be more productive and take fewer sick days than when they worked for the more traditional seven to eight hours per day.

Which of the following must be true in order for the above conclusion to be correct?

1. [ ] (A) People who work for six hours per day generally have more energy for managing various aspects of their lives than is had by people who work for more hours per day.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) Financial gains achieved by having people work more productively for fewer hours per day would exceed the costs of hiring additional people to make up for the reduction in hours worked by each person.
3. [ ] (C) People who go from working eight hours a day to working six hours per day generally report that they experience reductions in stress and increases in job satisfaction.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) It is always the case that a reduction in the number of hours per day a person works results in an increase in that person’s productivity.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) Most workers would prefer working for only six hours per day over working seven to eight hours per day.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

##### Question Level: easy



Breakdown of the Argument:


<u>Premise</u>: The results of a study indicate that, when people worked for six hours a day, they tended to be more productive and take fewer sick days than when they worked for the more traditional seven to eight hours per day.


<u>Conclusion</u>: For companies seeking to maximize profits, it would make sense to have more workers, each of them working for six hours rather than for seven to eight hours per day.


While the question does not use the word “assumption,” in asking “Which of the following must be true in order for the conclusion to be correct?” the question is asking for an assumption upon which the argument depends.


The argument goes from the premise, which states simply that people involved in a study were more productive and took fewer sick days when they worked for six hours a day than when they worked seven to eight hours per day, to the conclusion that therefore companies seeking to maximize profits would be better off using more workers working for six hours per day rather than fewer workers working for seven to eight hours per day. Making this leap requires making various assumptions.


One basic assumption is that there are no malicious variables that will enter into the equation and spoil the plan.


> <b>(A) People who work for six hours per day generally have more energy for managing various aspects of their lives than is had by people who work for more hours per day.
</b>


 This choice may explain why people who work fewer hours per day are more productive: they have more energy. However, we don’t need such an explanation.


The argument has already established that when people work for six hours a day they are more productive than when they work seven to eight hours per day. Arriving at the argument’s conclusion from that evidence does not require explaining why they are more productive.


In general, when we’re seeking an assumption upon which an argument rests, an answer choice that presents an explanation for one of the facts presented in the passage will not be the correct answer.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B) Financial gains achieved by having people work more productively for fewer hours per day would exceed the costs of hiring additional people to make up for the reduction in hours worked by each person.
</b>


 Maximizing profits requires not just maximizing productivity but also taking costs into account.


While workers who work for six hours a day might be more productive than workers working seven to eight hours per day, companies whose workers are working fewer hours per day would probably have to take on more workers. Thus, in order for companies to become more profitable by having people work for fewer hours per day, the financial gains achieved by using that strategy have to be greater than the expenses associated with hiring additional people as necessary to compensate for the reduction in hours worked per person.


After all, if financial gains achieved by having people work more productively for fewer hours per day would NOT exceed the costs of hiring additional people to make up for the reduction in hours worked by each person, then the strategy of having people work for fewer hours per day would not result in profit maximization, and the argument would fall apart.


Notice that, in general, this answer choice underscores the author’s belief that there are no malicious variables that will enter the equation and spoil the plan.


> <b>(C) People who go from working eight hours a day to working six hours per day generally report that they experience reductions in stress and increases in job satisfaction.
</b>


 This choice may explain why people who work fewer hours per day are more productive: they experience reductions in stress and increases in job satisfaction. However, we don’t need such an explanation.


The argument has already established that people are more productive when they work six hours per day than they are when they work seven to eight hours per day. Arriving at the argument’s conclusion from that evidence does not require explaining why they are more productive.


> <b>(D) It is always the case that a reduction in the number of hours per day a person works results in an increase in that person’s productivity.
</b>


 The argument is specifically about having people work six hours per day rather than seven to eight hours per day. So, it does not have to be the case that <em>any</em> reduction in the hours a person works per day will result in increased productivity in order for the argument to work.


Even if, for instance, a person going from working for three hours a day to working for one hour a day would become much less productive, barely having time to start working before the workday ended, it could still be the case that companies could maximize profits by having people work for six hours rather than for seven or eight hours per day.


> <b>(E) Most workers would prefer working for only six hours per day over working seven to eight hours per day.
</b>


 The conclusion depends only on the connection between the productivity of people who work only six hours per day and profitability. Making this connection does not require assuming that people prefer to work six hours per day rather than eight hours per day. Even if they do not prefer working for fewer hours per day, having them work for fewer hours per day could be more profitable.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

Let’s now discuss a type of incorrect answer that does not help to support the argument in the stimulus but rather is itself a conclusion supported by the information in the stimulus.
