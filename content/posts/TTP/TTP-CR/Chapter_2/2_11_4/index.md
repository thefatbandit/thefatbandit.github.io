## Lesson Content
Another type of incorrect answer choice that can appear in an Assumption question is a choice that states a conclusion that is supported by the passage rather than an assumption upon which the argument depends.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When answering an Assumption question, be careful not to choose an answer choice that states a conclusion supported by what the passage says. We must find a choice that expresses an assumption integral to the argument.
{{< /alert >}}

If such an answer choice appears in an Assumption question, the choice will be both tempting and incorrect, because we don’t need a conclusion supported by the passage to answer an Assumption question.

Consider the following example:

> <em>Senator: The recent housing crisis occurred because a significant percentage of homeowners had taken on more debt than made sense given their income levels. Of course, doing so was only possible because lenders were loaning money to homebuyers who were unlikely to be able to repay the loans. In order to avoid repeating the housing crisis, we must enact regulations to prevent this type of lending.</em>

> <em>Which of the following is an assumption upon which the above conclusion depends?</em>

> <em>(<u>Conclusion Supported by the Passage Choice</u>) The recent housing crisis would not have occurred had homeowners better managed their debt levels.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) Without regulations that prevent them from doing so, lenders will once again provide to homeowners loans that do not make sense given the homeowners’ incomes.</em>

The conclusion supported by the passage choice is tempting, because what it says makes perfect sense given what is said in the passage.

We are not, however, looking for a choice that merely makes sense given what the passage says.

Does the Senator’s conclusion depend on assuming that the recent housing crisis would not have occurred had homeowners better managed their debt levels? It does not.

The correct answer, on the other hand, does express an assumption that the Senator must be making.

Now, beware of choices that are conclusions supported by what the passages say as you consider the following examples.

##  Example 33 

In the City of Verdana, more than 50 percent of the population is considered obese.

Which of the following must be true in order for the above conclusion to follow from the evidence provided?

1. [ ] (A) Many of the residents of Verdana are not over the age of 40.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) Obesity is a common health problem among the residents of Verdana.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) Doctors in Verdana agree that sufficient daily exercise is vital for health and well-being.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) Residents of Verdana would be willing to engage in daily exercise.
5. [ ] (E) Public health officials in Verdana believe that the residents would benefit from daily exercise.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



<u>Background</u>: In the City of Verdana, more than 50 percent of the population is considered obese.


<u>Premise</u>: The main driver of this phenomenon is lack of sufficient daily exercise on the part of Verdana residents, most of whom are not fully aware of how beneficial exercise could be for their health.


<u>Premise</u>: Public health officials in Verdana plan to begin a campaign to educate the residents on the benefits of daily exercise.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Clearly, once this campaign is underway, the rate of obesity in Verdana will decrease.


The author has presented a plan. Any time an author presents a plan, he assumes that the plan will work. In other words, he assumes that there are not variables that will enter into the equation that will spoil the plan. In the TTP Course, we refer to such variables as “malicious variables.” When an author presents a plan, he assumes that there are no malicious variables.


Because this is an Assumption question, we’re seeking an answer choice that presents an unwritten premise that must be true in order for the argument to work.


> <b>(A) Many of the residents of Verdana are not over the age of 40.
</b>


What is the significance of being over the age of 40? If you chose this answer as correct, you likely constructed an unsupported story that people over 40 will have a hard time exercising daily or losing weight and that, therefore, the author must be assuming that many of the residents of Verdana are not over the age of 40.


Of course, nothing in the passage, or in reality, supports that story. None, some, many, or all of the residents of Verdana could be over the age of 40, and the argument could still work perfectly. The author certainly does not <em>need</em> to assume that many of the residents of Verdana are not over the age of 40.


Remember, it is important not to create unsupported stories.


> <b>(B) Obesity is a common health problem among the residents of Verdana.
</b>


The author states that more than 50 percent of the population in Verdana is considered obese. So, this choice could be tempting, because what this choice says is a conclusion supported by a fact stated by the author. In other words, the fact that more than 50 percent of the population of Verdana is obese indicates that obesity is common health problem in Verdana.


So, to avoid choosing this incorrect choice, we have to remember what we are looking for. We aren’t looking for a conclusion supported by the passage. We are looking for an assumption necessary in order for the argument’s evidence to support the argument’s conclusion. The author does not have to assume that obesity is a common health problem in Verdana in order for the argument to work.


The author has stated that more than 50 percent of Verdana’s population is obese. In doing so, he has indicated that obesity is common in Verdana. He certainly does not have to assume something that he has already indicated is the case.


In answering an Assumption question, we must be careful not to choose as correct a choice that is not a necessary assumption but rather a conclusion that follows from what the passage says.


> <b>(C) Doctors in Verdana agree that sufficient daily exercise is vital for health and well-being.
</b>


The passage presents as fact that “the main driver of this phenomenon is lack of sufficient daily exercise.”


So, the passage establishes that lack of daily exercise is responsible for the high rate of obesity in Verdana. That fact, when presented in the context of the other facts provided, is more than sufficient to support the argument’s conclusion.


The author need not assume that doctors in Verdana agree that sufficient daily exercise is vital for health and well-being. That assumption would simply serve to magnify or clarify what the passage has already told us about daily exercise. Maybe doctors agree that daily exercise is vital for health and well-being, or maybe they don’t. Either way, what the argument says about daily exercise is a fact that supports the conclusion.


When answering an Assumption question, we must be careful not to choose as correct a choice that essentially magnifies or clarifies a fact provided in the passage.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) Residents of Verdana would be willing to engage in daily exercise.
</b>


Public health officials in Verdana plan to begin a campaign to educate the residents on the benefits of daily exercise. The author concludes that, once they do so, the rate of obesity in Verdana will decrease.


Any time an author concludes that a plan will work, he assumes that there will not be unanticipated variables – malicious variables, as we call them here at TTP – that spoil the plan.


However, what if it were true that residents of Verdana would NOT be willing to engage in daily exercise? In that case, the public health officials in Verdana could run a million campaigns to educate the residents on the benefits of daily exercise, yet nothing would change, because the residents of Verdana would NOT be willing to engage in daily exercise. In other words, the unwillingness of Verdana residents to engage in daily exercise would be a malicious variable that would spoil the public health officials’ plan.


Therefore, the author must be assuming that residents of Verdana would be willing to engage in daily exercise. Notice that the argument would fall apart without this assumption.


> <b>(E) Public health officials in Verdana believe that the residents would benefit from daily exercise.
</b>


 This choice could be tempting because it follows from what the passage says.


The passage says that public health officials in Verdana plan to begin a campaign to educate the residents on the benefits of daily exercise. What makes sense, then, is that the public health officials believe that the residents would benefit from daily exercise. After all, why would public health officials begin a campaign to educate the residents on the benefits of daily exercise if the public health officials didn’t believe that the residents would benefit from daily exercise?


To avoid choosing this incorrect choice, we have to remember what we are looking for. We aren’t looking for a conclusion supported by the passage. We are looking for an assumption necessary in order for the argument’s evidence to support the argument’s conclusion, and the author does not have to assume that the public health officials in Verdana believe that the residents would benefit from daily exercise in order for the argument to work.


The author states the fact that more than 50 percent of the population in Verdana is considered obese. He then mentions a plan, and concludes that implementing the plan will result in a reduction in the rate of obesity in Verdana.


Implementing the plan could result in a reduction in the rate of obesity whether public health officials in Verdana believe or don’t believe that the residents would benefit from daily exercise.


In answering an Assumption question, we must be careful not to choose as correct a choice that is not a necessary assumption but rather a conclusion that follows from what the passage says.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Example 34 

In order to reduce smoking by citizens, the government both banned advertising designed to induce people to start smoking and began requiring that cigarette manufacturers place on packs of cigarettes graphic photographs depicting the damaging effects of smoking.

Which of the following is an assumption upon which the above conclusion depends?

1. [ ] (A) Stores that sell cigarettes also sell sleeves that fit over cigarette packs, which can be used to cover any photographs that may appear on the packs.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) When two new actions have been taken simultaneously by a government, it can be difficult to determine which of the actions has caused the effects that follow.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) Smokers and non-smokers have similar feelings about the damaging effects that smoking has on people’s health.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) Advertising designed to induce people to start smoking does not have a significant effect on people’s decisions regarding whether to smoke cigarettes.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) Smokers’ psychology is not such that a clear indication that someone is seeking to change their behavior causes them to increase engagement in the undesired behavior.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



In the scenario presented in this argument, the reduction in smoking may or may not have resulted from the government’s having required that there be on cigarette packs graphic photographs depicting the effects of smoking. People are asked how they feel about the photographs, and because they say that they found the photographs “repellent,” it is determined that the photographs caused at least part of the reduction in smoking.


<u>Conclusion</u>: The photographs caused at least part of the reduction in smoking.


This conclusion make sense, as, if the photographs repel people, then it seems likely that the photographs cause people to smoke less.


However, what’s likely to be true is not necessarily true, and clearly there is a gap in this argument. The evidence is people’s opinions of the photographs, while the conclusion is that the photographs caused a reduction in smoking. What is being assumed in this situation?


> <b>(A) Stores that sell cigarettes also sell sleeves that fit over cigarette packs, which can be used to cover any photographs that may appear on the packs.
</b>


 This choice is tempting because it is quite relevant to the discussion. After all, if people can easily cover the photographs, then perhaps the photographs don’t have much effect.


However, this choice is incorrect because it weakens rather than supports the argument. The fact that the photographs can be covered and, therefore, may not have much effect undermines the conclusion that they caused part of the reduction in smoking.


> <b>(B) When two new actions have been taken simultaneously by a government, it can be difficult to determine which of the actions has caused the effects that follow.
</b>


 This answer is tempting because it is a conclusion supported by what the passage says about it not being clear whether the ban on advertising or the photographs were the cause of the reduction in smoking.


However, to correctly answer the question, we don’t need a conclusion supported by the passage; we need an assumption upon which the argument depends.


Concluding that the photographs caused part of the reduction does not depend on assuming that it’s difficult to determine what has caused a change when a government has taken two actions simultaneously.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) Smokers and non-smokers have similar feelings about the damaging effects that smoking has on people’s health.
</b>


 This answer could be tempting because it is a conclusion supported by what the argument says about how smokers and non-smokers feel about the photographs.


However, since we don’t have to assume that they feel the same way about the health effects of smoking to conclude that the photographs caused part of the reduction, this choice is not the correct answer.


> <b>(D) Advertising designed to induce people to start smoking does not have a significant effect on people’s decisions regarding whether to smoke cigarettes.
</b>


 The conclusion is that the photographs caused part of the reduction in smoking, not all of it. So, the rest of the reduction could have been caused by the ban on advertising designed to induce people to start smoking.


Thus, arriving at the conclusion does not require assuming that such advertising does not have a significant effect on people’s decisions regarding whether to smoke cigarettes, because, even if such advertising does cause people to smoke cigarettes, and so, the ban on such advertising did cause a reduction in smoking, the photographs could still have caused part of the observed reduction in smoking.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E) Smokers’ psychology is not such that a clear indication that someone is seeking to change their behavior causes them to increase engagement in the undesired behavior.
</b>


 To people who <em>already</em> smoke, a ban on advertising designed to induce people to <em>start</em> smoking would not be a clear indication that someone is seeking to change their behavior.


On the other hand, repellent photographs required by the government on packs of cigarettes constitute a clear indication that someone is seeking to change the behavior of smokers.


So, if, contrary to what this choice says, smokers’ psychology IS such that a clear indication that someone is seeking to change their behavior causes them to increase engagement in the undesired behavior, then the photographs could have caused smokers to smoke more rather than smoke less.


Thus, the argument depends on the assumption that smokers’ psychology is NOT such that a clear indication that someone is seeking to change their behavior causes them to increase engagement in the undesired behavior. After all, if the presence of repellent photographs could have caused smokers to increase their smoking, then the conclusion is not supported by the evidence, and the argument falls apart.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
