## Lesson Content
Often, in an Assumption question, the argument will be about a plan, and often the conclusion of the argument in such a question is essentially that the plan will work.

In many such questions, one of the incorrect answer choices will mention a plan that is an alternative to the plan the argument concludes will work, and often this alternative plan will appear to be better than the plan that the argument is promoting.

“Better plan” and “alternative plan” answer choices can be tempting, because they mention something that the author of the argument in passage may not have considered.

The truth is, however, that concluding that a plan will work does not require assuming that there is not a better or alternative plan.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Concluding that a plan will work does not require assuming that there is not a better or alternative plan. A plan does not have to be the best possible plan or only possible plan in order for it to work.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example:

> <em>To address the budgetary problems that Edentown’s Department of Health Services is experiencing, the city’s mayor has proposed imposing a tax on the sale of soda in stores. Since, not only could the tax discourage the consumption of soda, which results in costly health problems, but also any resulting tax revenue could be directed to the Department of Health Services, the mayor feels that imposing the tax would partially solve the budgetary problems of the Department of Health Services.</em>

> <em>Which of the following is an assumption upon which the mayor’s reasoning depends?</em>

> <em>(<u>Better Plan Choice</u>) The budgetary problems of the Department of Health Services could not be completely solved via negotiating better deals with suppliers.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>The people in Edentown would not choose to completely avoid being affected by the soda tax by purchasing soda from stores in nearby Paradiseville.</em>

The mayor feels that imposing the tax would partially solve the budgetary problems of the Department of Health Services.

Notice what the better plan choice does.

Of course, it’s tempting to choose this choice as the correct answer.

The correct answer, on the other hand, does express an assumption required for coming to the conclusion that the plan will work.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

An argument that concludes that a plan will work does not depend on the assumption that there is not another plan that would work even better.
{{< /alert >}}

Notice the “better plan” or “alternative plan” answer choices as you consider the following example.

##  Example 35 

CEO of Dreamy Beans Coffee Shop Chain: At our current staffing levels, people who work in our shops are often seen chatting with customers rather than working.

Which of the following is an assumption upon which the CEO’s conclusion depends?

1. [ ] (A) Dreamy Beans could not reduce total expenses significantly by reducing the amount of supplies and inventory wasted every day.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) Most people who patronize coffee shops do not cite social relationships that they have with staff members as key reasons for choosing a particular shop.
3. [ ] (C) People who have gained knowledge and experience over years of working at Dreamy Beans are not valuable assets to the company.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) Adding new styles and flavors of coffee drinks to the menu would not increase profitability more than reducing staffing would.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) Understaffing of Dreamy Beans shops would not at times result in customers’ waiting in excessively long lines.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



Breakdown of the Argument:


<u>Premise</u>: People who work in Dreamy Beans shops are often seen chatting with customers rather than working.


<u>Premise</u>: We estimate that the time staff members are seen chatting amounts to on average approximately 8 percent of their time on the job.


<u>Conclusion</u>: It is clear that we can increase profitability significantly by reducing labor costs via staffing our shops with up to 8 percent fewer people.


It seems to make sense that, if the shops’ staff members are spending 8 percent of their time on the job chatting rather than working, the shops could operate with 8 percent fewer staff members and thus operate more profitably. At the same time, it could be that the chatting somehow positively impacts profitability. So, in coming to the conclusion, the CEO is assuming that eliminating chatting will not be eliminating something that is important for the profitability of the shops.


> <b>(A) Dreamy Beans could not reduce total expenses significantly by reducing the amount of supplies and inventory wasted every day.
</b>


This is an “alternative plan” answer choice. One might be tempted to assume that, if the CEO is considering one way of reducing expenses, he is assuming that an alternative way is not available.


The truth is, however, that the CEO’s conclusion is simply that reducing staffing would improve profitability. That conclusion would be supported by the evidence regardless of whether there are other ways to reduce expenses.


Remember that the conclusion that a plan will work does not require the assumption that there is not a better or alternative plan.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B) Most people who patronize coffee shops do not cite social relationships that they have with staff members as key reasons for choosing a particular shop.
</b>


 In coming to the conclusion that it is clear that Dreamy Beans can increase profitability by reducing staffing, the CEO is assuming that the positive effects on profitability of reducing staffing would be greater than any negative effects on profitability of eliminating time for chatting with customers.


If social relationships that customers have with staff members were key reasons for choosing a particular shop, then eliminating time for chatting with customers could result in significantly fewer people patronizing Dreamy Beans’ shops.


Thus, if it is true that, contrary to what this choice says, most people who patronize coffee shops DO cite social relationships that they have with staff members as key reasons for choosing a particular shop, then the conclusion is not supported by the evidence, and the argument falls apart.


Therefore, the CEO must be assuming that most customers do NOT cite such relationships as key reasons for choosing a particular shop.


> <b>(C) People who have gained knowledge and experience over years of working at Dreamy Beans are not valuable assets to the company.
</b>


This is not an assumption upon which the argument depends.


For one thing, the CEO did not say that reducing staffing would involve laying off people who have gained knowledge and experience over years of working at Dreamy Beans. For all we know, reducing staffing would be done via reducing hiring of new employees.


Furthermore, even if reducing staffing would involve laying off people who have gained knowledge and experience over years of working at Dreamy Beans, the CEO could believe that those people ARE valuable assets of the company and still conclude that reducing staffing will result in increased profitability.


> <b>(D) Adding new styles and flavors of coffee drinks to the menu would not increase profitability more than reducing staffing would.
</b>


This is a “better plan” choice.


The conclusion that reducing staffing would result in increased profitability does not depend on the assumption that there is not a way to increase profitability even more than reducing staffing would increase it. In other words, the CEO is not saying that there is not a better way to increase profitability; he’s simply saying that reducing staffing would result in increased profitability.


Remember that the conclusion that a plan will work does not require the assumption that there is not a better or alternative plan.


> <b>(E) Understaffing of Dreamy Beans shops would not at times result in customers’ waiting in excessively long lines.
</b>


 This choice is a tempting trap, because we could fall into the trap of thinking that the CEO’s plan would result in understaffing, and if understaffing WOULD at times result in customers’ waiting in excessively long lines, then the CEO’s plan could result in a reduction, rather than an increase, in profitability.


Notice, however, that we are not given reason to believe that reducing staffing by 8 percent would result in understaffing. On the contrary, what the CEO says seems to indicate that reducing staffing by 8 percent would result in a more appropriate level of staffing.


So, the CEO does not have to be assuming anything about understaffing, because the plan does not involve understaffing.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
