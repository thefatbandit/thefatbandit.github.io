## Lesson Content
A tricky type of incorrect answer choice that can appear in an Assumption question is one that supports the conclusion but is not an assumption upon which the argument depends.

To avoid choosing such an incorrect choice, we have to keep in mind that providing support for a conclusion and being an assumption upon which an argument depends are not necessarily the same thing.

To see how this type of incorrect choice can work, consider the following example.

> <em>Anthropologist: Local storytellers, who allegedly base their stories on historical accounts passed down to them by other storytellers, say that the Anabara people were killed off by invaders. However, examination of remains found in areas where Anabara people lived indicates that the Anabara died off as a result of a smallpox epidemic. Clearly, in this case, the storytellers are not providing an accurate account of what occurred.</em>

> <em>Upon which of the following does the anthropologist’s conclusion depend?</em>

> <em>(<u>Supports the Conclusion Choice</u>) In areas where Anabara communities were located, no signs of invasions have been found.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>Invaders did not take measures meant to cause the Anabara people to become infected with the smallpox virus.</em>

Notice what’s going on here.

Let’s be clear though.

So, the supports the conclusion choice supports the conclusion without being an assumption required by the argument.

The correct answer, on the other hand is required by the argument.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

There is a difference between an answer choice that simply supports the conclusion of an argument and one that is an assumption upon which the argument depends. To correctly answer an Assumption question, we need to find the answer choice that expresses an assumption upon which the argument depends.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 36 

Political Consultant: Rebecca Lim has twice run for the office of Mayor of Springville, and neither time did she win.

Which of the following is assumption upon which the consultant’s conclusion depends?

1. [x] (A) The citizens of Springville consider the qualifications of candidates in deciding whom to vote for.
2. [ ] (B) Rebecca Lim will not be elected mayor next time she runs without more voters becoming aware of her qualifications.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) The candidate who beat Rebecca Lim in the previous two elections will not be eligible to run in the next election.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) Rebecca Lim’s qualifications have not changed significantly since the last time she ran for Mayor of Springville.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) Candidates other than Rebecca Lim who will be running for Mayor are generally perceived by voters to be not well qualified for the position of Mayor of Springville.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

##### Question Level: medium



Having found that voters were not aware of Rebecca Lim’s qualifications when she ran in and did not win previous elections, the consultant reasons that making voters aware that Rebecca Lim is the candidate most qualified to be mayor of Springville will result in her winning the election next time she runs.


<u>Conclusion:</u> You can be sure that Rebecca Lim will win next time.


In arriving at that conclusion, the consultant is assuming that making voters aware that Rebecca Lim is the candidate most qualified to be mayor is sufficient for assuring that she will win. But is it really sufficient? What has to be true in order for that plan to work?


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A) The citizens of Springville consider the qualifications of candidates in deciding whom to vote for.
</b>


 In concluding that Rebecca Lim will win the election once voters are aware that she is the candidate most qualified to be mayor of Springville, the consultant must be assuming that voters will be swayed to vote for Rebecca Lim by the fact that she is most qualified.


In other words, the consultant’s reasoning depends on the assumption that the citizens of Springville consider the qualifications of candidates in deciding whom to vote for. Otherwise, making voters aware that Rebecca Lim is the candidate most qualified to be mayor of Springville would not make any difference.


> <b>(B) Rebecca Lim will not be elected mayor next time she runs without more voters becoming aware of her qualifications.
</b>


This choice is a tempting trap, since the consultant’s choice of campaign strategy could be perceived as indicating that the consultant assumes that Rebecca Lim will not win unless more voters become aware of her qualifications.


Notice, however, that while it may be the case that the consultant believes that Rebecca Lim won’t win unless more voters become aware of her qualifications, concluding that she will win if voters are aware of her qualifications does not require assuming that she will not win if they are not aware of her qualifications.


After all, even if she WILL be elected mayor if no more voters become aware of her qualifications, she could win if voters become aware that she is the candidate most qualified to be mayor.


> <b>(C) The candidate who beat Rebecca Lim in the previous two elections will not be eligible to run in the next election.
</b>


This choice may provide additional support for the conclusion that Rebecca Lim will win the election, but is it an assumption upon which the argument depends?


No.


The plan is to make voters aware that Rebecca Lim is the most qualified candidate. Implementing that plan could result in her winning the next election regardless of whether the candidate who won the last two times will be running the next time.


> <b>(D) Rebecca Lim’s qualifications have not changed significantly since the last time she ran for Mayor of Springville.
</b>


Notice that changes in Rebecca Lim’s qualifications have not necessarily made it less likely that she will win. Her qualifications could have changed in a way such that she has become even more likely to win.


So, the conclusion that she is sure to win does not depend on the assumption that her qualifications have not changed significantly since the last time she ran.


> <b>(E) Candidates other than Rebecca Lim who will be running for Mayor are generally perceived by voters to be not well qualified for the position of Mayor of Springville.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, as the fact that the other candidates running are perceived by voters as being not well qualified provides additional support for the conclusion that Rebecca Lim will win the election. However, we are not looking for additional support for the conclusion. We are looking for a statement that has to be true in order for the argument to work.


The consultant’s argument is not based on the premise that Rebecca Lim is merely well qualified. It’s based on the premise that she is the <em>most</em> qualified. Since only one candidate can be the most qualified, being sure that she will win does not require assuming that none of the other candidates are perceived as being well qualified.


So, the making voters aware that Rebecca Lim is the most qualified could result in her winning the next election even if, contrary to what this choice says, other candidates are NOT generally perceived by voters to be not well qualified.


So, this choice is not an assumption upon which the consultant’s argument depends.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)
