## Lesson Content
As we discussed in the previous chapter, an assumption is an unstated piece of information that must be true in order for the logic of an argument to work.

To see more clearly how assumptions function in arguments, let’s consider a simple argument:

> <em>Dogs like to bark.</em>

> <em>Therefore, Fido must like to bark.</em>

Notice that we have one premise:

> Dogs like to bark.

Given that premise, the author concludes the following:

> Fido must like to bark.

In making this argument, the author has made an important assumption.

The author has assumed that Fido is a dog.

The author has used the premise that dogs like to bark to support the conclusion that Fido likes to bark, but the only way in which that premise supports the conclusion is by relying on the assumption that Fido is a dog.

Sure, dogs like to bark.

We see that the assumption that Fido is a dog is an unstated piece of information that must be true in order for the argument to work.

Let’s add that assumption to the argument:

> <u>Premise</u>: Dogs like to bark.

> <u>Assumption</u>: Fido is a dog.

> <u>Conclusion</u>: Fido must like to bark.

OK, now the argument is more complete.

If this simple argument were turned into a simple Critical Reasoning question, it might look like the following:

> <em>Dogs like to bark. Therefore, Fido must like to bark.</em>

> <em>Which of the following is an assumption upon which the author relies in arriving at the conclusion presented?</em>

> <em>(A) Fido is a dog.</em>

Pretty simple right? This question asks us to find an unstated piece of information upon which the argument relies.

Let’s look at a slightly harder example.

> <em>Today is not a holiday.</em>

> <em>Therefore, the kids will be in school.</em>

The author has one premise:

> Today is not a holiday.

From that premise, he concludes the following:

> The kids will be in school.

Does the author make any assumptions? He does, right? In making his argument, he assumes that there could not be another reason why the kids would not be in school.

> <u>Premise</u>: Today is not a holiday.

> <u>Assumption</u>: Holidays are the only days when kids are not in school.

> <u>Conclusion</u>: Therefore, the kids will be in school.

Notice that, in order for this argument to work, it must be true that holidays are the only days when kids are not in school.

> <em>Today is not a holiday. Therefore, the kids will be in school.</em>

> <em>Which of the following is an assumption upon which the author relies in arriving at the conclusion presented?</em>

> <em>(A) Holidays are the only days when kids are not in school.</em>

Not too bad, right? The question is simply asking us what the author assumes in making the argument.

Let's now consider a somewhat more complex argument:

> <em>All people in houses in which communicable diseases are present should be quarantined. Two people in the yellow house on Maple Street came down with disease x yesterday. Therefore, all people in the yellow house on Maple Street should be quarantined.</em>

We can break this argument down as follows:

> <u>Premise 1</u>: All people in houses in which communicable diseases are present should be quarantined.

> <u>Premise 2</u>: Two people in the yellow house on Maple Street came down with disease x yesterday.

> <u>Conclusion</u>: All People in the yellow house on Maple Street should be quarantined.

As we will in answering any Assumption question, we must ask, “What is the missing information? What piece of information is unstated by the author yet must be true in order for the evidence presented to effectively support the conclusion?”

Can you tell what necessary piece of information is missing from this argument?

The missing piece of information is that disease x is a communicable disease.

Although the author does not explicitly state this information in making the argument, we can see that it is completely necessary to properly connect the premises to the conclusion, because without that assumption, there is a gap between the premises and the conclusion.

The only way this argument works is by including the assumption that disease x is in fact a communicable disease.

We can rewrite the structure of the argument:

> <u>Premise 1</u>: All people in houses in which communicable diseases are present should be quarantined.

> <u>Premise 2</u>: Two people in the yellow house on Maple Street came down with disease x yesterday.

> <u>Assumption</u>: Disease x is a communicable disease.

> <u>Conclusion</u>: All people in the yellow house on Maple Street should be quarantined.

Notice that this assumption is unstated information that MUST BE TRUE in order for the evidence presented to effectively support the conclusion.

Now, as you read that little argument about disease x, you may have on your own made the assumption that disease x is a communicable disease.

After all, can you imagine how hard it would be to have a conversation (or read an argument) if you had to stop every 10 seconds and ask for clarification on every assumption that was made? Having to do so would make communication almost impossible.

However, we have to become more conscious of assumptions when answering Critical Reasoning questions.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In answering Critical Reasoning questions, we must be conscious of the assumptions upon which arguments depend, read actively, and seek to be aware of gaps in arguments presented in the questions.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 1 

The Noodle House, which serves only noodles and has won the award for the best noodles in our state five years in a row, is opening a store in the Town of Redlands next month.

Which of the following is an assumption upon which the support for the above conclusion depends?

1. [ ] (A) The mayor of Redlands does not eat any meals at home.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) The Town of Redlands has a small number of noodle restaurants.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) Only award-winning restaurants open in the Town of Redlands.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) It costs less to dine at the Noodle House than at any other restaurant.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) The mayor of Redlands is not allergic to noodles.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



<u>Background</u>: The Noodle House, which serves only noodles and has won the award for the best noodles in our state five years in a row, is opening a store in the Town of Redlands next month.


<u>Premise</u>: The mayor of Redlands loves eating award-winning food.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Therefore, the mayor of Redlands will love eating at the Noodle House.


Notice that there is a large gap in the argument. The author provides us with only one premise, that the mayor of Redlands loves eating award-winning food. From that one premise, she concludes that the mayor of Redlands will love eating at the Noodle House. On what grounds does the author feel justified in reaching her conclusion? What must be true in order for her argument to be valid?


> <b>(A) The mayor of Redlands does not eat any meals at home.
</b>


 Does it have to be the case that the mayor does not eat ANY meals at home in order for the mayor to love eating at the Noodle House? Of course not. Even if the mayor eats some meals at home, the mayor could still love eating at the Noodle House. So, what this choice says is not an assumption necessary in order for the argument to work.


> <b>(B) The Town of Redlands has a small number of noodle restaurants.
</b>


 Perhaps the Town of Redlands has a small number of noodle restaurants. Perhaps the Town of Redlands has a ton of noodle restaurants. Either way, the argument’s conclusion that the mayor will love eating at the Noodle House follows from the fact that the mayor loves eating award-winning food.


Thus, the argument does not depend on any assumptions about how many noodle restaurants are in Redlands.


> <b>(C) Only award-winning restaurants open in the Town of Redlands.
</b>


 It could be that only award-winning restaurants open in the Town of Redlands. Alternatively, it could be that a wide range of restaurants open in the Town of Redlands. Either way, the fact that the mayor loves eating award-winning food remains true, and the argument’s conclusion could still follow from that premise.


> <b>(D) It costs less to dine at the Noodle House than at any other restaurant.
</b>


 Given that the Noodle House has won awards and that the mayor loves eating award-winning food, would it make sense to conclude that the mayor will love eating at the Noodle House even if the Noodle House is not the least expensive restaurant in the entire universe? Sure it would.


The argument is not that the mayor loves eating inexpensive food. So, the argument could certainly work even if it costs a little more to dine at the Noodle House than it costs to dine at the cheapest restaurant that exists.


What this choice says does not have to be assumed in order for the argument to work.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E) The mayor of Redlands is not allergic to noodles.
</b>


 What this answer choice says must be true for the argument to work.


After all, if the mayor of Redlands WERE allergic to noodles, then the argument would fall apart because the conclusion that the mayor will love eating at the Noodle House would no longer follow from the premise.


Regardless of how many awards the Noodle House has won, if the mayor is allergic to noodles, he certainly will not be heading over to the Noodle House for a bowl of noodles. Therefore, the author MUST assume that the mayor is NOT allergic to noodles.


Notice that the argument cannot possibly work unless what this answer choice says is true.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Example 2 

This past summer, the beach city of Hampton Falls began charging a ten dollar per person admission fee at one of its two public beaches, with the result that the city earned $50,000 in revenue during the summer beach season.

Which of the following must be true in order for the above conclusion to follow from the evidence provided?

1. [ ] (A) The people who worked as lifeguards at the Hampton Falls beaches this past summer will be willing to do so again during the next summer beach season.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) During the next summer beach season, Hampton Falls will not be more crowded with beachgoers than it usually is.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) The next summer beach season in Hampton Falls will be two weeks shorter than this past summer’s beach season.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) The beaches in Hampton Falls will not have to be closed for all of next summer because of high bacteria counts in the ocean water along the beaches.
5. [ ] (E) Some people who went to a beach in Hampton Falls last summer will not go to a beach in Hampton Falls during the next summer beach season.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



<u>Background</u>: This past summer, the beach city of Hampton Falls began charging a ten dollar per person admission fee at one of its two public beaches, with the result that the city earned $50,000 in revenue during the summer beach season.


<u>Premise</u>: The city council has recently decided to begin charging the same admission fee at its other public beach.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Therefore, during the next summer beach season, Hampton Falls will be able to earn at least $100,000 by simultaneously charging admission to both of its public beaches.


> <b>(A) The people who worked as lifeguards at the Hampton Falls beaches this past summer will be willing to do so again during the next summer beach season.
</b>


 Unless the people going to the beaches are going almost exclusively to see the lifeguards, the author need not assume that the people who worked as lifeguards at the Hampton Falls beaches this past summer will be willing to do so again during the next summer beach season.


Even if none of the same lifeguards come back, the fact that the city council has recently decided to begin charging the same admission fee at the other public beach may support the conclusion that, during the next summer beach season, Hampton Falls will be able to earn at least $100,000 by charging admission to both of its public beaches.


> <b>(B) During the next summer beach season, Hampton Falls will not be more crowded with beachgoers than it usually is.
</b>


Concluding that Hampton Falls will be able to earn at least $100,000 during the next summer beach season by charging admission to both beaches does not require assuming that Hampton Falls will not be more crowded with beachgoers than it usually is.


After all, if Hampton Falls IS more crowded with beachgoers than it usually is, then there should be plenty of people to pay admission to the two beaches, in which case it seems likely that Hampton Falls will be able to earn at least $100,000 by charging admission to both beaches.


So, what this choice says does not have to be true in order for the argument to work.


> <b>(C) The next summer beach season in Hampton Falls will be two weeks shorter than this past summer’s beach season.
</b>


 What this answer choices says goes in a direction opposite to the direction of the argument.


The conclusion is that Hampton Falls will be able to earn at least $100,000 by simultaneously charging admission to both of its public beaches. If next summer’s beach season in Hampton Falls will be two weeks shorter than this past summer’s beach season, then that is less time for Hampton Falls to earn $100,000. In that case, we have a bit less conviction that the plan will work.


Of course, we are not seeking an answer choice that weakens the argument. Rather, we’re seeking an assumption upon which the argument relies.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) The beaches in Hampton Falls will not have to be closed for all of next summer because of high bacteria counts in the ocean water along the beaches.
</b>


 What this answer choice says MUST be true for the argument to be valid. After all, any time an author proposes a plan, he assumes that the plan will work and that there are not variables that will enter into the equation that will spoil the plan. In the TTP Course, we refer to such variables as “malicious variables.” When an author presents a plan, he assumes that there are no malicious variables that will ruin the plan.


However, imagine if the beaches in Hampton Falls WILL have to be closed for all of next summer because of high bacteria counts in the ocean water along the beaches. Wouldn’t that fact ruin the plan, rendering the author’s conclusion invalid? Definitely! After all, if the beaches will be closed for all of next summer, Hampton Falls certainly will NOT be able to earn at least $100,000 by charging admission to both of its public beaches.


Therefore, in order for this argument to work, the author MUST assume that the beaches in Hampton Falls WILL NOT have to be closed for all of next summer because of high bacteria counts in the ocean water along the beaches.


Notice that what this answer choice provides is an unwritten premise that MUST be true in order for the argument to work. In other words, this answer choice states an assumption upon which the argument relies.


> <b>(E) Some people who went to a beach in Hampton Falls last summer will not go to a beach in Hampton Falls during the next summer beach season.
</b>


 What this answer choice states is a reasonable prediction.


After all, it is unlikely that every person who went to a beach in Hampton Falls last summer will go to a beach in Hampton Falls next summer.


So, it would make sense to predict that, as this choice says, some people who went to a beach in Hampton Falls last summer will not go to a beach in Hampton Falls during the next summer beach season.


Of course, we are not seeking a choice that states a reasonable prediction. We’re seeking an assumption upon which the argument relies, and going from the premise that Hampton Falls is going to charge admission to both of its public beaches to the conclusion that Hampton Falls will be able to earn at least $100,000 certainly does not require assuming that some people who went to the beaches this past summer won't go to the beaches next summer.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Example 3 

MowGreen, a company that uses sustainable methods to maintain lawns, has been expanding quickly, and its services are now available in many areas of the country.

Which of the following is an assumption logically necessary for coming to the conclusion of the above argument?

1. [x] (A) MowGreen’s management team considers profitability in determining whether to expand into an area.
2. [ ] (B) People in the area around Drawnville are not interested in using sustainable methods for maintaining their lawns.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) There remain in the country areas in which MowGreen is not currently operating into which it could profitably expand its operations.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) MowGreen operates profitably in all of the areas in which it currently operates.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) Sustainable methods for maintaining lawns are generally more expensive to use than are less environmentally friendly methods.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



Breakdown of the Argument:


<u>Background</u>: MowGreen has been expanding, and its services are available in many areas.


<u>Premise</u>: MowGreen would not be able to operate profitably in the Drawnville area.


<u>Conclusion</u>: One can be sure that MowGreen will not expand into the Drawnville area any time soon.


There is a gap in the argument. The premise says that MowGreen would not be able to <em>operate profitably</em> in the Drawnville area. The conclusion says that MowGreen will not <em>expand </em>into the area around Drawnville. What’s missing here? What must be true in order for the conclusion to be properly drawn from the premises? The correct answer to the question will fill in the gap between <em>would not be able to operate profitably</em> in the area and <em>will not expand … into the area</em>.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A) MowGreen’s management team considers profitability in determining whether to expand into an area.
</b>


 While it may seem obvious that, since MowGreen would not be able to operate profitably in the Drawnville area, MowGreen will not expand into that area any time soon, the truth is that going from the premise to the conclusion that MowGreen requires assuming that MowGreen’s management team considers profitability in determining whether to expand into an area.


After all, if MowGreen’s management team does NOT consider profitability in determining whether to expand into an area, then the fact that MowGreen would not be able to operate profitably in the Drawnville area does not support the conclusion, and this argument falls apart.


So, this choice states an assumption necessary for coming to the conclusion of the argument.


> <b>(B) People in the area around Drawnville are not interested in using sustainable methods for maintaining their lawns.
</b>


 While, if people in the Drawnville area were not interested in using sustainable lawn care methods, it would be unlikely that MowGreen would expand into that area, concluding that MowGreen will not expand into that area does not require assuming that people in that area are not interested in using sustainable lawn care methods.


After all, the fact that MowGreen would not be able to operate profitably in the Drawnville area could still support the conclusion even if, contrary to what this choice says, people in the area would be interested in using sustainable lawn care methods.


> <b>(C) There remain in the country areas in which MowGreen is not currently operating into which it could profitably expand its operations.
</b>


Concluding that MowGreen will not expand into an area in which it cannot operate profitably does not require assuming that there are areas into which it could profitably expand. MowGreen does not have to expand at all.


> <b>(D) MowGreen operates profitably in all of the areas in which it currently operates.
</b>


This choice may seem to be similar to choice (A), the correct answer, but there is a key difference.


Notice that expanding and operating are not the same thing.


Concluding that MowGreen will not expand into the area around Drawnville requires assuming that MowGreen does not <em>expand</em> into areas in which it cannot operate profitably.


However, it does not require assuming that MowGreen currently <em>operates</em> profitably in every area in which it already operates.


It could be that MowGreen expands into an area only if it can operate profitably in that area at the time when it expands into that area, yet for some reason, MowGreen is not currently operating profitably in all areas in which it operates. Perhaps things changed in certain areas in which MowGreen operates after it expanded into those areas, and as a result it no longer operates profitably there.


> <b>(E) Sustainable methods for maintaining lawns are generally more expensive to use than are less environmentally friendly methods.
</b>


 This choice is tempting because what it says both sounds plausible and may explain why MowGreen would not be able to operate profitably in Drawnville.


However, we are not looking for a statement that is merely plausible or for an explanation, and it is not necessary to assume that sustainable methods for maintaining lawns are more expensive to use than conventional methods in order to arrive the conclusion that MowGreen will not expand into an area in which it is not able to operate profitably.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)
