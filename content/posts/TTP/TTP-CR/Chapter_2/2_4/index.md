## Lesson Content
To answer Assumption questions correctly, follow this 6-step strategy.

<u>Step 1 </u><u>–</u><u> Carefully read and understand the passage.</u>

<strong>The foundation of arriving at the correct answer to an Assumption question is understanding the passage.</strong>

The importance of understanding the passage cannot be overemphasized.

So, your first job in answering an Assumption question is to carefully read the passage, developing understanding of what the passage says and noting key details of the passage.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The foundation for arriving at the correct answer to an Assumption question is understanding the passage.
{{< /alert >}}

<u>Step 2 </u><u>–</u><u> Read the question stem and identify what it is asking you to do.</u>

Next, read the question stem.

If the question is asking you to identify an assumption or idea upon which the conclusion or argument depends, it’s an Identify the Assumption question, and in order to get it correct, you have to choose the answer choice that expresses an assumption integral to the argument.

(Note: Some people prefer to read Critical Reasoning question stems before they read the passages.

<u>Step 3 – Go back to the passage and determine exactly what the conclusion of the argument is.</u>

At this point, you understand the passage and know that you have to find an assumption integral to the argument.

Of course, the assumption will be information that is necessary in order for the argument’s conclusion to be effectively supported by its premises.

So, the next step is to go back to the passage and identify exactly what the conclusion is.

<strong>Identifying the exact conclusion of the argument is possibly the single most crucial step you will take in answering an Assumption question.</strong>

If you have only a vague idea of what the conclusion is, you can easily fall for trap choices that support the wrong conclusion.

So, identify exactly what the conclusion is.

<u>Step 4 – Identify how the conclusion is supported.</u>

The correct answer to an Assumption question will be information that is necessary in order for the argument’s conclusion to be effectively supported by its premises.

So, of course, the next key step in answering an Assumption question is to identify the premises that support the conclusion and determine how they support it.

<u>Step 5 </u><u>–</u><u> Remove choices that are rather obviously not assumptions integral to the argument.</u>

Usually, in an Assumption question, there are three answer choices that are relatively clearly not assumptions integral to the argument.

Eliminating these choices is a good next step.

Notice something here.

On your first pass through the answer choices, keep choices that you are not sure about, even if doing so means that you are left with three or more choices with which to work.

Often people eliminate the correct answer first! Why? Because, often the correct answer says something that is not obviously connected to the argument.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Eliminating answer choices that are clearly not correct is not the same as eliminating choices that you are not sure about. On your first pass through the answer choices in an Assumption question, keep choices that you are not sure about.
{{< /alert >}}

<u>Step 6 </u><u>–</u><u> Choose the answer choice that is most clearly an assumption integral to the argument.</u>

After eliminating answer choices that are clearly not assumptions integral to the argument, you are at the final stage of finding the correct answer to an Assumption question.

If you find yourself stuck between two answer choices, ask yourself which really connects the premises to the conclusion? Remember, an assumption is a piece of missing information that MUST BE TRUE in order for the logic of the argument to work.

Also, if you are circling through answer choices with no clear reason to choose one over others, it may be that there is something in the passage that you need to see in order to choose between the choices you have left.

Most GMAT Critical Reasoning questions have one clearly correct answer that answers the question in a tightly logical way.

So, find that one choice that must be true in order for the argument’s logic to work.

<u>Summary of the Strategy for Identify the Assumption Questions</u>

To summarize, here is the 6-step strategy that you can use to answer Assumption questions correctly:

##  Example 5 

Until recently, quinoa was farmed via traditional methods that are relatively benign in terms of their effects on the environment.

Which of the following is an assumption upon which the logic of the above argument relies?

1. [ ] (A) It would be possible for quinoa farmers to meet current demand by using traditional farming methods on farms that utilize large land areas.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) There are other foods that contain the nutrients that quinoa contains in the exact same concentrations.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) The commercial farming methods used for farming quinoa are more detrimental to the environment than are the methods used for farming other food crops.
4. [ ] (D) Quinoa farmers were better off before increasing demand for quinoa catalyzed the shift to farming quinoa via modern commercial farming methods.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) Quinoa would not be as popular as it is if more people were aware of the environmental effects of quinoa farming.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



<u>Step 1 </u><u>–</u><u> Carefully read and understand the passage.</u>


We can see that the passage presents an argument and that the reasoning of the argument is the following.


Since, as quinoa has become increasingly popular, the farmers who produce it have been shifting to using methods that have significant negative effects on the environment, people should curtail their quinoa consumption and eat other foods instead.


<u>Step 2 </u><u>–</u><u> Read the question stem and identify what it is asking you to do.</u>


The question asks us to find an assumption on which the logic of the argument relies.


<u>Step 3 – Go back to the passage and determine exactly what the conclusion of the argument is.</u>


We can see that the statements in the passage lead up to and support the last statement in the passage:


<u>Conclusion</u>: People concerned about the environment should curtail their quinoa consumption and eat other foods instead.


So, that last statement is the conclusion of the argument.


Notice that we are using the exact conclusion in its entirety, rather than a paraphrase or summary of the conclusion. Even leaving out a few words, such as “and eat other foods instead,” could result in our choosing an incorrect choice.


<u>Step 4 – Identify how the conclusion is supported.</u>


Going through the passage, we see that the conclusion is supported by the following premise:


<u>Premise</u>: As quinoa, which contains key nutrients in high concentrations, has become increasingly popular, in order to produce quinoa in greater quantities, quinoa farmers have been shifting to using commercial farming methods that have significant negative effects on the environment.


So, the author has concluded that people concerned about the environment should curtail their quinoa consumption and eat other foods instead because the methods quinoa farmers are using have significant negative effects on the environment.


<u>Step 5 – Remove choices that are rather obviously not assumptions integral to the argument.</u>


Let’s examine each answer choice, remembering that we are looking to separate statements that are rather clearly not assumptions on which the argument relies from statements that could potentially be our correct answer.


<strong>(A) It would be possible for quinoa farmers to meet current demand by using traditional farming methods on farms that utilize large land areas.</strong>


The argument uses the premise that quinoa farmers have been using methods that have negative effects on the environment to support the conclusion that people should curtail their quinoa consumption.


Going from that premise to that conclusion does not require assuming that the farmers could use traditional methods instead of the methods that have negative effects.


Therefore, we can confidently eliminate choice (A).


<strong>(B) There are other foods that contain the nutrients that quinoa contains in the exact same concentrations.</strong>


The author of the argument concludes that people should curtail their consumption of quinoa, which “contains key nutrients in high concentrations,” and eat other foods instead.


Is it possible that the conclusion depends on what this choice says, that other foods contain the nutrients <strong>that quinoa contains in the exact same concentrations</strong>?


Perhaps.


After all, people eat food in order to take in nutrients. So, maybe they should not switch from eating quinoa to eating other foods unless what this choice says is true.


Let’s keep choice (B) for now.


<strong>(C) The commercial farming methods used for farming quinoa are more detrimental to the environment than are the methods used for farming other food crops.</strong>


This choice is interesting.


If what this choice said is not true and, thus, the methods used to farm quinoa are NOT more detrimental to the environment than are methods used to farm other foods, then, presumably, consuming quinoa is not more detrimental to the environment than consuming other foods.


In that case, even though the premise that quinoa farmers are using methods that have negative effects on the environment is true, that premise does not support the argument’s conclusion, because people’s curtailing quinoa consumption and consuming other foods instead wouldn’t make any difference in terms of negative effects on the environment.


So, it appears that what this choice says has to be true in order for the argument to work.


Let’s keep choice (C), which seems to be the best one so far.


<strong>(D) Quinoa farmers were better off before increasing demand for quinoa catalyzed the shift to farming quinoa via modern commercial farming methods.</strong>


The conclusion is that people concerned about the environment should curtail their quinoa consumption and eat other foods instead.


That conclusion is supported by the fact that, in order to produce quinoa in greater quantities, quinoa farmers have been shifting to using farming methods that have significant negative effects on the environment.


Does it have to be the case that farmers were better off before they started using the new methods in order for that evidence to support the conclusion? No, even if the farmers were NOT better off before, the premise could support the conclusion. After all, even if the farmers are just as well off as they were before they started using the new methods, the new methods have negative effects on the environment.


Choice (D) is another one that we can confidently eliminate.


<strong>(E) Quinoa would not be as popular as it is if more people were aware of the environmental effects of quinoa farming.</strong>


What this choice says is clearly connected to the argument, but does it fill a gap in the argument?


The conclusion is that people concerned about the environment should curtail their quinoa consumption and eat other foods instead.


Quinoa doesn’t have to become less popular in order for curtailing quinoa consumption to make sense.


In fact, this choice is a conclusion supported by the statements in the argument rather than an assumption that the logic of the argument depends on.


So, let’s eliminate choice (E).


<u>Step 6 – Choose the answer choice that is most clearly an assumption integral to the argument.</u>


We have eliminated choices (A), (D), and (E). We are left with choices (B) and (C).


Choice (B) does seem to be important in this situation, since people switching from eating one food to eating another might be rightly concerned about making sure that they are not missing out on any nutrients by doing so.


However, do the concentrations of nutrients in foods that people switch to have to be exactly the same as concentrations of nutrients in quinoa in order to conclude that people should switch? Not really. People eat many different foods, and they don’t all contain the exact same nutrients in the exact same concentrations.


Choice (C), on the other hand, fills a key gap in the argument.


The evidence provided is that, <strong>in order to produce quinoa in greater quantities, quinoa farmers have been shifting to using commercial farming methods that have significant negative effects on the environment</strong>.


The conclusion is that <strong>people concerned about the environment should curtail their quinoa consumption and eat other foods instead</strong>.


In order to go from that evidence to that conclusion, one has to assume what choice (C) says, that <strong>the commercial farming methods used for farming quinoa </strong><em><strong>are more detrimental to the environment</strong></em><strong> than are the methods used for farming other food crops</strong>.


After all, if the commercial methods used for farming quinoa are NOT more detrimental than the methods used for farming other food crops, then curtailing quinoa consumption and eating other foods instead won't help.


So, choice (C) is the correct answer to this question.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

##  Example 6 

Generally, jazz musicians are expected to be able to read sheet music in order to learn new songs.

The conclusion above is based on which one of the following assumptions?

1. [ ] (A) “Playing by ear” is an appropriate method of learning to play songs in any genre.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) Most expert jazz musicians who “play by ear” are not good at reading sheet music.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) The songs that amateur jazz musicians play can be learned as easily via reading sheet music as via “playing by ear.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) Amateur jazz musicians would rather “play by ear” than read sheet music.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) Expert jazz musicians learn songs more effectively than do amateur jazz musicians.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



The argument states that, while jazz musicians are expected to be able to learn new songs through reading sheet music, a recent query into methods has divided jazz musicians into two groups: amateurs, who rely on reading sheet music, and experts, who opt to “play by ear”.


Because “playing by ear” is the method used by the experts, the argument concludes that it must be the “more effective” method.


Of course, what experts do and what is most effective are necessarily not the same thing. So, in this argument, there is a gap between ‘expert jazz musicians learn new songs by “playing by ear”’ and ‘“playing by ear” is more effective.’


> <b>(A) “Playing by ear” is an appropriate method of learning to play songs in any genre.
</b>


 The author does not have to assume that “playing by ear” is an appropriate method of learning to play songs in ANY genre in order to go from the evidence, that expert jazz musicians play by ear, to the conclusion, that, for learning jazz songs specifically, “playing by ear” is more effective than reading sheet music.


> <b>(B) Most expert jazz musicians who “play by ear” are not good at reading sheet music.
</b>


 If true, what this choice says might partly explain why expert jazz musicians “play by ear,” but we are not looking for an explanation.


Meanwhile, since this choice provides an alternate reason for expert jazz musicians’ playing by ear – they aren’t good at reading sheet music – it actually weakens the support for the conclusion, by providing reason to believe that expert jazz musicians choose to “play by ear” for a reason other than the relative effectiveness of “playing by ear.”


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) The songs that amateur jazz musicians play can be learned as easily via reading sheet music as via “playing by ear. ”
</b>


 This choice is incorrect for two reasons.


One is that it discusses a subset of jazz music, the songs played by amateur musicians. Therefore, it does not address the need for an assumption the fills a gap in an argument about learning jazz music in general.


The second reason is that, by suggesting that reading sheet music works just as well as “playing by ear” for learning certain songs, this choice to a degree contradicts the conclusion.


> <b>(D) Amateur jazz musicians would rather “play by ear” than read sheet music.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, because what it says lines up fairly well with what the passage says and with the conclusion of the argument. One might think that, in order to argue that “playing by ear” is more effective than reading sheet music, one would have to assume that amateur jazz musicians would rather “play by ear” than read sheet music.


However, the truth is that going from the premises that amateurs use sheet music and that experts tend to “play by ear” to the conclusion that “playing by ear” is the more effective method does not require assuming anything about the preferences of amateurs, which could be based on factors other than effectiveness.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E) Expert jazz musicians learn songs more effectively than do amateur jazz musicians.
</b>


 Think about the missing information that must be true.


We’re told that expert jazz musicians learn to play new songs by “playing by ear.” Then, we’re presented with the conclusion that this method of learning is more effective than what the others do (read sheet music). To make the logic of this argument work, we must assume that expert jazz musicians do, in fact, learn new songs more effectively than do amateur jazz musicians.


After all, if this assumption were not true, the evidence would not support the conclusion, and the argument would fall apart.


Certainly, more evidence may be necessary to <em>prove</em> this conclusion; what we have done here is provide one assumption that is necessary for the evidence provided to <em>support</em> the conclusion.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
