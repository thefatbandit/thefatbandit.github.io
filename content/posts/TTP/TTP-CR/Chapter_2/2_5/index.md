## Lesson Content
In Critical Reasoning, we use the term “author” to refer to whoever is setting forth an argument in a question.

The “authors” of these arguments vary from question to question, but the one commonality that these authors all share is that they assume that they are correct, whatever their arguments may be.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In making the arguments found in Critical Reasoning questions, the authors of these arguments assume that they are correct; they assume that their arguments are sound and logical.
{{< /alert >}}

This point is not a small one, and remembering it will provide us an edge in finding the assumptions made by authors.

Because authors assume that their arguments are sound, they also assume that they have considered every variable that could affect their arguments, and that, after considering those variables, they’ve reached conclusions that are supported by their evidence.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Authors assume that they have considered every variable that could affect their arguments, and that, after considering those variables, they’ve reached conclusions that are supported by their evidence.
{{< /alert >}}

Another way of thinking about this is that authors assume that there will not be any unforeseen variables that will enter into the equation and render their arguments unsound.

In the TTP Course, we have a name for unforeseen variables that render arguments unsound.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A malicious variable is a variable that an author has failed to consider in the construction of an argument and that enters into the equation and spoils that argument, rendering the argument unsound.
{{< /alert >}}

Likewise, when an author presents a plan, the author assumes that there are no malicious variables that will cause the plan not to work.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When an author presents a plan, the author assumes that there are no malicious variables that will cause the plan not to work.
{{< /alert >}}

Malicious variables come in all shapes and sizes, but they have in common that they were not foreseen by the author, and that they ruin the author’s argument.

For example, consider the following mini-example:

> <em>Most residents of Spring Hills lack health insurance. Blue Health, a nationwide insurance company, just announced that it will offer health insurance for a deeply discounted price to all Spring Hills residents who do not have any preexisting conditions. Therefore, by next month, most Spring Hills residents will have health insurance.</em>

Let’s list some malicious variables that would ruin the argument, if true.

<u>Malicious Variable</u>: It will take many months for most residents of Spring Hills to learn about the health insurance being offered by Blue Health.

> <u>Assumption</u>: It will NOT take many months for most residents of Spring Hills to learn about the health insurance being offered by Blue Health.

<u>Malicious Variable</u>: Most residents of Spring Hills suffer from at least one medical condition that Blue Health categorizes as a preexisting condition.

> <u>Assumption</u>: Most residents of Spring Hills DO NOT suffer from at least one medical condition that Blue Health categorizes as a preexisting condition.

<u>Malicious Variable</u>: Most residents of Spring Hills would prefer to use the money that they would otherwise spend on health insurance on wellness-promoting services, such as personal trainers, meditation coaches, and nutritionists.

> <u>Assumption</u>: Most residents of Spring Hills would NOT prefer to use the money that they would otherwise spend on health insurance on wellness-promoting services, such as personal trainers, meditation coaches, and nutritionists.

<u>Malicious Variable</u>: Even at the deeply discounted price offered by Blue Health, the monthly cost of the health insurance it offers far exceeds what most residents of Spring Hills are able to pay.

> <u>Assumption</u>: The deeply discounted price for the health insurance offered by Blue Health DOES NOT exceed what most residents of Spring Hills are able to pay.

Notice that each of those malicious variables, if true, would ruin the argument.

So, how can we use this information on test day? <strong>We can use it to find the correct answers to Assumption questions by looking for a choice that states the author’s assumption that a certain malicious variable is not true</strong>.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The correct answer to an Assumption question will always say that some malicious variable that could wreck the argument or plan is not the case.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s transform that mini-example into a full example, so you can put these ideas into action.

For best results, take your time answering this question and carefully analyze each choice to learn to clearly see which one presents the author's assumption that a malicious variable is not the case.

##  Example 7 

Most residents of Spring Hills lack health insurance.

The conclusion above is based on which one of the following assumptions?

1. [ ] (A) No other health insurance companies will follow Blue Health’s lead and begin offering similar health insurance at a discounted price to residents of Spring Hills.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) It will take many months for most residents of Spring Hills to learn about the health insurance being offered by Blue Health.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) Being covered by health insurance is an important part of any wealth management strategy.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) Blue Health is able to offer health insurance for a deeply discounted price to Spring Hills residents because the cost of healthcare in Spring Hills is unusually low.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) Most residents of Spring Hills do not suffer from at least one medical condition that Blue Health categorizes as a preexisting condition.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Let’s break down the argument.


<u>Background</u>: Most residents of Spring Hills lack health insurance.


<u>Premise</u>: Blue Health, a nationwide insurance company, just announced that it will offer health insurance for a deeply discounted price to all Spring Hills residents who do not have any preexisting conditions.


<u>Conclusion</u>: By next month, most Spring Hills residents will have health insurance.


The author assumes that his argument is sound. He also assumes that he has carefully and comprehensively analyzed every variable that could affect his argument, and that, after analyzing those variables, he has reached a conclusion supported by the evidence presented. He assumes that there are not any malicious variables that will enter into the equation and render his argument invalid.


We’ll be seeking an answer choice that says something that must be true for the conclusion to properly follow from the premise.


> <b>(A) No other health insurance companies will follow Blue Health’s lead and begin offering similar health insurance at a discounted price to residents of Spring Hills.
</b>


 Regardless of whether other health insurance companies will follow Blue Health’s lead and begin offering similar health insurance at a discounted price to residents of Spring Hills, the author’s argument could be sound.


After all, the argument’s conclusion is based upon the premise that Blue Health just announced that it would offer health insurance for a deeply discounted price to all Spring Hills residents who do not have any preexisting conditions.


We can see that, even if many other health insurance companies follow suit, his argument still supports his conclusion (and is in fact stronger).


> <b>(B) It will take many months for most residents of Spring Hills to learn about the health insurance being offered by Blue Health.
</b>


 What this answer choice says weakens the argument.


After all, if it will take many months for most residents of Spring Hills to learn about the health insurance being offered by Blue Health, then it does not make sense to conclude that most of the residents will have health insurance by next month.


If what this choice says it true, the argument falls apart.


> <b>(C) Being covered by health insurance is an important part of any wealth management strategy.
</b>


 Regardless of whether being covered by health insurance is an important part of any wealth management strategy, the argument could be sound.


After all, the argument’s conclusion is based upon the premise that Blue Health just announced that it would offer health insurance for a deeply discounted price to all Spring Hills residents who do not have any preexisting conditions.


To make his argument, the author does not need to assume that being covered by health insurance is an important part of any wealth management strategy. Even if being covered by health insurance is not an important part of any wealth management strategy, the evidence presented could support the conclusion.


This answer choice could be tempting because what it says is probably true in reality. However, in answering Assumptions questions we’re not seeking a choice that’s probably true in reality; we’re seeking one that must be true for the argument to work.


> <b>(D) Blue Health is able to offer health insurance for a deeply discounted price to Spring Hills residents because the cost of healthcare in Spring Hills is unusually low.
</b>


 This answer choice is tempting because it explains why Blue Health is able to offer health insurance for a deeply discounted price to Spring Hills residents. The cost of healthcare in Spring Hills is unusually low.


However, we are not seeking an explanation for why Blue Health is able to offer health insurance for a deeply discounted price to Spring Hills residents. The argument has already established as fact that Blue Health will offer health insurance for a deeply discounted price. We don’t need an explanation for why that fact is true. Rather, we need an assumption that allows the conclusion to properly follow from the evidence.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E) Most residents of Spring Hills do not suffer from at least one medical condition that Blue Health categorizes as a preexisting condition.
</b>


 Let’s consider what would happen if most residents of Spring Hills DO suffer from at least one medical condition that Blue Health categorizes as a preexisting condition. What happens in that situation? In that situation, even though it is true that Blue Health will offer health insurance for a deeply discounted price to all Spring Hills residents who do not have any preexisting conditions, the conclusion that, by next month most residents of Spring Hills will have health insurance does not follow from the evidence.


In that case, the argument falls apart. So, if it were true that most residents of Spring Hills do suffer from such a condition, that fact would constitute a malicious variable, for sure.


However, the author assumes that his argument is correct and that there are no malicious variables. Therefore, he must assume that, as this choice says, most residents of Spring Hills do NOT suffer from at least one medical condition that Blue Health categorizes as a preexisting condition.


Notice that, in general, this answer choice underscores the author’s belief that there are no malicious variables that will enter the equation and spoil the argument.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Example 8 

People who do yoga tend to be healthier than people who do not do yoga.

Which of the following has the author assumed in making the prediction?

1. [x] (A) People will sign up for and attend the yoga classes.
2. [ ] (B) People at the company do not have time to attend yoga classes.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) Yoga improves people’s health by strengthening their hearts, muscles, and immunity.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) The healthier people are, the more productive they will be at work.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) Doing yoga is the only way for a person to become healthier.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Let’s break down the argument.


<u>Premise</u>: People who do yoga tend to be healthier than people who do not do yoga.


<u>Premise</u>: We are going to offer yoga classes at our company.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Therefore, the people who work at our company will become healthier.


Notice that the author has proposed a plan. Whenever an author proposes a plan, he or she inherently makes the assumption that the plan will work as it has been laid out, that there will not be malicious variables that enter the equation and derail the plan.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A) People will sign up for and attend the yoga classes.
</b>


 Notice that, if it is true that people WILL NOT sign up for and attend the yoga classes, then the argument falls apart. That fact would certainly constitute a malicious variable.


Of course, the author assumes that there are not any malicious variables that will enter into the equation and derail the plan. Therefore, the author must assume that people WILL sign up for and attend the yoga classes.


Notice that this choice underscores the idea that the author assumes that there will not be any malicious variables that enter the equation and derail the plan.


> <b>(B) People at the company do not have time to attend yoga classes.
</b>


 If people at the company do not have time to attend yoga classes, then the plan will fail. Therefore, what this answer choice says weakens the argument. In an Assumption question, an answer choice that weakens the argument can never be correct.


> <b>(C) Yoga improves people’s health by strengthening their hearts, muscles, and immunity.
</b>


 This answer choice is tempting because it explains how Yoga improves people’s health. In other words, it explains that Yoga increases people’s health by strengthening their hearts, muscles, and immunity.


However, we are not seeking an explanation for why Yoga improves health. The argument already establishes as fact that Yoga improves health. We don’t need an explanation for why that fact is true. Rather, we need an assumption that allows the conclusion to properly follow from the evidence.


> <b>(D) The healthier people are, the more productive they will be at work.
</b>


 The argument’s conclusion is that the people who work at our company will become healthier. Notice that this is a conclusion solely about health. To arrive at the conclusion, the author does not need to assume that there will be any particular results of that improved health, such as increased productivity. So, regardless of whether this answer choice is true, the argument could still be sound.


> <b>(E) Doing yoga is the only way for a person to become healthier.
</b>


 The argument’s conclusion that people who work at the company will become healthier is supported by the premises regardless of whether doing yoga is the only way for a person to become healthier.


After all, even if doing yoga is not <em>the only way</em> for a person to become healthier, doing yoga is <em>a way</em> for a person to become healthier. So, if the company is going to offer yoga classes, it makes sense to conclude that people who work at the company will become healthier.


So, the author does not have to assume what this choice says in order to arrive at the argument’s conclusion.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

##  Example 9 

150 years ago, the area where the Mininguela River meets the Dallora River was prime habitat for a variety of native species.

Which of the following must be assumed in making the above prediction?

1. [ ] (A) Only after native plant species have reestablished significant populations in the area will many animals choose to reside there.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) Factories did not discharge toxic wastes into the Mininguela River or the Dallora River.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) Without being reintroduced by humans, most native species will not return to the area where the Mininguela River meets the Dallora River.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) The effects of years of manufacturing in the area where the rivers meet have not rendered the local soil inhospitable to native species.
5. [ ] (E) Currently, there are no native species inhabiting the region surrounding the intersection of the Mininguela River and the Dallora River.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Here's a breakdown of the argument.


<u>Background</u>: 150 years ago, the area where the Mininguela River meets the Dallora River was prime habitat for a variety of native species.


<u>Background</u>: As time went on, the region became an industrial center where dozens of factories were located, at which point, for many native species, the area was not suitable habitat.


<u>Premise</u>: The factories are being torn down.


The conclusion of the argument is a prediction.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Native plants and animals will repopulate this once ecologically vibrant region.


The reasoning underlying this prediction is that the area was previously prime habitat for native species, and that the factories that rendered the area inhospitable to native species are being removed. So, it seems natural that the species would repopulate the area.


Is the fact that the factories are being torn down evidence sufficient for concluding definitively that native species will repopulate the area, or does something else also have to be true?


One key thing that we will see time and again is that, whenever anyone makes a prediction, he or she inherently makes the assumption that there will not be malicious variables that enter the equation and render the prediction incorrect.


The correct answer to this question highlights the basic assumption that, with the factories gone, there will not be something else preventing native species’ repopulating the area.


> <b>(A) Only after native plant species have reestablished significant populations in the area will many animals choose to reside there.
</b>


 What this choice says makes sense, and someone making a prediction about species’ repopulating an area would likely agree with what this choice says.


However, we are looking specifically for an assumption necessary for the evidence to support the prediction, and it does not have to be the case that the repopulation process will work as described by this choice in order for the fact that the factories have been torn down to support the prediction that native species will repopulate the area.


> <b>(B) Factories did not discharge toxic wastes into the Mininguela River or the Dallora River.
</b>


 This choice could be tempting, in that one could make the case that, if factories DID discharge toxic wastes into the rivers, the area might be so contaminated that native species would not repopulate it.


Notice, however, that we would have to go beyond what this choice says in order to make the case that, if factories did discharge toxic wastes into the rivers, the prediction is incorrect. We don’t have information clearly indicating that past discharging of toxic waste into the rivers would contaminate the area enough to keep species from repopulating it.


So, we don’t have information clearly indicating that what this choice says is an assumption necessary for making the prediction.


> <b>(C) Without being reintroduced by humans, most native species will not return to the area where the Mininguela River meets the Dallora River.
</b>


 Notice that this answer choice indicates that there is a malicious variable – the fact that, without being reintroduced, the species will not return – that could render the prediction incorrect.


In an Assumption question, an answer that presents a malicious variable will never be correct because the fact that a malicious variable exists will weaken the argument.


In an Assumption question, a choice that weakens the argument can never be correct.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) The effects of years of manufacturing in the area where the rivers meet have not rendered the local soil inhospitable to native species.
</b>


 Predicting that native species will repopulate the area requires assuming that manufacturing in the area has not rendered the soil in the area inhospitable to native species. Otherwise, the prediction would not make sense because, if the soil were inhospitable to native species, even after the removal of the factories, the species would not repopulate the area.


So, the fact that the factories will be removed is not sufficient evidence for concluding that native species will repopulate the region. In addition to the evidence provided in the passage, the argument requires the assumption stated by this choice.


Notice that the correct answer to this question highlights the basic assumption that, with the factories gone, there will not be something else, a malicious variable, preventing native species’ repopulating the area.


> <b>(E) Currently, there are no native species inhabiting the region surrounding the intersection of the Mininguela River and the Dallora River.
</b>


 What this choice says may seem to be supported by what the argument says.


However, we are not looking for a conclusion supported by the argument but rather for an assumption upon which the prediction depends, and the prediction that native species will repopulate the region does not depend on the assumption that absolutely no native species currently inhabit the region.


After all, even if some native species inhabit in the region, those species and others could still repopulate the area.


So, the prediction could be correct even this choice is not true.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Example 10 

On the island of Lakahoa, numerous giant stone statues have been found.

Which of the following is an assumption on which the argument relies?

1. [ ] (A) The ancient inhabitants of Lakahoa did not have the ability to transport the few remaining statues to sacred areas.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) There were no differences between the levels of quality of the statues used in sacred areas.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) The number of giant stone statues on Lakahoa in ancient times was the same as the number there now.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) The area around the quarry was not considered sacred by ancient inhabitants of Lakahoa.
5. [ ] (E) All of the ancient inhabitants of Lakahoa had the skills required for assessing the quality of a stone statue.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



Here’s a breakdown of the argument.


<u>Background</u>: On the island of Lakahoa, numerous giant stone statues have been found. It is believed that these statues were used for marking sacred areas by people who inhabited Lakahoa in ancient times.


<u>Premise</u>: A few of the statues remain in the area around the quarry where all of the statues were produced.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Clearly, therefore, ancient inhabitants of Lakahoa did not believe those few statues to be of sufficiently high quality for marking sacred areas.


We see that the author has gone from the simple fact that a few of the statues remain in the area around the quarry to a conclusion about what ancient inhabitants of Lakahoa believed about the quality of those statues.


In going from the premise to the conclusion, the author has assumed that a number of malicious variables that could wreck the argument are not the case.


To find the correct answer, we have to look for a choice that states the author’s assumption that a particular malicious variable that could wreck the argument is not the case.


> <b>(A) The ancient inhabitants of Lakahoa did not have the ability to transport the few remaining statues to sacred areas.
</b>


 Rather than state an assumption on which the argument depends, this choice weakens the argument.


After all, if, as this choice says, the ancient inhabitants of Lakahoa did not have the ability to transport the statues that remain around the quarry, then it could be that the reason those statues remain in that area is simply that there was no way to transport them.


In that case, the fact that those statues remain there would not be a reason to conclude that ancient inhabitants of Lakahoa did not believe those statues to be of sufficiently high quality for marking sacred areas.


In an Assumption question, a choice that weakens the argument can never be correct.


> <b>(B) There were no differences between the levels of quality of the statues used in sacred areas.
</b>


 Notice that, even if there WERE differences between the levels of quality of the statues used in sacred areas, the argument still works.


After all, even if there were differences between the levels of quality of the statues used in sacred areas, it could still be that the few statues that remain in the area around the quarry were lower in quality than all the rest. Also, it could still be that those few statues remain there because of what ancient inhabitants of Lakahoa believed about their quality.


Thus, even if this choice is not true, that fact is not a malicious variable that wrecks the argument.


So, the author doesn’t have to assume what this choice says in order for the argument to work.


> <b>(C) The number of giant stone statues on Lakahoa in ancient times was the same as the number there now.
</b>


 Notice that, even if the number of giant stone statues on Lakahoa in ancient times was NOT the same as the number there now, the argument still works.


After all, regardless of how many statues were on Lakahoa in ancient times, it could still be the case that the reason a few statues remain in the area around the quarry is that ancient inhabitants of Lakahoa did not believe those statues to be of sufficiently high quality for marking sacred areas.


Thus, even if this choice is not true, that fact is not a malicious variable that wrecks the argument.


So, the author doesn’t have to assume what this choice says in order for the argument to work.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) The area around the quarry was not considered sacred by ancient inhabitants of Lakahoa.
</b>


 If the area around the quarry WAS considered sacred by ancient inhabitants of Lakahoa, the argument falls apart. So, that fact would certainly constitute a malicious variable.


After all, if the area around the quarry was considered sacred, then it doesn’t make sense to conclude that ancient inhabitants of Lakahoa did not believe the statues remaining there to be of sufficiently high quality for marking sacred areas. In that case, it could instead be that those few statues remain there because ancient inhabitants used them to mark that area.


So, in making the argument, the author must be assuming that the area around the quarry was NOT considered sacred by ancient inhabitants of Lakahoa, as this choice says.


> <b>(E) All of the ancient inhabitants of Lakahoa had the skills required for assessing the quality of a stone statue.
</b>


 This choice is tempting because it’s close to correct. After all, it makes sense that, in concluding that ancient inhabitants of Lakahoa did not believe some statues to be of sufficiently high quality for marking sacred areas, the author assumes that some of the ancient inhabitants of Lakahoa had the skills required for assessing the quality of a stone statue.


However, must the author assume that ALL of the inhabitants had the skills required for assessing the quality of a stone statue? No.


After all, if even it is not the case that all of the inhabitants had such skills, it could still be the case that SOME of the inhabitants were able to determine that the statues that remain around the quarry were not of sufficiently high quality for marking sacred areas.


So, even if NOT all of the ancient inhabitants of Lakahoa had the skills required for assessing the quality of a stone statue, that fact is not a malicious variable that wrecks the argument.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

Let’s now discuss common ways in which assumption question correct answers work.
