## Lesson Content
When an author sets forth an argument, plan, proposal, or course of action, she assumes that there will be no offsetting factors that will somehow render that argument, plan, proposal, or course of action invalid.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When an author sets forth an argument, plan, proposal, or course of action, she assumes that there will be no offsetting factors that will somehow render that argument, plan, proposal, or course of action invalid.
{{< /alert >}}

For example, people taking an action may assume that other people will not seek to counter the effects of the action.

Let’s discuss some real life “offsetting factors” that have occurred and that spoiled some arguments, plans, proposals, and courses of action.

<u>Plan 1</u>: To help cigarette smokers quit smoking, a law was passed requiring cigarette manufacturers to lower the amount of nicotine, a highly addictive stimulant, found in cigarettes.

> <u>Assumption</u>: There will be no offsetting factors that ruin this plan; for example, smokers WILL NOT smoke more cigarettes to compensate for the lower amounts of nicotine found in each cigarette.

> <u>Outcome</u>: The law did not help smokers to quit smoking because of an unexpected offsetting factor: smokers actually did smoke more cigarettes to compensate for the lower amounts of nicotine found in each cigarette.

<u>Plan 2</u>: To help curb the consumption of sugary drinks, a state added a hefty tax to the price of all soda sold in the state.

> <u>Assumption</u>: There will be no offsetting factors that ruin this plan.

> <u>Outcome</u>: Soda sales did not decline because soda manufacturers did respond by lowering the price of their soda by exactly the amount of the tax, effectively keeping the cost of soda to consumers the same.

<u>Plan 3</u>: To lose weight, people significantly reduce the number of calories they consume.

> <u>Assumption</u>: There will be no offsetting factors that ruin this plan; for example, a person’s body WILL NOT respond to the reduction in calories consumed by reducing the number of calories it burns.

> <u>Outcome</u>: In fact, in some cases, though certainly not in all cases, people who reduce calorie intake do not lose weight because their bodies do respond by reducing the number of calories they burn.

The assumption that there will be no offsetting factors to ruin a plan is not always a good assumption (more on this in the Weaken the Argument Chapter).

Some of you are saying, “Hey – this section sounds a lot like the malicious variables section.” If you thought that, you are correct! The idea that an author assumes that there will not be any malicious variables that enter into the equation and spoil the plan is a general, overarching theme in Assumption questions.

Let’s put this idea into practice.

##  Example 11 

Beer sold in the city of Hooksville has always contained seven percent alcohol by volume.

Which of the following must be assumed in making the above prediction?

1. [ ] (A) Consuming too much alcohol is harmful for a person’s mental and physical wellbeing.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) The cost of beer sold in Hooksville will not substantially increase because of the new law.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) Some residents of Hooksville consume wine but do not consume other alcoholic beverages.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) Hooksville residents will not compensate for the lower alcohol volume in beer by drinking twice as much beer as they do now.
5. [ ] (E) Many nearby towns have enacted similar laws, which have resulted in residents of those towns consuming far less alcohol than they consumed prior to the laws’ being enacted.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A) Consuming too much alcohol is harmful for a person’s mental and physical wellbeing.
</b>


 This answer choice is tempting because it explains why there is concern that residents of Hooksville are consuming too much alcohol.


However, we are not seeking an explanation for why there is concern that residents of Hooksville are consuming too much alcohol. The argument already establishes as fact that there is concern that residents of Hooksville are consuming too much alcohol. We don’t need an explanation for why that fact is true.


Rather, we need an assumption that allows the conclusion to properly follow from the evidence.


> <b>(B) The cost of beer sold in Hooksville will not substantially increase because of the new law.
</b>


 If the cost of beer sold in Hooksville were to substantially increase because of the new law, then we’d have more faith that, in the near future, Hooksville residents who drink beer will consume less alcohol than they currently do.


After all, simple laws of economics tell us that, as prices rise (especially substantially), demand decreases. In other words, if the cost of beer substantially increased, the plan would be even more likely to work.


So then, certainly the author is not assuming that the cost of beer sold in Hooksville will not substantially increase because of the new law.


> <b>(C) Some residents of Hooksville consume wine but do not consume other alcoholic beverages.
</b>


 Regardless of whether some residents of Hooksville consume wine but do not consume other alcoholic beverages, the argument could still work. After all, the argument centers on the people who DO drink beer in Hooksville.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) Hooksville residents will not compensate for the lower alcohol volume in beer by drinking twice as much beer as they do now.
</b>


Any time an author sets forth an argument, plan, proposal, or course of action, she assumes that there will be no offsetting factors that will somehow render that argument, plan, proposal, or course of action invalid. Therefore, the author must assume that Hooksville residents will not compensate for the lower alcohol volume in beer by drinking twice as much beer as they do now.


After all, if Hooksville residents DO compensate for the lower alcohol volume in beer by drinking twice as much beer as they do now, then the plan falls apart.


> <b>(E) Many nearby towns have enacted similar laws, which have resulted in residents of those towns consuming far less alcohol than they consumed prior to the laws’ being enacted.
</b>


 Although what this answer choice says is in line with the argument’s reasoning and conclusion, the author does not need to assume what this choice says. Even if no nearby town has enacted a similar law and seen results like those the author predicts will be achieved in Hooksville, the law could could still work in Hooksville.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Example 12 

In the country of Junipira, mangos are being grown, processed, and shipped to markets in Junipira at the maximum possible rate of 2,000 mangos per day.

Which of the following is an assumption upon which the argument depends?

1. [ ] (A) Most residents of Junipira will begin to substantially reduce their mango consumption.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) Mango production in Junipira is limited by the relatively small number of acres available in the country for growing mangos.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) Almost all residents of Junipira will not begin to travel to and purchase mangos in the nearby country of Mangosteen, where mangos are less expensive than they are in Junipira.
4. [ ] (D) There are other fruits sold in Junipira that have the same taste, texture, and consistency as mangos.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) Over the past few months, mango farmers have developed innovative ways to increase the number of mangos that they are able to grow per acre of farmland.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



> <b>(A) Most residents of Junipira will begin to substantially reduce their mango consumption.
</b>


What this answer choice says weakens the argument.


After all if,most residents of Junipira will begin to substantially reduce their mango consumption, then there is a good chance that the level of demand will drop back below the level of supply. In this case, we would have less faith that the price of mangos sold in Junipira will soon increase.


In general, when we’re seeking an assumption upon which an argument rests, an answer choice that says something that weakens the argument will not be correct.


> <b>(B) Mango production in Junipira is limited by the relatively small number of acres available in the country for growing mangos.
</b>


This answer choice is tempting because it helps explain why mangos are being grown, processed, and shipped to markets in Junipira at the maximum possible rate of 2,000 mangos per day: mango production in Junipira is limited by the relatively small number of acres available in the country for growing mangos.


However, the argument establishes as fact that mangos are being grown, processed, and shipped to markets in Junipira at the maximum possible rate of 2,000 mangos per day. We don’t need an explanation for why that fact is true.


Rather, we need an assumption that allows the conclusion to properly follow from the evidence.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) Almost all residents of Junipira will not begin to travel to and purchase mangos in the nearby country of Mangosteen, where mangos are less expensive than they are in Junipira.
</b>


 Any time an author sets forth an argument, plan, proposal, or course of action, she assumes that there will be no offsetting factors that will somehow render that argument, plan, proposal, or course of action invalid. Therefore, the author must assume that almost all residents of Junipira will not begin to travel to and purchase mangos in the nearby country of Mangosteen. After all, if almost all residents of Junipira WILL begin to travel to and purchase mangos in the nearby country of Mangosteen, then the plan falls apart.


> <b>(D) There are other fruits sold in Junipira that have the same taste, texture, and consistency as mangos.
</b>


 If, contrary to what this choice says, there were NO other fruits sold in Junipira that have the same taste, texture, and consistency as mangos, then we’d have a little bit more faith in the argument. After all, if no other fruits sold in Junipira have the same taste, texture, and consistency as mangos, then probably there are not any viable substitutes for mangos, a situation in which mango demand is likely to remain strong.


So then, certainly the author is not assuming that there ARE other fruits sold in Junipira that have the same taste, texture, and consistency as mangos.


> <b>(E) Over the past few months, mango farmers have developed innovative ways to increase the number of mangos that they are able to grow per acre of farmland.
</b>


 The conclusion is supported by the premise that mangos are being grown, processed, and shipped to markets in Junipira at the maximum possible rate of 2,000 mangos per day.


The effect of this choice is to explain how farmers have achieved that maximum possible rate. They have developed innovative ways to increase the number of mangos that they are able to grow per acre of farmland.


Of course, the premise will be a fact that supports the conclusion regardless of how the farmers have achieved that maximum possible rate. So, the author does not have to assume anything about how the farmers have achieved that maximum possible rate in order to support the conclusion.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

Now, let’s discuss correct answers that highlight assumptions that cause-and-effect arguments rely on.
