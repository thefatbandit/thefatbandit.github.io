## Lesson Content
Many arguments that appear in the GMAT involve cause-and-effect reasoning, something that we’ll often mention in the chapters that follow, but we’ll begin building our expertise in cause-and-effect reasoning here.

An author makes a cause-and-effect claim when she maintains that some factor x is the cause of some outcome y.

When an author makes a cause-and-effect claim, she assumes that her cause-and-effect claim is correct; she assumes that there could not be an alternative explanation for the observed events.

Another way of saying this is that, when an author makes a cause-and-effect claim, she posits that the cause she has described is the ONLY possible cause of the effect.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When an author makes a cause-and-effect claim, she assumes that her cause-and-effect claim is correct; she assumes that there could not be an alternative explanation for the observed events.
{{< /alert >}}

For example, let’s say that an author notices that, when the speed limit on a certain highway was reduced, the number of vehicular collisions on that highway decreased.

In other words, she assumes that there could not be an alternative explanation for the decrease in the number of collisions.

Let’s list out some plausible alternative explanations for the decrease in the number of collisions that occurred after the speed limit on the highway was reduced.

> The number of vehicles using the highway substantially decreased immediately after the speed limit was reduced.

> For the first time, a new law mandated that all vehicles on the highway be equipped with advanced collision avoidance systems.

> Large trucks, which had been responsible for most of the collisions on the highway, were for the first time not allowed to use the highway.

> The highway surface, which had lapsed into a state of dangerous disrepair, was recently restored to like-new condition, a substantial improvement to the safety of the highway.

Notice that each of those could be a plausible reason why the number of collisions decreased.

So, if it were determined that any of those statements were true, her argument would fall apart.

For another example of cause-and-effect reasoning, consider the following argument:

> <em>In a recent medical study, all of the study participants who had abused alcohol in the past five years were found to be suffering with depression. Thus, it must be the case that abusing alcohol causes depression. </em>

Notice that the argument relies on cause-and-effect logic.

Does the author provide convincing evidence that abusing alcohol actually causes depression? No, the only premise is that the study participants who abused alcohol were found to be suffering with depression.

In fact, there are several possibilities here:

> 1) The author is correct; abusing alcohol does cause depression.

> 2) The cause-effect relationship is reversed: the depression actually caused the alcohol abuse.

> 3) A third factor caused both conditions.

> 4) The conditions developed independently and are not related.

So, think carefully here.

> - The cause-effect relationship is not actually reversed.

> - There isn’t a third factor that is the true cause of both alcohol abuse and depression.

> - The two conditions did not develop independently.

In other words, the author is assuming that he is correct in his conclusion about the events that he has observed.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

An Assumption question correct answer can state an assumption that must be true in order for a cause-and-effect conclusion to be correct.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 13 

Steven was asked to take a polygraph, i.e., lie detector, test prior to being hired as a security guard at Company X.

In drawing the conclusion, Company X assumed which of the following?

1. [ ] (A) Using a polygraph test for hiring purposes is legal in the state where Steven lives.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) Steven was not suffering from an illness that causes excess sweating, a common cause of polygraph failure.
3. [ ] (C) When he created his resume, Steven was not aware that he would be asked about its contents in the course of a polygraph test.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) Company X has a legal interest in hiring security guards who demonstrate only the highest levels of integrity.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) Steven had previously failed a polygraph test at another company, Company Y.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



First let’s identify the conclusion made by Company X.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Steven must have lied on his resume.


This conclusion is based on Steven’s failure on the section of the polygraph test in which he was asked about his resume.


Notice that the company is assuming that there could not be another reason why Steven failed that section of the polygraph test. In other words, we see that the Company assumes that the only potential cause of Steven’s polygraph failure is his lying on his resume. After all, if there is something else that could have caused Steven’s polygraph failure, then the logic of the argument no longer works.


So then, what unstated information is necessary for the logic of the argument to work? An assumption that there is not another potential cause for Steven’s polygraph failure.


> <b>(A) Using a polygraph test for hiring purposes is legal in the state where Steven lives.
</b>


 In deciding to use a polygraph test for hiring purposes, Company X may be assuming that is it legal to do so.


However, going from the fact that Steven failed the part of the test related to his resume to the conclusion that he lied on his resume does not require assuming that using the test is legal. The argument is sound regardless of whether using the test is legal.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B) Steven was not suffering from an illness that causes excess sweating, a common cause of polygraph failure.
</b>


 Going from the fact that Steven failed the test to the conclusion that he lied on his resume requires the assumption (among other assumptions), that he didn’t fail because of an illness that made him sweat. If Steven was in fact suffering from an illness that that causes excess sweating, a common cause of polygraph failure, then the fact that he failed the test would not be reason to believe that he was lying.


Notice that what this answer choice says serves to rule out an alternative explanation for why Steven failed the polygraph.


Company X must assume that Steven was not suffering from an illness that causes excess sweating, a common cause of polygraph failure, because if he WERE, then the argument would fall apart.


> <b>(C) When he created his resume, Steven was not aware that he would be asked about its contents in the course of a polygraph test.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, since one could think that, had Steven been aware that he would be asked about his resume in the course of a polygraph test, he would not have lied on his resume, and that, therefore, concluding that he lied on his resume requires assuming that he was not aware that he would be asked about his resume in the course of a polygraph test.


However, what this choice says does not have to be true in order for the argument to work. Steven could have lied even though he was aware that he would be asked about his resume in the course of a polygraph test.


Overall, going from the evidence, the test results, to the conclusion, that Steven lied, does not require assuming anything about Steven’s thinking. Really, all one has to assume is that the cause of his failing the test was lying.


> <b>(D) Company X has a legal interest in hiring security guards who demonstrate only the highest levels of integrity.
</b>


This statement makes sense, and may explain why Company X is using a polygraph test, but it is unrelated to the matter of concluding that Steven lied because he failed the polygraph test.


In answering Assumption questions, we must be careful to not choose as correct answer choices that say things that make sense yet are not assumptions upon which arguments depend.


In this case, the argument could work regardless of whether Company X has a legal interest in hiring security guards who demonstrate only the highest levels of integrity.


> <b>(E) Steven had previously failed a polygraph test at another company, Company Y.
</b>


This statement tends to lend credibility to the conclusion that Steven is lying. Thus, it is a tempting answer choice.


However, we are not simply looking for more support for the conclusion that Steven lied. We are looking for an assumption necessary for connecting the results of the test Steven took for Company X with the conclusion that he lied on his resume, and the assumption that Steven failed a different test at a different company is not necessary for making that connection.


In other words, this argument could be sound regardless of whether Steven had previously failed a polygraph test at another company, Company Y.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

##  Example 14 

In a recent medical study, all of the study participants who had abused alcohol in the past five years were found to be suffering with depression.

Each of the following is an assumption upon which the argument depends EXCEPT:

1. [x] (A) Both alcohol abuse issues and depression can be solved via the use of therapy specially designed for the treatment of alcohol abuse issues.
2. [ ] (B) The depression and alcohol abuse of the study participants were not both results of heavy use of illicit drugs.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) The study participants were not found to be suffering with depression before they started abusing alcohol.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) The study was not set up in a way such that people who were not suffering with depression would be very unlikely to participate.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) The concurrence of alcohol abuse and depression seen in the people studied was not merely a coincidence.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



The argument in this question has a cause-and-effect conclusion, which is based on the fact that people who were known to abuse alcohol were found to be suffering with depression.


Arriving at that conclusion from the evidence requires assuming that the observed pattern was not the result of something other than alcohol abuse causing depression. In other words, the author assumes that she is correct in her evaluation of the situation. She assumes that it is in fact the abuse of alcohol that is the cause of depression. She assumes that there is not an alternative explanation for the pattern of alcohol abuse and depression.


Because this is an “EXCEPT” question, four of the answers will be assumptions upon which the argument depends; these will be incorrect answers. Our job is to find the one answer that is NOT an assumption upon which the argument depends


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A) Both alcohol abuse issues and depression can be solved via the use of therapy specially designed for the treatment of alcohol abuse issues.
</b>


 This choice could seem to be an assumption that the author must be making, because it could seem necessary to assume that therapy designed for treatment for alcohol abuse issues could solve both alcohol abuse issues and depression in order to conclude that abusing alcohol causes depression.


Notice, however, that the truth is that this choice is not an assumption necessary for going from the evidence about alcohol abuse and depression to the conclusion that abusing alcohol causes depression. Rather, this choice is a conclusion that follows from the author’s conclusion. It makes sense to conclude that, if abusing alcohol causes depression, therapy designed to treat alcohol abuse issues could solve both issues.


Meanwhile, coming to the conclusion that alcohol abuse causes depression does not require assuming anything about therapy designed for treating alcohol abuse issues. One could arrive at the argument’s conclusion even if such therapy did not even exist.


Assumption upon which the argument depends? No.


> <b>(B) The depression and alcohol abuse of the study participants were not both results of heavy use of illicit drugs.
</b>


 Going from the evidence that the participants in the study both abused alcohol and were found to be depressed to the conclusion that alcohol abuse causes depression requires assuming that a third factor, use of illicit drugs, did not cause both the alcohol abuse and the depression. After all, if a third factor, use of illicit drugs, DID cause both the alcohol abuse and the depression, then the conclusion would be incorrect.


Thus, if the depression and alcohol abuse of the study participants WERE both results of heavy use of illicit drugs, the argument falls apart. Therefore, the author must assume that the depression and alcohol abuse of the study participants were NOT both results of heavy use of illicit drugs.


Assumption upon which the argument depends? Yes.


> <b>(C) The study participants were not found to be suffering with depression before they started abusing alcohol.
</b>


 The conclusion that alcohol abuse causes depression depends on the assumption that the depression seen in the study participants did not precede the alcohol abuse. After all, if the study participants WERE found to be suffering with depression before they started abusing alcohol, then it could not be the case that abusing alcohol caused the depression.


Assumption upon which the argument depends? Yes.


> <b>(D) The study was not set up in a way such that people who were not suffering with depression would be very unlikely to participate.
</b>


 If the study had been set up in such a way that people who were not depressed would have been very unlikely to participate, then it could very well be that the reason that all the participants who abused alcohol were depressed was that everyone who participated in the study was depressed. In other words, non-depressed people did not partake in the study. In this case, we’d have an alternative explanation for why all of the study participants who had abused alcohol in the past five years were found to be suffering with depression: everyone in the study was depressed anyway. In this case, the author’s argument would fall apart.


So, in order to connect the alcohol abuse and depression of the study participants to the conclusion that alcohol abuse causes depression, the author must assume that the study was not set up in a way such that people who were not depressed would have been very unlikely to participate.


Assumption upon which the argument depends? Yes.


> <b>(E) The concurrence of alcohol abuse and depression seen in the people studied was not merely a coincidence.
</b>


 When two events, such as alcohol abuse and depression, occur together, it is possible that there is no cause and effect relationship between the two events, that the pattern observed is merely a coincidence. After all, the world can be a random place, and there are many patterns to be found that may seem to result from cause-and-effect relationships but result from nothing more than chance.


In the case of this argument, if it were true that there really is no cause-and-effect relationship between alcohol abuse and depression, we’d have an alternative explanation for the observation made by the author, and the argument would fall apart.


Therefore, the author must assume that the concurrence of alcohol abuse and depression seen in the people studied was not merely a coincidence.


Assumption upon which the argument depends? Yes.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)
