## Lesson Content
In Assumption questions, we often encounter arguments that conclude that some plan will not work or that some outcome will not occur.

However, the evidence that she provides will not be sufficient alone for the argument to work.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Whenever an author concludes that a plan will not work or that some outcome will not occur, she must assume that there is not some other way or some other path (that she has failed to consider) via which the plan could work or via which the outcome could occur.
{{< /alert >}}

In other words, whenever an author concludes that a plan will not work or that some outcome will not occur, she assumes that she is correct and that she has taken into consideration all possible variables that could have an impact on the plan or the outcome.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Whenever an author concludes that a plan will not work or that, in general, some outcome will not occur, she assumes that she is correct and that she has taken into consideration all possible variables that could have an impact on the plan or the outcome. She assumes that she has not failed to consider any variable that could make her argument unsound.
{{< /alert >}}

In this type of question, the correct answer will be one that states (in one way or another) an assumption that there is not another way or path via which the plan could work or via which the outcome could occur.

In the following mini example, notice that the author concludes that a certain plan will not work.

> <em>Community leaders in Kanelston are encouraging residents to grow their own vegetables. However, most residents of Kanelston live in apartment buildings and thus do not have yards in which they could plant gardens. Therefore, what the community leaders are doing is not going to make much difference.</em>

> <em>Which of the following is an assumption upon which the conclusion depends?</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>The roofs of most apartment buildings in Kanelston are not good locations for rooftop gardens.</em>

Notice, the author’s conclusion that what the community leaders are doing is not going to make much difference is based on the fact that one path to having a garden, planting in a yard, is not available to the residents.

Let’s look at another mini example in which the assumption is that there is not another path via which an outcome could occur:

> <em>In each of the past three years, Dustin Manufacturing has succeeded in increasing production without adding to its work space. However, now, Dustin Manufacturing’s facilities are at a point such that there is no more room for either more employees or more equipment. Therefore, if Dustin Manufacturing does not add to its work space, its production will not increase this year.</em>

> <em>Which of the following is an assumption upon which the argument relies?</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>There is no way for employees at Dustin manufacturing to produce more per employee.</em>

The author concludes that production will not increase without an increase in work space.

Notice that the correct answer states the argument’s unstated assumption that there is not an alternative path via which production could increase, an increase in production per employee.

In general, if the conclusion of the argument in an Assumption question is that a plan will not succeed, an outcome cannot occur, or that anything cannot, will not, or did not happen, it is likely that the correct answer choice will state an assumption that a certain alternative path is not or was not available.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In an Assumption question, an argument may depend on the assumption that an alternative path to an outcome is not available.
{{< /alert >}}

Let's practice identifying this type of assumption.

##  Example 15 

Because residents of Mergate had complained for years about the noise associated with Mergate airport, the city took the radical step of closing the airport entirely.

Which of the following is an assumption necessary for arriving at the conclusion drawn?

1. [ ] (A) Most businesses in Mergate depend on spending by vacationers for the majority of their income.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) Residents of Mergate have not noticed a significant reduction in noise pollution since the airport was closed.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) The improvement in the ambience in Mergate resulting from the closing of the airport will not cause vacationers to feel that making some extra effort to get there is worthwhile.
4. [ ] (D) In Mergate, growth in business not dependent on spending by vacationers will not make up for any city tax revenue lost because of a reduction in vacationer related business.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) The closing of the airport has not benefited any businesses located in Mergate.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



The conclusion of the argument is the following:


<u>Conclusion</u>: Vacation related businesses in Mergate will no longer be able to successfully compete with similar businesses in Wave City.


The conclusion is supported by the fact that one path via which businesses in Mergate were able to compete with businesses in Wave City, vacationers’ having the ability to fly directly into Mergate, has been eliminated. Of course, in order to arrive at the conclusion from this one premise, the author must assume that there will not be another path via which businesses in Mergate will be able to successfully compete with businesses in Wave City. In other words, the author believes that she has carefully and completely evaluated the situation and that there is not another way that businesses in Mergate will be able to successfully compete with businesses in Wave City.


> <b>(A) Most businesses in Mergate depend on spending by vacationers for the majority of their income.
</b>


 This answer could be tempting, because it could seem to reinforce the idea that Mergate businesses will have difficulty competing with businesses in Wave City as a result of the closing of the airport.


Notice, however, that this choice supports the wrong conclusion. The conclusion is not that most Mergate businesses will have difficulty competing. It’s that certain businesses, the vacation related businesses, will have difficulty competing.


Going from the premise that traveling to Mergate by air requires flying into Wave City airport to the conclusion that vacation related businesses in Mergate will no longer be able to successfully compete does not require assuming that most businesses in Mergate depend on spending by vacationers. Even if only a small fraction of businesses in Mergate depend on spending by vacationers, the conclusion could be supported by the evidence.


> <b>(B) Residents of Mergate have not noticed a significant reduction in noise pollution since the airport was closed.
</b>


 It is not necessary to assume that closing the airport did not result in a noticeable change in noise pollution in order to conclude that closing the airport has made competing with businesses in Wave City difficult for certain businesses in Mergate. In other words, the argument could be sound regardless of whether what this answer choice says is true.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) The improvement in the ambience in Mergate resulting from the closing of the airport will not cause vacationers to feel that making some extra effort to get there is worthwhile.
</b>


 The conclusion that closing the airport has created a situation such that vacation related business in Mergate cannot successfully compete with similar businesses in Wave City depends on the assumption that closing the airport has not made available a new path via which the Mergate businesses can compete.


Thus, the argument does not work unless what this choice says, that the closing of the airport has NOT given vacationers a reason to make some extra effort to get Mergate, is true.


After all, what if it were true that the closing of the airport HAS given vacationers a reason to make some extra effort to get Mergate? In that case, the premise that traveling to Mergate by air requires flying into Wave City airport would not support the conclusion.


> <b>(D) In Mergate, growth in business not dependent on spending by vacationers will not make up for any city tax revenue lost because of a reduction in vacationer related business.
</b>


 The conclusion is not that Mergate will experience a net loss in tax revenue but rather that certain businesses in Mergate will not be able to compete with businesses in Wave City. So, since this choice supports a conclusion about tax revenue rather than one about businesses competing, this answer choice supports the wrong conclusion.


> <b>(E) The closing of the airport has not benefited any businesses located in Mergate.
</b>


 Concluding that certain businesses in Mergate will not be able to compete as a result of the closing of the airport does not require assuming that no businesses at all in Mergate will benefit from the closing of the airport. In other words, the argument could be sound regardless of whether what this answer choice says is true.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

##  Example 16 

While diamonds are not as rare as some other types of gemstones, the companies that mine diamonds restrict the number of diamonds that they make available and thus create the impression that diamonds are rare and, therefore, valuable.

Which of the following is an assumption upon which the above conclusion depends?

1. [ ] (A) Demand for diamonds will increase to a point such that diamond prices would remain at their current levels even if supply were not artificially restricted.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) Demand for non-diamond gemstones, such as rubies and emeralds, will not increase substantially.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) It would not become more difficult for diamond mining companies to restrict the number of diamonds that they make available, were additional large, minable deposits of diamonds found.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) Laboratories that make artificial diamonds will never produce and sell in significant quantities diamonds that are indistinguishable from mined diamonds.
5. [ ] (E) The price of a diamond not only depends on the supply of and demand for diamonds but also is affected by the quality of the diamond.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



Breakdown of the argument:


<u>Background</u>: Companies that mine diamonds restrict the number that they make available and thus create the impression that diamonds are rare and valuable.


<u>Premise</u>: Demand for diamonds is increasing.


<u>Conclusion</u>: As long as the companies that mine diamonds continue to restrict the number of diamonds that they make available, diamond prices will not decrease substantially.


Notice that author is concluding that a certain outcome will occur (diamond prices will not decrease substantially). This argument is based on what seems to be a straightforward case of restricted supply and increasing demand working together to support prices.


Remember that, whenever an author concludes that some outcome will not occur, she assumes that she is correct and that she has taken into consideration all possible variables that could have an impact on the outcome. She assumes that she has not failed to consider any variable that could make her argument unsound.


Therefore, this argument depends on the assumption that there is not some path other than via diamond mining companies via which additional supply of diamonds could come into the market and thus cause a decrease in the price of diamonds.


> <b>(A) Demand for diamonds will increase to a point such that diamond prices would remain at their current levels even if supply were not artificially restricted.
</b>


 Notice that the conclusion is that diamond prices will not decrease <em>as long as companies that mine diamonds continue to restrict the number of diamonds that they make available</em>.


So, arriving at the conclusion does not require assuming anything about what would happen if companies that mine were not to continue to restrict the supply of diamonds.


> <b>(B) Demand for non-diamond gemstones, such as rubies and emeralds, will not increase substantially.
</b>


 This answer might somehow appear to be correct, as one could get the impression that, if demand for other types of gemstones were to increase, demand for diamonds would decrease. Therefore, one might conclude that, in order for diamond prices to remain at their current levels, demand for other types of gemstones must not increase substantially.


However, the truth is that we have no reason to believe that demand for diamonds would decrease if demand for other types of stones were to increase. In fact, demand for all types of gemstones could increase simultaneously.


So, it is not the case that this answer has to be true in order for the conclusion to hold.


> <b>(C) It would not become more difficult for diamond mining companies to restrict the number of diamonds that they make available, were additional large, minable deposits of diamonds found.
</b>


 This choice is a trap, since what it is related to, the idea that companies that mine diamonds will be <em>able </em>to continue to restrict the number of diamonds that they make available, may seem to be a key aspect of the support for the conclusion of the argument. However, this choice is incorrect in multiple ways.


For one thing, difficulty restricting the number of diamonds available does not equate to restricting the number of diamonds available being impossible. The companies could still restrict the number of diamonds available even if it were more difficult to do so.


Secondly, and more importantly, the conclusion of the argument does not depend on diamond mining companies’ being able to continue to restrict the number of diamonds that they make available. Rather, the conclusion is that, IF the companies continue to restrict the number of diamonds that they make available, diamond prices will not decrease substantially.


So, this choice supports a conclusion different from the argument’s conclusion. This choice supports the conclusion that the diamond mining companies will be able to continue to restrict supply.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) Laboratories that make artificial diamonds will never produce and sell in significant quantities diamonds that are indistinguishable from mined diamonds.
</b>


 In concluding that the price of diamonds will not decrease substantially as long as the companies that mine diamonds continue to restrict the number of diamonds that they make available, the argument depends upon the assumption that diamonds will not come into the marketplace via a new path, a path other than via the mining companies. If diamonds were to come from another source, then the supply of diamonds could increase even if the mining companies were to continue to restrict the number that they make available.


Therefore, the argument depends on the assumption that laboratories will not begin making significant quantities of diamonds that are indistinguishable from mined ones. After all, if laboratories WILL begin making significant quantities of diamonds that are indistinguishable from mined ones, then the evidence does not support the conclusion, and the argument falls apart.


> <b>(E) The price of a diamond not only depends on the supply of and demand for diamonds but also is affected by the quality of the diamond.
</b>


 This answer could be tempting because it is relevant to the argument, as it seems to be saying, basically, that the price of diamonds depends on factors other than the supply of and demand for diamonds, a statement that could potentially be seen as weakening the argument.


However, while such a statement might be relevant to the argument and might weaken it, we are looking, not for a weakener, but rather for an assumption integral to the argument.


In general, when we’re seeking an assumption upon which an argument relies, an answer choice that says something that weakens the argument will not be correct.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

Let’s now talk about another assumption that could appear in a correct answer: that a solution to a problem will not cause the same problem or a similar one.
