## Lesson Content
As we now know, many arguments in the GMAT focus on plans or courses of action.

Another way of thinking about this is that, when an author sets forth a plan or course of action, she believes that she has correctly and completely analyzed whatever situation the plan or course of action centers on.

So then, in Assumption questions, authors typically make the assumption that a plan or a course of action they suggest for solving a problem will not itself result in the same problem in a new way.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In an Assumption question, an argument may depend on the assumption that a plan or course of action considered the path to solving a problem will not itself result in the same problem.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following argument:

> <em>Overfishing in the waters around Seatown has begun to significantly deplete the populations of fish in them. WildWater Fisheries Inc. has proposed setting up fish farms in these waters. By focusing on farming fish rather than on catching the wild fish that live in the waters near Seatown, we will stop the depletion of the wild fish stocks.</em>

The above argument makes the case for taking a course of action, setting up fish farms rather than fishing for wild fish.

If this argument were in an Assumption question, it might be followed by an answer choice like the following.

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) Crowding fish together in offshore farms does not create conditions that support the multiplication of organisms that infect and decimate wild fish populations.</em>

Notice that the above answer choice states an assumption necessary for the logic of the argument to work.

In other words, if fish farms become breeding grounds for organisms that decimate wild fish populations, then setting up fish farms would cause the same problem that setting up fish farms was meant to solve.

##  Example 17 

Homeowners Association Member: Some members of our community have expressed concerns about the number of mice living here.

Which of the following, if true, is an assumption upon which the above argument depends?

1. [ ] (A) Mice carry and transmit more diseases than are carried and transmitted by other types of rodents.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) Mice would not become ill if they were to eat cayenne pepper placed around the foundation of a house.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) There are more mice living in the community in question than there were a few years ago.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) Predators, which are necessary for keeping mouse populations controlled, would not completely avoid an area with a scent of cayenne pepper.
5. [ ] (E) Cayenne pepper repels not only mice but also ants.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



The homeowners association member is proposing a plan. What plan? To place cayenne pepper around foundations of houses.


The goal of the plan is to reduce the number of mice in the community.


Has she considered the plan well? Is there some way that the plan might result in the problem it is meant to solve or a similar one? Might placing cayenne pepper around houses actually result in an increase in the number of mice?


> <b>(A) Mice carry and transmit more diseases than are carried and transmitted by other types of rodents.
</b>


 Notice that the conclusion is not that reducing the number of mice in the community is important for some reason, such as that mice carry and transmit diseases.


The conclusion is that, by placing cayenne pepper around the foundations of houses, people in the community will reduce the number of mice in the community. In other words, the conclusion is simply that the plan for reducing the number of mice in the community will work.


Of course, the plan could work regardless of whether mice carry more diseases than other rodents carry.


So, what this choice says is not an assumption upon which the argument relies.


> <b>(B) Mice would not become ill if they were to eat cayenne pepper placed around the foundation of a house.
</b>


 The conclusion of the argument is that, by placing cayenne pepper around the foundations of their houses, people will reduce the number of mice in their community.


The evidence used is that mice are repelled by cayenne pepper. In other words, the evidence is that mice don’t like cayenne pepper and avoid it.


Could the plan work even if mice WOULD become ill if they were to eat the cayenne pepper?


Common sense tells us that mice would not eat the pepper, and that, even if they did, for some insane reason, eat the pepper and become ill, they would continue to be repelled by cayenne pepper, in which case the plan could work.


So, the argument does not depend on the assumption that mice WOULD NOT become ill if they were to eat the cayenne pepper placed around a house.


> <b>(C) There are more mice living in the community in question than there were a few years ago.
</b>


The conclusion is simply that the plan for reducing the number of mice in the community will work.


Placing cayenne pepper around the foundations of houses could result in a reduction in the number of mice in the community regardless of whether there are more, fewer, or just as many mice as there were in the community a few years ago. In other words, the number of mice present does not affect whether mice in general are repelled by cayenne pepper.


So, arriving at the conclusion that the plan will work does not require assuming that there are more mice in the community than there were a few years ago.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) Predators, which are necessary for keeping mouse populations controlled, would not completely avoid an area with a scent of cayenne pepper.
</b>


As you now know, one common assumption that authors typically make is that a plan to solve a problem will not itself result in the same problem in a new way.


In this case, the homeowners association member assumes that placing cayenne pepper around the foundations of houses will not somehow exacerbate the mouse issue. After all, if placing cayenne pepper around the foundations of houses would somehow result in an increase in the number of mice, then the argument would fall apart.


So, imagine if predators, which are necessary for keeping mouse populations controlled, WOULD completely avoid an area with a scent of cayenne pepper? In that case, the plan to solve the problem would itself cause the same problem in a new way because, without the predators, which are necessary for keeping mouse populations controlled, the mouse population would be uncontrolled. The argument would fall apart.


Therefore, the author must assume that predators, which are necessary for keeping mouse populations controlled, would NOT completely avoid an area with a scent of cayenne pepper.


> <b>(E) Cayenne pepper repels not only mice but also ants.
</b>


 The author’s conclusion is that “if we all place cayenne pepper around the foundations of our houses, we will certainly reduce the number of mice in our community.”


Notice that this conclusion, and the argument in general, is completely about reducing the number of mice in the community, and that the plan for reducing the number of mice could work regardless of whether cayenne pepper repels ants.


Therefore, the author does not need to assume that cayenne pepper repels not only mice but also ants.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Example 18 

Rowenton City Council Member: While crime rates in Rowenton continue to drop, the police still receive a steady stream of reports of people breaking into parked automobiles at night to steal items left in them.

Which of the following is an assumption upon which the city council member relies in coming to the conclusion above?

1. [ ] (A) People generally feel safer at night if the streets via which they are travelling are well lit.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) Putting out public service messages advising people to refrain from leaving items in automobiles at night would, at best, cause only a temporary change in habits.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) There are no reasons other than to deter stealing from parked automobiles for installing additional streetlights in Rowenton.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) In Rowenton, the rate of nighttime automobile break-ins has not been dropping as fast as have the rates of other types of crime.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) People who steal items from cars do not depend on light from streetlights in determining whether there is in a car anything worth breaking in for.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



Breakdown of the Argument:


<u>Background</u>: People are breaking into parked cars at night to steal items left in them.


<u>Proposal</u>: Install streetlights on streets that are currently dark at night.


<u>Expected Outcome</u>: By doing so, we will achieve a reduction in the number of nighttime automobile break-ins.


In suggesting that installing streetlights on streets that are currently dark at night will result in a reduction in nighttime automobile break-ins, the city council member is assuming that the light from streetlights will deter breaking into cars and not somehow make the problem worse. After all, what if the light from the streetlights would somehow make the problem worse? In such a case, the argument would fall apart.


Remember that when an author sets forth a plan, he believes that that plan will work; he believes that he has correctly and completely analyzed the situation. In believing that the plan will work, the author assumes that the plan he suggests for solving a problem will not result in the same problem in a new way.


> <b>(A) People generally feel safer at night if the streets via which they are travelling are well lit.
</b>


 While what this choice says seems plausible and seems to be in line with the reasoning of the argument, the conclusion is specifically about reducing automobile break-ins by installing streetlights. Since it does not have to be the case that people feel safer on well-lit streets in order for it to be the case that installing streetlights would be effective in reducing automobile break-ins, what this choice says is not an assumption upon which the city council member relies in making the argument.


In answering Assumption questions, we must be careful to not choose as correct answer choices that say things that are plausible yet are not assumptions upon which the arguments depend.


> <b>(B) Putting out public service messages advising people to refrain from leaving items in automobiles at night would, at best, cause only a temporary change in habits.
</b>


 This choice could be tempting, since a test-taker might feel that, if the city council member is recommending installing street lights, the city council member must be assuming that simply advising people to refrain from leaving items in cars overnight would not do much to solve the break-in problem, possibly because people would only temporarily change their habits.


However, the truth is that the city council member does not have to assume anything about other strategies for reducing break-ins in order to propose adding streetlights to achieve a reduction in break-ins. In other words, the city council member is saying not that adding streetlights is the only strategy that would work but rather that adding streetlights is a strategy that would work.


One concept that we will see over and over as this course progresses is the concept of the “different plan” or the “better plan” trap answer. If we look carefully at what this answer choice says, we see that it mentions a different plan. In general, unless an author claims that his plan is the only plan or the best plan, he need not assume that there are not other plans that could work.


> <b>(C) There are no reasons other than to deter stealing from parked automobiles for installing additional streetlights in Rowenton.
</b>


 That the city council member is focusing on reducing break-ins by installing streetlights does not mean that the city council member must be assuming that reducing break-ins is the only reason for installing streetlights. In other words, he never makes the claim that reducing break-ins is the only reason for installing streetlights. His argument leaves open the possibility that there could be many other reasons for installing the streetlights.


> <b>(D) In Rowenton, the rate of nighttime automobile break-ins has not been dropping as fast as have the rates of other types of crime.
</b>


 The fact that the city council member said that crime rates continue to drop but is still suggesting installing streetlights in order to cause a reduction in nighttime automobile break-ins might seem to indicate that the city council member believes that the rate of nighttime automobile break-ins has not been dropping as fast as have the rates of other types of crime.


However, regardless of whether what the passage says even supports what this choice says, we are not looking for a conclusion that follows from what is stated in the passage. We are looking for an assumption necessary for coming to the city council member’s conclusion.


The city council member’s conclusion is simply that a reduction in nighttime automobile break-ins could be achieved by installing additional streetlights. The success of that plan does not depend on there being a difference between how fast the rate of nighttime automobile break-ins has been dropping and how fast the rates of other types of crime have been dropping.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E) People who steal items from cars do not depend on light from streetlights in determining whether there is in a car anything worth breaking in for.
</b>


 If people who break into cars at night depend on light from streetlights for determining whether to break into cars, then it could be the case that, rather than result in a decrease in nighttime break-ins, adding streetlights would result in an increase in break-ins.


Therefore, the city council member must be assuming that people don’t depend on the light from streetlights for determining whether to break into cars.


Notice that this assumption is a piece of missing information that MUST BE TRUE in order for the logic of the argument to work. Also, notice that this answer choice confirms that a course of action considered the path to solving a problem will not result in the same problem in a new way. Finally notice that if People who steal items from cars DO depend on light from streetlights in determining whether there is in a car anything worth breaking in for, then the argument falls apart.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Example 19 

While there is often heavy traffic congestion on Coastal Highway in Dolya, the number of vehicles present on Coastal Highway during peak driving hours is actually slightly less than the estimated congestion free capacity of the highway.

Which of the following is an assumption upon which the argument relies?

1. [ ] (A) Even without the revenue generated via toll collection on Coastal Highway, Dolya would be able to maintain a balanced budget.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) The congestion around the toll collection stations is caused by cars entering Coastal Highway via entrance ramps near the stations rather than by the stations themselves.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) Most people who drive to destinations readily accessible via Coastal Highway do not avoid Coastal Highway because of the tolls that they would have to pay to use it.
4. [ ] (D) Even during off peak driving hours, significant congestion is caused by the toll collection stations on Coastal Highway.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) The number of drivers driving at excessive speeds on Coastal highway would increase were the toll collection stations to be removed.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



The argument makes the following case:


<u>Background</u>: There is often heavy traffic congestion on Coastal Highway in Dolya.


<u>Premise</u>: The number of vehicles present on Coastal Highway during peak driving hours is actually slightly less than the estimated congestion free capacity of the highway.


<u>Premise</u>: Most of the congestion occurs around the toll collection stations.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Removal of the toll collection stations would solve most traffic congestion problems on Coastal Highway.


Notice that the author has set forth a plan, which is to remove the toll collection stations on Coastal Highway. When an author sets forth a plan, he believes that that plan will work. In believing that the plan will work, the author assumes that the plan he suggests for solving a problem will not result in the same problem in a new way.


So, in this argument, one assumption necessary for coming to the conclusion is that removing the toll collection stations would not itself somehow cause traffic congestion on the highway. After all, if the removal of the toll collection stations would cause congestion, then the implementation of the plan would cause in a new way the same problem that the plan is meant to solve.


> <b>(A) Even without the revenue generated via toll collection on Coastal Highway, Dolya would be able to maintain a balanced budget.
</b>


 While the revenue generated by the toll collection stations may be worth considering in evaluating a plan to remove them, the argument’s conclusion is not that Dolya will be able to maintain a balanced budget. It’s that removing the stations would solve traffic congestion problems.


So, this choice supports the wrong conclusion and does not have any effect on this argument.


> <b>(B) The congestion around the toll collection stations is caused by cars entering Coastal Highway via entrance ramps near the stations rather than by the stations themselves.
</b>


 What this choice says actually weakens the argument.


If cars entering the highway, rather than the toll collection stations, are causing the congestion, then removing the stations would have no effect on the congestion. So, the author can’t be making this assumption.


In general, when we’re seeking an assumption upon which an argument rests, an answer choice that says something that weakens the argument will not be correct.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) Most people who drive to destinations readily accessible via Coastal Highway do not avoid Coastal Highway because of the tolls that they would have to pay to use it.
</b>


 If “most” people going to places accessible via Coastal Highway avoid using it because of the tolls, then more than half of the people who would otherwise use the highway are not using it because of the tolls. So, removing the tolls could result in a doubling of the number of cars using the highway.


Now, notice that a premise of the argument states that the number of cars on the highway is “slightly less than the highway’s congestion free capacity.” Therefore, doubling the number of cars on the highway seems certain to push the number well above the congestion free capacity, and thus cause congestion.


So, the argument relies on assuming that most people who drive to destinations readily accessible via Coastal Highway DO NOT avoid Coastal Highway because of the tolls, because if the tolls are keeping all those people from using the highway, then removing the tolls would cause congestion, rather than solve congestion problems.


Notice that what this choice says is a missing piece of information that must be true in order for the logic of the argument to work; this is the assumption. Notice also that this answer choice confirms that a course of action considered the path to solving a problem will not itself cause the same problem in a new way.


So, if what this choice says is not true, then the argument falls apart.


> <b>(D) Even during off peak driving hours, significant congestion is caused by the toll collection stations on Coastal Highway.
</b>


 This choice could be tempting, because one could follow a faulty line of thinking along the lines of the following: “If there is no traffic congestion around the toll collection stations during off peak hours, then removing the toll collection stations would not affect congestion during off peak hours, and maybe any congestion during off peak hours is somewhere else, and thus, hmm, removing them may not make much difference. So, maybe we have to assume that there is congestion around the toll collection stations even during off peak hours.”<br/><br/>
Of course, the truth is that the passage makes clear that <em>most </em>of the congestion occurs around the toll collection stations. So, it is not necessary to <em>assume </em>that there is congestion around the toll collection stations at any particular time, because that most of the congestion that occurs around the stations has been stated as <em>fact</em>.


In general, we must remember that the premises are always considered factual, so an answer choice that says something that appears to confirm a premise will never be correct.


> <b>(E) The number of drivers driving at excessive speeds on Coastal highway would increase were the toll collection stations to be removed.
</b>


 While what this choice says seems reasonable given what we know about the real world, we are not looking for a statement that says something reasonable. We are looking for an assumption integral to the argument, and assuming that more people will drive at excessive speeds if the stations are removed is not necessary for concluding that removing the stations will result in the elimination of most traffic congestion.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

Let’s now talk about correct answers that highlight assumptions that things that are likely true are actually true.
