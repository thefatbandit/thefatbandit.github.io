## Lesson Content
Sometimes in an Assumption question, an author will base her argument on an idea that is probably true but is not guaranteed to be true.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Sometimes, an argument in an Assumptions question depends on the assumption that something that is likely true is actually true.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example:

> <em>Three years ago, some of the students attending Rockville High School began for the first-time attending weekend classes designed to help them pass a state-wide assessment test. Since that time, the percentage of Rockville High School students passing the test has been greater than it has been any other time in the school’s history. Clearly, the weekend classes are responsible for this increased pass rate of Rockville High School students. </em>

> <em>Upon which of the following does the logic of the argument above depend?</em>

> <em><u>(Correct Answer</u></em><em>) At least some of the Rockville High School students who have attended the weekend classes have taken the state-wide assessment test.</em>

Notice that the author makes an inconspicuous, yet important, assumption.

Some of you are saying, “Wait – that statement is obvious.

Look back to the argument.

This is the point.

So, without stating that some of the students who have attended the weekend classes have actually taken the test, the author concludes that the weekend classes are responsible for the increased test pass rate of Rockville High School students.

In general terms, we see that the author assumes that what is likely true is actually true.

Notice what would happen if it were not true that at least some of the students who have attended the weekend classes have actually taken the state-wide assessment test.

After all, we’d be saying, “NONE of the students who have attended the weekend classes have taken the state-wide assessment test.” In this, case it would not make sense for the author to conclude that the weekend classes are responsible for the increased pass rate.

If you have a hard time seeing that the argument would be busted, add that fact into the argument:

> <em>Three years ago, some of the students attending Rockville High School began for the first-time attending weekend classes designed to help them pass a state-wide assessment test. Since that time, the percentage of Rockville High School students passing the test has been greater than it has been any other time in the school’s history. <strong>None of the students who have attended the weekend classes designed to help them pass the state-wide assessment test have taken the test.</strong></em>

Does it make sense to conclude from what the above passage says that the weekend classes are responsible for the increased pass rate of Rockville High School students? Clearly, it does not.

So, again, the author must be assuming that at least some of the students who began attending the weekend classes have taken the test.

Consider the following examples.

##  Example 20 

The unemployment rate in Catunah was greater than 10 percent for years.

Which of the following is an assumption necessary for arriving at the above conclusion?

1. [ ] (A) The government of Catunah will continue to offer free work skills training until the unemployment rate is at what is considered a normal level, 6 percent.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) The training offered by the government was designed to help people acquire key skills required for working at certain types of companies located in Catunah.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) The reason the unemployment rate in Catunah decreased is that many unemployed people left to find work in other countries.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) Some people who received the work skills training offered by the government of Catunah subsequently found employment.
5. [ ] (E) The unemployment rate in Catunah will decrease further if the government continues to offer free work skills training.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Let’s break down the argument.


<u>Premise</u>: The unemployment rate in Catunah was greater than 10 percent for years.


<u>Premise</u>: The government started offering free work skills training to unemployed people.


<u>Premise</u>: The unemployment has decreased to around 7 percent.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Helping people to acquire work skills facilitates their finding employment.


The premises indicate that the government offered work skills training and subsequently unemployment decreased. The conclusion is that helping people to gain work skills facilitates their finding employment.


Of course, there is a gap between the premises and the conclusion, as the argument does not clearly establish that the decrease in the unemployment rate was in fact related to the work skills training.


While it is likely that there was at least some connection between the two, the argument relies on the assumption that there was in fact such a connection.


In other words, the validity of this argument rests on the assumption that something that is likely true is actually true.


> <b>(A) The government of Catunah will continue to offer free work skills training until the unemployment rate is at what is considered a normal level, 6 percent.
</b>


 The argument is about the effects of work skills training and is based on evidence from the past. We don’t need to assume that the government will offer work skills training in the future in order to make an argument about the effect that offering work skills training had in the past.


> <b>(B) The training offered by the government was designed to help people acquire key skills required for working at certain types of companies located in Catunah.
</b>


 What this answer choice says may <em>explain</em> why it is that people who went through the training found employment. However, we are not looking for an explanation, and what this choice expresses is not an assumption that is necessary for arriving at the conclusion.


We don’t have to assume that the training was designed to help people gain key skills required for working at certain types of companies in order to go from the fact that work skills training was followed by a decrease in unemployment to the conclusion that helping people to develop work skills facilitates their finding employment.


In general, when we’re seeking an assumption upon which an argument rests, an answer choice that presents an explanation for one of the facts presented in the passage will not be the correct answer.


> <b>(C) The reason the unemployment rate in Catunah decreased is that many unemployed people left to find work in other countries.
</b>


 What this answer choice says actually weakens the argument.


If the reason Catunah’s unemployment rate decreased is that unemployed people left Catunah, then the government’s offering work skills training is not the reason why the unemployment rate decreased. In that case, the conclusion that helping people to acquire work skills facilitates their finding employment is not supported by the premises.


So, the argument definitely does not depend on what this answer choice says.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) Some people who received the work skills training offered by the government of Catunah subsequently found employment.
</b>


 We know that the unemployment rate decreased, and given what is said in the passage, and what we know about the real world, one could say that it seems likely that people who acquired work skills through the training found employment.


However, there is a difference between what is likely and what is factual. In order to go from the premises of this argument to the conclusion that helping people to acquire work skills facilitates their finding employment, one has to assume that some people who took part in the training did in fact find employment. This piece of missing information must be true in order for the logic of the argument to work. After all, what would happen to the argument if we learned that none of people who took part in the training did in fact find employment? The argument would no longer work – right?


Notice that this answer choice confirms that something that is likely true is actually true, a key assumption in this argument.


> <b>(E) The unemployment rate in Catunah will decrease further if the government continues to offer free work skills training.
</b>


 Even if, for some reason, it were not the case that the unemployment rate in Catunah would continue to decrease if the government were to continue to provide free work skills training, it could still be the case that the decrease in the unemployment rate that did occur resulted from the government’s offering free work skills training. So, what this choice expresses is not an assumption necessary for arriving at the argument’s conclusion.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Example 21 

Satellite Media, a new cellular phone service provider, will soon begin advertising heavily on the internet.

Which of the following is an assumption logically necessary for coming to the conclusion of the above argument?

1. [ ] (A) Satellite Media will put competing cellular phone service providers out of business.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) No residents of Brookville have land lines that they use for telephone service.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) Some of the residents of the town of Brookville use the internet.
4. [ ] (D) Satellite Media’s cellular phone plans cost less than the other cellular phone plans available to Brookville residents.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) Some residents of Brookville are happy with the quality of the cellular phone reception provided by the cellular phone service providers they currently use.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



Breakdown of the argument:


<u>Premise</u>: Satellite Media, a new cellular phone service provider, will soon begin advertising heavily on the internet.


<u>Premise</u>: Many residents of the town of Brookville have long complained about the poor cellular phone reception they experience.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Once Satellite Media begins its internet advertising campaign, residents of Brookville will begin switching their cellular phone service to plans provided by Satellite Media.


In going from the argument’s premises to the conclusion, the author is making a number of assumptions. For instance, does Satellite Media even provide cellular phone service in Brookville? The author is assuming that it does.


Of course, we don’t have to come up with any assumptions the author is making on our own. The assumption we have to find will be stated by the correct answer, and in this question, the correct answer states something that is likely true that the author has assumed is actually true.


> <b>(A) Satellite Media will put competing cellular phone service providers out of business.
</b>


This choice is tempting because it is clearly related to what the passage says. However, this choice is not correct because it’s not an assumption necessary for arriving at the argument’s conclusion. Instead, it’s a conclusion that is supported, at least somewhat, by what the passage says.


After all, if the argument’s conclusion that people in Brookville will switch to using Satellite Media’s cellular phone service is correct, then maybe others will switch to using Satellite Media as well. So, given what the passage says, it seems possible that, as this choice says, Satellite Media will put competing cellular phone service providers out of business.


Of course, we’re not looking for a conclusion supported by the passage. We’re looking for an assumption upon which the argument depends, and the conclusion that people in Brookville will start switching to using Satellite Media’s cellular phone service is supported by the premises regardless of whether Satellite Media will put other cellular phone service providers out of business.


In order to avoid choosing a choice like this one, we have to remember what we are looking for and not get tempted into choosing a choice just because it’s closely related to what the argument says.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B) No residents of Brookville have land lines that they use for telephone service.
</b>


Notice that the argument is about people switching their cellular phone service providers. Does the author have to assume that no residents of Brookville have land lines in order to conclude that people complaining about cellular reception will switch providers? Not at all. Some people could have land lines, and it would still make sense to conclude that, once people learn from advertising that there is a new cellular phone service provider, people who are not happy with their cellular phone reception will begin switching to using the new provider.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(C) Some of the residents of the town of Brookville use the internet.
</b>


 Notice that we’re told that Satellite Media will soon begin advertising heavily on the internet. Great. But what happens if none of the residents of Brookville use the internet? In this situation, the argument will be busted; it will fall apart, because the evidence that Satellite Media will soon begin advertising heavily on the internet will not support the conclusion.


Some are you are saying, “Of course some of the residents of Brookville use the internet.” However, that some of them do is not obvious at all. While it is likely true that some residents of Brookville use the internet, it is not guaranteed to be true.


Look back to the argument. Where do we see conclusive evidence that any of the residents of Brookville use the internet? Maybe none do, right? Although it is likely true that some of the residents of Brookville use the internet, that fact is not stated by the author.


So, without stating that some residents of Brookville use the internet, the author concludes that, once Satellite Media begins its internet advertising campaign, residents of Brookville will begin switching their cellular phone service to plans provided by Satellite Media.


Therefore, the author is assuming that at least some of the residents of the town of Brookville use the internet.


In general terms, we see that the author assumes that what is likely true is actually true.


> <b>(D) Satellite Media’s cellular phone plans cost less than the other cellular phone plans available to Brookville residents.
</b>


The author need not assume that Satellite Media’s cellular phone plans cost less than the other cellular phone plans available to Brookville residents.


After all, the author tells us that many residents of the town of Brookville have long complained about the poor cellular phone reception they experience. Of course, if the residents are unhappy with their cellular phone reception, they may decide to switch providers even if the plans they would switch to do not cost less than the plans they have.


So, even if Satellite Media’s cellular phone plans DO NOT cost less than the other cellular phone plans available to Brookville residents cost, the author’s conclusion could still follow from the premises.


> <b>(E) Some residents of Brookville are happy with the quality of the cellular phone reception provided by the cellular phone service providers they currently use.
</b>


 We’re told that that many residents of the town of Brookville have long complained about the poor cellular phone reception they experience. The author bases her conclusion on this fact.


Certainly, she need not assume that some residents of Brookville are happy with the quality of the cellular phone reception provided by the cellular phone service providers they currently use.


After all, her conclusion is that the residents who are not happy will switch providers. Of course, the premises would support that conclusion even if it is not the case that some residents of Brookville are happy with their cellular phone service.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

Let’s now discuss another type of assumption an author can make – that changes occurring over time don’t somehow undermine the connection between the evidence used in making an argument and the conclusion to that argument.
