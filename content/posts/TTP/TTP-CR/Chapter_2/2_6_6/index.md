## Lesson Content
Arguments about topics such as plans, predictions, or explanations can include elements that are from different time periods.

What all of these arguments have in common are assumptions that a conclusion about something occurring in one time period can be logically based on evidence derived from events in another time period.

For instance, in arriving at a conclusion about events that occurred a thousand years ago by observing certain conditions that exist today, one may be assuming that the relevant conditions that exist today are similar to conditions that existed a thousand years ago.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When an author uses data from one time period to support a conclusion about a different time period, she assumes that that data can actually be used to support her conclusion. That is, she assumes that things have not changed or will not change over time in a way that would invalidate her conclusion.
{{< /alert >}}

For example, imagine that in 1970 an author researching green technologies learned that, although they have tried hard for many years, scientists have not been able to create a battery that could store enough energy to power a car.

In making that argument, the author assumes that things will not change in such a way that at some point in the future scientists will be able to create a battery that stores enough energy to power a car.

Consider the following mini example:

> <em>Automobile Company CEO: The difficulty that we have had in selling the large cars that we make began when the price of fuel increased last year. Typically, when fuel prices have increased, people have switched to using smaller cars that use less fuel than our large cars use. Then, once fuel prices have decreased, people have switched back to using relatively large cars. So, once fuel prices decrease, our large cars will begin to sell again.</em>

> <em>Upon which of the following does the CEO rely in making the prediction?</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>Most people have not recently decided to permanently stop driving relatively large cars because of environmental concerns.</em>

In this example, the CEO makes a prediction that is based on patterns that occurred in the past.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In an Assumption question, an argument may depend on the assumption that a change occurring over time does not render the conclusion invalid.
{{< /alert >}}

Ok, now consider how the fact that things may have changed over time is related to the correct answer to the following question.

##  Example 22 

Ten years ago, the city of Verdantville began a major campaign to reduce violence.

Which of the following is logically necessary for arriving at the conclusion?

1. [x] (A) Ten years ago, people in Verdantville were not generally less likely to report acts of violence than they have been in more recent years.
2. [ ] (B) Similar campaigns in cities located near Verdantville have not resulted in reductions in numbers of reported acts of violence.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) The early childhood education program that was started five years ago in Verdantville has not had time to result in a reduction in violence.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) The number of police patrolling the streets of Verdantville is approximately the same today as it was ten years ago.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) If the campaign to reduce violence in Verdantville had not been undertaken, the number of acts of violence reported per year in Verdantville would have increased.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

##### Question Level: medium



In this argument, the fact that the number of acts of violence reported per year in Verdantville has remained approximately the same is taken as evidence that the campaign to reduce violence has not had any effect.


<u>Conclusion</u>: The campaign is having no effect on the amount of violence occurring in Verdantville.


There are, however, at least two gaps in this argument:


<u>Gap</u>: The evidence is about acts of violence reported, while the conclusion is about violence occurring. It could be that the number of acts of violence reported is not directly correlated with the amount of violence occurring.


<u>Gap</u>: The evidence used to support the conclusion compares the number of acts of violence reported ten years ago with the number reported today. Perhaps, such reporting is somehow different today from how it was ten years ago.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A) Ten years ago, people in Verdantville were not generally less likely to report acts of violence than they have been in more recent years.
</b>


 The conclusion is based on comparisons between the number of acts of violence reported for the year ten years ago and the numbers reported during the years that have followed. What if the proportion of acts of violence that are reported each year has changed over time, and, therefore, this comparison is faulty? If the proportion of acts of violence reported increased over the past ten years, then a decrease in the amount of violence occurring could result in no change in the number of acts of violence reported each year. So, the conclusion relies on the assumption that ten years ago people were not less likely to report acts of violence than they have been in recent years. In other words, if ten years ago people WERE less likely to report acts of violence than they have been in recent years, then the argument falls apart. Notice that this answer choice confirms that a change occurring over time does not render the conclusion invalid, a key assumption upon which this argument rests.


> <b>(B) Similar campaigns in cities located near Verdantville have not resulted in reductions in numbers of reported acts of violence.
</b>


 The fact that similar campaigns in other cities have not resulted in reductions in numbers of reported acts of violence could potentially be seen as adding slightly to the credibility of the conclusion that Verdantville’s campaign has not been effective.


However, we are looking not for more evidence to support the argument’s conclusion but, rather, for an assumption necessary for connecting the evidence that we already have to the argument’s conclusion.


In general, when we’re seeking an assumption upon which an argument rests, an answer choice that provides <em>new </em>support for the conclusion of the argument will not be correct.


> <b>(C) The early childhood education program that was started five years ago in Verdantville has not had time to result in a reduction in violence.
</b>


 What this choice says might help to explain why the program has not had an effect, if it indeed has not, but we are looking for an assumption, not an explanation. One doesn’t have to assume that a specific program has not yet had an effect in order to conclude that program as a whole has not affected the amount of violence occurring.


> <b>(D) The number of police patrolling the streets of Verdantville is approximately the same today as it was ten years ago.
</b>


 This choice could be tempting, as one might feel that, in order for the amount of violence occurring to remain at the same level, the number of police on the streets has to remain at the same level. We are not, however, looking for a reason why the amount of violence occurring didn’t change. We are looking for an assumption necessary for coming to the conclusion given the evidence already presented. In general, when we’re seeking an assumption upon which an argument rests, an answer choice that presents an explanation for one of the facts presented in the passage will not be the correct answer.


> <b>(E) If the campaign to reduce violence in Verdantville had not been undertaken, the number of acts of violence reported per year in Verdantville would have increased.
</b>


 Given what this choice says about an increase in violence, it could seem to be in line with what the argument says. However, careful analysis of this choice reveals that it weakens the argument.


The evidence presented in the argument seems to indicate that the amount of violence occurring has not increased. If it were really true, as this choice says, that the amount of violence occurring would have increased had the campaign not been undertaken, then the fact that the amount of violence occurring did not increase indicates that the campaign has been successful in at least keeping the amount of violence from increasing, which conclusion is counter to the conclusion that the campaign has had no effect on the amount of violence occurring. In general, when we’re seeking an assumption upon which an argument rests, an answer choice that says something that weakens the argument will not be correct.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

Now that we are more familiar with some types of assumptions that we might see, let’s talk about a key aspect of many of them, how inconspicuous they can be.
