## Lesson Content
One thing that often surprises people is how inconspicuous assumptions can be.

For example:

> <em>Henrietta bakes the best apple pies in Clay County. The best apple pie entries win the Clay County Southern Charm baking contest every year without fail. Therefore, Henrietta’s apple pie will win the Southern Charm baking contest this year.</em>

Let’s look at the structure of the argument:

> <u>Premise</u>: Henrietta’s apples pies are the best apple pies.

> <u>Premise</u>: The best apple pie entries always win the contest.

> <u>Conclusion</u>: Henrietta will win the contest.

If, after reading that argument, you said, “Of course Henrietta’s apple pie will win the Southern Charm baking contest this year,” you unconsciously (or maybe even consciously) filled in one major gap in the argument.

The problem with filling these gaps in as we read is that it becomes harder for us to spot assumptions, so when reading we need to be aware of gaps in arguments.

After all, she can have the best pies in the world, but if she does not enter them in the contest, she won’t win.

So, in reality, the argument works this way:

> <u>Premise</u>: Henrietta’s apples pies are the best apple pies.

> <u>Premise</u>: The best apple pie entries always win the contest.

> <u>Assumption</u>: Henrietta will enter her apple pies in the contest.

> <u>Conclusion</u>: Henrietta will win the contest.

Simply put, the author of the argument (and, perhaps, you), took for granted that Henrietta would actually <em>enter</em> one of her apple pies into the Southern Charm baking contest! Her pies may be known far and wide as the best in the county, but she won’t win if she doesn’t enter.

Now – stop right here.

Upon closer inspection, we can see that this “small” assumption – that Henrietta will actually enter the contest is not so small after all.

This next mini example will now be easier:

> <em>A TV show holds a televised talent contest for all eligible singers. Monica has a beautiful soprano voice. So, she will likely make it onto the show. </em>

The conclusion is that Monica will make it onto the show, and this conclusion is based on the evidence that the contest showcases eligible singers.

Yes, there is.

Assumptions made in GMAT arguments are often inconspicuous.

So, an assumption is not always information connected to an argument in a fascinating, logically complex way.

##  Example 23 

The boom in the freshly brewed tea business in Krztanza will end when the consumer learns that a serving of Krztanzian style freshly brewed tea often contains an amount of caffeine equivalent to that in up to eight cans of a popular energy drink and has as many calories as does an entire meal.

Which of the following is an assumption upon which the argument depends?

1. [ ] (A) Consumers in Krztanza do not mind high levels of caffeine and high calorie counts in their freshly brewed coffee.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) Although Krztanzian style freshly brewed tea is less healthy than packaged tea, it has become very popular.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) Consumers in Krztanza do not prefer high calorie beverages that contain high levels of caffeine.
4. [ ] (D) Krztanzian style freshly brewed tea could be improved via reducing its levels of calories and caffeine.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) Ultimately, the consumption of tea in Krztanza will decrease because packaged tea contains fewer calories and caffeine.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

##### Question Level: easy



Breakdown of the argument:


<u>Premise</u>: Packaged tea contains less caffeine and has fewer calories than does Krztanzian style freshly brewed tea and is available in most grocery stores and even in many stores where freshly brewed coffee is sold.


<u>Conclusion</u>: The boom in the freshly brewed tea business in Krztanza will end when the consumer learns that a serving of Krztanzian style freshly brewed tea often contains an amount of caffeine equivalent to that in up to eight cans of a popular energy drink and has as many calories as does an entire meal.


The reasoning of the argument is that, since packaged tea contains less caffeine and has fewer calories than Krztanzian style freshly brewed tea, and since packaged tea is widely available, the boom in the freshly brewed tea business in Krztanza will end when people find out about the high caffeine and caloric levels of Krztanzian style freshly brewed tea.


However, while, given the way it is structured and worded, the argument seems to imply that people want to avoid caffeine and calories, it never actually says that consumers prefer beverages with relatively low caffeine and caloric levels over beverages with high caffeine and caloric levels. After all, what if people DO NOT prefer beverages with relatively low caffeine and caloric levels over beverages with high caffeine and caloric levels? In such a case, the argument falls apart.


> <b>(A) Consumers in Krztanza do not mind high levels of caffeine and high calorie counts in their freshly brewed coffee.
</b>


The wording of this choice makes it seem somehow relevant to the argument. However, Critical Reasoning is not a word matching game, and the fact that a choice seems to match what the argument says does not make that choice correct.


In fact, what we really need to notice about this choice is that its effect is basically the opposite of what we need in order to complete the argument.


The fact that people do not mind high levels of caffeine and calories in their coffee is in conflict with the conclusion that they will reduce their consumption of Krztanzian style freshly brewed tea once they become aware of its caffeine and calorie levels. After all, if they do not mind high levels of calories and caffeine in their coffee, then it’s quite possible that they don’t mind high levels of calories and caffeine in general.


In general, when we’re seeking an assumption upon which an argument rests, an answer choice that says something that weakens the argument will not be correct.


> <b>(B) Although Krztanzian style freshly brewed tea is less healthy than packaged tea, it has become very popular.
</b>


 This choice adds virtually nothing to the argument. From what we the argument says, we already have reason to believe that freshly brewed tea has become very popular.


If we were looking for a conclusion supported by what the passage says, this choice might work. However, we are looking for an assumption integral to the argument, not a conclusion.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) Consumers in Krztanza do not prefer high calorie beverages that contain high levels of caffeine.
</b>


The author must assume what this choice says.


Notice that, if, contrary to what this choice say, people in Krztanza DO prefer high caffeine beverages that are high in calories, then learning that Krztanzian style freshly brewed tea is higher in caffeine and calories than packaged tea won’t cause them to reduce their consumption of freshly brewed tea.


Thus, if consumers in Krztanza DO prefer high calorie beverages that contain high levels of caffeine, then the evidence that packaged tea contains less caffeine and has fewer calories than Krztanzian style freshly brewed tea does not support the conclusion, and the argument falls part.


So, the argument works only if what this choice says is true.


Notice how, even though what this choice says is rather basic and is obviously integral to the argument, it still constitutes an assumption without which the logic of the argument falls apart.


> <b>(D) Krztanzian style freshly brewed tea could be improved via reducing its levels of calories and caffeine.
</b>


Concluding that the boom in the freshly brewed tea business in Krztanza will end does not require assuming that freshly brewed tea could be improved.


In fact, if it is the case that freshly brewed tea could be improved, then perhaps tea makers will improve freshly brewed tea and people will continue to consume it, or even increase their consumption of it, in which case, the boom will not end.


> <b>(E) Ultimately, the consumption of tea in Krztanza will decrease because packaged tea contains fewer calories and caffeine.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, because the argument’s conclusion is that the boom in the freshly brewed tea business will end, and this choice is about a decrease in tea consumption.


Notice, however, that the point of the argument is not that tea in general will be consumed less. The point is that people will consume less Krztanzian style freshly brewed tea once they find out about its high caffeine and calorie levels.


So, the argument could still work even if, contrary to what this choice says, the overall consumption of tea in Krztanza will NOT decrease, as long as consumption of Krztanzian style freshly brewed tea in particular will decrease.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

Let’s now consider the use of limiting words, such as <em>“except”</em> or <em>“not,”</em> in question stems.
