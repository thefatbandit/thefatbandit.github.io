## Lesson Content
Assumption questions will sometimes include in their question stems the word “except” or “not.” The use of question stems that use such words can make questions a bit more challenging.

The following two question stems are examples of Assumption question stems that contain “except” or “not”:

> <em>The logic of the argument above depends on all of the following EXCEPT:</em>

> <em>Which of the following is <u>not</u> an assumption on which the argument depends?</em>

When an Assumption question has the word “except” or “not” in the stem, four out of the five answer choices will be assumptions integral to the argument.

The correct answer will be the one piece of information that <em>does not</em> supply a link between the evidence and the conclusion.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When an Assumption question has the word “except” or “not” in the stem, four out of the five answer choices will be assumptions integral to the argument. These answer choices will be incorrect. The correct answer will be the choice that does not link the evidence and the conclusion; the correct answer will not be an assumption upon which the argument depends.
{{< /alert >}}

Since an “except” or “not” question asks us to do the opposite of what we normally do in answering an assumption question, in order to avoid becoming confused when answering such a question, we can mark each of the choices with a “Yes,” if the choice states an assumption integral to the argument, or with a “No,” if what the choice says is not an assumption integral to the argument.

##  Example 24 

In order to reduce the number of vehicles traveling over the speed limit, the Town of Commerce recently installed on its highways automated cameras that detect speeding vehicles.

Each of the following, if true, is an assumption upon which the argument depends EXCEPT:

1. [ ] (A) The number of vehicles using the highways in Commerce has not significantly declined since the introduction of the cameras.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) Drivers of vehicles on the highways of Commerce do not slow to the speed limit when approaching the cameras, and then exceed the speed limit after passing the cameras.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) The cameras recently installed to detect speeding vehicles on the highways in Commerce have not critically malfunctioned since their installation.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) Most drivers who speed on highways in Commerce have not changed their habits recently, going from doing most of their driving during daylight hours to doing most of it at night.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) The population of the Town of Commerce has not significantly increased over the period of time since the cameras to detect speeding vehicles were installed.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



The argument is pretty simple.


Commerce recently installed on its highways automated cameras that detect speeding vehicles. Since the introduction of the cameras, the number of speeding vehicles detected each day by the cameras has sharply declined.


The author concludes from that evidence that the presence of the cameras is deterring from speeding those drivers who would otherwise speed.


Notice that the author makes a cause-and-effect claim. She concludes that the installation of the cameras is causing the decrease in the number of speeding vehicles detected each day.


Whenever an author makes a cause-and-effect claim, he or she assumes that there cannot be an alternative explanation for the observation made. In other words, in making this argument, the author assumes that there is not an alternative explanation for the decrease in the number of speeding vehicles detected each day. In her mind, the only explanation is the one she has posited.


Now, because this is an “EXCEPT” question, four of the five choices <em>will</em> be assumptions necessary in order for the premises of the argument to support the argument’s conclusion.


The correct answer will be the choice on which the argument does <em>not</em> depend.


> <b>(A) The number of vehicles using the highways in Commerce has not significantly declined since the introduction of the cameras.
</b>


 What this answer choice says is an assumption upon which the argument depends.


If, contrary to what this choice says, the number of vehicles using the highways in Commerce HAS significantly declined since the introduction of the cameras, then we have a pretty darn good alternative explanation for the decline in the number of speeding vehicles detected each day by the cameras.


After all, it makes sense that, as the number of vehicles using the highways declines, we’ll see a decline in the number of speeding vehicles.


Therefore, in making her argument, the author must assume that the number of vehicles using the highways in Commerce HAS NOT significantly declined since the introduction of the cameras. Otherwise, how could she make the claim that the cameras are deterring drivers from speeding?


Necessary assumption? Yes.


> <b>(B) Drivers of vehicles on the highways of Commerce do not slow to the speed limit when approaching the cameras, and then exceed the speed limit after passing the cameras.
</b>


What this answer choice says is an assumption upon which the argument depends.


If, contrary to what this choice says, drivers of vehicles on the highways of Commerce DO slow to the speed limit when approaching the cameras, and then exceed the speed limit after passing the cameras, then we have a pretty darn good alternative explanation for the decline in the number of speeding vehicles detected each day by the cameras.


After all, in that case, there has not really been a decline in the number of vehicles speeding. Instead, the vehicles speed on the highways until they approach the cameras, at which point they slow down. Once past the cameras, the cars resume speeding.


Therefore, in making her argument, the author must assume that drivers on the highways of Commerce DO NOT slow to the speed limit when approaching the cameras, and then exceed the speed limit after passing the cameras. Otherwise, how could she make the claim that the cameras are deterring drivers from speeding?


Necessary assumption? Yes.


> <b>(C) The cameras recently installed to detect speeding vehicles on the highways in Commerce have not critically malfunctioned since their installation.
</b>


 What this answer choice says is an assumption upon which the argument depends.


If, contrary to what this choice says, the cameras recently installed to detect speeding vehicles on the highways in Commerce HAVE critically malfunctioned since their installation, then we have a pretty darn good alternative explanation for the decline in the number of speeding vehicles detected each day by the cameras: the cameras don’t work. After all, if the cameras don’t work, how can they detect speeding vehicles?


Therefore, in making her argument, the author must assume that the recently installed cameras HAVE NOT critically malfunctioned since their installation. Otherwise, how could she make the claim that the cameras are deterring drivers from speeding?


Necessary assumption? Yes.


> <b>(D) Most drivers who speed on highways in Commerce have not changed their habits recently, going from doing most of their driving during daylight hours to doing most of it at night.
</b>


 What this answer choice says is an assumption upon which the argument rests.


Notice that the passage tells us that the cameras cannot detect speeding vehicles at night. So, what if, contrary to what this choice says, most drivers who speed on highways in Commerce HAVE changed their habits, going from doing most of their driving during daylight hours to doing most of it at night?


In that case, we’d have a pretty darn good alternative explanation for the decline in the number of speeding vehicles detected each day by the cameras: most drivers who speed switched to driving at night, a time when the cameras can’t detect speeding vehicles.


Therefore, in making her argument, the author must assume that most drivers who speed on highways in Commerce HAVE NOT changed their habits from doing most of their driving during daylight hours to doing most of it at night. Otherwise, how could she make the claim that the presence of the cameras is deterring drivers from speeding?


Necessary assumption? Yes.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E) The population of the Town of Commerce has not significantly increased over the period of time since the cameras to detect speeding vehicles were installed.
</b>


 What this choice says is NOT an assumption upon which the argument depends.


What if, contrary to what this choice says, the population of the Town of Commerce HAS significantly increased over the period of time since the cameras were installed? How would that fact affect the validity of the argument?


If anything, that fact would indicate that, since there are more people in Commerce, there are likely more cars on the highways in Commerce than there were when the cameras were installed. If there are likely more cars on the highways, we would expect there to be more speeding vehicles on the highways, but the number of speeding vehicles detected each day by the cameras has declined.


So, if what this choice says were NOT true, we would have even more reason to suspect that something, such as the cameras, is deterring drivers from speeding.


So, what this choice says is certainly not an assumption upon which the argument depends.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Example 25 

Fish that swim in water containing PCBs, which are chemicals toxic to many lifeforms, absorb PCBs via their gills.

Each of the following is an assumption upon which the argument depends EXCEPT:

1. [ ] (A) Fish cannot take in PCBs by feeding on fish that have previously inhabited water containing PCBs.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) Fish do not manufacture PCBs internally via natural processes.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) Plants that fish consume do not absorb PCBs from the soil in which they grow.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) Fish in which PCBs have built up in fatty tissues do not represent only a small proportion of fish that have inhabited water containing PCBs.
5. [ ] (E) PCBs are never passed from a mother fish that has absorbed PCBs to her young.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



<u>Premise</u>: Fish that swim in water containing PCBs, which are chemicals toxic to many lifeforms, absorb PCBs via their gills.


<u>Premise</u>: Having entered fish’s systems, PCBs can build up in the fatty tissues of fish that swim in such water.


<u>Conclusion</u>: If PCBs are present in a fish’s fatty tissues, it must be the case that that fish has inhabited water containing PCBs.


Notice that the argument jumps from the premise that PCBs can build up in the fatty tissues of fish that swim in water containing PCBs to the conclusion that, if PCBs are present in a fish’s fatty tissues, it must be the case that that fish has inhabited water containing PCBs.


Making that jump requires assuming that the only way in which PCBs can get into the fatty tissues of fish is through fish’s inhabiting water containing PCBs.


After all, if there are other ways in which PCBs could get into the fatty tissues of fish, then it’s possible that PCBs could be present in a fish’s fatty tissues even if that fish did not inhabit water containing PCBs. In that case it would not make sense to conclude that, if PCBs are present in a fish’s fatty tissues, it <em>must</em> be the case that that fish has inhabited water containing PCBs.


So, without the assumption that only way in which PCBs can get into the fatty tissues of fish is through fish’s inhabiting water containing PCBs, the argument falls apart.


Now - because this is an “EXCEPT” question, four of the five answer choices <em>will</em> be assumptions that will be necessary in order for the premises of the argument to support the argument’s conclusion. These will be incorrect choices. The correct answer will be the answer choice that the argument does <em>not</em> depend on.


> <b>(A) Fish cannot take in PCBs by feeding on fish that have previously inhabited water containing PCBs.
</b>


What this answer choice says is an assumption upon which the argument rests.


The conclusion is that, if PCBs are present in a fish’s fatty tissues, it must be the case that that fish has inhabited water containing PCBs. In order for the presence of PCBs to be <em>sufficient </em>evidence that a has fish inhabited such water, there have to be no ways other than inhabiting such water for a fish to take in PCBs.


Therefore, in order for the argument to work, it must be true that fish cannot take in PCBs by feeding on fish that have previously inhabited water containing PCBs. After all, imagine what would happen if fish CAN take in PCBs by feeding on fish that have previously inhabited water containing PCBs. The argument would fall apart.


Necessary assumption? Yes.


> <b>(B) Fish do not manufacture PCBs internally via natural processes.
</b>


 What this answer choice says is an assumption upon which the argument rests.


The conclusion is that, if PCBs are present in a fish’s fatty tissues, it must be the case that that fish has inhabited water containing PCBs. In order for the presence of PCBs to be <em>sufficient </em>evidence that a fish has inhabited such water, there have to be no ways other than through a fish’s inhabiting such water for PCBs to build up in a fish’s fatty tissues.


Therefore, in order for the argument to work, it must be true that fish do not manufacture PCBs internally via natural processes. After all, imagine what would happen if fish DO manufacture PCBs internally via natural processes. The argument would fall apart.


Necessary assumption? Yes.


> <b>(C) Plants that fish consume do not absorb PCBs from the soil in which they grow.
</b>


What this answer choice says is an assumption upon which the argument rests.


The conclusion is that, if PCBs are present in a fish’s fatty tissues, it must be the case that that fish has inhabited water containing PCBs. In order for the presence of PCBs to be <em>sufficient </em>evidence that a fish has inhabited such water, there have to be no ways other than inhabiting such water for fish to take in PCBs.


Therefore, in order for the argument to work, it must be true that plants that fish consume do not absorb PCBs from the soil in which they grow. After all, imagine what would happen if plants that fish consume DO absorb PCBs from the soil in which they grow. The argument would fall apart.


Necessary assumption? Yes.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) Fish in which PCBs have built up in fatty tissues do not represent only a small proportion of fish that have inhabited water containing PCBs.
</b>


This choice may seem to be an assumption necessary for arriving at the conclusion. One might think something along the lines of, “If this statement were not true, and it were the case that fish in which PCBs have built up fatty tissues DO represent a only a small proportion of fish that have inhabited water containing PCBs, then the presence of PCBs in a fish’s fatty tissues would not necessarily indicate that that fish has inhabited water containing PCBs.”


Notice, however, that what this choice says does not have to be true in order for the presence of PCBs in a fish’s fatty tissues to indicate that that fish has inhabited water containing PCBs.


Yes, if it were the case that fish in which PCBs have built up fatty tissues DO represent a only a small proportion of fish that have inhabited water containing PCBs, then an <em>absence</em> of PCBs in a fish’s fatty tissues would not reliably indicate that that fish <em>has not inhabited</em> water containing PCBs.


However, even in such a case, the <em>presence</em> of PCBs in a fish’s fatty tissues could indicate that that fish <em>has inhabited</em> water containing PCBs.


Necessary assumption? No.


> <b>(E) PCBs are never passed from a mother fish that has absorbed PCBs to her young.
</b>


What this answer choice says is an assumption upon which the argument depends. This choice, like choices (A), (B), and (C), eliminates an alternative way in which PCBs could become present in a fish’s fatty tissues. After all, if PCBs ARE sometimes passed from a mother fish that has absorbed PCBs to her young, then the argument falls apart.


Necessary assumption? Yes.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
