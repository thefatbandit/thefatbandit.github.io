## Lesson Content
At this point, you know well that an assumption is an unstated piece of information that must be true in order for the given evidence to lead to the conclusion.

> <em>After all, what would happen to the argument if we were to discover that there WERE other ways in which a person could become exposed to Compound X? In such a case, the argument would fall apart – right? </em>

> <em>In other words, what would happen to the argument if we were to discover that NO people who gained work skills through the training subsequently found employment? The argument would fall apart – right? </em>

> <em>After all, if these lights WILL somehow aid breaking into cars, the plan will not work because the street lights will be making the problem that they are intended to help solve worse.</em>

What have we been doing in these situations?

Once we’ve found the assumption on which an argument depends, we’ve been showing you – confirming for you – that if that assumption were NOT true (if the assumption were false), the argument would fall apart.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Because an assumption is a piece of missing information that MUST BE true for the logic of an argument to work, if we were to find an assumption upon which an argument depends to be false, the argument would no longer work.
{{< /alert >}}

Why is all of this important? It’s important because we can use this understanding of how assumptions work as a powerful tool for finding the assumption upon which the argument depends.

How it works is simple.

On the other hand, if we negate a potential assumption and the argument is no worse for the wear (it’s unaffected), then that potential assumption must not have been necessary.

To see negation in action, let’s revisit our argument about disease x:

> <em>All people in houses in which communicable diseases are present should be quarantined. Two people in the yellow house on Maple Street came down with disease x yesterday. Therefore, all people in the yellow house on Maple Street should be quarantined.</em>

> <em>Which</em> of the following is an assumption upon which the argument depends?

> <em>(A) Disease x is a communicable disease. </em>

> <em>(B) People should be quarantined only when they have a communicable disease.</em>

We already know that the author assumes that disease x is a communicable disease.

Here’s how we can use the negation technique.

> <em><u>Choice (A)</u></em><em>:</em> <em>Disease x is a communicable disease.</em>

> <em><u>Negated Choice (A)</u></em><em>:</em> <em>Disease x is <strong>NOT</strong> a communicable disease.</em>

If the negated choice (A) were true, the argument would fall apart.

Let’s look now at choice (B) and negate it:

> <em><u>Choice (B)</u></em><em>:</em> <em>People should be quarantined only when they have a communicable disease.</em>

> <em><u>Negated Choice (B)</u></em><em>:</em> <em>It is <strong>NOT </strong>the case that</em> <em>people should be quarantined only when they have a communicable disease.</em>

Does this negation of choice (B) weaken the support for the conclusion? No, not at all, because, even if it is NOT the case that people should be quarantined only when they have a communicable disease, the conclusion that, all people in the yellow house on Maple Street should be quarantined could still make sense.

Let’s have another look at Monica and her beautiful voice:

> <em>A TV show holds a televised talent contest for all eligible singers. Monica has a beautiful soprano voice, so she will likely make it onto the show. </em>

Remember we said that the necessary assumption here is that Monica must be eligible for the TV show.

Think of it this way.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

If we want to tell whether something is necessary, then try getting rid of it. If the argument falls apart in its absence, that thing was necessary for the argument to be sound.
{{< /alert >}}

Here’s the four-step approach to successfully use the negation technique.

<strong>Step 1: Break down the Argument.</strong> Do so just as you would with any Critical Reasoning question, making sure to identify the conclusion and evidence.

<strong>Step 2: Write down the negation of each answer.</strong> It doesn’t have to be a complete sentence; shorthand is efficient and works well.

> - Negate the main verb (e.g.

> - Negate a quantity word (e.g.

> - Get rid of a “not” (e.g.

> - Simply put “it is not the case that” before the statement.

<strong>Step 3: Eliminate choices that<em> do not hurt</em> the conclusion when negated</strong>.

<strong>Step 4: Choose the answer that most specifically hurts the Argument.</strong> If we are down to two choices, we re-read the evidence and the conclusion again.

(Occasionally, the negation test may leave you with two choices the negations of which seem equally damaging to the conclusion.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When we negate the answer choices in an Assumption question, the negated version of the correct answer will destroy the argument.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 26 

MowGreen, a company that uses sustainable methods to maintain lawns, has been expanding quickly, and its services are now available in many areas of the country.

Which of the following is logically necessary for coming to the conclusion of the above argument?

1. [x] (A) MowGreen’s management team considers profitability in determining whether to expand into an area.
2. [ ] (B) People in the Drawnville area are not interested in using sustainable methods for maintaining their lawns.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) There remain in the country areas in which MowGreen is not currently operating into which it could profitably expand its operations.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) MowGreen operates profitably in all of the areas in which it currently operates.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) Sustainable methods for maintaining lawns are generally more expensive to use than are less environmentally friendly methods.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Breakdown of the Argument:


<u>Background</u>: MowGreen has been expanding, and its services are available in many areas.


<u>Premise</u>: MowGreen would not be able to operate profitably in the area around Drawnville.


<u>Conclusion</u>: One can be sure that MowGreen will not expand into the area around Drawnville any time soon.


There is a gap in the argument. The premise says that MowGreen would not be able to operate profitably in the Drawnville area. The conclusion says that MowGreen will not expand into the Drawnville area. What’s missing here? What must be true in order for the conclusion to be properly drawn from the premise? The correct answer to the question will fill in the gap between “would not be able to operate profitably in the area” and “will not expand into the area.”


Let’s look at choice (A) and negate it:


MowGreen’s management team <strong>DOES NOT</strong> consider profitability in determining whether to expand into an area.


If this negation were true, the argument would fall apart. Thus, choice (A) presents an assumption necessary to the argument. This is the correct answer.


Now, let’s negate each of the incorrect choices.


Negated (B): People in the area around Drawnville <strong>ARE</strong> interested in using sustainable methods for maintaining their lawns.


Negated (C): There <strong>NO LONGER</strong> remain in the country areas in which MowGreen is not currently operating into which it could profitably expand its operations.


Negated (D): MowGreen <strong>DOES NOT</strong> operate profitably in all of the areas in which it currently operates.


Negated (E): Sustainable methods for maintaining lawns are <strong>NOT</strong> generally more expensive to use than are less environmentally friendly methods.


We can see that none of the negated versions of these incorrect options weaken the argument. Thus, none of these incorrect options is an assumption that is necessary to this argument.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

##  Example 27 

While the practice of allowing members of the public to donate to political campaigns may have corrupting effects, if candidates for elected office were not allowed to accept donations for covering campaign expenses, only the wealthy would have the financial resources generally necessary for winning elections, and people of more modest financial means would rarely be elected.

Which of the following is an assumption necessary for arriving at the above conclusion?

1. [ ] (A) Wealthy people generally have unfair financial advantages over people of more modest personal financial means.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) People of relatively modest financial means never become wealthy and, thus, able to finance their own election campaigns.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) Wealthy people elected to public offices will not safeguard the interests of citizens of modest financial means.
4. [ ] (D) The costs of running an election campaign have been increasing over time.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) A corrupt person of modest financial means will, if elected to public office, safeguard the interests of citizens of modest financial means.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



The argument is based on the following premise:


<u>Premise</u>: If candidates for elected office were not allowed to accept donations for covering campaign expenses, only the wealthy would have the financial resources generally necessary for winning elections, and people of modest financial means would rarely be elected.


It jumps from those premises to the following conclusion:


<u>Conclusion</u>: Donations to campaigns must be allowed in order to facilitate the election of people who will safeguard the interests of citizens of modest financial means.


There is a gap between the premise and the conclusion, as the argument jumps from evidence that is focused on people’s levels of financial means to a conclusion about safeguarding the interests of citizens of modest financial means, without making any sort of clear connection between a person’s level of financial means and whether that person will safeguard the interests of citizens of modest financial means.


Now, let’s negate each choice to see which is an assumption integral to the argument.


(A) <u>Negated</u>: Wealthy people generally <strong>DO NOT </strong>have unfair financial advantages over people of more modest personal financial means.


<strong>Incorrect. </strong>This statement does not destroy the argument.


Even if wealthy people do not have “unfair” financial advantages, the evidence presented, that, if candidates for elected office were not allowed to accept donations for covering campaign expenses, only the wealthy would have the financial resources generally necessary for winning elections, could still support the conclusion that donating to campaigns must be allowed in order to facilitate the election of people who will safeguard the interests of people of modest financial means.


(B) <u>Negated</u>: People of relatively modest financial means<strong> sometimes</strong> become wealthy and, thus, able to finance their own election campaigns.


<strong>Incorrect. </strong>This statement does not destroy the argument.


Even if people of modest financial means sometimes become wealthy, it could still be the case that the wealthy would win most elections if campaign donations were not allowed and that, therefore, donations to campaigns must be allowed in order to facilitate the election of people who will safeguard the interests of citizens of modest financial means.


After all, if a person of modest financial means becomes wealthy and, thus able to finance an election campaign, that person is no longer a person of modest financial means.


(C) <u>Negated</u>: Wealthy people elected to public offices <strong>WILL <s>not</s> </strong>safeguard interests of citizens of modest financial means.


<strong>Correct.</strong> This statement destroys the argument.


If wealthy people WILL safeguard the interests of citizens of modest financial means, then, even if wealthy people would be the only ones to have the financial resources necessary for winning elections, people who would safeguard the interests of citizens of modest financial means could be elected.


So, if what this choice says is NOT true, the conclusion is not supported and the argument falls apart. Thus, this choice must state an assumption upon which the argument depends.


(D) <u>Negated</u>: The costs of running an election campaign have <strong>NOT </strong>been increasing over time.


<strong>Incorrect. </strong>This statement does not affect the argument.


The conclusion of the argument is not based on the trend in the costs of running an election campaign. As long as significant financial resources are required for winning an election, the argument makes sense.


(E) <u>Negated</u>: A corrupt person of modest financial means will <strong>NOT</strong>, if elected to public office, safeguard the interests of citizens of modest financial means.


<strong>Incorrect. </strong>This statement does not affect the argument.


The conclusion is not that any person of modest financial means elected to office will safeguard the interests of citizens of modest financial means.


In other words, while the argument suggests that allowing donating to campaigns is <em>necessary </em>for enabling the election of someone who will safeguard the interests of citizens of modest financial means, it does not suggest that allowing donating to campaigns is <em>sufficient </em>for achieving that end.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)
