## Lesson Content
To find the correct answer to a Weaken question, follow this 6-step strategy.

<u>Step 1 </u><u>–</u><u> Carefully read and understand the passage.</u>

<strong>The foundation of arriving at the correct answer to a Weaken question is understanding the passage.</strong>

The importance of understanding the passage cannot be overemphasized.

So, your first job in answering a Weaken question is to carefully read the passage, developing understanding of what the passage says and noting key details of the passage.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The foundation for arriving at the correct answer to a Weaken question is understanding the passage.
{{< /alert >}}

<u>Step 2 </u><u>–</u><u> Read the question stem and identify what it is asking you to do.</u>

Next, read the question stem.

If the question is asking you to weaken the argument or to undermine the support for or call into question the conclusion, it’s a Weaken the Argument question.

<u>Step 3 – Go back to the passage and determine exactly what the conclusion of the argument is.</u>

At this point, you understand the passage and know that you have to weaken the argument.

Of course, the correct answer will be information that undermines the support for the conclusion or otherwise serves to call the conclusion into question.

So, the next step is to go back to the passage and identify exactly what the conclusion is.

<strong>Identifying the exact conclusion of the argument is possibly the single most crucial step you will take in answering a Weaken question.</strong>

If you have only a vague idea of what the conclusion is, you can easily fall for trap choices that weaken the support for the wrong conclusion.

So, identify exactly what the conclusion is.

<u>Step 4 – Identify how the conclusion is supported.</u>

Generally, the correct answer to a Weaken question will attack the connection between the premises and the conclusion or otherwise undermine the support for the conclusion.

So, of course, the next key step in answering a Weaken question is identifying the premises that support the conclusion and determining how they support it.

<u>Step 5 </u><u>–</u><u> Go through the answer choices one by one, seeking to remove “strengtheners” and choices that do not affect the argument.</u>

Usually in a Weaken question, there are only two reasonably “good” answer choices.

So, the next step in answering a Weaken question is to remove the easiest-to-eliminate choices.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When evaluating the answer options in Weaken the Argument questions, it’s important to remove answer options that either strengthen the conclusion or have no impact on the conclusion.
{{< /alert >}}

Now – it’s important that we leave room for the fact that upon our initial analysis, we can sometimes eliminate the correct answer.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Just as it is important to eliminate answers that we feel are incorrect, we must remain flexible, in that we may need to revisit answer options that we have eliminated.
{{< /alert >}}

<u>Step 6 </u><u>–</u><u> Choose the answer choice with the clearest weakening impact.</u>

Once you have eliminated the answer choices that either strengthen the argument or rather obviously do not affect the argument, in most case, you will be left with one or two choices.

If you find yourself left with two answer choices, both of which seem to have a deleterious effect on the support for conclusion, ask yourself, “Which one weakens the support for the conclusion <em>the most clearly</em>?”

There should be a recognizable difference between the effect on the argument of the correct answer and the effect of the second-best choice.

Look for subtle differences in logic.

The logic of the relationship between the second-best choice and the argument will be such that, even though the second-best choice may seem to weaken the argument, it does not really.

In contrast, generally there will be a tight logic to why, unlike any of the other answer choices, the correct choice answers the question.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When answering a Weaken question, once you are down to two final contenders for the correct answer, choose the one with the greatest and clearest negative impact on the support for the argument’s conclusion.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 5 

Three years ago, the town of Amity renewed two blocks on its waterfront by replacing abandoned warehouses with green, retail, and restaurant space, with the result that the town was able to collect substantial sales taxes from the new businesses.

Which of the following, if true, would most seriously weaken the argument?

1. [ ] (A) If property tax rates in Amity were increased slightly, the resulting additional revenue would exceed the revenue currently collected in sales taxes from the renewal area businesses.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) Most of the restaurants in the waterfront area are filled to capacity every weekend.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) The volume of restaurant and retail business decreases when the national economy slows down.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) In the nearby town of Friendship, neither retail business nor restaurant business has ever been very strong.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) Since the completion of the first renewal project, it has become common for all streets leading to and in Amity’s waterfront area to be congested with slow moving traffic.

##### Question Level: hard



<u>Step 1 </u><u>–</u><u> Carefully read and understand the passage.</u>


We can see that the passage presents an argument and that the reasoning of the argument is the following.


Because the two-block waterfront renewal project done three years ago resulted in sales tax revenue and there are two more blocks available for renewal, the town can double sales tax revenue collected from waterfront renewal area businesses by renewing the other two blocks.


<u>Step 2 </u><u>–</u><u> Read the question stem and identify what it is asking you to do.</u>


We see that the question stem is “Which of the following, if true, would most seriously weaken the argument?”


So, this is a Weaken the Argument question, and our task is to undermine the support for or call into question the conclusion.


<u>Step 3 – Go back to the passage and determine exactly what the conclusion of the argument is.</u>


We can see that the statements in the passage lead up to and support the last statement in the passage:


<u>Conclusion</u>: The town of Amity can, by renewing the two available blocks, double the amount of sales tax revenue it will receive from businesses in waterfront renewal areas.


So, that last statement is the conclusion of the argument.


Notice that we are using the exact conclusion in its entirety, rather than a paraphrase or summary of the conclusion. Even leaving out a few words, such as “in waterfront renewal areas,” could result in our choosing an incorrect choice.


<u>Step 4 – Identify how the conclusion is supported.</u>


Going through the passage, we see that the conclusion is supported by the following premises:


<u>Premise 1</u>: Three years ago, the town of Amity renewed two blocks on its waterfront by replacing abandoned warehouses with green, retail, and restaurant space, with the result that the town was able to collect substantial sales taxes from the new businesses.


<u>Premise 2</u>: There are two more blocks available for renewal on the waterfront.


So, the author has seen what occurred when the first two blocks were renewed and concluded that, since there are two more blocks available for renewal, the town can repeat what happened the first time.


Thus, the argument is also supported by the following assumption:


<u>Assumption</u>: The author makes the unstated logical leap that the situation in Amity is as conducive to new business as it was when the first renewal project was completed.


In arriving at the conclusion, the author is assuming that what was true in the past will be true again. Is this a good assumption? Maybe. Maybe not. Such an assumption certainly is vulnerable to attack.


<u>Step 5 </u><u>–</u><u> Remove choices that strengthen the argument or rather obviously have no effect on the argument.</u>


Let’s examine each answer choice, remembering that we are looking to separate statements that either strengthen the argument or do not affect the argument from statements that seem to weaken the argument.


<strong>(A) If</strong><strong> property tax rates in Amity were increased slightly, the resulting additional revenue would exceed the revenue currently collected in sales taxes from the renewal area businesses.</strong>


To see why this choice is not the correct answer, remember what the conclusion of the argument is:


<u>Conclusion</u>: The town of Amity can, by renewing the two available blocks, double the amount of sales tax revenue it will receive from businesses in waterfront renewal areas.


So, the conclusion of the argument is specifically that renewing the second set of two blocks will result in a doubling of sales tax revenue. The conclusion is not that some other plan would not generate more revenue.


So, this choice has absolutely no effect on the support for the conclusion. After all, regardless of how much revenue the town could generate by increasing property taxes, the fact that there are two more blocks in the waterfront area that could be renewed supports the conclusion that the town can double the amount of sales tax revenue it will receive from businesses in waterfront renewal areas.


Notice how important identifying the conclusion is. If we are not clear about what the conclusion is, we could easily choose this choice.


Since this choice focuses on another plan – not the plan mentioned – and thus does not affect the conclusion of the argument, we can put it aside with a high degree of confidence.


<strong>(B) Most </strong><strong>of the restaurants in the waterfront area are filled to capacity every </strong><strong>weekend.</strong>


This choice indicates that the restaurant business in the waterfront area is strong. Therefore, this choice supports the idea that repeating the renewal project will result in a doubling of business and of sales taxes. So, if anything, this choice is a strengthener rather than a weakener, and we can put it aside with a high degree of confidence.


<strong>(C) The </strong><strong>volume of restaurant and retail business decreases when the national economy slows down.</strong>


This choice is interesting. If the national economy were to slow and the volume of restaurant and retail business therefore decrease, sales tax revenue would also decrease. So, this choice seems to have the potential to weaken an argument about increasing sales tax revenue.


Let’s keep this one.


<strong>(D) In the </strong><strong>nearby town of Friendship, neither retail business nor restaurant business has ever been very strong.</strong>


At first this choice might seem to affect the argument by indicating something negative about prospects for new business.


Notice, however, that not only is this choice saying something about Friendship rather than Amity but also it states that the restaurant business in Friendship has “never been very good.”


Of course, if the restaurant business in Friendship has never been very good, then, even when the first waterfront renewal project was undertaken in Amity, the restaurant business in Friendship was not very good.


OK then, so, what this choice really means is that, even when the restaurant business in Friendship was not good, a renewal project in Amity worked.


Thus, this choice does not provide any reason to believe that a second renewal project would be any less successful than the first one, and therefore this choice does not affect the argument.


So, we can confidently eliminate this choice.


<strong>(E) Since </strong><strong>the completion of the first renewal project, it has become common for all streets leading to and in Amity’s waterfront area to be congested with slow moving traffic.</strong>


What this choice discusses, streets congested with slow moving traffic, may not seem related to sales tax revenue at first glance. However, what this choice says seems to indicate that people get to the waterfront area by automobile and that their ability to do so has run up against a constraint, the capacity of the streets.


So, this choice indicates that the current situation is likely not as conducive to an increase in business as was the situation before the first renewal project was completed. In other words, this choice points out that times have changes since the renewal of the first two blocks on the waterfront.


This choice could be our answer.


<u>Step 6 </u><u>–</u><u> Choose the answer choice that most clearly weakens the argument.</u>


Choice (C) may seem to weaken the argument, as what it says seems to indicate that sales tax revenue could be negatively affected by economic factors.


However, let’s think about this.


If the second waterfront renewal were as financially successful as the first, in any economic environment, strong or weak, sales tax revenue would be approximately <em>double what it would have been</em>. So, even if the economy were to slow, it could be the case that, by renewing two more blocks, Amity can double the amount of sales tax revenue that it will receive, because the revenue could be double what it would have been in the same situation if the two blocks had not been renewed. So, what this choice says actually has no effect on the conclusion of the argument.


Choice (E), on the other hand, provides a clear reason to doubt the validity of the conclusion. How? It indicates that the author’s assumption was not a good one, by indicating that there is a difference between the situation preceding the prospective renewal of the second two blocks and the situation that preceded the renewal of the first two blocks.


Now, driving to the waterfront area has become difficult, and common sense tells us that it could be difficult to double the amount of business done in the waterfront renewal area if people would have trouble getting there.


Notice how clearly this choice weakens the argument by providing a clear reason for calling into question the idea that, by renewing the second two blocks, the town could double sales tax revenue from waterfront renewal area businesses.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
