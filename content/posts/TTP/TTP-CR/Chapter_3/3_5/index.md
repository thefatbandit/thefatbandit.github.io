## Lesson Content
When we hear the phrase “weaken the argument,” we may feel that our job is to destroy the argument.

In fact, much of the time, the correct answer to a Weaken question, especially a more difficult one, will not provide an earth-shattering attack.

Thus, we need to be adept at finding subtle ways to weaken an argument.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Often, the correct answer to a Weaken the Argument question will not totally destroy the argument but instead weaken it in a subtle yet important way.
{{< /alert >}}

On legal television shows, lawyers talk about proving a case “beyond a reasonable doubt.” Think of Weaken the Argument questions in the following way: we don't need to find an option that proves the conclusion false, just an option that provides reasonable doubt about the conclusion.

Casting even a small amount of doubt onto an argument’s conclusion is enough to weaken it.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Casting even a small amount of doubt onto an argument’s conclusion is enough to weaken it.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example:

> <em>Factory pollution of the water of Wattachen Lake has resulted in a reduction in the axolotl population that once thrived in the lake. Experts recommend that the factory be relocated in order to restore the ecological conditions in and surrounding Wattachen Lake. Once the factory is gone, they claim, the axolotl population will certainly return to its original pre-factory levels within two years.</em>

<em>Which of the following most seriously undermines the strength of the experts’ argument?</em>

The experts’ conclusion is the following:

> <u>Conclusion</u>: Once the factory is gone, the axolotl population will certainly return to its original pre-factory levels within two years.

What is one assumption made by the experts in arriving at that conclusion? As we know, any time someone presents a plan, he assumes that the plan will work.

For example, the experts assume that the pollution from the factory will not remain in the water after the factory has been relocated.

First, let’s look at an answer choice that clearly destroys the argument:

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) The factory pollutants in the water will begin to disintegrate only several years after removal of the source.</em>

If it takes “several years” for removal of the factory to have an effect on levels of pollutants, then the relocation of the factory will have no effects within the first two years.

Ok – that correct answer pretty blatantly destroys the argument.

Here is a more difficult to analyze answer that also blatantly destroys the argument:

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) Once its habitat is threatened, the reproductive rate of the axolotl changes in a way such that each successive generation produces only half the number of offspring produced by the previous generation.</em>

Using some soft math, we can deduce that the axolotl population as a whole will get smaller and smaller as the generations pass.

On the GMAT, we will rarely see such direct rebuttals of the argument.

The following answer choice provides a reason to doubt the experts’ conclusion without definitively ruling it out:

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) The population of tilapia, a natural predator of the axolotl, has been held in abeyance by the factory pollutants.</em>

Now, we cannot say for sure whether the resurgence of the tilapia population that is suggested by this choice would impede growth of the axolotl population.

At the same time, we can see that there is reason to <em>question</em> whether, with the return of this predator, the axolotl population would quickly rebound.

Notice that this answer choice presents a malicious variable and, thus, weakens the argument in a subtle way.

So, we can see that, to weaken an argument, all we must do is cast a reasonable amount of doubt that the argument is sound.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

To weaken an argument, all we must do is cast a reasonable amount of doubt that the argument is sound.
{{< /alert >}}

Here is another answer choice that subtly casts doubt on the certainty of the resurgence of axolotls:

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) Genetic adaptations in the axolotls during the factory’s presence have rendered them and subsequent generations unable to subsist on their pre-factory food source.</em>

This statement tells us that the axolotls have changed recently and can no longer eat what they used to eat.

Notice, again, that this statement does not clearly destroy the argument.

##  Example 6 

Last week, the Silver Bullet Tea Company, which recently opened a chain of high-end tea shops in the affluent City of Silverton, began a marketing campaign, which involved television, newspaper, and internet marketing, aimed at letting residents of Silverton know that its tea shops are now open for business.

Which of the following, if true, most weakens the argument above?

1. [ ] (A) In the City of Silverton, the law permits tea shops to operate only if they do not violate city ordinances.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) The Silver Bullet Tea Company spent less than half of the budget that it had originally allocated for the public marketing campaign in the City of Silverton.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) Most residents of the Town of Silverton would be willing to purchase the expensive tea sold at a high-end tea shop.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) Because they are into active, outdoor activities, most of the residents of Silverton rarely watch television, read the newspaper, or use the internet.
5. [ ] (E) When asked in a city-sponsored survey what the City of Silverton needs most, many residents responded that it needs more high-end tea and coffee shops.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Breakdown of the argument:


<u>Premise</u>: Last week, the Silver Bullet Tea Company, which recently opened a chain of high-end tea shops in the affluent City of Silverton, began a public marketing campaign, which involved television, newspaper, and internet marketing, aimed at letting residents of Silverton know that its tea shops are now open for business.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Its tea shops will soon be filled with residents of Silverton purchasing tea.


The Silver Bullet Tea Company has a marketing plan. Any time someone presents a plan, he assumes that the plan will work. In other words, he assumes that there are not variables that will enter into the equation that will spoil the plan.


In the TTP Course, we refer to such variables as “malicious variables.” When an author presents a plan, he assumes that there are no malicious variables.


What malicious variables might the author have missed? What things might the author have overlooked that would spoil the plan?


> <b>(A) In the City of Silverton, the law permits tea shops to operate only if they do not violate city ordinances.
</b>


 If you choose this choice as correct, you likely created an unsupported story involving the Silver Bullet Tea Company’s violating city ordinances.


Notice, however, that we do not have any evidence that the Silver Bullet Tea Company has violated or will violate any ordinances.


Since what this choice says is not itself a reason to question the conclusion that Silver Bullet’s tea shops will be filled with residents of Silverton purchasing tea, this choice does not weaken the argument.


We can’t make a choice into a weakener by making up an unsupported story.


> <b>(B) The Silver Bullet Tea Company spent less than half of the budget that it had originally allocated for the public marketing campaign in the City of Silverton.
</b>


 This choice is a pseudo-weakener that may seem to affect the support for the conclusion but doesn’t really.


If you chose this answer choice as correct, you likely assumed that, if the Silver Bullet Tea Company spent less than half of the budget that it had originally allocated for the public marketing campaign in the City of Silverton, then it must not have spent enough money, and thus the campaign will not work as planned.


Be careful not to do that. The Silver Bullet Tea Company’s marketing budget could be ginormous, and half of it could still be enormous. In other words, we really don’t know what “less than half” means, and so, this choice’s effect on the support for the conclusion is not clear.


Watch out for pseudo-weakeners that say things that seem to make a difference but don’t clearly weaken the support for the conclusion.


> <b>(C) Most residents of the Town of Silverton would be willing to purchase the expensive tea sold at a high-end tea shop.
</b>


 What this answer choice says strengthens the argument.


After all, the information that most residents of the Town of Silverton would be willing to purchase the expensive tea sold at a high-end tea shop makes us more confident that soon the Silver Bullet Tea Company’s tea shops will be filled with residents of Silverton purchasing tea.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) Because they are into active, outdoor activities, most of the residents of Silverton rarely watch television, read the newspaper, or use the internet.
</b>


 Notice that what this answer choice says brings up a reason why the plan might not work.


After all, if, because they are into active, outdoor activities, most of the residents of Silverton rarely watch television, read the newspaper, or use the Internet, then most of the potential customers of the Silver Bullet Tea Company might never know the tea shops are open for business, because they would not be aware of the marketing campaign, which involves television, newspaper, and internet marketing.


Notice that this choice does not completely destroy the argument. The fact that most of the residents of Silverton rarely watch television, read the newspaper, or use the internet does not prove without a doubt that the marketing campaign won’t work.


At the same time, the fact that most of the residents of Silverton rarely watch television, read the newspaper, or use the internet is certainly a reason to question the conclusion that, as a result of the marketing campaign, Silver Bullet’s shops will soon be filled with residents of Silverton.


Notice that this answer choice presents us with a malicious variable. Thus, this choice weakens the argument.


> <b>(E) When asked in a city-sponsored survey what the City of Silverton needs most, many residents responded that it needs more high-end tea and coffee shops.
</b>


 Notice that what this answer choice says strengthens the argument.


After all, if when asked in a city-sponsored survey what the City of Silverton needs most, many residents responded that it needs more high-end tea and coffee shops, then we have more faith that the Silver Bullet Tea Company’s tea shops will soon be filled with residents of Silverton purchasing tea.


Of course, we’re not seeking a strengthener; we’re seeking a weakener.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Example 7 

Blades of Glory skating rink, one of three skating rinks in Greenville, draws revenue from admissions and from food and drink concessions during its day and evening open-skate sessions.

Which of the following, if true, most seriously calls into question the soundness of management’s plan?

1. [ ] (A) At some skating rinks, daytime food sales are a source of significant profits.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) One hungry member of a group of skaters can influence the group’s decision regarding which rink to go to.
3. [ ] (C) Sometimes when people go to the rink’s concessions stand to buy drinks, they decide to buy food as well.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) Many of the people who skate at Blades of Glory go during evening sessions on some days and during daytime sessions on others.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) Most people who would have bought both food and drinks at Blades of Glory will still buy drinks even if food is not available.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



Management’s decision is based on the following line of reasoning:


<u>Premise</u>: Daytime food sales not profitable.


<u>Assumption</u>: There is not some way in which offering food in the daytime is helping overall profitability.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Increase overall profitability by ceasing to offer daytime food.


Notice the important assumption in the argument: offering food during the daytime is not somehow helping overall profitability. After all, if the rink were somehow making more profit overall in the daytime because of offering food, even if food sales themselves were not profitable, it would no longer make sense to believe that ceasing to offer food during the day would increase overall profitability.


Remember, since the stated goal of the rink is to increase profitability, any considerations that are not somehow connected to profitability will not affect the conclusion. The correct answer will be somehow related to profitability.


At the same time, notice that the correct answer does not have to destroy the conclusion. It has only to provide a good reason to question the conclusion. Be on the lookout for subtle ways to weaken the argument.


> <b>(A) At some skating rinks, daytime food sales are a source of significant profits.
</b>


 Notice that the argument already states as fact that, at Blades of Glory skating rink, daytime food sales are not profitable. The fact that daytime food sales at other rinks are profitable does nothing to change the fact that daytime food sales are not profitable at Blades of Glory.


So, this answer choice does not affect the conclusion, which is about how Blades of Glory skating rink's overall profitability will be affected by the rink’s ceasing to offer food in the daytime.


In general, we must remember that the premises of GMAT Critical Reasoning arguments are always considered factual. So, an answer choice that says something that appears to weaken a premise will never be correct.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B) One hungry member of a group of skaters can influence the group’s decision regarding which rink to go to.
</b>


This choice offers a clear reason why ceasing to offer food during the day in order to increase profitability may not be a sound plan. If one hungry member of a group can influence where the entire group goes to skate, then offering food at Blades of Glory skating rink could influence entire groups of skaters to skate at Blades of Glory, and ceasing to offer food could result in their going to other rinks that offer food.


So, this choice casts doubt on the conclusion that ceasing to offer food will result in increased profitability.


Note that, while this choice does not destroy the argument, it certainly casts some doubt on the conclusion. This choice brings up factor that management may have overlooked and, thus, exposes a gap in the chain of reasoning. Notice that this answer choice directly (although subtly) attacks an assumption made by the author. Notice also that this answer choice presents us with a malicious variable.


> <b>(C) Sometimes when people go to the rink’s concessions stand to buy drinks, they decide to buy food as well.
</b>


 Regardless of what this choice says, we already know from the argument that daytime food sales are not profitable. Remember, we can’t weaken a GMAT argument by attacking the premises. This choice may seem to attack the premise that daytime food sales are not profitable, but it doesn’t affect the validity of that premise.


> <b>(D) Many of the people who skate at Blades of Glory go during evening sessions on some days and during daytime sessions on others.
</b>


Regardless of what this choice says, we already know from the argument that daytime food sales are not profitable.


Remember, we can’t weaken a GMAT argument by attacking the premises. In other words, the fact that some of the same skaters who attend the evening session also attend the daytime sessions does not change the fact that most skaters do not buy food during the day.


Thus, this choice has no clear impact on the support for the conclusion that ceasing to offer food during the day will increase profitability.


> <b>(E) Most people who would have bought both food and drinks at Blades of Glory will still buy drinks even if food is not available.
</b>


If anything, this option strengthens, rather than weakens, the management’s argument. By indicating that most people who would have bought food and drinks will still buy drinks even if food is not offered, it eliminates a potential weakener that the sales of drinks may decline if food is not offered.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
