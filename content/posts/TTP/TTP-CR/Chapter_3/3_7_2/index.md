## Lesson Content
We’ve learned a bit about cause-and-effect arguments in the Assumptions chapter.

We must be careful whenever a speaker makes a cause-and-effect argument, because determining whether one thing actually causes another can be difficult.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

We must be careful whenever a speaker makes a cause-and-effect argument, because determining whether one thing actually causes another can be very difficult.
{{< /alert >}}

In fact, making such determinations is so difficult that, for decades, courts of law did not acknowledge that cigarette smoking causes lung cancer.

Cigarette companies argued that people who smoke were likely to also drink alcohol, eat poor diets, get insufficient exercise, and generally live stressful lives, all of which are known to cause lung cancer.

There are a number of unique, but related ways, in which cause and effect can appear in Critical Reasoning questions.

When an author confuses correlation with causation, that author claims that the fact that two events happen simultaneously or sequentially indicates that they must have a causal relationship.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When an author confuses correlation with causation, that author claims that, because two events happen simultaneously or sequentially, they must have a causal relationship.
{{< /alert >}}

That is, the author assumes that one event must have caused the other event to occur.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

It’s important to remember that the fact that two events happen together does not mean that one of the events causes the other event.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 11 

A recent study compared data on the overall health and lifespans of people who abstain from drinking alcoholic beverages with the overall health and lifespans of people who drink moderately.

Which of the following, if true, most clearly calls into question the validity of the above conclusion?

1. [ ] (A) What constitutes consuming alcohol in moderation is not defined in exactly the same way by every researcher.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) Many people who abstain from consuming alcohol do so because they are not healthy.
3. [ ] (C) The alcohol content of different types of alcoholic beverages varies widely.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) The people who consumed alcoholic beverages moderately did not do so in order to experience health benefits.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) The study was designed so that the effects of other lifestyle related variables, such as diet, did not distort the results.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Here’s a breakdown of the argument:


<u>Premise</u>: Results of a study showed that, on average, people who drink alcohol moderately are in better health and live longer than people who abstain from drinking.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Consuming alcohol in moderation is beneficial for health.


A key detail to notice is that the argument mentions people who “regularly drink moderate amounts of alcohol.” So, people who consume large amounts of alcohol or people who rarely consume alcohol are not the subjects of the argument.


Notice that the author uses cause-and-effect reasoning in his argument. Any time an author makes an argument that rests on cause-and-effect reasoning, we must be skeptical of the cause-and-effect claim as well as the soundness of the argument. Whenever an author makes a cause-and-effect claim, he makes the assumption that his cause-and-effect claim is correct and that there is not an alternative explanation for the pattern that he seeks to explain. Often, we’ll see that this assumption is a bad one.


The argument posits that regular consumption of moderate amounts of alcohol is the cause of better health and longer lives. What is the author assuming? Is there more to the story?


> <b>(A) What constitutes consuming alcohol in moderation is not defined in exactly the same way by every researcher.
</b>


The fact that different researchers define consuming alcohol in moderation in different ways does not clearly weaken the argument, because the fact that different researchers have different definitions of consuming alcohol in moderation does not give us a clear reason to doubt that the people studied were consuming alcohol moderately.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B) Many people who abstain from consuming alcohol do so because they are not healthy.
</b>


 What this answer says suggests that the reason people who drink alcohol moderately are generally healthier than people who do not is that people who are <em>already </em>unhealthy tend to refrain from drinking alcohol.


By providing reason to believe that people who consume alcohol in moderation are not healthy because of their alcohol consumption but, rather, consume alcohol because they are healthy, this choice indicates that the relationship between moderate alcohol consumption and better health is the reverse of the one that the argument concludes exists.


Thus, this choice weakens the argument.


Notice that, in general, what this answer choice shows is that the author’s assumption that there could not be an alternative explanation for the observation she cites is a poor one.


> <b>(C) The alcohol content of different types of alcoholic beverages varies widely.
</b>


While a test-taker might be tempted to construe this choice as weakening the argument by somehow calling into question whether the people studied were actually consuming alcohol in moderation, what this choice says gives us no clear reason to believe other than that they were consuming alcohol moderately. In other words, in order for what this choice says to affect the argument, we would need additional information clearly connecting the varying amounts of alcohol in beverages to a problem in the study, such as that the researchers did not correctly account for the amount of alcohol in beverages consumed by people.


> <b>(D) The people who consumed alcoholic beverages moderately did not do so in order to experience health benefits.
</b>


While this choice mentions things involved in the argument, drinking moderately and health, it does not affect the argument, because even if those consuming alcohol did not do so in order to gain health benefits, consuming alcohol could still have health benefits.


> <b>(E) The study was designed so that the effects of other lifestyle related variables, such as diet, did not distort the results.
</b>


This choice supports the idea that the results of the study are not distorted by confounding factors Therefore, what it says strengthens rather than weakens the argument.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

##  Example 12 

Health Guru: For two years following their reaching the summit of Mount Everest, people who have safely returned from the summit of Mount Everest have an incidence of heart attacks lower than the incidence of heart attacks in people in general.

Which of the following, if true, most calls into question the health guru's conclusion?

1. [ ] (A) There is little difference between the incidence of heart attacks in people who have viewed Mount Everest from airplanes and the incidence of heart attacks in other people.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) The incidence of heart attacks in people who regularly climb indoor climbing walls is lower than the incidence of heart attacks in people who do not.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) It is generally the case that only people who have achieved a high level of fitness succeed in climbing to the summit of Mount Everest.
4. [ ] (D) Climbing to the summit of Mount Everest costs much more than it costs to join a local gym for five years.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) Many people who have experienced heart attacks could have prevented them by reducing their body weights or quitting smoking.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



The health guru concludes the following:


<u>Conclusion</u>: One can decrease one’s chances of experiencing a heart attack by climbing to the summit of Mount Everest every two years.


The conclusion is based on the correlation between people’s having climbed to the summit of Mount Everest and safely returned and those people’s having a lower-than-normal incidence of heart attacks over the two years following their climbs.


Notice that the author uses cause-and-effect reasoning in her argument. Any time an author makes an argument that rests on cause-and-effect reasoning, we must be skeptical of the cause-and-effect claim as well as the soundness of the argument. Whenever an author makes a cause-and-effect claim, she makes the assumption that her cause-and-effect claim is correct and that there is not an alternative explanation for the pattern that she seeks to explain. Often, we’ll see that this assumption is a bad one.


Is the true cause of the lower incidence of heart attacks the people’s climbing to the summit of Mount Everest? Or could there be another cause?


> <b>(A) There is little difference between the incidence of heart attacks in people who have viewed Mount Everest from airplanes and the incidence of heart attacks in other people.
</b>


While this choice mentions Mount Everest, it doesn’t weaken the argument, as viewing Mount Everest from an airplane is an activity significantly different from climbing Mount Everest. So, the fact that viewing Mount Everest from an airplane does not seem to prevent heart attacks does not affect the argument.


> <b>(B) The incidence of heart attacks in people who regularly climb indoor climbing walls is lower than the incidence of heart attacks in people who do not.
</b>


This choice could be tempting because it discusses something that is somewhat similar to climbing Mount Everest, climbing indoor climbing walls.


However, this choice is not a weakener, as it shows that, not only is there a correlation between climbing Mount Everest and a lower incidence of heart attacks, but also there is a correlation between climbing indoor climbing walls and a lower incidence of heart attacks.


Bringing up another pattern similar to the one that supports the argument certainly doesn’t weaken the argument.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) It is generally the case that only people who have achieved a high level of fitness succeed in climbing to the summit of Mount Everest.
</b>


 This choice indicates that people who climb to the summit of Mount Everest are generally fitter than the average person. It is common knowledge that fitter people are generally less likely to experience heart attacks. So, given what this choice says, it could be the case that climbing to the summit of Mount Everest does not prevent heart attacks, but rather, that the people who climb to the summit of Mount Everest are less likely to have heart attacks because they have achieved high levels of fitness.


Thus, this choice weakens the argument by showing that there is a third factor, high levels of fitness, that underlies both people’s reaching the summit of Mount Everest and those same people’s having a relatively low incidence of heart attacks.


Notice that, in general, what this answer choice shows is that the author’s assumption that there could not be an alternative explanation for the observation she cites is a poor one.


> <b>(D) Climbing to the summit of Mount Everest costs much more than it costs to join a local gym for five years.
</b>


This answer could be tempting, as it indicates that preventing heart attacks by simply joining a gym may be better than preventing them by climbing Mount Everest, because joining a gym costs much less.


However, the conclusion is not that climbing Mount Everest is the best way to prevent heart attacks, but rather that it does prevent heart attacks. So, this choice does not affect the conclusion.


In general, unless an author claims that his plan is the only plan or the best plan, he need not assume that there are no other plans that could work or assume that there is not a better plan than his. Therefore, the mention of a different plan or a better plan will not weaken the argument.


> <b>(E) Many people who have experienced heart attacks could have prevented them by reducing their body weights or quitting smoking.
</b>


The fact that there are various ways in which people can prevent heart attacks does not affect the conclusion that people can prevent heart attacks by climbing Mount Everest.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

##  Example 13 

Relationship Advisor: When people ask me about causes of divorce, I point to statistics, which show that couples who live together before getting married are more likely to get divorced than are those who do not live together before marriage.

Which of the following, if true, is most damaging to the relationship advisor’s conclusion?

1. [x] (A) In comparison with more conservative minded people, more liberal minded people tend to be both more open to living together before marriage and more open to getting divorced.
2. [ ] (B) In some countries, the divorce rates have steadily decreased over the past 30 years.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) Couples who live together before getting married have an opportunity to learn to work together as a team before they get married.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) For preventing divorce, there is no substitute for having good communication skills.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) For many couples who live together before getting married, marriage does not immediately lead to significant changes in their lifestyles.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



First, let’s break down the argument:


<u>Premise</u>: Couples who live together before marriage are more likely to divorce than are those who do not live together before marriage.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Living together before marriage is one of the causes of divorce.


Notice that the author uses cause-and-effect reasoning in her argument. Any time an author makes an argument that rests on cause-and-effect reasoning, we must be skeptical of the cause-and-effect claim as well as the soundness of the argument. Whenever an author makes a cause-and-effect claim, she makes the assumption that her cause-and-effect claim is correct and that there is not an alternative explanation for the pattern that she seeks to explain. Often, we’ll see that this assumption is a bad one.


In order to weaken the argument, we need an answer choice that provides information that serves to call into question the causal relationship, that living together prior to marriage causes divorce.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A) In comparison with more conservative minded people, more liberal minded people tend to be both more open to living together before marriage and more open to getting divorced.
</b>


 What this answer choice says indicates that whether a couple’s mindset is conservative or liberal can affect both (1) whether the couple are likely to live together before marriage and (2) whether the couple are likely to get divorced.


So, this choice provides an alternate explanation for the correlation between living together before marriage and getting divorced: a third variable, liberal mindset, causes both of them.


Thus, this choice suggests that there is a strong possibility that the correct explanation for the observed relationship between living together before marriage and getting divorced is not the explanation upon which the author bases her claim. Thus, this choice weakens the argument.


It’s important to remember that a powerful way in which we can weaken a cause-and-effect argument is to show that perhaps there is a third variable (in this case, a liberal mindset), that causes both of the other two variables (in this case, living together and divorce).


Notice that this choice directly attacks the author’s assumption.


> <b>(B) In some countries, the divorce rates have steadily decreased over the past 30 years.
</b>


Notice that the conclusion of the argument is based on the fact that there is a correlation between living together before marriage and getting divorced.


The fact that <em>overall</em> divorce rates in some countries have decreased does not change the fact that people who live together before marriage are more likely to get divorced than people who don’t live together before marriage, and the fact that overall divorce rates have decreased does not affect the support that the correlation between living together before marriage and getting divorced provides for the argument’s conclusion.


> <b>(C) Couples who live together before getting married have an opportunity to learn to work together as a team before they get married.
</b>


This answer choice could be tempting, as it could seem to weaken the argument by presenting a reason why living together before marriage would prevent divorce rather than cause it: people living together before marriage have an opportunity to learn to work together as a team.


However, even though what is described by this choice seems to be an advantage to living together before marriage, it remains the case that couples who live together before marriage are more likely to get divorced than are couples who do not live together before marriage. So, what this choice says does not affect the argument.


In general, we must remember that the premises are always considered factual, so an answer choice that says something the appears to weaken a premise will never be correct.


> <b>(D) For preventing divorce, there is no substitute for having good communication skills.
</b>


This choice may seem to suggest that a couple’s level of communication skills is more important than whether they live together before marriage in determining whether they will remain married.


However, even if level of communication skills is more important than whether a couple live together, living together before marriage could still cause divorce. So, what this choice says does not affect the argument.


It’s important not to choose as correct answer choices that say things that are probably true in reality yet do not weaken the argument.


> <b>(E) For many couples who live together before getting married, marriage does not immediately lead to significant changes in their lifestyles.
</b>


Since this choice may be taken as suggesting that living together before marriage is little different from marriage, a test-taker might be tempted to select this choice as a result of reasoning that something similar to marriage would not be the cause of divorce.


However, the truth is that, regardless of whether marriage is significantly different from living together before marriage, for some reason, couples who live together before marriage are more likely to get divorced than are couples who don’t live together before marriage. So, what this choice says does not provide a clear reason to believe that the correct explanation for this correlation is not that living together before marriage causes divorce.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)
