## Lesson Content
When an author uses a sample of a population as evidence in his argument, he assumes that that sample provides solid support for his conclusion.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When an author uses a sample of a population as evidence in his argument, he assumes that that sample provides solid support for his conclusion. In Weaken the Argument questions, we’ll see that that assumption can be a poor one.
{{< /alert >}}

Authors sometimes make generalizations, drawing conclusions about populations by considering small samples of those populations.

For example, some hedge-fund managers engage in insider trading.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Be cautious if a generalization made about all members of a group is based on characteristics of members of a sample taken from that group.
{{< /alert >}}

When the argument in a Weaken question makes a broad generalization about members of a group that is based on information on only some of the members of that group, be aware that the correct answer will likely demonstrate that the fact that some members of a group have attribute X does not mean that all members of the group have attribute X.

<u>Flaws in Sampling </u>

One important theme that falls into this category is that, whenever we take a sample of a population, for example in setting up an experiment or taking a survey, and then make generalizations about the overall population, we may run into trouble.

We say that, in order for a conclusion based on characteristics of a sample to be well supported, we must have a “representative sample.” In other words, the sample must have characteristics that are the same as (or similar enough to) the characteristics of the overall population.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}

In order for a conclusion based on characteristics of sample to be well supported, we must have a “representative sample.” In other words, the sample must have characteristics that are the same as (or similar enough to) the characteristics of the overall population.
{{< /alert >}}

If the sample is not representative of the population, then observations made about the sample may not be applicable to the population.

##  Example 14 

Fitness Company Executive: Our new fitness program was designed to be easy to stick with.

Which of the following, if true, would most undermine the executive’s conclusion?

1. [ ] (A) If a person discontinues using a fitness program, some of the results achieved by using the program can be retained for a relatively long period of time.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) Generally, the people who volunteer for testing the effectiveness of a new fitness program are unusually motivated to become fit.
3. [ ] (C) When another fitness program was tested by the company, only 50 percent of the volunteers using it stuck with it for at least a year.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) Often, a person following a fitness program will begin to see significant effects within about 45 days of starting to follow it.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) Many of the volunteers who participated in the testing of the program had little previous experience with fitness programs.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



This argument concludes that, because almost 90 percent of the volunteers who tested the fitness program stuck it out for at least a year, almost 90 percent of the fitness company’s customers who use the program will stick with it for at least a year.


The author’s assumption is that the volunteers are not different in some important way from the company’s customers as a whole. Right? In other words, the argument overlooks the possibility that most customers may act differently from how the volunteers acted.


In general, one theme to think about is that, whenever we look at sample of a population and then seek to make sweeping generalizations applicable to the overall population, we may find that our sample is not representative of the population.


> <b>(A) If a person discontinues using a fitness program, some of the results achieved by using the program can be retained for a relatively long period of time.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, because the fact one can retain some results of using a fitness program for a while after discontinuing use of it could be perceived as a reason why people would discontinue using a fitness program. Thus, this choice could be perceived as providing a reason to believe the less than 90 percent the customers who use the new program will stick with it.


The truth is, however, that this potential reason for discontinuing use of the fitness program was present when 90 percent of volunteers stuck with the program for at least a year. So, even with this potential reason to quit, 90 percent of volunteers stuck with the program, and this choice provides no reason to doubt that 90 percent of the company’s customers will also stick with it.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B) Generally, the people who volunteer for testing the effectiveness of a new fitness program are unusually motivated to become fit.
</b>


 This choice indicates that the people who volunteered to test the program are probably different from the company’s customers in general, as the people who volunteered were likely unusually motivated to become more fit. In such a case, the fact that a large proportion of them stuck with the program does not imply that a similarly large proportion of the customers, who may not be as motivated, will stick with the program.


Thus, what this answer choice says weakens the argument by highlighting that the sample of people upon which the conclusions is based is a sample that is likely unrepresentative of the company’s customers in general. That is, the sample was biased.


So, this choice attacks the author’s assumption that the volunteers are not different in some important way from the company’s customers as a whole.


Notice that this answer choice presents a malicious variable.


> <b>(C) When another fitness program was tested by the company, only 50 percent of the volunteers using it stuck with it for at least a year.
</b>


This choice has virtually no bearing on the argument at hand. This choice is about a different program. How many volunteers stuck with a different program has no impact on the given argument.


> <b>(D) Often, a person following a fitness program will begin to see significant effects within about 45 days of starting to follow it.
</b>


Whether it takes one month, six months, or more than a year to see benefits, such information will have no impact on the argument. Whatever number of days it takes to see significant benefits, 90 percent of the volunteers stuck with the program for at least a year, and the conclusion is based on this fact. Thus, this choice has no impact on the argument.


> <b>(E) Many of the volunteers who participated in the testing of the program had little previous experience with fitness programs.
</b>


We might be tempted to make this choice seem to be a weakener by making up a story about why people who had no previous fitness experience would have acted differently from the company’s customers.


However, we have no information on whether the customers in general have previous experience with fitness programs. Thus, this choice has no clear impact on the argument.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

##  Example 15 

Presentiment is said to be a phenomenon characterized by a person’s bodily functions, such as heart rate, shifting into response mode before a stressful event actually occurs.

Which of the following, if true, most seriously undermines the researchers’ conclusion?

1. [ ] (A) The results of the studies that indicated that presentiment is a real phenomenon did not all show that it is with the same statistical significance.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) People serving as subjects in studies of parapsychological phenomena such as presentiment are often compensated for their participation.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) The people studied who appeared to be experiencing presentiment often were not fully aware that they were, though monitoring equipment reported changes in bodily functions.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) The methods used in most of the studies reviewed were rigorous enough that the results that they generated could be considered reliable.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) When, in the process of studying a phenomenon, researchers get unexciting results, they often forgo publishing their findings.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



Breakdown of the Argument:


<u>Background</u>: Presentiment is said to be a phenomenon characterized by a person’s body functions shifting into response mode before a stressful event actually occurs.


<u>Background</u>: A group of researchers reviewed all of the studies of presentiment the results of which were published during the previous fifty years .


<u>Premise</u>: The vast majority of the studies that the researchers reviewed provided statistical evidence confirming that presentiment is a real phenomenon.


<u>Conclusion</u>: The researchers concluded that presentiment is a real phenomenon.


We need to weaken the support for the conclusion, which is based on study results.


One key detail to notice is that the argument mentions that the results considered had been “published.” How could this detail be important?


> <b>(A) The results of the studies that indicated that presentiment is a real phenomenon did not all show that it is with the same statistical significance.
</b>


Notice that this answer choice doesn’t say that the statistical significance of any study’s results was low. It just says that the statistical significance was <em>not the same </em>in all cases.


The fact that the statistical significance of results was not the same in all cases does not mean that the results were not solid evidence for the conclusion that presentiment is real.


So, what this choice says has no effect on the strength of the argument.


> <b>(B) People serving as subjects in studies of parapsychological phenomena such as presentiment are often compensated for their participation.
</b>


This answer choice could be taken as indicating that the participants in the studies mentioned could have been influenced by compensation to do things to make presentiment seem to be real even if it is not.


The truth is, however, that we don’t have any clear reason to believe that compensation influenced the study participants in that way. In fact, for all we know, the compensation influenced the study participants to do their best to generate accurate results.


Furthermore, we have no clear reason to believe even that the participants had the ability to affect the study results.


So, there is no clear path via which this choice would weaken the argument.


When answering Weaken questions, we must be careful to not choose as correct answer choices that state facts that seem significant yet do not weaken the argument.


> <b>(C) The people studied who appeared to be experiencing presentiment often were not fully aware that they were, though monitoring equipment reported changes in bodily functions.
</b>


Regardless of whether the people studied were aware that they were experiencing changes in bodily functions that appeared to constitute presentiment, they were experiencing those changes. Therefore, what this choice says does not affect the validity of the study results or affect the researchers’ conclusion based on the study results.


> <b>(D) The methods used in most of the studies reviewed were rigorous enough that the results that they generated could be considered reliable.
</b>


Since what this choice says serves to confirm the reliability of the study results upon which the researchers’ conclusion is based, it strengthens, rather than weakens, the support for the researchers’ conclusion.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E) When, in the process of studying a phenomenon, researchers get unexciting results, they often forgo publishing their findings.
</b>


It is likely that study results that indicate that presentiment is not a real phenomenon would be considered unexciting.


Therefore, by saying that, when researchers studying a phenomenon get unexciting results, they often forgo publishing their findings, this choice provides a reason to believe that any researchers whose study results indicated that presentiment is not a real phenomenon may have decided not to bother publishing their results.


Thus, we can see that the set of published studies upon which the group of researchers’ conclusion is based may not have included some studies the results of which indicated that presentiment is not a real phenomenon. In other words, what this choice says indicates that the set of studies the group of researchers reviewed may have been a biased sample of studies of presentiment.


Thus, by indicating that the group of researchers may have based their conclusion on a sample that was biased in a way such that it would indicate that presentiment is a real phenomenon even if it isn’t real, this answer choice undermines the credibility of their conclusion.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
