## Lesson Content
When an author uses a comparison as evidence in an argument, she assumes that that comparison provides solid support for her conclusion.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When an author uses a comparison as evidence in an argument, she assumes that that comparison provides solid support for her conclusion. Often, in Weaken the Argument questions, we’ll see that that assumption is a poor one, because authors often make faulty comparisons.
{{< /alert >}}

When an author of an argument makes a faulty comparison, she attempts to support the argument by comparing two things that are fundamentally different in an important way such that the comparison does not actually support the conclusion.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When an author of an argument makes a faulty comparison, she attempts to support the argument by comparing two things that are fundamentally different in an important way such that the comparison does not actually support the conclusion.
{{< /alert >}}

For example, imagine someone supporting an argument by making a comparison between wild wolves and domesticated dogs, on the basis of the fact that the two are closely related genetically and look similar.

It’s generally risky to assume that, just because two things seem the same in one way or even in multiple ways, they are similar enough that one can be used as a valid analog for the other, and in Weaken questions, correct answers can weaken arguments based on comparisons by showing that the comparisons are faulty and, therefore, don’t really support the conclusions of the arguments.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The correct answer to a Weaken question can weaken an argument based on a comparison by indicating that items in the comparison are different in key ways, and that, therefore, the comparison is faulty.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 16 

House cats that are adopted as kittens and experience at least three hours of human interaction per day are significantly more docile than cats that are adopted as adults or experience little day-to-day human interaction.

Which of the following, if discovered to be true, would most undermine the conclusion above?

1. [ ] (A) Cheetahs are generally more docile than are wild cats of other species.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) Wild animals born in captivity are more similar to domesticated animals than are wild animals born in nature preserves or in natural environments.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) In the last 50 years, there have been few deadly incidents involving zookeepers and wild cats that were born in captivity.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) Since, unlike wild cats, house cats have been bred for generations to coexist with humans, they have a temperament that is easily influenced by human interaction.
5. [ ] (E) Zookeepers take safety precautions even when dealing with wild cats born in captivity.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



This argument bases its conclusion about wild cats on an analogous situation involving house cats. So, the argument is based on the assumption that house cats and wild cats are similar enough that they will respond in similar ways to factors in their environments.


Is this a good assumption?


Hint: If an argument is based on a comparison, often the correct answer will mention a comparison.


> <b>(A) Cheetahs are generally more docile than are wild cats of other species.
</b>


This choice could be tempting because the passage mentions cheetahs, and because what this choice says could be taken as indicating that wild cats other than cheetahs are something along the lines of never docile. So, we could perceive this choice as weakening the argument by indicating that, unlike cheetahs, cats of other species raised in captivity would not be significantly more docile than non-captive cats of those species.


Notice, however, that this choice differentiates between cheetahs and other species of wild cats, while the argument is about differences between captive and non-captive wild cats. Thus, regardless of what this choice says, it could still be the case that wild cats that are born in captivity can be expected to be significantly more docile than non-captive wild cats.


After all, even if most wild cats born in captivity are not as docile as cheetahs, wild cats born in captivity may still be way more docile than non-captive wild cats.


So, this choice has no effect on the strength of the argument.


> <b>(B) Wild animals born in captivity are more similar to domesticated animals than are wild animals born in nature preserves or in natural environments.
</b>


 If anything, this option supports, rather than weakens the support for, the conclusion.


By saying that wild animals born in captivity are more similar to domestic animals than are wild animals born in more natural circumstances, this choice provides a little more reason to believe the conclusion that captive wild cats are likely to be more docile than non-captive wild cats.


Thus, this choice mildly strengthens, rather than weakens, the argument, doing the opposite of what the correct answer has to do.


> <b>(C) In the last 50 years, there have been few deadly incidents involving zookeepers and wild cats that were born in captivity.
</b>


 Because it is not clear why there have been few deadly incidents involving zookeepers and wild cats that were born in captivity, this choice does not really affect the argument.


Also, the fact that there have been few deadly incidents involving zookeepers and wild cats that were born in captivity certainly does not conflict with the argument’s conclusion.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) Since, unlike wild cats, house cats have been bred for generations to coexist with humans, they have a temperament that is easily influenced by human interaction.
</b>


The argument depends on the assumption that house cats and wild cats are influenced in a similar way by their environments. This choice points out the vulnerability of this assumption by saying that house cats have been bred for generations to coexist with humans and are thus different from wild cats: while house cats can be easily influenced by human interactions, given what this choice says, wild cats are probably not as easily influenced.


Thus, this choice casts doubt on the conclusion by showing that the comparison between wild cats and house cats is faulty.


> <b>(E) Zookeepers take safety precautions even when dealing with wild cats born in captivity.
</b>


This choice implies that even captive wild cats are not particularly docile and is therefore tempting.


Notice, however, that the conclusion of the argument is not that captive wild cats are rather docile or somehow safe to interact with. The conclusion is that wild cats born in captivity can be expected to be <em>more</em> docile than non-captive wild cats. Of course, a captive wild cat could be significantly more docile than a non-captive wild cat and still not be safe to interact with.


So, this choice does not cast any doubt on the conclusion of the argument.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Example 17 

By restoring key, previously longstanding relationships among various animal and plant species in the Yellowstone area of the United States, reintroducing wolves to the area resulted in a restoration of the area’s ecosystem.

Which of the following, if true, most clearly serves to call the conclusion into question?

1. [ ] (A) While dingoes and wolves descended from common ancestors, they have evolved separately for thousands of years.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) In some areas of Australia where dingoes are not currently present, when dingoes were present, they killed livestock.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) Because of the unstable nature of the climate in Australia, relationships among animal and plant species constantly change there.
4. [ ] (D) Like wolves, dingoes serve as apex predators in areas in which they are present.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) Efforts aimed at restoring ecosystems in Australia by eradicating species of animals not native to Australia have been largely unsuccessful.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



Getting the correct answer to this question requires noticing a key difference between things being compared. To see what’s going on, let’s first analyze the argument.


The conclusion of the argument is the following:


<u>Conclusion</u>: Reintroducing dingoes where they have been missing in Australia would achieve results similar to those achieved by reintroducing wolves in the United States, restoring the ecosystems of the areas in which dingoes have been missing.


This conclusion is supported by two ideas:


<u>Premise</u>: Reintroducing wolves in an area restored longstanding relationships among plant and animal species and thus caused the restoration of the area’s ecosystem.


<u>Premise</u>: Dingoes are wild dogs similar to wolves.


<u>Premise</u>: Dingoes have been missing from areas of Australia.


This plan seems to make sense. So, how could the conclusion be called into question? Perhaps there is a key difference between things being compared.


Wolves and dingoes are obviously being compared. At the same time, there is another, less obvious, comparison being made, the comparison between the ecosystem restored in the United States and ecosystems in Australia.


> <b>(A) While dingoes and wolves descended from common ancestors, they have evolved separately for thousands of years.
</b>


While this answer may seem to indicate that wolves and dingoes are somehow so different that they don’t have similar effects on the ecosystems of the areas that they inhabit, the truth is that this choice does not really tell us that wolves and dingoes are significantly different. Yes, they have evolved separately, but that this separate evolution has resulted in significant differences between the two is not clearly indicated by what this choice says. For all we know from what this choice says, even though they have evolved separately, they are still almost exactly the same.


Furthermore, the conclusion is based on the stated premise that dingoes “are wild dogs somewhat similar to wolves.” So, the argument has already addressed the possible issue highlighted by what this choice says by making it clear that, regardless of how long dingoes and wolves have evolved separately, the are somewhat similar.


Thus, given what this choice says, what we really know about wolves and dingoes remains that they are “somewhat similar.” So, this choice does not constitute a clear weakener.


> <b>(B) In some areas of Australia where dingoes are not currently present, when dingoes were present, they killed livestock.
</b>


While this choice indicates that the plan may have a certain drawback, that reintroduced dingoes may kill livestock, indicating that there is a drawback is not the same as indicating that the plan won’t work.


We could say that this choice weakens the support for the wrong conclusion, the conclusion that the plan has no drawbacks.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) Because of the unstable nature of the climate in Australia, relationships among animal and plant species constantly change there.
</b>


 By highlighting a key difference between the ecosystem restored in the United States and the ecosystems in Australia, this choice weakens the argument.


To understand how this choice weakens the argument, we have to notice a key detail.


In the Yellowstone area of the United States, reintroduction of wolves resulted in the restoration of the ecosystem because it restored “longstanding relationships among … species.”


On the other hand, this choice indicates that, in Australia, ecosystems are not characterized by longstanding relationships among species; rather, the relationships “constantly change.”


So, because of this key difference between the ecosystem of the Yellowstone area and ecosystems in Australia, what worked in the United States may not work in Australia.


Thus, this choice calls the argument’s conclusion into question by showing that the conclusion is based on a faulty comparison.


> <b>(D) Like wolves, dingoes serve as apex predators in areas in which they are present.
</b>


By bringing up a similarity between wolves and dingoes, this choice strengthens, rather than weakens, the conclusion that reintroducing dingoes will have effects similar to those achieved via reintroducing wolves.


> <b>(E) Efforts aimed at restoring ecosystems in Australia by eradicating species of animals not native to Australia have been largely unsuccessful.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, as what it says could be taken as indicating that restoring ecosystems in Australia has been made difficult by the presence of non-native species.


However, all this choice is really saying is that methods other than reintroducing dingoes have not worked. That those methods have not worked does not mean that reintroducing dingoes wouldn’t work.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)
