## Lesson Content
When an author presents as evidence statistical data, such as numbers and percentages, she assumes that (1) that data provides solid support for her conclusion and (2) that she has properly analyzed that data.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When an author presents as evidence statistical data, such as numbers and percentages, she assumes that (1) that data provides solid support for her conclusion and (2) that she has properly analyzed that data. Often in Weaken the Argument questions, neither of those assumptions is a good one.
{{< /alert >}}

Critical reasoning questions often attempt to confuse us with numbers.

In general, if a set is small, a large percentage of that set is still a small number of items.

So, we have to be careful when answering questions involving percentages, because the real number behind a percentage may be large, small, or somewhere in between.

Furthermore, percentages can be misleading in other ways.

Consider the following:

> <em>This year Jack’s salary decreased 50 percent and Betty’s salary increased 15 percent.</em>

Are we able to assume that Betty made more than Jack this year? No, because we have no information regarding the original salary for either Jack or Betty.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Be extra careful when you encounter arguments centered on numbers and percentages; such arguments can lead to unfounded conclusions.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 18 

According to a recent study of hospital safety, 10 percent of patients who underwent cardiac bypass surgery at a local hospital experienced serious complications, while 30 percent of patients who underwent cardiac bypass surgery at a hospital in a neighboring town experienced similarly serious complications.

Which of the following, if true, most seriously calls into question the conclusion above?

1. [ ] (A) The neighboring hospital has fewer patients who suffer serious complications after liver transplants than does the local hospital.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) Since cardiac bypass surgeries are among the most complex ones, a complication rate below 35 percent is considered exemplary.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) The neighboring hospital performed more than twice as many cardiac bypass surgeries last year as in any previous year.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) The number of patients who underwent cardiac bypass surgery at the local hospital was eight times the number who underwent such surgery at the hospital in the neighboring town.
5. [ ] (E) The neighboring hospital is world-renowned for the quality of the work done there.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

##### Question Level: easy



The argument concludes the following:


<u>Conclusion</u>: The neighboring hospital must have had more patients (a greater <em>number</em> of patients) who experienced serious complications in undergoing cardiac bypass surgery than the local hospital had.


The support for the conclusion is that the neighboring hospital had a higher <em>percentage</em> of patients who suffered from serious complications following bypass surgery.


Remember, that a higher <em>percentage</em> does not always coincide with a larger <em>number.</em>


> <b>(A) The neighboring hospital has fewer patients who suffer serious complications after liver transplants than does the local hospital.
</b>


This fact is irrelevant, as the argument discusses cardiac bypasses, not liver transplants. Don’t fall into the test-maker’s trap of presenting information about condition X when the argument is about condition Y.


> <b>(B) Since cardiac bypass surgeries are among the most complex ones, a complication rate below 35 percent is considered exemplary.
</b>


This choice does not affect the support for the conclusion. It indicates that both hospitals have done exemplary work given that their complication rates are below 35 percent. However, the conclusion is not about the quality of the work done at the hospitals.


> <b>(C) The neighboring hospital performed more than twice as many cardiac bypass surgeries last year as in any previous year.
</b>


This choice presents an irrelevant comparison; it compares a number from last year with numbers from previous years, rather than provides information that can be used for comparing one hospital with another. While this choice is slightly more appealing than (B), which contains no comparison at all, it ultimately has no bearing on the conclusion.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) The number of patients who underwent cardiac bypass surgery at the local hospital was eight times the number who underwent such surgery at the hospital in the neighboring town.
</b>


 For the purpose of analyzing the effect of this choice, let's assume that the local hospital has 80 bypass patients a year, meaning that it has 80(0.1) = 8 patients with severe complications.


In this case, the neighboring hospital has 1/8 * 80 = 10 bypass patients, meaning that it has 10(0.3) = 3 patients with serious complications.


So, we can see that, even though a larger percentage of patients experienced complications at the neighboring hospital, that percentage represented a smaller number of actual people.


Thus, what this choice says destroys the support for the conclusion that the neighboring hospital had “more patients” who experienced serious complications after cardiac bypass surgery.


> <b>(E) The neighboring hospital is world-renowned for the quality of the work done there.
</b>


This option could be tempting, as one could perceive what it says about quality of work as providing reason to believe that patients at the hospital in the neighboring town would be less likely to experience complications than patients at other hospitals. Given that take, one could perceive this choice as casting doubt on the conclusion that the hospital in the neighboring town must have had more patients who experienced serious complications in undergoing cardiac bypass surgery than the local hospital had.


However, what this choice says neither changes the fact that 30 percent of patients who underwent cardiac bypass surgery at the hospital in the neighboring town experienced serious complications or gives us any clear reason to question the argument’s conclusion.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
