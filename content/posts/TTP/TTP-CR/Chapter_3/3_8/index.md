## Lesson Content
One key to correctly answering Weaken questions is being able to see the difference between trap choices and correct answers.

> <u>Trap Choice</u>: In general, a trap choice in a Weaken the Argument question says something that seems detrimental to the conclusion and that is somehow related to the argument, and at the same time, what the trap choice says is <em>not</em> logically connected to the argument in a way such that the choice actually weakens the argument.

> <u>Correct Answer</u>: Often, at first glance, a correct answer may not seem to be directly related to the conclusion of the argument.

Consider the following example:

> <em>A survey has found that parents who smoke are more likely than parents who don’t smoke to have children who are involved in juvenile delinquency. Therefore, it’s clear that parental smoking causes children to engage in destructive behavior.</em>

> <em>Which of the following, if true, would most seriously weaken the argument?</em>

> <em>(<u>Trap Choice</u>)</em> <em>Some of the children who most actively engage in destructive behavior have parents who do not smoke.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>Living in stressful circumstances can lead parents to smoke and children to engage in destructive behavior.</em>

Notice the difference between the trap choice and the correct answer.

The trap choice says something about children who “most actively engage in destructive behavior,” and the wording of the trap choice makes it sound important.

Notice, however, that the fact that some children whose parents do not smoke engage in destructive behavior does not conflict with the conclusion that parental smoking causes children to engage in destructive behavior.

The correct answer, on the other hand, indicates that it could be that the true cause-and-effect relationship is not that parental smoking causes children’s destructive behavior but, rather, that a third variable, living in stressful circumstances, underlies both the parents’ smoking and the children’s destructive behavior.

Notice this choice attacks the assumption that a parent’s smoking is the cause of a child’s juvenile delinquency.

So, the correct answer says something that clearly serves to call the conclusion into question.

Now that we better understand the difference between a trap choice and a correct answer to a Weaken question, here is some Targeted Practice that includes some tricky trap choices.

## Targeted Practice: 1

Transportation Analyst: A study done over the last five years shows that using car seatbelts reduced the incidence of serious injury in crashes that occurred at a speed limit of 60 miles per hour by 48 percent. Once our state passes a law requiring the use of seatbelts, we can expect that, by wearing seatbelts, people will reduce their likelihood of being injured in crashes to around half of what it is now.

1. [ ] (A) The data used in the study came only from crashes that were written up in police reports.
    > <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) People using protective devices such as seatbelts often feel more comfortable exceeding the speed limit and driving less carefully than they otherwise would.

## Targeted Practice: 2

A home energy use consulting firm offers insulation installation among its services, which also include solar panel installation, windmill installation, and smart heating system design and installation. Partly because of competition from individuals specializing in insulation, the profitability of providing insulation services is relatively low for the firm. The firm would be better off not providing insulation services and focusing on the more profitable aspects of its business.

1. [x] (A) The firm often sells comprehensive energy systems to people who initially were interested only in its insulation services.
2. [ ] (B) Many individuals make above average incomes by providing home insulation services.
    > <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

## Targeted Practice: 3

City Health Official: Recently, computer analysis of data from our city departments has discovered a disturbing trend. When street vendors’ sales of frozen fruit items increase, the rate of petty crime in the city increases as well. Apparently, along with the other issues associated with eating sweet foods is that eating frozen fruit products can result in a tendency to engage in criminal behavior.

1. [x] (A) A significant percentage of petty crimes are committed by teenagers, who are more likely to stay home on cold or rainy days.
2. [ ] (B) Some people report that after eating frozen fruit products they feel happy and peaceful.
    > <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

## Targeted Practice: 4

Consumer Goods Manufacturing Company CEO: The government has informed us that we have to take measures aimed at reducing the incidence of industrial accidents at our company. Compliance will take up management’s time, and installing the required equipment will involve substantial costs. Thus, implementing the safety measures is going to be hurtful to the profitability of our business.

1. [ ] (A) Implementing the safety measures will not affect the company’s ability to continue a recently begun project, the aim of which is to cut costs and increase efficiency by streamlining manufacturing processes.
    > <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) Consumers prefer to buy from companies that have track records of low incidences of industrial accidents.

## Targeted Practice: 5

Tennis Center Owner: The trend in our region is that with each passing year people are spending more of their leisure time cycling. In fact, projections show that, whereas currently people in our region spend five percent of their leisure time playing tennis and one percent cycling, within five years they are expected to spend three percent of their leisure time playing tennis and five percent cycling. If these projections are correct, our business is in trouble.

1. [ ] (A) Because of manufacturing innovations, the cost of high quality tennis equipment, and thus the cost of taking up the sport, are expected to decrease substantially.
    > <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) With the increased use of automation, the amount of leisure time had by people living in the region surrounding the tennis club is expected to double.

 This choice clearly undermines the support for the conclusion.

Notice that this choice attacks the author’s assumption that all people wearing seat belts would experience results the same as those achieved by a specific set of people, people who were wearing seatbelts of their own volition while driving at a certain speed.

(A) <strong>Trap.</strong> The appeal of this answer lies in the fact that it seems to weaken the argument by attacking one of the premises, the premise that people are going to reduce the percentage of their leisure time that they spend playing tennis. One could think, “Wait, playing tennis is going to cost less. So, actually people will play more.”

Notice that the conclusion starts with, “If these projections are correct.”

<i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span>CORRECT ANSWER


 Using some simple math, we can see that this answer choice destroys the argument.

Thus, given what this choice says, in the future, people will be spending 2 x 3% = 6% of today’s leisure time playing tennis.


So, if the projections are correct, the amount of time people spend playing tennis will increase rather than decrease, going from 5% of today’s leisure time to 6%.


So, this choice completely destroys the argument.

##  Example 24 

Marketing Executive: We use independently administered customer satisfaction surveys to rate the performance of our company, and we recently switched from using one survey provider to using another.

Which of the following, if true, would most undermine the executive’s interpretation of the survey results?

1. [ ] (A) The provider of the new survey has only half as many company clients as has the provider of the previous survey.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) The new survey has been in use for only five months, while the previous one was used for three years.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) The more positive a person’s relationship with a company the more likely it is that the person will take the time to respond to a long, detailed survey for the company.
4. [ ] (D) Designing a survey that accurately captures multiple aspects of the satisfaction of a company’s customers requires detailed analysis of what to ask.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) The first few questions in the new survey are nearly identical to the first few questions that were in the previous survey.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



The marketing executive’s conclusion, that the ratings generated by the new survey are more accurate than those generated by the previous survey, is based on the premise that the new survey is longer and more detailed.


Notice that the marketing executive is essentially making a cause-and-effect claim in saying that it was the accuracy of the longer survey that produced the higher ratings. The author makes the assumption that there could be no other explanation for the more favorable ratings. He believes that the only explanation is that the longer survey generates more accurate ratings.


Was it accuracy that was responsible? Could some other factor have caused the increase? The correct answer will somehow call into question the idea that the longer and more detailed survey has generated more accurate ratings.


> <b>(A) The provider of the new survey has only half as many company clients as has the provider of the previous survey.
</b>


This choice is tempting, as one could take it to be indicating that the provider of the new survey is somehow not as good as the provide or the previous survey and that, therefore, the new survey is likely not as accurate as the previous survey.


However, the fact that one provider has more clients than the other has does not clearly indicate anything about the quality of either provider or about the quality of the survey provided by each.


So, this choice does not have a clear effect on the argument.


> <b>(B) The new survey has been in use for only five months, while the previous one was used for three years.
</b>


This answer could be tempting since the fact that the new survey has been used for only five months could be taken as indicating that the new survey is somehow lacking something had by the previous survey, which had been used for three years. Any such lack could result in the new survey’s generating ratings that are less accurate than those generated by the previous survey.


However, the truth is that the fact that the new survey has been used for only five months does not provide any solid reason to doubt the accuracy of the ratings generated by the new survey. If the survey is well designed, it could have generated accurate ratings on the first day it was used.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) The more positive a person’s relationship with a company the more likely it is that the person will take the time to respond to a long, detailed survey for the company.
</b>


 This choice casts doubt on the conclusion that the higher ratings generated by the longer, more detailed survey are more accurate than the ratings generated by the previous survey


After all, if people with more positive relationships with the company are more likely to respond to the longer, more detailed new survey, then the ratings generated by this new survey are not likely to include a representative cross section of customer opinions of the company’s performance. Instead, the ratings generated by the new survey are likely to be disproportionately positive.


So, this choice indicates that it may be that bias, rather than accuracy, is responsible for the fact that the ratings generated by the new survey are higher than those generated by the previous survey.


Notice how clearly this answer choice weakens the argument. No conjecture is necessary for linking this choice to the conclusion. Rather, this choice provides a clear reason to doubt that the conclusion follows from the premise.


In general, whenever an author uses survey evidence to support his conclusion, he assumes that that survey has produced evidence that can in fact support the conclusion; he assumes that there are no malicious variables lurking in the survey evidence.


This answer choice underscores that that assumption was a bad one.


> <b>(D) Designing a survey that accurately captures multiple aspects of the satisfaction of a company’s customers requires detailed analysis of what to ask.
</b>


The fact that designing a survey requires detailed analysis of what to ask does not serve to call into question the accuracy of the new survey, as we are not given reason to believe that detailed analysis was not included in the process of creating the new survey.


Don’t be fooled into thinking that a general statement like this one casts doubt on the validity of a conclusion.


> <b>(E) The first few questions in the new survey are nearly identical to the first few questions that were in the previous survey.
</b>


The fact that the first few questions of the two surveys are similar could be construed as indicating that the two surveys are similar and, therefore, that concluding that the second is more accurate does not make sense.


However, the fact remains that the second survey is different from the first in that it is longer and more detailed. So, this choice does not affect the argument at all.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

Let's now discuss common incorrect answer choices in weaken questions.
