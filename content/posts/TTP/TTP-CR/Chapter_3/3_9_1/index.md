## Lesson Content
At this point, you understand that a Weaken the Argument question asks us to find a choice that does just that.

Don’t think you’d ever select an answer choice that strengthens an argument when you’re being asked to weaken it? Don’t be so sure; these opposite answer choices can be seductive.

Consider the following sample conclusion and strengthening answer choice:

> <em><u>Conclusion</u></em><em>:</em> <em>Clearly, eating bananas is the key move that resulted in powerlifter’s strength almost doubling last year.</em>

> <em>(<u>Answer Choice that Strengthens</u>) The only change the powerlifter made to his routine last year was to add eating bananas daily.</em>

Notice that the answer choice that strengthens strengthens the support for the conclusion because it shows that the only change that the powerlifter made was to add bananas to his regimen, increasing the likelihood that the bananas were in fact responsible for the change.

Selecting such an answer choice would be doing the opposite of what we want to do to correctly answer Weaken the Argument questions.

One reason that these “opposite” answers can be particularly tempting is that they stick to the scope of the argument; they have the virtue of being relevant.

Let’s look at one more example:

> <em><u>Conclusion</u></em><em>: The city will benefit financially from providing tax incentives that encourage the production of Broadway style shows. </em>

> <em>(<u>Answer Choice that Strengthens</u>) People who come to the city to attend Broadway style shows tend to spend money in the city both before and after the shows.</em>

Once again, this answer choice, if true, would <em>support</em> the conclusion above it – the opposite of what you want to do.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When answering Weaken the Argument questions, be on the lookout for choices that strengthen rather than weaken the support for the conclusions.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 25 

After determining that a low percentage of drivers were wearing seat belts while driving vehicles in Gloucester, the council of Gloucester passed a new law making it legal for police to stop and issue a traffic ticket to any driver seen not wearing a seat belt while driving in Gloucester.

Which of the following, if true, most seriously weakens the argument above?

1. [ ] (A) All car insurance companies selling insurance to drivers of vehicles in Gloucester will double the amount they charge to drivers ticketed for not wearing a seat belt.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) Since the passage of the seat belt law, the Gloucester police department has increased the number of police patrols tasked with issuing traffic tickets.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) Two years ago, the nearby town of Markham instituted a similar seat belt law and then experienced a significant increase in the percentage of drivers who wear seat belts there.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) Wearing a seat belt while driving a vehicle has been shown to substantially decrease the risk of injury or death associated with vehicular collisions.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) A large proportion of the vehicles driven in Gloucester have dark-tinted windows, making it impossible for police to see into the vehicles.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



A low percentage of drivers were wearing seat belts while driving vehicles in Gloucester. So, the council of Gloucester made it legal for police to stop and issue a traffic ticket to any driver seen not wearing a seat belt while driving in Gloucester. Of course, as we’re told, drivers do not like paying traffic tickets. So, the author concludes that the new law will be effective at compelling almost all drivers to wear their seat belts while driving.


The author has presented a plan. Any time an author presents a plan, she assumes that the plan will work. In other words, she assumes that there are not variables that will enter into the equation that will spoil the plan.


What might the author have failed to consider? What variables might have gone unnoticed?


> <b>(A) All car insurance companies selling insurance to drivers of vehicles in Gloucester will double the amount they charge to drivers ticketed for not wearing a seat belt.
</b>


Notice that this choice strengthens the argument by giving us more reason to believe that the plan will work.


The passage says that police will be able to ticket drivers who are not wearing seat belts and that drivers do not like paying traffic tickets. Now, if what this choice says is true, then drivers in Gloucester will have even more reason not to want to be ticketed for not wearing seat belts. Not only will the tickets themselves be an incentive to wear a seat belt, but also the prospect of having to pay more for insurance will be an incentive.


Of course, we are not seeking a choice that strengthens the argument; we’re seeking one that weakens it.


> <b>(B) Since the passage of the seat belt law, the Gloucester police department has increased the number of police patrols tasked with issuing traffic tickets.
</b>


 Notice that this choice strengthens the argument by giving us more reason to believe that the plan will work.


After all, if the Gloucester police department has increased the number of police patrols tasked with issuing traffic tickets, then there are more police watching for drivers not wearing seat belts than there were before the passage of the seat belt law. Of course, an increase in the number of police tasked with finding these drivers increases the likelihood that these drivers will not go unnoticed.


However, we are not seeking a choice that strengthens the argument; we’re seeking one that weakens it.


> <b>(C) Two years ago, the nearby town of Markham instituted a similar seat belt law and then experienced a significant increase in the percentage of drivers who wear seat belts there.
</b>


 What this choice says goes in the same direction as the argument.


The GMAT may not consider this choice a clear strengthener, because there could be significant differences between Gloucester and the nearby town of Markham. All the same, the fact that Markham instituted a similar seat belt law and then experienced a significant increase in the percentage of drivers who wear seat belts there gives us more conviction that Gloucester’s plan will work.


Of course, we’re not seeking a choice that goes in the same direction as the argument; we’re seeking one that weakens it.


> <b>(D) Wearing a seat belt while driving a vehicle has been shown to substantially decrease the risk of injury or death associated with vehicular collisions.
</b>


 As always, we must pay close attention to the argument’s conclusion, which is that the new law will be effective at compelling almost all drivers to wear their seat belts while driving vehicles in Gloucester.


If anything, what this answer choices says, that wearing a seat belt while driving a vehicle has been shown to substantially decrease the risk of injury or death associated with vehicular collisions, slightly strengthens the argument. After all, if riding in a car is much safer with a seat belt on, then maybe people will wear seat belts.


Because what this answer choice says goes in the same direction as the argument, it certainly does not weaken the argument.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E) A large proportion of the vehicles driven in Gloucester have dark-tinted windows, making it impossible for police to see into the vehicles.
</b>


 Notice that the author’s plan depends upon the police’s ability to see that drivers are not wearing seat belts. Once a driver is “seen not wearing a seat belt,” police may stop that driver and issue a ticket.


So, what if the police can’t see many of the drivers? What if, as this answer choice says, a large proportion of the vehicles driven in Gloucester have dark-tinted windows, making it impossible for police to see into the vehicles?


The information provided by this choice makes the argument less convincing by indicating that the new law might NOT be effective at compelling almost all drivers to wear their seat belts while driving vehicles in Gloucester, because the police will not be able to see into a large proportion of vehicles, and thus will not have cause to issue tickets to drivers of those vehicles who are not wearing seat belts.


Notice that this answer choice presents a malicious variable that the author did not consider.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Example 26 

Box office revenue from fantasy genre movies was at an all-time high last year, while the total sales revenue from fantasy genre books significantly decreased.

Which of the following, if true, most calls the conclusion into question?

1. [ ] (A) Noting a history of consistently high sales in the fantasy book genre, marketers of fantasy films last year targeted potential fantasy book buyers.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) Enthusiasts of the fantasy genre increasingly prefer the communal nature of movie-watching to the solitary nature of reading a book.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) Many fantasy book publishing and film studios garner marketing funding from the same sources, which have limited funds to allocate.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) Lending programs at libraries significantly increased their inventory of newly released fantasy books as a result of the high box office sales of fantasy movies.
5. [ ] (E) Fantasy book exchanges that facilitate low or no cost trading of fantasy books between readers of the books saw no increase in volume last year.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



The author gives a reason for an observed phenomenon:


<u>Premise</u>: Fantasy movie sales up, while fantasy book sales down.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Likely, this disparity occurred because potential fantasy book readers opted to watch fantasy movies instead.


What assumption is made by the author? One assumption is that a decrease in book<em> revenue</em> is the same as a decrease in <em>the number of people reading</em> the books. Does a decrease in book revenue necessarily equate to a decrease in the number of people reading books? Not necessarily – right? Could there be another cause of the observed change?


> <b>(A) Noting a history of consistently high sales in the fantasy book genre, marketers of fantasy films last year targeted potential fantasy book buyers.
</b>


This is an opposite answer, a strengthener rather than a weakener, as it promotes the idea that potential fantasy book buyers were “stolen” by the movie industry. The idea of increased movie marketing efforts supports the conclusion.


Be careful not to select a choice that strengthens the argument. We need a choice that weakens it.


> <b>(B) Enthusiasts of the fantasy genre increasingly prefer the communal nature of movie-watching to the solitary nature of reading a book.
</b>


This choice strengthens the idea that, as the years pass, (“increasingly”), fantasy book buyers turn into fantasy movie watchers.


Be careful not to select a choice that strengthens the argument. We need a choice that weakens it.


> <b>(C) Many fantasy book publishing and film studios garner marketing funding from the same sources, which have limited funds to allocate.
</b>


 This choice seems to indicate that, since marketing budgets for fantasy books and movies are limited, marketing budgets for fantasy books or movies would increase as marketing budgets for the fantasy products of the other type, movies or books, would simultaneously decrease.


So, a test-taker could use what this choice says to come up with a convoluted story about marketing budgets and sales that would seem to somehow weaken, or somehow strengthen, the argument.


However, regardless of what happened to marketing budgets, it would still be the case that sales of fantasy movies increased, while sales of fantasy books decreased, and it could still be that the changes occurred because people opted to watch the movies rather than read the books.


So, this choice has no effect on the argument.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) Lending programs at libraries significantly increased their inventory of newly released fantasy books as a result of the high box office sales of fantasy movies.
</b>


This choice offers an alternate explanation for the lower sales of fantasy books: People weren’t buying them; they were reading them for free instead (but still reading them nonetheless).


Of course, this choice does not destroy the argument; despite the libraries’ efforts, fantasy book readers may still have opted for the movies in large enough numbers to allow the speaker’s conclusion to hold. Still, this choice casts enough doubt to weaken the argument.


Notice that this choice attacks the author’s assumption that a decrease in book<em> revenue</em> means that there was a decrease in <em>the number of people reading</em> the books.


> <b>(E) Fantasy book exchanges that facilitate low or no cost trading of fantasy books between readers of the books saw no increase in volume last year.
</b>


This choice does the opposite of what we need, because what it says is a reason to eliminate an alternate theory of why books sales went down, the theory that people were still reading them but got them from book exchanges rather than purchasing them. By eliminating that alternative theory, it strengthens the case for believing the argument’s conclusion that sales went down because people were actually reading fantasy books less and going to fantasy movies more.Be on the lookout for subtle strengtheners like this one.


Opposite wrong answer choices like (E) are especially appealing if they happen to resemble any weakeners that you may have thought of; if the topic in an answer choice seems to resemble an idea that occurred to you on your own, be doubly careful to ensure that that choice weakens – rather than strengthens – the argument.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

</details></h5>


Let’s now consider a type of choice that does not really affect the strength of an argument but can be very tempting, a pseudo-weakener.
