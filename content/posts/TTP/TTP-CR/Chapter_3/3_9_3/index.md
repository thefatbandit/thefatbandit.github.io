## Lesson Content
Consider an argument that supports Conclusion X.

When a frameshift occurs, an incorrect answer choice will cast doubt on a conclusion that is related to or very similar to Conclusion X but is not Conclusion X.

If we’re not careful, it’s quite easy to fall for a frameshift because of the similarity between the actual conclusion of the argument and the conclusion called into question by the incorrect choice.

Consider this simple example:

> <em>Kindergarten students who are introduced to classical music as infants test better in both math and reading than their peers who were not introduced to classical music do. Clearly, classical music has a positive effect on early development.</em>

> <em>Which of the following, if true, most seriously weakens the argument?</em>

> <em>(<u>Frameshift Choice</u>) High school students who listen to classical music at home score no higher on standardized tests than do students who don’t listen to classical music.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Choice</u>) Parents who take the time to play classical music for their infants tend to be considerably more invested in the success of their children than those who do not. </em>

Notice what’s going on here.

The frameshift choice casts doubt on the wrong conclusion, one about high school students rather than early development.

The correct answer, on the other hand, clearly casts doubt on the argument’s conclusion by providing an alternative explanation for why kindergarten students who are introduced to classical music as infants test better in both math and reading than their peers who were not introduced to classical music do.

Let’s consider another:

> <em>A survey of local nursing homes found that more than 80 percent of residents preferred DVD players and video streaming services to VCRs. Thus, it must be that elderly people adapt to technology more easily than stereotypes suggest.</em>

> <em>Which of the following, if true, most strongly calls into question the conclusion above?</em>

> <em>(<u>Frameshift Choice</u>) A survey of nursing homes found that, on average, residents spend only a small percentage of their time watching movies on their own devices.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) The survey was mailed only to nursing home residents who had recently purchased a monthly subscription to at least one magazine covering trends in home movie theater technology. </em>

Notice the shift to casting doubt on a different conclusion, one about how the residents spend their time, rather than the argument’s conclusion about ease of adapting to technology.

At the same time, notice that the correct answer does actually weaken the argument by casting doubt on the value of the survey results.

The correct answer underscores that that assumption was a bad one.

💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A frameshift answer choice to a Weaken question casts doubt on a conclusion other than the conclusion of the argument, and it may mislead us into believing that the two conclusions are the same. Don’t fall for such a trick.

##  Example 28 

In the past, most personal computer users used the Circlix operating system.

Which, if true, most seriously undermines the force of the evidence cited?

1. [x] (A) Whereas, before ten years ago, computer users tended to be experts, now many computer users are novices or occasional users.
2. [ ] (B) For many users of personal computers, ease of use is not as important as top notch security.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) The number of minutes spent on the phone per personal computer technical support call has not significantly increased over the past decade.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) Marketers are not concerned that sales of the Orange operating system will be affected by concerns about difficulty using it.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) The company that produced Orange dedicates far more engineer hours to developing Orange now than the company that produced Circlix dedicated to developing Circlix in the past.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

##### Question Level: medium



This speaker argues the following:


<u>Premise</u>: Over the period of time during which Orange has become more popular, the number of calls made to support centers by people seeking help with operating their computers has increased.


<u>Conclusion</u>: It must be the case that Orange is more difficult to use than Circlix.


First, let’s think about what assumption this author is making. She’s assuming that it’s in fact a more difficult to use operating system (Orange versus Circlix), that is causing the increased number of calls. Because she utilizes cause-and-effect reasoning, she’s assuming that there is not an alternative explanation for the observation she makes. Is this a good assumption? Could there be an alternative reason for the increased number of calls?


A frameshift answer choice will focus on something other than the connection between the number of calls and the conclusion that Orange is more difficult to use, and such a choice may cast doubt on a conclusion different from the argument’s conclusion, perhaps by focusing either on the number of calls or on the ease-of-use issues, but not on both at the same time.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A) Whereas, before ten years ago, computer users tended to be experts, now many computer users are novices or occasional users.
</b>


This choice provides an alternate explanation for the increased number of calls: more people unfamiliar with computers are using computers. These people may be calling the support centers because of their own unfamiliarity or ignorance, not necessarily because the Orange operating system is particularly difficult to use. Also, earlier, when mostly experts used computers, these experts probably resolved issues on their own, without calling support centers.


When an argument involves cause-and-effect reasoning (it’s the new operating system causing the increased number of calls), one solid way to weaken such an argument is to provide a plausible alternative cause (novices using computers today versus mostly expert users ten years ago).


Notice this answer choice attacks the author’s assumption that Orange is causing the increased number of calls.


> <b>(B) For many users of personal computers, ease of use is not as important as top notch security.
</b>


 This frameshift choice doesn’t cast doubt on the conclusion of the argument. Instead, it addresses a related issue, the importance of ease of use to computer users.


Notice that the fact that, for many users of personal computers, ease of use <em>is not as important</em> as top-notch security does not conflict with or call into question the conclusion that Orange <em>is more difficult to use</em> than Circlix.


> <b>(C) The number of minutes spent on the phone per personal computer technical support call has not significantly increased over the past decade.
</b>


 What this frameshift choice says about <em>the minutes per call</em> does not change the fact that <em>the number of calls</em> has increased. So, this choice does not affect the support for the argument’s conclusion.


> <b>(D) Marketers are not concerned that sales of the Orange operating system will be affected by concerns about difficulty using it.
</b>


 The conclusion is not about sales of the Orange operating system, but rather about how difficult Orange is to use. So, this frameshift choice, which is about concerns about sales, does not add any new information that undermines the support for the conclusion of the argument.


> <b>(E) The company that produced Orange dedicates far more engineer hours to developing Orange now than the company that produced Circlix dedicated to developing Circlix in the past.
</b>


 This choice may provide reason to believe that the Orange operating system is a high-quality product and, therefore, should not be difficult to use, but we don’t actually have information that clearly indicates a connection between engineer hours and ease of use. In fact, engineers spending all that time working on Orange could have created a very complicated, difficult to use product.


So, this choice is a tempting trap choice that doesn’t clearly affect the conclusion that we are seeking to call into question.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)
