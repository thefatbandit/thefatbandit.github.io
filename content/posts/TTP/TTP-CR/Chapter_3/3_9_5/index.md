## Lesson Content
As we have discussed, the correct answer to a Weaken the Argument question will never be one that attacks a premise of the argument, because premises in GMAT arguments are taken to be true and therefore cannot be undermined.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Premises in GMAT arguments are taken to be true and therefore cannot be undermined. Therefore, an answer choice that says something that appears to undermine a premise will not weaken the argument.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider this example:

> <em>MaraSport makes running gear meant for people who run in marathons. The number of people entering marathons has been increasing steadily over the past several years, and the trend is expected to continue. Certainly then, sales of MaraSport’s running gear will continue to increase.</em>

> <em>Which of the following, if true, most seriously weakens the argument above?</em>

> <em>(<u>Choice that Appears to Attack a Premise</u>) No more marathons were held last year than were held in the previous year.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) RunningRush, which makes running gear virtually identical to that sold by MaraSport, recently began selling its gear at prices much lower than the prices at which MaraSport sells its gear.</em>

Notice what’s going on in the argument above.

The answer choice that appears to attack a premise says that <em>no more marathons</em> were held last year than were held in the previous year, an assertion that could be seen as conflicting with and therefore undermining the premise that more people are entering marathons.

If we weren’t careful, we might therefore take that answer choice to be a weakener.

Also, notice that the correct answer does weaken the argument, by showing that there is a factor that the author failed to consider.

Here’s another example in which an answer choice may appear to, but does not, undermine a premise.

> <em>There are advantages to using the new method to harvest apples on farms, advantages that make the new method clearly superior to any method now commonly used. Therefore, the new method will soon become the one most widely used.</em>

> <em>Which of the following, if true, most seriously calls into question the above prediction?</em>

> <em>(<u>Choice that Appears to Attack a Premise</u>) The difference between the effectiveness of apple harvesting methods currently used and the effectiveness of the new method is not as great when a particularly well-trained team uses the methods.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) The cost of the new equipment necessary for apple farmers to switch to the new method is more than what most are willing to spend. </em>

If we were not paying close attention, we might determine that the choice that appears to attack a premise weakens the argument.

We’re told that “there are advantages to using the new method to harvest apples on farms, <em>advantages that make the new method clearly superior to any method now commonly used</em>.” In other words, we must accept as fact that the new method is clearly superior to any method commonly used.

Notice that the correct answer, on the other hand, does weaken the argument by providing a malicious variable that the author failed to consider.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Beware of answer choices that pose as weakeners by appearing to undermine premises of arguments.
{{< /alert >}}

The following examples include multiple answer choices that appear to attack the premises of the arguments.

##  Example 30 

When companies in growing industries experience profit growth, they generally increase their numbers of employees.

Which of the following, if true, casts the most doubt on the prediction made?

1. [ ] (A) Until three years ago, SuperPower had not had a profitable year.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) Overall battery industry growth is not as great as growth currently occurring in some other industries.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) The percentage growth in profits experienced by SuperPower last year was not as great as the profit growth the company experienced the year before last year.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) Engineers at SuperPower recently figured out how to automate what has been one of the most labor-intensive aspects of SuperPower’s manufacturing processes.
5. [ ] (E) While SuperPower has five divisions, only one of the five is experiencing significant growth in profits.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



This argument is based on two key premises. The first premise is the existence of a pattern.


<u>Premise</u>: When companies in growing industries experience profit growth, they generally increase their numbers of employees.


The second premise is about SuperPower specifically.


<u>Premise</u>: SuperPower is experiencing a surge in profits as the battery industry grows as a result of certain trends.


Combined, the two premises support the conclusion.


<u>Conclusion</u>: SuperPower can be expected to increase its number of employees soon.


To weaken this argument, we have to call into question the idea that the conclusion follows from the premises. What will not work, because this is a GMAT question, is seeking to undermine the premises themselves. So, to get this question right, we have to be careful to avoid being tempted into choosing an answer choice that seems to, though doesn’t really, undermine one or more of the premises of the argument.


> <b>(A) Until three years ago, SuperPower had not had a profitable year.
</b>


This choice could be tempting if one were to perceive it as weakening the premise that SuperPower’s profits are growing.


Of course, we can’t weaken a premise, and the truth is that, even if SuperPower has been profitable for only a few years, it is still profitable and still experiencing profit growth currently. So, this choice does not weaken the support for the conclusion.


> <b>(B) Overall battery industry growth is not as great as growth currently occurring in some other industries.
</b>


This choice is written to seem to somehow undermine the premise that the battery industry is growing enough to warrant hiring new employees.


Notice, however, that this choice does not actually conflict with the idea that the battery industry is growing. While the battery industry is not growing as fast as some other industries are, it’s still growing.


So, this choice does not really affect the support for the conclusion.


> <b>(C) The percentage growth in profits experienced by SuperPower last year was not as great as the profit growth the company experienced the year before last year.
</b>


This choice is an attempt to create the impression that SuperPower’s profits are not growing, and, thus, to attack the premise that SuperPower is experiencing profit growth.


However, slower profit growth is still profit growth, and the fact stated in the passage, that SuperPower has been experiencing a surge in profit growth, remains true.


So, this choice does not really affect the support for the conclusion.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) Engineers at SuperPower recently figured out how to automate what has been one of the most labor-intensive aspects of SuperPower’s manufacturing processes.
</b>


 By saying that engineers at SuperPower have figured out how to automate what has been a labor-intensive aspect of SuperPower’s manufacturing processes, this choice indicates that SuperPower may be able to increase production without hiring more employees, or even while employing fewer people.


So, this choice clearly weakens the support for the prediction that SuperPower will soon increase the number of people it employs.


This answer choice presents us with a malicious variable that the author has failed to consider.


> <b>(E) While SuperPower has five divisions, only one of the five is experiencing significant growth in profits.
</b>


By saying that only one of SuperPower’s five divisions is experiencing profit growth, this choice might seem to undermine the premise that SuperPower is experiencing profit growth. However, the argument states as fact that SuperPower is experiencing a surge in profit growth, and the fact that growth at only one of five divisions is responsible for that surge in profit growth does not change the fact that that surge in profit growth is occurring.


So, this choice has no effect on the support for the conclusion.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Example 31 

In the past, electric motorcycles were impractical because of their heavy weights, below average performance, and short ranges.

Which of the following, if true, most serves to call into question the above prediction?

1. [ ] (A) By choosing a smaller, more basic gasoline powered motorcycle, a rider can come close to duplicating the low operating costs of an electric motorcycle.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) Duplicating in an electric motorcycle the range of a gasoline powered motorcycle will require significant innovations in the design of batteries that power electric motorcycles.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) A significant percentage of motorcycle riders base motorcycle purchasing decisions on sensory qualities, such as the roaring exhaust sound made by some gasoline powered engines.
4. [ ] (D) Some of the best engineered gasoline powered motorcycles are not much more difficult to maintain than are electric motorcycles.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) Past years’ sales data show that electric motorcycles have not been popular with younger riders, who will make up a large percentage of purchasers of motorcycles over the next decade.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



The speaker argues the following:


<u>Premise</u>: Electric motorcycle weight, performance, and range will soon match the weight, performance, and range of gasoline powered motorcycles.


<u>Premise</u>: Electric motorcycles are easier to maintain and cost less to operate than gasoline powered motorcycles.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Within a decade, electric motorcycles will have almost entirely replaced gasoline powered motorcycles on the roads.


What assumption is the author making here? What gap lies between the premises and the conclusion?


Since this is a Weaken question, we will have to find an answer that undermines the idea that the conclusion about electric motorcycles replacing gasoline powered motorcycles follows from the premises.


Also, in order to get this one correct, we’ll have to avoid choosing a choice that seems to undermine a premise.


> <b>(A) By choosing a smaller, more basic gasoline powered motorcycle, a rider can come close to duplicating the low operating costs of an electric motorcycle.
</b>


This choice says something that could be construed as weakening the premise that electric motorcycles cost less to operate than gasoline powered motorcycles.


Notice, however, that, in spite of what this choice says, that premise is still true.


Also, by the way, this choice implies that only smaller, more basic motorcycles come close to electric motorcycles in terms of operating costs.


So, this choice does not weaken the argument.


> <b>(B) Duplicating in an electric motorcycle the range of a gasoline powered motorcycle will require significant innovations in the design of batteries that power electric motorcycles.
</b>


This choice may appear to be counter to the premise that soon the range of electric motorcycles will match that of gasoline powered motorcycles, but is it really?


The truth is that this choice merely discusses the path to matching the range. The premise is still true.


So, this choice does not affect the argument.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) A significant percentage of motorcycle riders base motorcycle purchasing decisions on sensory qualities, such as the roaring exhaust sound made by some gasoline powered engines.
</b>


 This choice does undermine the conclusion, because it indicates that, in spite of all the premises being true, there is reason to believe that a significant percentage of people who ride motorcycles will continue to ride gasoline powered motorcycles. Their choices of motorcycles are based on sensory qualities, rather than on the qualities, such as cost to operate, in which electric motorcycles have an advantage.


Notice that this answer choice presents us with a malicious variable that the author has failed to consider.


> <b>(D) Some of the best engineered gasoline powered motorcycles are not much more difficult to maintain than are electric motorcycles.
</b>


This choice may appear to counter the premise that electric motorcycles are easier to maintain than are gasoline powered motorcycles.


Notice however, it does not truly conflict with the premise. It merely qualifies the situation, and the fact remains; electric motorcycles are easier to maintain. Besides, the conclusion says “almost entirely replace.” Even if a few people would continue to use “some of the best engineered” gasoline motorcycles, the support for the conclusion would not be weakened.


So, this choice does not affect the argument.


> <b>(E) Past years’ sales data show that electric motorcycles have not been popular with younger riders, who will make up a large percentage of purchasers of motorcycles over the next decade.
</b>


The argument is based on characteristics electric motorcycles have now and will have in the future. So, data from sales of electric motorcycles in the past, when electric motorcycles did not have those characteristics and were “impractical,” does not affect the argument.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)
