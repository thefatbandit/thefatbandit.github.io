## Lesson Content
You are probably familiar with how many scientific experiments work: a hypothesis is presented, and then experiments are done in which variables are changed to see how changes in variables affect results.

For instance, a researcher who feels that children’s eating sugar makes learning difficult for them could confirm that hypothesis by having a control group and an experimental group.

If the children in the control group were to learn more easily than the children in the experimental group, the researcher could say that the results support the hypothesis that sugar consumption was causing the children in the experimental group to have difficulty learning.

Strengthen the Argument questions also can use the results of changes in variables to support hypotheses or conclusions.

To see how this type of confirmation can work in a Strengthen question consider the following example:

> <em>An extraordinarily high percentage of the people in the coastal town of Mergate suffer with symptoms of mercury poisoning. Since tuna can contain mercury in large amounts, and since the people who live in Mergate are known for regularly catching and eating tuna, it is likely that the mercury poisoning symptoms seen in people who live in Mergate result from people’s accumulating mercury in their bodies via tuna consumption.</em>

> <em>Which of the following, if true, most helps to confirm the conclusion above?</em>

Here’s the conclusion that we have to confirm in order to correctly answer the question:

> <u>Conclusion</u>: It is likely that the symptoms result from people’s accumulating mercury in their bodies via tuna consumption.

The conclusion is already supported by the following facts:

> <u>Premise</u>: Tuna can contain mercury in high levels.

> <u>Premise</u>: The people who live in Mergate are known for regularly catching and eating tuna.

How could we further confirm this conclusion? One way in which we could do so is by eliminating a variable.

What if we were to find all the people in Mergate who <em>do not</em> eat tuna and see whether they exhibit symptoms of mercury poisoning? If they didn’t exhibit such symptoms, then we would have some confirmation that the high incidence of mercury poisoning symptoms in Mergate results from all the tuna eating.

Here’s a correct answer to the question:

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>No people in Mergate who avoid eating tuna because of allergic reactions they have had to tuna show any symptoms of mercury poisoning.</em>

Showing the results of eliminating the tuna eating variable is just what the correct answer does for us, by telling us that people who avoid eating tuna because of allergic reactions they have had don’t exhibit symptoms of mercury poisoning.

Notice that, for all intents and purposes, the correct answer demonstrates there is a control group of people who do not have mercury poisoning.

Let’s look at this example from another angle, an important angle.

##  Example 8 

The population of frogs in the Andes has declined precipitously.

Which of the following, if true, most strengthens the argument?

1. [ ] (A) It is widely believed that chytrid fungus was introduced into the ecosystem of the Andes by human activity.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) The greatest concentrations of frogs remaining in the Andes can be found at elevations at which chytrid fungus cannot survive.
3. [ ] (C) When air temperatures are above a certain maximum level, chytrid fungus generally does not infect frogs.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) Researchers have yet to determine what proportion of frog species have developed any effective resistance to infection by chytrid fungus.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) The geographic spread of chytrid fungus can be accelerated by warmer temperatures resulting from planetary climate change.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

##### Question Level: medium



The argument that we have to strengthen supports the following conclusion:


<u>Conclusion</u>: The likely cause of the decline in the frog population in the Andes is a spreading of chytrid fungus.


The passage includes some evidence to support the conclusion:


<u>Premise</u>: Chytrid fungus is now common in the Andes.


<u>Premise</u>: Chytrid fungus can fatally infect frogs.


Notice that the author is making a cause-and-effect claim; she claims that it is the chytrid fungus that is the cause of the decline in the frog population. How could we strengthen the case for her claim?


> <b>(A) It is widely believed that chytrid fungus was introduced into the ecosystem of the Andes by human activity.
</b>


 In saying that it is widely believed that chytrid fungus was introduced into the ecosystem of the Andes by human activity, this choice presents an explanation for why chytrid fungus can be found in the Andes. At the same time, we already know from the passage that chytrid fungus can be found in the Andes, and explaining how it got there does not add new evidence that supports the argument.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B) The greatest concentrations of frogs remaining in the Andes can be found at elevations at which chytrid fungus cannot survive.
</b>


 This choice strengthens the argument by showing what happens when a variable, the fungus, is eliminated.


By saying that where the fungus cannot survive is where the greatest concentrations of frogs are, this answer choice indicates that, when we take away the cause (the chytrid fungus), the effect (the frog population decline) also goes away, thus strengthening the argument.


Another way of thinking about this situation is that the frogs living at elevations at which chytrid fungus cannot survive represent a sort of control group because we eliminate the variable of interest (the fungus) from this group of frogs. Because this population of frogs is not showing the same decline (in fact, the greatest concentrations are found here), we have more faith that it is in fact the fungus that is responsible for the precipitous declines in frog populations elsewhere in the Andes.


> <b>(C) When air temperatures are above a certain maximum level, chytrid fungus generally does not infect frogs.
</b>


 This choice could be tempting, because it incorporates some things mentioned in the passage, warmer temperatures and chytrid fungus.


However, the truth is that describing conditions under which chytrid fungus does not infect frogs is not a way to support the conclusion that chytrid fungus is in fact infecting frogs and causing a decline in the frog population.


> <b>(D) Researchers have yet to determine what proportion of frog species have developed any effective resistance to infection by chytrid fungus.
</b>


 This answer choice could be tempting, because it could be perceived as indicating that it is likely that some species of frogs in the Andes have not developed effective resistance to infection by chytrid fungus. Thus, this choice could be perceived as expressing the unstated assumption that frogs in the Andes can in fact be infected by chytrid fungus.


Notice, however, that this choice says only that researchers have yet to determine what proportion of species of frogs have developed effective resistance to infection by chytrid fungus. In other words, this choice does not clearly indicate what proportion of frogs have developed such resistance and leaves open the possibility that all species of frogs in the Andes have developed such resistance.


So, this choice does not add any information that clearly supports the conclusion.


> <b>(E) The geographic spread of chytrid fungus can be accelerated by warmer temperatures resulting from planetary climate change.
</b>


 This choice could be tempting, because it incorporates two topics from the passage, rising temperatures and chytrid fungus, and because it seems to indicate that rising temperatures make chytrid fungus more prevalent.


However, the implications of “can be accelerated by warmer temperatures” are not clear. How fast was the fungus spreading before the warmer temperatures existed? Was there chytrid fungus in the Andes all along? Who knows? Right?


Moreover, the passage has already stated that chytrid fungus <em>is now common in the Andes</em>. If we already have the information that chytrid fungus is common in the Andes, then adding the information that there exists presently a factor, rising temperatures, that causes chytrid fungus to spread faster does not make the scenario clearly different. After all, the fact that the fungus is common in the Andes means that it has spread there.


Thus, adding the fact that the geographic spread of chytrid fungus can be accelerated by warmer temperatures doesn’t result in a significant change in the scenario presented or a strengthening of the support for the conclusion.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
