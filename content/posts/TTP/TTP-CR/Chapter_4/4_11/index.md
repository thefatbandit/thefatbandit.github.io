## Lesson Content
In most Strengthen the Argument questions, along with the correct answer, there are one or more answer choices that can appear to strengthen the argument while not actually doing so.

Noticing the difference between these so called “trap” choices and the correct answer is generally the key final move in choosing the correct answer to a Strengthen the Argument question.

> <u>Trap Choice</u>: A trap choice in a Strengthen the Argument question says something that is somehow related to the argument and that seems to strengthen the argument, and at the same time, what the trap choice says is <em>not</em> logically connected to the argument in a way such that it strengthens the argument.

> <u>Correct Answer</u>: Often, at first glance, a correct answer to a Strengthen the Argument question may not seem to be directly related to the conclusion of the argument.

Consider the following example:

> <em>The city council of Argentium wants to reduce the number of pedestrians struck by motor vehicles every year. In order to achieve their goal, they have decided to replace the white stone borders of many of the pedestrian crosswalks on the busiest streets in the city with borders of flashing lights.</em>

> <em>Which of the following, if true, provides the most support to the idea that the council’s plan will fulfill their aim?</em>

> <em>(<u>Trap Choice</u>)</em> <em>Because the borders of flashing lights will be installed as part of scheduled street repaving, the installation of the new borders will not significantly impact traffic flow on the city’s busiest streets.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) Research conducted by engineers in Argentium has shown crosswalk borders of flashing lights to be 220 percent more effective in getting the attention of speeding or distracted drivers than white stone borders.</em>

Notice the difference between the trap choice and the correct answer.

The trap choice says something that sounds positive about the plan and that is related to what the argument said about the busiest streets in the city, “the installation of the new borders will not significantly impact traffic flow on the city’s busiest streets.” The choice has some appeal, as trap choices always do, because what it says about the plan indicates that the plan is practical.

Notice, however, that the fact that implementing the plan will not significantly affect traffic flow on the city’s busiest streets does not add support to the idea that the plan will achieve the council’s goal of <em>reducing the number of pedestrians struck by cars</em>.

The correct answer, on the other hand, says something that clearly supports the idea that the plan will work – the lighted crosswalk borders are more effective than are white stone borders in getting the attention of drivers.

##  Example 9 

Mayor: I propose that we no longer allow the use of paper money at local businesses.

Which of the following, if true, most strongly indicates that implementing the Mayor’s plan would have the desired effect?

1. [ ] (A) Some citizens of the city have access to neither credit cards nor smartphones.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) The mayor does not own stock in any company that produces and sells contactless forms of payment.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) Most citizens carefully wash their hands with soap and hot water immediately after handing paper money.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) Scientists have discovered that paper money can easily transmit diseases among people handling it.
5. [ ] (E) Most local businesses are already equipped to accept contactless forms of payment.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Breakdown of the argument:


<u>Background</u>: I propose that we no longer allow the use of paper money at local businesses.


<u>Background</u>: Instead, all business will accept only contactless forms of payment, such as credit cards and payments via smartphones.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Implementing this plan would help protect and preserve the health of our citizens.


Notice that the mayor has not provided us with any evidence to support her argument. She moves from information on what her plan is – that all business will accept only contactless forms of payment, such as credit cards and payments via smartphones – to her conclusion that the plan would help protect and preserve the health of our citizens. Why does she believe her plan will help protect and preserve the health of our citizens?


Because no evidence has been provided in the argument, we’ll be seeking an answer choice that provides a premise that supports the mayor’s conclusion.


> <b>(A) Some citizens of the city have access to neither credit cards nor smartphones.
</b>


 What this choice says goes in the wrong direction.


What this choice says indicates that the plan might not be easy to pull off. That fact does not weaken the argument, but it certainly does not give us more faith that the plan will work.


If anything, what this answer choice says seems to be a very small vote against the plan’s working. However, we’re seeking a choice that increases our faith that the plan will work.


> <b>(B) The mayor does not own stock in any company that produces and sells contactless forms of payment.
</b>


 This choice is a trap choice. It sounds like something that would strengthen the argument, but it doesn’t really strengthen the argument.


This choice is tempting because, by saying that the mayor does not own stock in any company that produces and sells contactless forms of payment, this choice tends to confirm that the mayor does not have an ulterior motive for coming up with the plan of using only contactless forms of payment, and it’s easy for us to go one step further and decide that confirmation that the mayor does not have an ulterior motive for coming up with the plan indicates that the plan is likely to work.


However, the truth is that the fact that the mayor does not own stock in any company that produces and sells contactless forms of payment does not really help to confirm the mayor’s conclusion.


After all, even if the mayor did own stock in a company that produces and sells contactless forms of payment, switching to using only contactless forms of payment could help to protect and preserve the health of the citizens. Meanwhile, the plan could be totally useless even if the mayor does not own stock in any company that produces and sells contactless forms of payment


Since the fact that the mayor does not own stock in any company that produces and sells contactless forms of payment does not provide any clear indication that the plan will work, this choice does not strengthen the support for the mayor’s conclusion.


> <b>(C) Most citizens carefully wash their hands with soap and hot water immediately after handing paper money.
</b>


 The fact that most citizens carefully wash their hands with soap and hot water immediately after handing paper money would certainly not strengthen the argument.


So what if they do? How would that fact give us any more faith that their ceasing to use paper money would help protect and preserve their health?


If you choose this choice as correct, you likely did so because what it says seems plausible.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) Scientists have discovered that paper money can easily transmit diseases among people handling it.
</b>


 As mentioned, the mayor provides no evidence that accepting only contactless forms of payment would help protect and preserve the health of the citizens. She assumes that there is something about paper money that would be harmful to the health of citizens, but she never provides that evidence.


Notice that what this answer choice says, that scientists have discovered that paper money can easily transmit diseases among people handling it, provides that missing evidence, strengthening the argument.


If it is true that scientists have discovered that paper money can easily transmit diseases among people handling it, then we have more reason to believe that the mayor’s plan will work. After all, if paper money can easily transmit diseases among people handling it, then doing away with paper money transactions should help protect and preserve the health of the citizens.


Notice that what this answer choice provides is a fact that the mayor, in making her argument, assumed to be true but did not state directly.


> <b>(E) Most local businesses are already equipped to accept contactless forms of payment.
</b>


 This choice is tempting trap because it supports a conclusion related to the mayor’s conclusion. It supports a conclusion along the lines of that the plan will be easy to execute. So, this choice could seem to be a strengthener because it says something that seems positive for the prospects of the plan’s working.


However, since the mayor’s conclusion is not that the plan will be easy to execute, this choice does not strengthen the argument.


The mayor’s conclusion is that implementing this plan would help protect and preserve the health of citizens, and the fact that most local businesses are already equipped to accept contactless forms of payment does not help to confirm that implementing the plan would help protect and preserve the health of citizens.


As always, to avoid choosing a trap choice, we must pay careful attention to the conclusion.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Example 10 

In an effort to make college education more affordable, the government of Grendania plans to subsidize college education by paying up to fifteen percent of college tuition for any Grendanian who attends college full time.

Which of the following most clearly supports the conclusion above?

1. [ ] (A) The government plans to leave in place programs that fund research engaged in by university professors.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) Universities in Grendania base the levels of their tuition on the amounts that people are willing to pay.
3. [ ] (C) When college education is more affordable, more people choose to attend college full time.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) In Grendania, the cost of attending college has been increasing at a rate much faster than the general rate of price inflation.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) Even if the average college tuition in Grendania were fifteen percent lower, many people would still have difficulty affording to attend college.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



Breakdown of the Argument:


<u>Background</u>: The government of Grendania plans to subsidize college education by paying up to fifteen percent of college tuition.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Rather than make college more affordable for students, implementing this plan will serve only to increase the revenue of universities.


Notice, there is in the passage no support for the conclusion. All the passage provides is a description of the government’s plan and a conclusion about the results of the plan. So, the correct answer to the question will be a premise that supports the conclusion.


In order to figure out which choice supports the conclusion, we have to clearly understand what the conclusion is stating. The conclusion is stating two things about the results of implementing the plan: that affordability of college will not improve and that the revenue of universities will increase.


The correct choice will give us reason to believe that those effects will result from implementing the plan.


> <b>(A) The government plans to leave in place programs that fund research engaged in by university professors.
</b>


This choice could be a tempting trap, as it gives us reason to believe that universities will receive funding, or revenue, from the government.


Notice, however, that this choice merely indicates that a source of revenue that is not directly related to the subsidy plan will <em>remain unchanged</em>. Therefore, it does not really provide reason to believe that revenue will<em> increase</em> as a result of implementing the plan.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B) Universities in Grendania base the levels of their tuition on the amounts that people are willing to pay.
</b>


 At first, this choice may seem to be irrelevant to the argument, as the argument is about the government’s subsidizing education, while this choice is about what people are willing to pay. If, however, we carefully consider the implications of what this choice says, we can see the following.


Without the government subsidy, universities in Grendania base their tuition levels on what students are willing to pay.


Once the government starts subsidizing college education, the universities will base their tuition levels on the sum of what the students are willing to pay and what the government is willing to pay.


So, notice what will happen.


Once the government starts subsidizing education, the universities will increase tuition. As a result, the students will be paying what they are willing to pay, and the government will pay some as well.


Thus, college won’t become any more affordable for students. The students will be paying what they have been paying all along. At the same time, since the government will be paying some as well, the revenue of the universities will increase.


So, we can see that this choice provides very strong support for the conclusion of the argument.


> <b>(C) When college education is more affordable, more people choose to attend college full time.
</b>


This answer is a tempting trap, as it could seem to indicate that university revenue would increase given the implementation of a government plan with a purpose of making college more affordable. If more people attend college because college is more affordable, then universities should receive more tuition.


However, this choice clearly does not support the conclusion, since this choice is about what happens when college education <em>is</em> more affordable, and the conclusion is about a situation in which revenue increases even though college education <em>is </em><em>not</em> more affordable.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) In Grendania, the cost of attending college has been increasing at a rate much faster than the general rate of price inflation.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, because it provides a reason to believe that, even with government subsidies, people might still have difficulty affording college tuition.


Also, this choice could be taken as indicating that university revenue will increase because colleges will continue to increase the costs of attendance faster than the rate of inflation.


Notice, however, that the conclusion is about <em>the effects of the plan</em>, and this choice does not give us reason to believe that the subsidies would not make college at least more affordable than it would have been without the subsidies.


Also, this choice does not support the idea that the implementation of the plan will itself result in an increase in university revenue. The increase mentioned in this choice is not occurring because of the planned subsidies.


> <b>(E) Even if the average college tuition in Grendania were fifteen percent lower, many people would still have difficulty affording to attend college.
</b>


While this choice may give us reason to believe that affording college would be difficult for some Grendanians even with the subsidies, it does not give us reason to believe that the subsidies would not at least make college <em>more </em>affordable that it would have been without the subsidies.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

Let’s now discuss common incorrect choices in strengthen questions.
