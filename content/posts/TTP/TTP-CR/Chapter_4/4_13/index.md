## Lesson Content
The most blatantly wrong answer choices in Strengthen questions simply do not affect the arguments.

In many cases, such choices are fairly easy to eliminate.

Consider the following example:

> <em>The government of Golado has recently increased the taxes imposed on the sale of all alcoholic beverages in Golado. While the tax increase is sure to result in a significant decrease in consumption of alcohol in Golado, government officials believe that increasing the taxes on sales of alcohol will result in a reduction in Golado’s government budget gap.</em>

> <em>Which of the following, if true, provides evidence suggesting that the government officials’ belief is correct?</em>

> <em>(<u>Choice That Does not Affect the Argument</u>)</em> <em>In Golado, the portion of the prices paid for alcohol that is represented by taxes is greater than the portion of cigarette prices represented by taxes.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>In Golado, all of the organizations involved in dealing with health problems resulting from the consumption of alcohol are funded by the government.</em>

Here’s the conclusion for which we need support:

> <u>Conclusion</u>: Increasing the taxes on sales of alcohol will result in a reduction in Golado’s government budget gap.

Notice that the comparison made in the choice that does not affect the argument makes no difference in the support of the conclusion.

The comparison might <em>sound</em> interesting and important, but this comparison of alcohol taxes with cigarette taxes does not tell us what the effect of the increase in alcohol taxes will be, and the effect of that increase is what matters.

Now let’s consider the correct answer.

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>In Golado, all of the organizations involved in dealing with health problems resulting from the consumption of alcohol are funded by the government.</em>

The passage states that the tax increase is sure to result in a significant decrease in the consumption of alcohol in Golado.

Therefore, by saying that organizations involved in dealing with health problems resulting from the consumption of alcohol are funded by the government, the correct answer strengthens the support for the conclusion.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Some incorrect choices in Strengthen questions simply do not affect the arguments, because what they say is either not material to the conclusions or not even directly related to the foci of the arguments.
{{< /alert >}}

As mentioned above, these choices that have no effect on arguments are the ones that are most blatantly wrong.

One reason why people choose answers that don’t affect the arguments is that they can sound important.

A second reason why people choose choices that have no effect on the arguments is that these choices can be spun into convoluted stories that do affect the arguments.

For example we could say:

> “If the portion of the prices paid for alcohol that is represented by taxes is greater than the portion of cigarette prices represented by taxes, the new alcohol taxes will definitely help with closing the budget gap, because everyone knows that cigarette taxes help with closing budget gaps, and these alcohol taxes are even bigger than cigarette taxes!”

Is that story supported by the information that we have? Does it really make sense? No, but it does connect that answer choice to the conclusion.

Convoluted, unsupported stories are not the paths to choosing the right answers to Strengthen questions.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

To avoid choosing incorrect answers that have no effect on arguments, be careful not to spin convoluted stories that are not really supported by the information presented or have little to no basis in common sense.
{{< /alert >}}

Now with that said, it is VERY IMPORTANT that we are not under the bad assumption that the lines of reasoning we’ll need to use in answering Strengthen the Argument questions will not be involved; many questions, especially those on the more difficult end of the difficulty spectrum WILL utilize fairly involved, often sophisticated, lines of reasoning, BUT those lines of reasoning will be completely logical, and no illogical or unsupported leaps will be necessary for deciding that a choice supports a prediction.

##  Example 11 

It must be the case that consuming excessive amounts of caffeine results in damage to interpersonal relationships.

Which of the following, if true, most strengthens the argument?

1. [ ] (A) Mindfulness practice and guided meditation have both been shown to substantially improve a person’s ability to demonstrate self-control.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) Consuming excessive amounts of alcohol can result in damage to interpersonal relationships.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) It is well-documented that, when a person acts impulsively and irrationally during a discussion between two people, the relationship between them becomes damaged.
4. [ ] (D) Most people who become impulsive and irrational while under the effects of caffeine would act impulsively and irrationally in the same situation if not under the effects of caffeine.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) Not all people who consume excessive amounts of caffeine act impulsively and irrationally during interpersonal interactions.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Breakdown of the argument:


<u>Premise</u>: Caffeine is well-documented to reduce a person’s ability to demonstrate self-control.


<u>Premise</u>: During a discussion between two people, a person who may act calm and controlled without the use of caffeine may act impulsively and irrationally while under the effects of caffeine.


<u>Conclusion</u>: It must be the case that consuming excessive amounts of caffeine results in damage to interpersonal relationships


Notice that there is a gap in the reasoning of the argument. The author says that caffeine is well-documented to reduce a person’s ability to demonstrate self-control and thus causes the person to act impulsively and irrationally. From those facts, she concludes that it must be the case that consuming excessive amounts of caffeine results in damage to interpersonal relationships.


Is she making an assumption? Definitely. She assumes that it is true that acting impulsively and irrationally during a discussion between two people WILL damage their relationship. Notice that the author does not provide us with any proof that acting in that way damages relationships.


Furthermore, if it is NOT true that acting impulsively and irrationally during a discussion between two people WILL damage their relationship, then the argument will fall apart.


> <b>(A) Mindfulness practice and guided meditation have both been shown to substantially improve a person’s ability to demonstrate self-control.
</b>


 The fact that mindfulness practice and guided meditation have both been shown to substantially improve a person’s ability to demonstrate self-control does not give us more conviction that excessive consumption of caffeine can result in damage to interpersonal relationships.


Regardless of what the effects of mindfulness practice and guided meditation may be, excessive consumption of caffeine may or may not result in damage to interpersonal relationships.


So, this choice has absolutely no effect on the strength of the argument.


> <b>(B) Consuming excessive amounts of alcohol can result in damage to interpersonal relationships.
</b>


 Notice that the argument’s conclusion is that consuming excessive amounts of caffeine results in damage to interpersonal relationships.


The fact that consuming a different compound, alcohol, can result in damage to interpersonal relationships does not give us any more conviction in the validity of the argument related to caffeine. After all, there may be material differences between caffeine and alcohol; in fact, common knowledge tells us that there are.


So, this choice has absolutely no effect on the strength of the argument.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) It is well-documented that, when a person acts impulsively and irrationally during a discussion between two people, the relationship between them becomes damaged.
</b>


 The author assumes that it is true that acting impulsively and irrationally during a discussion between two people WILL actually damage the interpersonal relationship between them. However, her argument provides no evidence to support that assumption.


What this answer choice says, that it is well-documented that when a person acts impulsively and irrationally during a discussion between two people, the relationship between them becomes damaged, confirms the assumption made by the author. In other words, what this answer choice says provides as fact a piece of evidence that was missing from and necessary to the argument.


As you progress through this course, you’ll see that one common way to strengthen arguments is to confirm as fact an assumption made by an author.


> <b>(D) Most people who become impulsive and irrational while under the effects of caffeine would act impulsively and irrationally in the same situation if not under the effects of caffeine.
</b>


 Notice that what this choice says weakens the argument.


If most people who become impulsive and irrational while under the effects of caffeine would also act impulsively and irrationally in the same situation while not under the effects of caffeine, then we are less sure that consuming caffeine makes any difference in people’s behavior or affects whether people’s interpersonal relationships are damaged by their behavior.


After all, what this answer choice says tends to indicate that these people consuming caffeine would have interpersonal issues even without the caffeine consumption.


Of course, we are not looking for information that weakens the argument; we’re looking for a strengthener.


> <b>(E) Not all people who consume excessive amounts of caffeine act impulsively and irrationally during interpersonal interactions.
</b>


 What this choice says does not affect the argument.


The author has supported the conclusion by stating as fact that caffeine is well-documented to reduce a person’s ability to demonstrate self-control. Furthermore, the author has provided the example that, during a discussion between two people, a person who may act calm and controlled without the use of caffeine may act impulsively and irrationally while under the effects of caffeine.


The fact that not <em>all</em> people who consume excessive amounts of caffeine act impulsively and irrationally during interpersonal interactions does not negate those premises. The argument is based on the fact that caffeine consumption <em>can</em>, at least sometimes, lead to impulsive and irrational behavior. Caffeine consumption need not always lead to such behavior in order for the argument to work.


So, what this choice says neither weakens nor strengthens the support for the conclusion. The information has no impact on the argument.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

##  Example 12 

Unlike the cost of the corn from which cornflakes are made, the cost of the fuel used in producing and transporting cornflakes is only a small fraction of the total costs involved in bringing cornflakes to market.

Which of the following, if true, provides the strongest support for the economists’ prediction?

1. [x] (A) The higher the price of fuel, the more profitable it becomes to convert corn into ethanol, which can be used to fuel many types of machines.
2. [ ] (B) The wholesale price of cornflakes has not increased significantly for several years.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) When fuel prices increase, people generally end up with less money to spend on products other than fuel.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) Cornflakes have consistently been one of the most popular types of cereal among the many types that people have to choose from.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) Before last year’s increase in fuel prices, the wholesale prices of both cornflakes and fuel had been lower than their averages for recent times.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



The question asks us to find a choice that provides support for the following prediction:


<u>Conclusion</u>: If fuel prices remain at the level that they reached when they increased substantially over the past year, the wholesale price of cornflakes will increase by a large percentage.


Notice, the passage does not provide any support for that prediction. Rather, the prediction is made despite what the passage says about the fraction of the cost of bringing cornflakes to market that is represented by the cost of fuel.


In analyzing the answer choices to determine which provides support for the prediction, we have to be careful not to spin a convoluted story that makes a choice seem to support the prediction even though that choice does not really.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A) The higher the price of fuel, the more profitable it becomes to convert corn into ethanol, which can be used to fuel many types of machines.
</b>


This choice indicates that an increase in fuel prices results in increased use of corn in the making of fuel. Of course, increased use of corn in the making of fuel means increased demand for corn, which will likely result in an increase in the price of corn.


What the passage says indicates that the cost of corn constitutes a significant proportion of total the cost of bringing cornflakes to market. So, the combination of what this choice says with what the passage says clearly supports the prediction that the price of cornflakes will increase if the price of fuel remains at its current level.<br/><br/>
Notice that, while the line of reasoning that makes this choice correct is fairly involved, it is completely logical, and no illogical or unsupported leaps are necessary for deciding that this choice supports the prediction.


> <b>(B) The wholesale price of cornflakes has not increased significantly for several years.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, because a test-taker could make it seem to support the conclusion by coming up with a story like the following:


“Everyone knows that prices go up eventually. So, the fact that the wholesale price of cornflakes has not increased in years means that it’s ready to go up now. So, this choice supports the prediction that the price is going to increase.”


Of course, that story is not really supported by common sense or by what the passage or this choice says. In fact, one could just as easily run the other way with this choice, by saying that the fact that cornflake prices have not increased for several years indicates that they won’t increase any time soon.


So, this choice has no effect on the strength of the argument.


> <b>(C) When fuel prices increase, people generally end up with less money to spend on products other than fuel.
</b>


The combination of what the passage says about fuel prices having increased with what this choice says about people having less money to spend on products other than fuel when fuel prices increase tends to indicate that, currently, people are relatively low on money to spend on products other than fuel, such as cornflakes. Of course, people’s being low on money to spend on cornflakes would not support the prediction that the price of cornflakes will increase.


> <b>(D) Cornflakes have consistently been one of the most popular types of cereal among the many types that people have to choose from.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, because a test-taker could make it seem to support the conclusion by coming up with a story like the following:


“Since cornflakes have consistently been one of the most popular types of cereal, they are due for a price increase. So, this choice supports the prediction that the price is going to increase.”


Of course, that story is not really supported by common sense or by what the passage or the answer choice says. In fact, one could just as easily run the other way with this choice, by saying that the fact that cornflakes have been consistently popular indicates that their popularity has not changed recently and that, therefore, there is no reason to expect their price to increase.


So, this choice has no effect on the strength of the argument.


> <b>(E) Before last year’s increase in fuel prices, the wholesale prices of both cornflakes and fuel had been lower than their averages for recent times.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, because a test-taker could make it seem to support the conclusion by coming up with a story like the following:


“Since the price of cornflakes was below its average, it is sure to increase now, because things tend to revert to their averages.”


Of course, that story is not really supported by common sense or by what the passage or the answer choice says. Not everything that is below its past average level reverts to that level.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)
