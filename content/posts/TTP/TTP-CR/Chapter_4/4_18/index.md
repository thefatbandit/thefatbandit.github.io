## Lesson Content
Some answers to Strengthen questions are subtly written in a way such that they seem to strengthen the arguments, and, at the same time, they don’t really get the job done.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Some answers to Strengthen questions are subtly written in a way such that they seem to strengthen the arguments, and, at the same time, they don’t really get the job done. So, we could call them “pseudo-strengtheners.”
{{< /alert >}}

A pseudo-strengthener may say something that does not conflict with the argument’s conclusion but does not support the conclusion either.

In this example, the pseudo-strengthener choice neither conflicts with nor supports the conclusion:

> <em>A new method of changing the color of a car involves wrapping the car in colored vinyl rather than repainting the car in a new color. The makers of the colored vinyl expect most car owners who want to change the colors of their cars to unconventional colors to select this method over repainting their cars.</em>

> <em>Which of the following, if true, provides the most support for the expectation?</em>

> <em>(<u>Pseudo-Strengthener Choice</u>)</em> <em>The vinyl used for changing the colors of cars to unconventional colors</em><em> is produced via the use of innovative manufacturing techniques that did not exist until recently.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>Since, unlike paint, vinyl is easy to remove, someone selling a vinyl wrapped car can easily restore the car to its original color, which is likely to be a color popular with potential buyers.</em>

Here’s the expectation for which we have to find support:

> <u>Conclusion</u>: Most car owners who want to change the colors of their cars to unconventional colors will select vinyl wrapping over repainting.

The pseudo-strengthener answer choice is a tempting trap, since it uses the wording “innovative manufacturing techniques that did not exist until recently” to make the vinyl sound new and exciting and, thus, could be perceived as indicating that people will choose vinyl wrapping.

Notice, however, the issue with the pseudo-strengthener choice.

In other words, OK, great.

So, the pseudo-strengthener choice provides absolutely no support for the conclusion.

The correct answer, on the other hand, provides clear support for the expectation that most people who want to change the colors of their cars to unconventional colors will choose vinyl wrapping over repainting.

Now, let’s consider another type of example.

> <em>Transportation company GreenPS uses electric trucks to transport shipments via highway. Even though its trucks already travel at the maximum allowable speed on the highways, the logistics experts at GreenPS maintain that, as GreenPS replaces the trucks that it is currently using with new models of electric trucks, it will be able to move shipments from place to place faster.</em>

> <em>Which of the following, if true, supports the logistics experts’ contention?</em>

> <em>(<u>Pseudo Strengthener Choice</u>)</em> <em>Since the batteries that power the new trucks are more powerful than the batteries in the currently used trucks, the new trucks are capable of achieving higher speeds.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>Unlike the currently used trucks, the new trucks are equipped with technology such that their batteries can be fully charged in minutes, rather than in an hour, at highway stops.</em>

Here’s the conclusion that we have to support:

> <u>Conclusion</u>: As GreenPS replaces the trucks that it is currently using with new models of electric trucks, it will be able to move shipments from place to place faster.

The pseudo strengthener choice is rather tempting, as the passage says that GreenPS transports shipments by highway, and so it seems to make sense that a new truck that can achieve a speed higher than can be achieved by currently used trucks could transports shipments faster than they are transported currently.

Notice, however, that the passage has ruled out this potential strengthener, by saying that GreenPS’s trucks <em>already travel at the maximum allowable speed on the highways</em>.

The correct answer, on the other hand, supports the contention, by providing reason to believe that the new trucks will get from place to place faster.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When answering a Strengthen question, beware of answer choices that seem to support the conclusion but don’t really get the job done.

##  Example 18 

Casinos utilize pastel colors, including light greens, purples, and blues, in the screens of their digital betting games because these colors are the most visually soothing colors.

Which of the following, if true, does the most to strengthen the argument above?

1. [ ] (A) Non-pastel colors, such as bright reds, oranges, and yellows, are rarely used on the TV screens of commercial airplanes to catch the attention of passengers during safety demonstrations.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) Game technicians employed by casinos know how to switch the screens of digital betting games from using pastel colors to using non-pastel colors.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) The more the colors used in the screens of digital casino betting games catch people’s attention, the more money casinos will earn on those games.
4. [ ] (D) Casinos often seek to gain the attention of potential customers by using gaudy décor involving bright colors.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) Non-pastel colors, including bright reds, oranges, and yellows, are used in traffic lights because these colors rapidly catch the attention of drivers of vehicles.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



Breakdown of the argument:


<u>Background</u>: Casinos utilize pastel colors, including light greens, purples, and blues, in the screens of their digital betting games because these colors are the most visually soothing colors.


<u>Background</u>: When a gambler feels visually soothed, he or she is more likely to play casino games for an extended period of time.


<u>Premise</u>: Non-pastel colors, such as bright reds, oranges, and yellows, are proven to catch people’s attention much better than pastel colors.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Casinos that switch to using non-pastel colors in the screens of their digital betting games will maximize the money they earn on those games.


See the gap in the author’s reasoning?


He says that, because non-pastel colors, such as bright reds, oranges, and yellows, are proven to catch people’s attention much better than pastel colors, casinos that switch to using non-pastel colors in the screens of their digital betting games will maximize the money they earn on those games.


Notice that, at the moment, there is no clear link between “the catching of people’s attention in the games” and “the maximizing of the money they earn on those games.” The author must assume that the more the colors used in the screens of digital casino betting games catch people’s attention, the more money casinos will earn on those games.


Our job is to find an answer choice that strengthens the argument.


> <b>(A) Non-pastel colors, such as bright reds, oranges, and yellows, are rarely used on the TV screens of commercial airplanes to catch the attention of passengers during safety demonstrations.
</b>


 What this choice says goes in the wrong direction.


After all, safety demonstrations on airplanes would certainly seem to be something one would want people to pay attention to. So then, why are non-pastel colors rarely used on the TV screens of commercial airplanes to catch the attention of passengers during safety demonstrations?


To be clear, what this answer choice says does not weaken the argument, because we already know from the passage that non-pastel colors are proven to catch people’s attention much better than pastel colors, and catching the attention or airline passengers might be quite different from catching the attention of gamblers. However, it certainly does not go in the direction we’re seeking; we’re seeking a choice that strengths the argument, and this choice certainly does not.


It’s always helpful to be cognizant of the direction in which an answer choice takes us.


> <b>(B) Game technicians employed by casinos know how to switch the screens of digital betting games from using pastel colors to using non-pastel colors.
</b>


 This choice is tempting because it supports a conclusion related to the author’s conclusion. It supports the conclusion that switching to non-pastel colors would be relatively easy for casinos to do. After all, if game technicians employed by casinos know how to switch the screens of digital betting games from using pastel colors to using non-pastel colors, then it seems likely that making the switch would be no big deal.


Of course, the author’s conclusion is not about how easy it would be for casinos to make the switch; it’s that making the switch will result in the casinos’ maximizing the money they earn on digital betting games.


So, this choice provides support for the wrong conclusion and has no effect on the strength of the argument.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(C) The more the colors used in the screens of digital casino betting games catch people’s attention, the more money casinos will earn on those games.
</b>


 Notice that what this answer choice says strengthens the argument by stating as fact an assumption made by the author in making his argument.


As mentioned in the introduction to this explanation, the author assumes that the more the colors used in the screens of digital casino betting games catch people’s attention, the more money casinos will earn on those games.


Is that assumption a good one or a bad one? If it is a good assumption, the argument is stronger. If it is a bad one, the argument is weaker.


What this answer choice does is confirm that that assumption made by the author is a good one, by stating that assumption as fact.


Whenever we can take an assumption made by an author and turn that assumption into a premise (a fact), the argument will be stronger.


> <b>(D) Casinos often seek to gain the attention of potential customers by using gaudy décor involving bright colors.
</b>


 This choice is a tempting pseudo-strengthener.


The fact that casinos often seek to gain the attention of potential customers by using gaudy décor involving bright colors may seem to strengthen the support for the conclusion that casinos that switch to using non-pastel colors in the screens of their digital betting games will maximize the money they earn on those games. It could seem to indicate that using bright colors, rather than pastel colors, in the games is a good idea.


However, the truth is that what this choice says makes no difference in the support for the conclusion.


After all, the fact that casinos seek to gain the attention of potential customers by using gaudy décor involving bright colors does not indicate anything about whether casinos will maximize earnings by using bright colors rather than pastel colors in digital betting machines. The passage tells us that pastel colors are soothing and that bright colors are attention getting, and this choice does not tell us whether soothing gamblers using digital betting machines or getting their attention results in greater earnings.


In fact, this choice does not even tell us whether casinos that seek to gain the attention of potential customers by using gaudy décor involving bright colors succeed in any way as a result of their using bright colors.


All this choice tells us is that casinos often use bright colors to seek to gain attention, and we already know from the passage that bright colors attract attention.


In order to avoid choosing a pseudo-strengthener like this one, we have to pay attention to the effects of the answer choices we are considering rather than go with something that seems to in some vague way strengthen an argument.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E) Non-pastel colors, including bright reds, oranges, and yellows, are used in traffic lights because these colors rapidly catch the attention of drivers of vehicles.
</b>


 We must remember that the premises provided to us in the argument are statements of fact. There is no way to strengthen or “make better” a statement of fact.


What have we been told by the passage? We have been told that non-pastel colors, such as bright reds, oranges, and yellows, are proven to catch people’s attention much better than pastel colors. We can’t make that fact any more of a fact.


However, if you chose this choice as the correct answer, you tried to do just that. You said, “If non-pastel colors, including bright reds, oranges, and yellows, are used in traffic lights because these colors rapidly catch the attention of drivers of vehicles, then we have more faith that the author’s plan will work.”


However, we already know that non-pastel colors, such as bright reds, oranges, and yellows, are proven to catch people’s attention much better than pastel colors. So, although what this answer choice says certainly goes in the same direction as the argument, it does not strengthen it.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

##  Example 19 

Last year, Brilliant Broadcasting doubled, from the amount that it spent the year before, the amount of money that it spent to advertise to potential viewers its hit television show <em>Who wants to win the most?</em> All the same, television rating services indicated that the average number of people who watched <em>Who wants to win the most?</em> each week last year was virtually unchanged from the average number who watched the show each week the year before.

Which of the following, if true, most strongly supports the executives’ claim?

1. [x] (A) Last year, two other major broadcasting companies began airing shows that were essentially thinly disguised but well produced copies of <em>Who wants to win the most?</em>
2. [ ] (B) Brilliant Broadcasting makes past episodes of <em>Who wants to win the most?</em> available for online viewing.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) <em>Who wants to win the most?</em> was Brilliant Broadcasting’s most popular show last year.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) Last year, the percentage of people who knew what time of day <em>Who wants to win the most?</em> comes on was greater than the percentage who knew the previous year.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) The numbers provided by the television rating services are not exact counts of viewers but rather estimates based on samples.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



To answer this question, we need a choice that supports the following claim made by Brilliant Broadcasting’s executives:


<u>Conclusion</u>: The increase in advertising spending paid off.


The executives’ claim seems to be in conflict with the information presented in the passage, as the company doubled spending on advertising, but the number of viewers of the show did not increase.


To get this question correct, we have to find a choice that supports the executives’ claim despite the fact that the number of viewers of the show did not increase.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A) Last year, two other major broadcasting companies began airing shows that were essentially thinly disguised but well produced copies of <em>Who wants to win the most?</em>
</b>


 If, last year, two other major broadcasters began airing shows that were essentially copies of <em>Who wants to win the most?</em>, one might have reasonably expected that those two new shows would have taken some viewers from Who <em>wants to win the most?</em>. However, even though there were two new shows that could have taken its viewers, <em>Who wants to win the most?</em> had the same number of viewers last year as it had the year before.


The fact that its number of viewers didn’t decrease, even though there were two shows that could have taken some of its viewers, could be taken as an indication that the advertising paid off, by keeping the number of viewers the same in the face of new competition.


So, this choice supports the claim that the increase in spending paid off.


> <b>(B) Brilliant Broadcasting makes past episodes of <em>Who wants to win the most?</em> available for online viewing.
</b>


 We could spin a story about how the extra advertising probably somehow paid off through increases in online viewers, even as the number of people who watched <em>Who wants to win the most?</em> on television remained the same.


However, this choice does not provide us with any information on the number of online viewers. It tells us only that the shows are available online. So, the idea that the advertising spending paid off by affecting the number of online viewers has no basis in information that we are given.


In answering Critical Reasoning questions, we have to be careful not to concoct unsupported convoluted stories.


> <b>(C) <em>Who wants to win the most?</em> was Brilliant Broadcasting’s most popular show last year.
</b>


 This choice is a pseudo-strengthener, as what it says about the relative popularity of the show could seem to indicate that the advertising spending paid off.


However, the truth is that this choice does not support the claim. For one thing, it could be that none of Brilliant Broadcasting’s other shows are popular at all, and so <em>Who wants to win the most?</em> is simply popular by default.


Whatever the case is, a comparison of the popularity of <em>Who wants to win the most?</em> with the popularity of the company’s other shows does not give us a clear reason to believe that the advertising spending paid off. After all, even if what this choice says is true, the fact remains that the number of viewers of <em>Who wants to win the most?</em> did not increase.


> <b>(D) Last year, the percentage of people who knew what time of day <em>Who wants to win the most?</em> comes on was greater than the percentage who knew the previous year.
</b>


 This choice is a pseudo-strengthener, as the fact that more people know what time the show is on could seem to indicate that the increase in advertising spending paid off.


However, the truth is that the passage has already stated that the number of viewers did not increase, and an increase in the number of people who know what time the show is on without any increase in viewers is virtually worthless to Brilliant Broadcasting. So, regardless of the fact that there was an increase in the number of people who knew what time the show is on, we still don’t have support for the conclusion that there was any actual payoff from the increase in advertising spending.


> <b>(E) The numbers provided by the television rating services are not exact counts of viewers but rather estimates based on samples.
</b>


 The fact that the numbers provided by the television rating services are not exact counts of viewers but rather estimates based on samples could be taken as indicating that it is possible that the agencies’ numbers are incorrect and that, therefore, it is possible that, in actuality, there was an increase in viewers of <em>Who wants to win the most?</em>.


However, having a reason to consider something possible is not the same as having support for the conclusion that it is true.


So, this choice provides no support for the claim that the increase in advertising spending did pay off.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

Let’s wrap up our discussion of Strengthen question incorrect choice types by discussing choices that do the opposite of strengthen arguments.
