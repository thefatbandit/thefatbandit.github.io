## Lesson Content
As just mentioned, strengthening an argument involves providing new information that strengthens the support for the argument’s conclusion.

To see strengthening in action, let’s consider an example:

> <em>Alice knows all about making smoothies, and she makes smoothies that all her friends like. Clearly, her new smoothie shop will be a success. </em>

We can strengthen that argument by providing new information that makes the case for the conclusion more convincing.

> <em>Her new smoothie shop will be a success.</em>

So, to strengthen the argument, we need information that makes us more convinced that Alice’s new smoothie shop will be a success.

> <em>Alice’s new smoothie shop is going to be located right next to a bike path on which thousands of people who like smoothies bike every day.</em>

Wow.

Now, as we saw in the case of the above example, before we can strengthen an argument, we must first understand the logic of that argument.

Also, in the Assumption chapter and the Weaken the Argument chapter, a major theme presented was that of identifying the missing information in arguments.

To see how identifying missing information applies to strengthening arguments, let’s revisit the disease x argument that we discussed in previous chapters:

> <em>All people in houses in which communicable diseases are present should be quarantined. Two people in the yellow house on Maple Street came down with disease x yesterday. Therefore, all people in the yellow house on Maple Street should be quarantined.</em>

From the past chapters, you already know that the author assumes that disease x is a communicable disease.

So, one way in which we could strengthen this argument is to make it known that disease x is in fact a communicable disease.

So, as you progress through this chapter, it’s important to continue to look for missing information.

Consider the following example.

##  Example 1 

It has been discovered that the larvae of a certain kind of wax moth can eat polyethylene, a type of plastic.

Which of the following, if true, most strongly supports the hypothesis?

1. [ ] (A) Wax moth larvae normally hatch and grow on beeswax, which is somewhat similar chemically to polyethylene.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) When wax moth larvae consume polyethylene, they convert it into a non-plastic compound.
3. [ ] (C) People create approximately 80 million tons of polyethylene per year.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) The large amounts of plastic in landfills and ocean dumps are reducing the quality of life on our planet.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) It may be that the wax moth larvae would not continue to consume plastic should they encounter it outside of the specific conditions created in laboratory experiments.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

##### Question Level: easy



Breakdown of the Argument:


<u>Premise</u>: The larvae of a certain kind of wax moth can eat polyethylene, a type of plastic.


<u>Premise</u>: The larvae can consume large quantities of polyethylene relatively quickly.


<u>Conclusion</u>: The use of such moth larvae in large numbers could significantly help in the reduction of plastic pollution.


The scientists’ hypothesis is supported by the facts that the larvae eat plastic and that they eat it quickly. Those facts provide some reason to believe that the use of such moth larvae in large numbers could significantly help in the reduction of plastic pollution.


Now, to correctly answer the question, we need to find the choice that provides additional support for that hypothesis.


> <b>(A) Wax moth larvae normally hatch and grow on beeswax, which is somewhat similar chemically to polyethylene.
</b>


 This choice doesn’t have the effect we need. It may help to explain why the larvae eat plastic, but we already know that they eat plastic, and explaining why that fact is true does not make the support for the conclusion any stronger.


So, this choice does not affect the support for the conclusion.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B) When wax moth larvae consume polyethylene, they convert it into a non-plastic compound.
</b>


 The choice provides additional support for the conclusion.


We already know that the larvae eat plastic.


Now, by confirming that, when the larvae eat plastic, they change the plastic into something other than plastic, this choice provides additional support for the conclusion that the use of the larvae could significantly help in the reduction of plastic pollution.


After all, the fact that, when the larvae eat plastic, the larvae don’t just chop the plastic into little pieces of plastic but instead change it into something other than plastic makes the case for the conclusion more convincing than it was.


> <b>(C) People create approximately 80 million tons of polyethylene per year.
</b>


 While this choice tends to confirm that, if the larvae could be used to reduce plastic pollution, such use would be quite valuable, it does not provide information that confirms that the larvae could in fact be used in that way.


> <b>(D) The large amounts of plastic in landfills and ocean dumps are reducing the quality of life on our planet.
</b>


 While this choice provides a reason for reducing the amount of plastic on the planet, it does not confirm that using the larvae would be an effective way of reducing the amount of plastic.


> <b>(E) It may be that the wax moth larvae would not continue to consume plastic should they encounter it outside of the specific conditions created in laboratory experiments.
</b>


 This choice weakens, rather than strengthens, the argument, by providing a reason to question the effectiveness of using wax moth larvae to reduce plastic pollution.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
