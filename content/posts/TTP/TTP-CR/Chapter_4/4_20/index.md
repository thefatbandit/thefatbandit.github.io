## Lesson Content
Strengthen the Argument questions will sometimes include words such as “except,” “not,” or “least” in their question stems to make the questions a bit more challenging.

> <em>Each of the following</em><em>, if true, provides additional support for the above conclusion EXCEPT:</em>

> <em>If all of the following are true, which of them does <u>not</u> provide further justification for the contention made?</em>

> <em>Assuming that all of the following statements are true, which does the LEAST to strengthen the argument?</em>

Let’s consider the following question stem:

> <em>All of the following, if true, strengthen the argument, EXCEPT:</em>

In answering such a question, our job would be to find and eliminate four answer choices that strengthen the argument.

This process seems easy, but test-takers presented with such questions often make a predictable mistake in logic: they search for an answer that weakens the argument.

We must avoid this mistake.

So – just to be clear, if we were faced with a question that asked, “All of the following, if true, strengthen the argument, EXCEPT,” our job would be to eliminate four of the five choices.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When Strengthen the Argument questions include words such as “except,” “not,” or “least” in their question stems, our job is to eliminate four choices that do strengthen the argument. The fifth, and correct, answer choice will either weaken the argument or have no effect on the strength of the argument.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 21 

Once native to mainland Australia, but more recently found living only on islands, the Tasmanian Devil thrived in large numbers until about 1990, when its population began to decline.

All of the following, if true, would strengthen the biologists’ conclusion, EXCEPT:

1. [ ] (A) The Dingo, which biologists believe to be the primary predator of the Tasmanian Devil on the mainland, has never colonized the islands on which the Devils live.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) On the islands on which it lives, the natural habitat of the Tasmanian Devil did not significantly decrease during 1990 or the years following 1990.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) On an island on which no Tasmanian Devils are afflicted with DFTD, the Tasmanian Devil population is flourishing.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) Over time, Tasmanian Devils have learned to evade traps, snares, and other devices used by hunters.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) Some Tasmanian Devils afflicted with DFTD have been able to reproduce and to effectively care for their young.

##### Question Level: medium



We are told that the Tasmanian Devil thrived in large numbers until about 1990, when its population began to decline. We are also told that, around the same time, Devil Facial Tumor Disease (DFTD), which can lead to starvation and death, was observed in a large number of Tasmanian Devils. As a result of these events’ occurring at about the same time, biologists concluded the following:


<u>Conclusion</u>: DFTD was likely the cause of the decline in the Tasmanian Devil population.


In other words, these biologists concluded that, of all of the things that could cause a decline in the Tasmanian Devil population, the one thing that was responsible was DFTD. This is a big assumption, right? The biologists assumed that there were no other explanations for the decline. Of course, the author is making a cause-and-effect claim.


Let’s think about how we could weaken this argument. One way in which we could weaken the argument would be to come up with some plausible alternative explanations for the decline in the Tasmanian Devil population. In doing so, we’d be effectively attacking the biologists’ assumption that their cause-and-effect claim is correct and that there are no other alternative explanations for the observation.


Let’s be creative and list some potential reasons:


<em>The food source of the Tasmanian Devil disappeared. </em>


<em>Hunters began trapping the Tasmanian Devil to sell its fur.</em>


<em>The climate changed in a way that was disadvantageous to the Tasmanian Devil. </em>


We could continue adding potential weakeners, but you get the idea. Now, consider the following statement:


<em>The food source of the Tasmanian Devil did not disappear. </em>


Would such a statement, if true, strengthen or weaken the argument? Let’s think carefully about this statement. We know that the following statement weakens the argument:


<em>The food source of the Tasmanian Devil disappeared. </em>


As we already know, it weakens the argument by introducing an alternative cause. So, if that statement weakens the argument by introducing an alternative cause, then this statement strengthens the argument by eliminating an alternative cause:


<em>The food source of the Tasmanian Devil did not disappear.</em>So, given a cause-and-effect argument, any time we can eliminate an alternative cause for the effect, we strengthen the argument.


Now, since this question is a Strengthen “EXCEPT” question, we have to eliminate four choices that strengthen the argument – those choices are the incorrect choices – and we have to find the one choice that does not strengthen the argument, which is the correct answer.


> <b>(A) The Dingo, which biologists believe to be the primary predator of the Tasmanian Devil on the mainland, has never colonized the islands on which the Devils live.
</b>


 By saying that dingoes have not colonized the islands on which the Tasmanian Devils live, this choice eliminates an alternate cause of the decline and, thus, strengthens the argument.


Strengthens? Yes.


> <b>(B) On the islands on which it lives, the natural habitat of the Tasmanian Devil did not significantly decrease during 1990 or the years following 1990.
</b>


 By saying that the habitat of the Tasmanian Devils on the islands did not decrease, this choice eliminates an alternative cause of the decline and, thus, strengthens the argument.


Strengthens? Yes.


> <b>(C) On an island on which no Tasmanian Devils are afflicted with DFTD, the Tasmanian Devil population is flourishing.
</b>


 By discussing what has occurred on an island where no Tasmanian Devils are afflicted with DFTD, this choice shows the results of eliminating a variable, DFTD. On that island, the Devil population is flourishing.


Since, without DFTD, a population of Tasmanian Devils is flourishing rather than declining, what this choice says tends to confirm the conclusion that DFTD is the cause of the population decline.


Strengthens? Yes.


> <b>(D) Over time, Tasmanian Devils have learned to evade traps, snares, and other devices used by hunters.
</b>


 By indicating that it is likely that Tasmanian Devils are not being caught in traps and snares, this choice eliminates an alternate cause of the population decline, traps and snares, and, thus, strengthens the argument.


Strengthens? Yes.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E) Some Tasmanian Devils afflicted with DFTD have been able to reproduce and to effectively care for their young.
</b>


 By indicating that some Tasmanian Devils afflicted with DFTD have been able to continue to reproduce and care for young, this choice slightly weakens the argument that DFTD is the cause of the population decline.


Strengthens? No.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
