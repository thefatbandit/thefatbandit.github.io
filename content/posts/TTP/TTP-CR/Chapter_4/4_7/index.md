## Lesson Content
As you well know, an assumption is a piece of missing information that must be true in order for the logic of the argument to hold.

Every argument relies upon many assumptions; it isn’t possible to include every single piece of relevant information in an argument.

When an assumption is uncontroversial (it is something that “everyone would agree is true”), the presence in the argument of that unstated assumption has no impact on the strength of the argument.

For example, when an argument involves mathematics, there is no need to state that putting the same values into a particular formula produces the same results every time; leaving aside unusual cases, that is something upon which everyone agrees.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When an assumption upon which an argument relies is uncontroversial (it is something that “everyone would agree is true”), the presence in the argument of that unstated assumption has no impact on the strength of the argument.
{{< /alert >}}

The problem lies with controversial assumptions, ones that may well be false.

When an argument relies upon a controversial assumption, that assumption is a point of weakness in the argument.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When an argument relies upon a controversial assumption, that assumption is a point of weakness in the argument. Since the assumption is effectively a premise in the argument (an unwritten one), if it were false, then the conclusion would not be fully supported, and the argument would not be sound.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider this simple example:

> <em>All men are mortal. Therefore, Socrates is mortal.</em>

In this argument, that Socrates is a man is a controversial assumption.

Therefore, we could strengthen this argument by explicitly stating as fact the author’s assumption that Socrates is a man:

> <em>All men are mortal. Socrates is a man. Therefore, Socrates is mortal.</em>

Now the argument is much stronger.

Let’s look at another more detailed example:

> <em>Biochemical X contains estrogen-mimicking hormones that decrease the fertility of several species of fish, including salmon, trout, and bass. Therefore, allowing the biochemical to leak into Lake Garda will have a permanent and irreversible impact, wiping out all of its fish stocks.</em>

This argument depends on two major assumptions – first, that Lake Garda contains only species of fish that will be affected by the biochemical, and second, that the fish cannot adapt.

So, how do we strengthen an argument that relies upon controversial assumptions? The most effective method is to explicitly state that the assumptions are true.

> <em>Lake Garda’s fish are all vulnerable to biochemical X and would be unable to adapt in such a way as to neutralize its effects.</em>

Adding the above information makes the argument much stronger.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

An argument can be strengthened via the explicit stating of an unstated assumption upon which the argument relies.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 4 

A study was done and found that, when CEOs were dealing with negative situations, they were much more likely to take action when they were presented with the consequences of doing nothing.

Which of the following statements, if true, most supports the conclusion drawn from the study?

1. [ ] (A) Many CEOs seek the counsel of others when they encounter negative problems.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) Sometimes, when a CEO is faced with a negative situation, doing nothing is a viable option, as it allows the passage of time to positively affect the situation.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) It is generally the case that, when a CEO is faced with a negative situation, taking action produces better results than does doing nothing.
4. [ ] (D) CEOs tend to make decisions more quickly when information they receive comes from people whom they trust.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) It is normal in business for CEOs to base decisions that they make on secondhand information.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

##### Question Level: medium



The conclusion of the argument is the following:


<u>Conclusion</u>: CEOs faced with negative situations should always be made aware of the consequences of doing nothing.


The conclusion is based on the following premise:


<u>Premise</u>: A study has found that CEOs are more likely to take action when they are made aware of the consequences of doing nothing.


Notice that an assumption connects the premise to the conclusion. The argument assumes that a CEO’s taking action is beneficial.


Is this a good assumption? Maybe not, right? After all, it could very well be the case that doing nothing is more beneficial than doing something, right?


So, if this were a Weaken the Argument question, our job would be to attack that assumption. However, in this case, our job is to help the author bolster the argument. We can do so by confirming (as factual) one of her assumptions.


> <b>(A) Many CEOs seek the counsel of others when they encounter negative problems.
</b>


 The fact that CEOs seek the counsel of others indicates that CEOs would likely be open to hearing from others about the consequences of doing nothing. However, it does not add support to the conclusion that CEOs should be made aware of the consequences of doing nothing.


> <b>(B) Sometimes, when a CEO is faced with a negative situation, doing nothing is a viable option, as it allows the passage of time to positively affect the situation.
</b>


 This choice clearly does not strengthen the argument. Whereas the argument is that CEOs should be made aware of the consequences of doing nothing, because, when CEOs are made aware of those consequences, they are more likely to take action, this choice provides a reason not to take action. In other words, what this answer choice says weakens the argument, doing the opposite of what this question asks us to do.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) It is generally the case that, when a CEO is faced with a negative situation, taking action produces better results than does doing nothing.
</b>


 This choice directly states an assumption upon which the argument depends, that a CEO’s taking action generally results in an outcome better than one that results from a CEO’s doing nothing.


If taking action did NOT generally result in a better outcome, then concluding that one should always do something that increases the likelihood of a CEO’s taking action would not make sense.


So, by confirming that taking action DOES generally result in a better outcome, this choice significantly strengthens the support for the argument’s conclusion.


> <b>(D) CEOs tend to make decisions more quickly when information they receive comes from people whom they trust.
</b>


 The fact that CEOs make decisions more quickly when the information they receive comes from people whom they trust does not strengthen the support for the conclusion, which is specifically that CEOs faced with negative situations should be made aware of the consequences of doing nothing.


The conclusion is not about whether people whom CEOs trust should be the ones to provide CEOs with information. It’s about the effect of providing them with information.


> <b>(E) It is normal in business for CEOs to base decisions that they make on secondhand information.
</b>


 This is a trap choice, because it could be taken as implying that a CEO’s becoming aware of the consequences of a course of action and acting on that information is normal in business, and that, therefore, making CEOs aware of those consequences makes sense.


The truth is that the value of a CEO’s being made aware of the consequences of doing nothing does not depend on whether it is normal for CEOs to base decisions on secondhand information. What’s normal and what should be done are not the same thing.


So, this choice has no effect on the support for the conclusion.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

Let’s now discuss how we can strengthen an argument by addressing an issue that could affect the validity of the conclusion.
