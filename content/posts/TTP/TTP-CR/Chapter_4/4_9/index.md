## Lesson Content
In the arguments found in Strengthen the Argument questions, authors will often use cause-and-effect reasoning; in other words, they will link an effect to a cause.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Whenever an author makes a cause-and-effect claim, he assumes that there cannot be an alternative explanation for the effect observed. In other words, he assumes that his cause-and-effect claim is completely accurate.
{{< /alert >}}

In other words, whenever we can rule out an alternative explanation for an observed effect, we have strengthened the argument.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

One way to strengthen the support for a cause-and-effect conclusion is to rule out alternative causes.
{{< /alert >}}

Remember, we don’t have to prove a conclusion correct to strengthen support for it.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When an argument uses cause-and-effect reasoning, by eliminating even one credible alternative cause for the observed effect, we can strengthen the support for the conclusion.
{{< /alert >}}

For example, let’s say that an author has found that lizards living in Madagascar are, on average, significantly larger than lizards of the same species living in other locations.

Ok – it’s clear that the author believes that it is the UV light that is causing the Madagascan lizards to grow so large.

Definitely, right? Let’s think of some:

> In Madagascar, in contrast to other locations, there are unusually abundant food sources that promote lizard growth.

> In Madagascar, unlike in other areas, there are unusually many predators that eat large quantities of baby lizards, which tend to be significantly smaller than older lizards.

> In Madagascar, there are significantly better sources of camouflage, which protect large lizards from predators, than there are in other locations.

> Unlike in other locations, the river water found in Madagascar, from which the lizards drink, contains an unusually high level of potent growth-promoting vitamins and minerals.

Any of those explanations (and others) are possible alternative explanations for why the Madagascan lizard are growing so large, but of all the thousands of possible explanations for why these lizards living in Madagascar are growing so large, the author believes (1) that none of those alternative explanations are true, and (2) that UV light is the only possible explanation.

So, therefore, we could strengthen the author’s cause-and-effect argument by ruling out any alternative explanation.

> There ARE NOT unusually abundant food sources that promote lizard growth in Madagascar than there are in other locations.

> In Madagascar, there ARE NOT unusually many predators that eat large quantities of baby lizards, which tend to be significantly smaller than older lizards.

> In Madagascar, there ARE NOT significantly better sources of camouflage, which protect large lizards from predators, than there are in other locations.

> The river water found in Madagascar, from which the lizards drink, DOES NOT contains an unusually high level of potent growth-promoting vitamins and minerals.

The more alternative explanations that we can rule out, the stronger the argument becomes.

Consider the following mini-example:

> <em>If pipistrelle bats are exposed to noise at levels greater than 50 decibels, they are unable to navigate effectively. If the bats cannot navigate, they cannot feed and starve to death. Over the past year, there has been a 75 percent increase in the number of dead bats found within a 2-kilometer radius of Lincoln town center. Therefore, it is likely that there has been noise above 50 decibels in that area.</em>

> <em>Which of the following, if true, would add the most support to the above conclusion?</em>

The conclusion of this argument is not unreasonable, but the premises do not provide particularly strong support.

Therefore, the argument could be strengthened via the addition of a premise that rules out explanations other than noise.

Here is a possible correct answer:

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer)</u></em> <em>When many of the dead bats were tested for the presence of pathogens associated with diseases that kill bats, only two percent of the tested bats harbored one or more of those pathogens.</em>

It’s important to see what is happening here.

With that idea in mind, you can rule out alternate causes to correctly answer this example question.

##  Example 7 

A year ago, Greenville Elementary School’s playground was renovated, with the goal of making it safer.

Which of the following, if true, most strengthens the conclusion?

1. [x] (A) Last year, the supervisors at Greenville Elementary School’s playground and the playground rules were the same as they had been the previous year.
2. [ ] (B) After becoming aware of the decrease in injuries on Greenville Elementary School’s playground, the administration of a nearby school decided to renovate that school’s playground.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) The students who used the playground both years said that they preferred using the newly renovated playground.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) Because of unusually rainy weather last year, students spent less time on Greenville Elementary’s playground last year than students did the previous year.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) Last year, fewer injuries occurred at Greenville Elementary’s renovated playground than occurred at the playground at Amity Elementary School.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

##### Question Level: easy



The author concludes the following.


<u>Conclusion</u>: The changes made Greenville Elementary School’s playground a safer place.


This is clearly a cause-and-effect argument, as the author argues that the changes to the playground directly contributed to the reduction of injuries.


Notice the assumption made in this argument, that there is no other explanation for the playground becoming a safer place. Is this a good assumption? Maybe, but maybe not – right?


Isn’t it possible that the changes made to the playground did not in fact cause the reduction in injuries? Couldn’t there be another, different, cause? Couldn’t there be an alternative explanation that would explain the decrease in the number of injuries? Couldn’t the author be missing something?


One way to strengthen this argument is to eliminate other potential causes.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A) Last year, the supervisors at Greenville Elementary School’s playground and the playground rules were the same as they had been the previous year.
</b>


 This information removes year-to-year differences in supervision and playground rules as possible explanations for the difference in rates of playground injuries. In other words, this fact rules out two alternative causes for the reduction in injuries.


Notice that this answer choice doesn’t eliminate all potential alternative explanations for the reduction in injuries, but it does address two factors that could affect student safety.


By providing reasons for ruling out alternative explanations for the reduction in injuries, this answer strengthens support for the conclusion.


> <b>(B) After becoming aware of the decrease in injuries on Greenville Elementary School’s playground, the administration of a nearby school decided to renovate that school’s playground.
</b>


 While the fact that another school’s administration decided to renovate that school’s playground after seeing the reduction in injuries at Greenville Elementary’s playground may be a vote of confidence in the idea that renovating the playground made it safer, that fact does not truly indicate that the renovation caused the change.


> <b>(C) The students who used the playground both years said that they preferred using the newly renovated playground.
</b>


 This choice does not support the conclusion of this argument. The conclusion we have to support is a cause-and-effect conclusion about safety, not one about what students prefer.


> <b>(D) Because of unusually rainy weather last year, students spent less time on Greenville Elementary’s playground last year than students did the previous year.
</b>


 This information weakens the argument, by providing a possible alternative reason for the decrease in injuries, since it could be that fewer injuries occurred at the playground, not because of the renovation, but because of what this choice mentions: students spent less time on the playground last year than students did the previous year.


> <b>(E) Last year, fewer injuries occurred at Greenville Elementary’s renovated playground than occurred at the playground at Amity Elementary School.
</b>


This choice supports the wrong conclusion. The conclusion is not that the renovations made Greenville Elementary’s playground safer than other schools’ playgrounds.


Also, for all we are aware, fewer injuries occurred at Greenville Elementary School’s playground than occurred at Amity Elementary School’s playground even during the years before the renovation of Greenville Elementary School’s playground. So, the comparison made in this choice does not affect the support for the argument’s conclusion.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)
