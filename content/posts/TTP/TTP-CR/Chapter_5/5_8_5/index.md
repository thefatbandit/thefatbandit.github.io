## Lesson Content
Among the answer choices in a Paradox question can be choices that have no effect on the paradox presented by the stimulus.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Information that has no effect on the paradox will never resolve the paradox.
{{< /alert >}}

To tell the difference between incorrect choices that have no effect on the paradox and the correct answer, we have to carefully consider what effect each choice has on the scenario presented in the stimulus and<strong> be careful not to make up convoluted stories</strong> that give answer choices effects that they don’t really have.

Let’s look at an example:

> <em>On a short stretch of highway in Dolya along which there were many billboards, many car crashes were occurring during the afternoon rush hour. Since drivers can be distracted by billboards, Dolya’s government decided to ban the use of billboards along that stretch. Amazingly, once the billboards were taken down, the number of crashes per day increased.</em>

<em>Which of the following, if true, best explains why the outcome was the opposite of what might have been reasonably expected?</em>

So what’s the paradox here?

> Fact 1: Potentially distracting billboards were removed from a stretch of highway on which many car crashes had been occurring during the afternoon rush hour.

> Fact 2: The number of crashes increased.

<u>What has to be explained</u>: Why would it be that, once potentially distracting billboards were taken down, the number of crashes on the stretch of highway along which the billboards had been standing increased?

Let’s consider some answer choices, starting with the following:

> <em>(<u>Incorrect Choice that Has No Effect</u>) Without the billboards, the land bordering that stretch of highway looks more natural.</em>

We could somehow spin this choice into a story explaining why the number of crashes increased, perhaps a story about how people in natural surroundings are more likely to fall asleep while driving home in the afternoon.

Let’s consider another:

> <em>(<u>Incorrect Choice that Has No Effect </u>) The billboards often showed advertisements for retail stores in communities further along the highway.</em>

We could spin this choice into a story about how people became so excited to go to the stores that they became more alert in their driving, but that story is just way too farfetched.

OK, now for a more interesting choice:

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer Choice</u>) The billboards had been positioned in a way such that they had blocked light from the afternoon sun from going directly into the eyes of drivers.</em>

Notice that the correct answer choice resolves the paradox.

If once the billboards were taken down, there was more afternoon sunlight going into the eyes of drivers, and so, the drivers had difficulty seeing, that fact could for sure explain why the number of crashes increased after the billboards were taken down.

So, we found a choice that has the effect we need without our coming up with a farfetched story.

When answering a paradox question, we haven’t found the correct answer until we have found one that is connected to the paradox in a way like the way in which this last choice is.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

To avoid choosing a Paradox question answer choice that introduces information that has no effect on the paradox, carefully consider whether the way in which you are connecting a choice to the paradox really makes sense.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 17 

The First-Sale Doctrine holds that someone who has legally purchased copyrighted material, such as music or a novel, is legally entitled to dispose of that material in whatever way he or she sees fit.

Which of the following, if true, best explains this seemingly contradictory action?

1. [x] (A) When digital content is sold on a secondary market, there is no way to verify that the seller has “disposed of,” and thus no longer possesses, the material.
2. [ ] (B) Not all potential secondary-market consumers own the equipment necessary to play MP3s or to read e-books.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) Book publishers and record companies have attempted to shut down secondary markets for books and CDs as well.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) Digital content did not exist when the First-Sale Doctrine was created.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) Many modern authors and performance artists typically make their content available in both digital and physical forms.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Rephrased as a question, the paradox presented in the passage would look something like:


<u>What has to be explained</u>: Why is it that, even though the First-Sale Doctrine allows for the re-sale of copyrighted materials, it is illegal to sell some copyrighted materials (digital copies)?


We are looking for an answer that explains the difference in how the two types of materials are treated legally, and that explanation has to work in a way that doesn’t require a convoluted story.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A) When digital content is sold on a secondary market, there is no way to verify that the seller has “disposed of,” and thus no longer possesses, the material.
</b>


 This answer choice describes a fundamental difference between the two types of copyrighted materials in question and explains why the term “disposed” does not apply to digital materials in the way in which the term applies to physical materials. Given that a concept, disposal, that plays a key part in the First-Sale Doctrine doesn’t apply to copyrighted digital content, it reasonably follows that the Doctrine could not be used to create a legal secondary market for copyrighted digital content. Thus, this answer choice resolves the paradox.


> <b>(B) Not all potential secondary-market consumers own the equipment necessary to play MP3s or to read e-books.
</b>


 This choice presents information that does not explain the difference between how sales of digital content are treated and how sales of other types of materials are treated legally.


While we could potentially make up a convoluted story about how the government wants to protect people from buying things that they can’t use, the truth is that that story does not make sense given what we know about the real world. After all, in the real world, not everyone has equipment necessary for playing CDs or for using other types of non-digital copyrighted materials. So, the characteristic of digital content described by this choice is also a characteristic of other types of copyrighted materials, meaning that what this choice describes is not a difference between the two types of materials, but rather, a characteristic that they share. Of course, the fact that the two types of materials are the same in this way would not explain why they are treated differently.


We can’t explain why two things are treated different by mentioning something that is a characteristic of both of them.


> <b>(C) Book publishers and record companies have attempted to shut down secondary markets for books and CDs as well.
</b>


This choice shows how two different things (physical content and digital content) are similar. Remember, the paradox is that one type of secondary market is illegal and the other is legal. We need to figure out what makes these two types of content different from one another. Bringing up a way in which they are similar does not help to explain why they are treated differently.


> <b>(D) Digital content did not exist when the First-Sale Doctrine was created.
</b>


This fact could seem to affect the paradox if we come up with a convoluted story.


We could come up with a story about how, since the First-Sale Doctrine was created before the advent of digital content, it does not apply to digital content.


However, is that really the way things work? Is it really the case that, by not applying to something, a law makes that thing illegal? Not usually, and not necessarily.


So, the truth is that only by making up something that we don’t really have good reason to believe can we use this choice to resolve the paradox.


> <b>(E) Many modern authors and performance artists typically make their content available in both digital and physical forms.
</b>


We could come up with a convoluted story like the following:


“Authors and performance artists who make their content available in both digital and physical forms shouldn’t have to deal with both types being of media being resold. So, reselling digital material is not legal.”


However, the truth is that that story is not supported by what is said in the stimulus or what goes on in the real world. So, this information presented by this choice does not affect the paradox.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)
