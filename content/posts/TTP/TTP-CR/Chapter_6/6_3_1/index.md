## Lesson Content
Mistakes involving sequencing happen when we notice that one event, X, occurs before another event, Y, and, on this basis alone, we conclude that the first event, X, must have caused the second, Y.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

We’re apt to make an error in reasoning any time we notice that one event, X, occurs before another event, Y, and, on this basis alone, we conclude that the first event, X, must have caused the second, Y.
{{< /alert >}}

For example, you have a headache, and then you take an aspirin.

It may seem intuitively correct that the aspirin caused the headache to disappear, especially given that your taking the aspirin immediately preceded the disappearance of your headache.

- Perhaps the headache would have disappeared without the aspirin.

- Perhaps the headache was caused by dehydration, and the water you drank with the aspirin cured the headache.

- Perhaps your roommate stopped playing the drums soon after you took the aspirin.

- Perhaps you received a call that you were just accepted into the business school of your choice, and since you are now relieved about a matter that had you deeply concerned and stressed, the headache has disappeared.

- Perhaps, just prior to taking the aspirin, you were at your favorite juice bar and consumed a shot of turmeric, a plant substance the consumption of which has been shown to reduce headaches.

We could keep building this list of alternative possibilities, but you get the idea.

For another example, think back to the example of the old woman and the rooster.

Here’s the takeaway: the simple fact that one event precedes another does not necessarily imply that the first event caused the second event.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The fact that some event X occurred before some event Y does not necessarily indicate that event X caused event Y.
{{< /alert >}}

In later sections of this chapter, we’ll carefully discuss how to weaken arguments that involve cause-and-effect reasoning, but for now let’s consider a Weaken the Argument example question that underscores how this sequencing reasoning error can make its way into Critical Reasoning questions.

##  Example 3 

In 2012, the Muscat Sound, which is inhabited by manatees, was struck by an outbreak of algae blooms.

Which of the following, if true, most weakens the argument?

1. [ ] (A) The populations of many types of fish that inhabit Muscat Sound have not declined at all since the outbreak of algae blooms began.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) Far fewer motor boats have been present on the sound since the outbreak of the algae blooms.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) The increase in algae blooms is the result of increased levels in the sound of pollutants that are known to cause fatal illnesses in manatees.
4. [ ] (D) The algae release toxins that kill the manatees’ main food source, seagrass.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) A similar outbreak of algae blooms has occurred in a different area, which has not experienced increased mortality in marine life.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



The argument presents two events that occurred, one before the other: the outbreak of the algae blooms and the death of the manatees. The argument concludes that the outbreak of the algae blooms is the cause of the death of the manatees.


Did the algae blooms really cause the deaths of the manatees? Might there be an alternative explanation? In order to weaken the argument, we can present a plausible alternative cause for the manatees’ deaths. In other words, we need to be on the lookout for another cause that could have the effect of the manatees’ deaths.


> <b>(A) The populations of many types of fish that inhabit Muscat Sound have not declined at all since the outbreak of algae blooms began.
</b>


By reasoning that, if populations of fish do not seem to be affected negatively by the algae, the algae may not negatively affect manatees either, one might mistakenly decide that this choice weakens the argument.


However, what we have to see is that, while populations of many types of fish that inhabit Muscat Sound are not declining, the passage states as fact that the manatees are dying off, and what this choice says does not change that fact.


So, regardless of whether the algae affects fish negatively, something is affecting the manatees negatively, and what this choice says provides no reason to believe that the algae is not what’s causing the manatees to die off.


Remember, we must take the premises provided to us as factual.


> <b>(B) Far fewer motor boats have been present on the sound since the outbreak of the algae blooms.
</b>


This choice could weaken the argument by providing an alternate reason for the increase in the manatee death rate if we had a connection between the presence of motor boats and the welfare of manatees. For example, if food thrown by people on motorboats were the primary source of sustenance for the manatees, then a decrease in the number of boats would cause a decrease in food supply and, therefore, an increase in the manatee death rate.


However, since there is no information presented by this question or any common knowledge that clearly supports any such scenario, this choice doesn’t weaken the argument.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) The increase in algae blooms is the result of increased levels in the sound of pollutants that are known to cause fatal illnesses in manatees.
</b>


This choice successfully weakens the argument by establishing an alternate cause for the rising mortality rate of the manatees: pollutants.


By saying that levels of pollutants that cause fatal illnesses in manatees have increased, this choice indicates that the manatee deaths may be being caused by pollutants.


Thus, this choice indicates that the algae blooms may not be the cause of the deaths of the manatees.


> <b>(D) The algae release toxins that kill the manatees’ main food source, seagrass.
</b>


This choice strengthens the argument by explaining how algae blooms cause the deaths of manatees. If the algae kill the manatees’ main food source, the manatees are likely to die. This choice, thus, supports, rather than weakens, the conclusion that algae blooms must be causing the deaths of manatees.


Now, here, one may raise a doubt that it is not the algae blooms directly that are causing the deaths of the manatees and that it is probably the absence of the manatees’ main food source that is causing the deaths.


However, it doesn’t matter whether the algae blooms are “very directly” causing the deaths as long as they are causing something that eventually leads to the deaths of the manatees.


Think about it.


Even if the algae were not killing the manatees’ food source but “directly” killing the manatees, we could still say that algae are damaging the organs of the manatees, and the damage is leading to the deaths of the manatees. We could still argue that not the algae but the damage of the organs is killing the manatees. Couldn’t we?


So, as long as the algae blooms are eventually leading to the deaths of the manatees, we can say that algae blooms are the cause.


> <b>(E) A similar outbreak of algae blooms has occurred in a different area, which has not experienced increased mortality in marine life.
</b>


This choice says that the outbreak of algae blooms in another area hasn’t caused the deaths of marine life. If there were a manatee population that hadn’t declined in that area, this choice would provide a reason to believe that algae blooms don’t cause the deaths of manatees. In such a case, this choice would have weakened the argument.


However, this choice doesn’t include any information about whether there are manatees in that other area. So, this choice does not indicate whether algae blooms affect manatees.


Thus, this choice has no impact on the argument.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

##  Example 4 

Many states relaxed their fireworks laws two years ago by allowing a wider variety of fireworks to be sold.

Which of the following, if true, most strengthens the people’s position?

1. [ ] (A) The variety of fireworks recently allowed to be sold doesn’t include more powerful fireworks.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) Fireworks companies have expanded their distribution in the last two years to reach areas that were not catered to by any fireworks company before.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) Some of the states that relaxed their fireworks laws did not see an increase in the number of fireworks sold.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) The study also found that the number of severe fireworks related burns in adults neither increased nor decreased over the past two years.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) States that did not relax their fireworks laws did not see an increase in the number of severe fireworks related burns among minors.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



People are urging lawmakers to roll back their recent relaxations in the fireworks laws. These people are doing so because they have concluded that, X, allowing a greater variety of fireworks to be sold caused, Y, an increase in serious burns to minors. This cause-and-effect claim is supported by the fact that X directly preceded Y.


One way to strengthen this argument is to provide additional support for the conclusion that X caused Y.


> <b>(A) The variety of fireworks recently allowed to be sold doesn’t include more powerful fireworks.
</b>


This choice eliminates a potential strengthener.


Think about it. If the recently allowed fireworks did include more powerful fireworks, we could reasonably expect to see more injuries, such as severe burns. In such a case, the argument would have been strengthened, because our belief would have been increased that the relaxations are leading to an increase in severe firework burns.


However, this choice says that it was NOT more powerful fireworks that were recently allowed to be sold. Thus, this choice eliminates this potential strengthener. Therefore, it certainly does not strengthen the argument.


> <b>(B) Fireworks companies have expanded their distribution in the last two years to reach areas that were not catered to by any fireworks company before.
</b>


This choice weakens the argument because it presents an alternative cause for the increase in the number of severe burns.


If more areas are now getting access to fireworks, it could be that this increased access, rather than the relaxations, that caused the increase in the number of severe burns. Thus, this choice indicates that the relaxations might not be the cause of the increase in the number of burns and thus weakens the people’s position regarding the roll back of the relaxations.


Remember, we can weaken a cause-and-effect argument by presenting a plausible alternative cause for the effect.


> <b>(C) Some of the states that relaxed their fireworks laws did not see an increase in the number of fireworks sold.
</b>


This choice has no effect on the argument.


Notice that, according to the passage, the changes in laws allowed for a greater <em>variety </em>of fireworks to be sold. So, the conclusion is not that the increase in the number of burns was caused by an increase in the number of fireworks sold. It’s that the increase in the number of burns was caused by a change in the variety of fireworks sold.


So, the fact that the number of fireworks sold did not increase does not affect the support for the conclusion, which is about the results of allowing a greater variety of fireworks to be sold.


> <b>(D) The study also found that the number of severe fireworks related burns in adults neither increased nor decreased over the past two years.
</b>


This answer neither strengthens nor weakens the support for the conclusion.


Notice that, regardless of what this choice says about adults, the fact remains that the number of severe fireworks related burns among minors has increased. Notice also that the conclusion of the people mentioned in the passage is simply that that increase in the number of burns was caused by the relaxation of the laws.


In other words, since the argument is restricted to supporting a conclusion about why the increase in the number of severe burns among minors occurred, this information about adults affects nothing about the argument.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E) States that did not relax their fireworks laws did not see an increase in the number of severe fireworks related burns among minors.
</b>


This choice successfully strengthens the argument.


By saying that the states that did not relax their fireworks laws did not see an increase in the number of severe fireworks burns among minors, this choice indicates that, when X (relaxations) was not there, Y (increased number of severe burns) was also not there.


In doing so, this choice strengthens the case for concluding that there is a cause-and-effect link between the relaxations and the increase in the number of burns, because it indicates that only where there have been relaxations has there been an increase in the number of burns.


In general, when we are trying to support a conclusion that X caused Y, we can support that conclusion by saying that, when X did not occur, Y also didn’t occur.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

Now, let’s discuss co-occurrence.
