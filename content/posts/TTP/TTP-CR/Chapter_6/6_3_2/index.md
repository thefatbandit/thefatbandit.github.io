## Lesson Content
A second mistake in reasoning happens when two or more events occur simultaneously, and we conclude, on this basis alone, that they are causally linked.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

We’re apt to make an error in reasoning any time two or more events occur simultaneously, and we conclude, on the basis of their simultaneous occurrence alone, that they are causally linked.
{{< /alert >}}

For example, consider two events, X and Y, that occurred at the same time.

For example, many people who abuse illegal drugs are also found to suffer from depression.

Are there other possible ways in which drug abuse and depression could be related? Isn’t it possible that people suffering from depression take illegal drugs to self-medicate? Perhaps, then, it’s the depression that causes the illegal drug use.

Alternatively, maybe it’s the case that drug use does not cause depression and depression does not cause drug use.

Here’s the point: the fact that two events happen at the same time (that is, they co-occur) does not necessarily mean that a cause-and-effect relationship exists between the two events.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The simultaneous occurrence of two events does not necessarily indicate that one causes the other.
{{< /alert >}}

Again, in later sections of this chapter, we’ll carefully discuss how to weaken arguments involving cause-and-effect reasoning, but for now, let’s consider a Weaken the Argument example question that underscores how this co-occurrence reasoning error can make its way into Critical Reasoning questions.

##  Example 5 

In 1994, it was found that 98 percent of homicides in Country X occurred inside the victims’ own homes.

Which of the following, if true, calls into question the politicians’ conclusion?

1. [ ] (A) Between 1978 and 1994, gun ownership and overall homicide rates in Country X remained the same.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) In Country X, while the number of homicides occurring annually in the victim’s home decreased from 1994 to 2000, the overall rate of domestic violence did not change.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) The years 1994 to 2000 saw a significant decline in all forms of serious crime in Country X.
4. [ ] (D) The suicide rate in Country X rose 2 percent between 1994 and 2000.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) From 1994 to 2000, there was no decrease in the number of guns sold annually to members of households in Country X.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



The politicians concluded the following:


<u>Conclusion</u>: The decrease in the homicide rate must certainly be due to the decrease in household gun ownership.


The conclusion is supported by the co-occurrence of (1) the drop in gun ownership as a result of the gun buy-back program and (2) the decrease in homicide rate.


An excellent way to weaken this argument would be to show that, despite the fact that two events occurred simultaneously, something else may be the cause of the decrease in homicides.


> <b>(A) Between 1978 and 1994, gun ownership and overall homicide rates in Country X remained the same.
</b>


 This choice tempts us with discussion of dates, gun ownership, and the homicide rate.


However, the events mentioned in this choice occurred during a time period different from the one that the argument is about.


Further, if anything, what this choice indicates, that the homicide rate did not decrease when household gun ownership did not decrease, would tend to confirm the conclusion that the decrease in the homicide rate was the result of the buyback program, as we would have the following pattern:


From this choice: no decrease in gun ownership – no decrease in homicide


From the passage: decrease in gun ownership – decrease in homicide


In other words, when we don’t have X, a decrease in gun ownership, we don’t have Y, a decrease in homicide, and when we do have X, we do have Y.


> <b>(B) In Country X, while the number of homicides occurring annually in the victim’s home decreased from 1994 to 2000, the overall rate of domestic violence did not change.
</b>


 This choice is a tempting trap, in that one could take it as conflicting with and somehow undermining the premise that there was a decrease in homicides that occurred in the victim’s own home.


However, as discussed in the chapter on Weaken the Argument questions, in GMAT Critical Reasoning questions, premises are taken as true and cannot be undermined.


Meanwhile, the truth is that domestic violence and homicide in homes are not necessarily the same thing. So, the fact that overall domestic violence did not decrease does not mean that homicide in homes did not decrease.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) The years 1994 to 2000 saw a significant decline in all forms of serious crime in Country X.
</b>


If all serious crime, not just homicide in the victim’s own home, declined in Country X from 1994 to 2000, then it could be that some universal factor, such as a change in culture, is at play here.


If a universal factor caused this overall decline in serious crime, that universal factor may also have caused the decline in the number of homicides in the victims’ own homes. Thus, given what this choice says, the decline in gun ownership or the buy-back policy may not have caused that decline in homicides.


Thus, this choice calls into question the politicians’ conclusion.


Remember, any time we provide an alternative cause of the effect mentioned in a cause-and-effect argument, we are weakening that argument. This choice suggests that there is an alternative cause for the decline in the number of homicides.


> <b>(D) The suicide rate in Country X rose 2 percent between 1994 and 2000.
</b>


Like choice (B), this choice attempts to tempt us with information that could be perceived as undermining the premise that the homicide rate decreased.


However, as discussed in the chapter on Weaken the Argument questions, in GMAT Critical Reasoning questions, premises are taken as true and cannot be undermined.


In this case, since suicide and homicide are not the same thing, the fact that the suicide rate increased does not affect the strength of the argument at hand, which is focused on homicide.


> <b>(E) From 1994 to 2000, there was no decrease in the number of guns sold annually to members of households in Country X.
</b>


 This answer could tempt us, as it could be perceived as undermining the premise that the number of households with guns decreased.


However, in GMAT Critical Reasoning questions, the premises are taken to be true as stated, and the passage states as fact that number of households with guns dropped by 30 percent.


In other words, the fact that <em>sales of guns</em> to members of households did not decrease does not mean that the <em>number of guns owned</em> by members of households did not decrease. After all, it could be that more guns were bought back by the government than were bought by members of households.


So, this choice has no effect on the argument.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

##  Example 6 

A recent study found that people living in areas with high levels of outdoor nighttime light reported more dissatisfaction with the quality and quantity of their sleep than was reported by people living in areas with low levels of outdoor nighttime light.

Which of the following, if true, most strengthens the support for the researchers’ conclusion?

1. [ ] (A) Municipalities with high levels of outdoor nighttime light could save money by reducing the numbers of street lights that they have to maintain.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) Residents of areas with high levels of outdoor nighttime light can block the light from going into their homes by using heavy shades on windows.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) People who watch television before bed are reported to have more trouble sleeping than is had by people who read before bed.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) Of the areas mentioned in the study, those with higher levels of outdoor nighttime light did not have higher numbers of noisy disturbances at night.
5. [ ] (E) Residents living in areas with high levels of outdoor nighttime light tend to value quality of sleep over quantity of sleep.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



The researchers have concluded the following:


<u>Conclusion</u>: Municipalities with high levels of outdoor nighttime light could improve both the quality and the quantity of sleep of their residents by reducing the municipalities’ numbers of street lights.


The evidence that the researchers have is the co-occurrence of (1) high levels of outdoor nighttime light and (2) more dissatisfaction with quality and quantity sleep.


The researchers’ conclusion depends on the assumption that high levels of outdoor nighttime light actually cause people to be dissatisfied with both the quality and quantity of their sleep. In other words, the researchers make the assumption that there can be no other explanation for the observation made. Our job in answering the question is to confirm the validity of that assumption.


> <b>(A) Municipalities with high levels of outdoor nighttime light could save money by reducing the numbers of street lights that they have to maintain.
</b>


This choice is tempting, because it adds another reason for reducing the number of streetlights in an area, saving money.


However, since the purpose of the researchers’ conclusion is solely about improving “the quality and quantity of sleep of … residents,” this choice, which brings up a different benefit of reducing the numbers of streetlights, does not strengthen the support for the researchers’ conclusion.


> <b>(B) Residents of areas with high levels of outdoor nighttime light can block the light from going into their homes by using heavy shades on windows.
</b>


The fact that residents can block light from streetlights from entering their homes is not a reason to believe that municipalities with high levels of outdoor nighttime light could improve both the quality and the quantity of sleep of their residents by reducing the municipalities’ numbers of street lights. If anything, it tends to weaken the support for that conclusion, by indicating that residents may have blocked the light from entering their homes, in which case the light would not be the reason for the sleep related problems experienced by the residents. Something else would have to be causing the problems.


> <b>(C) People who watch television before bed are reported to have more trouble sleeping than is had by people who read before bed.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, because it compares the troubles in sleeping of two sets of people: people who read before bed and people who watch TV before bed. So, it could be perceived as indicating something connected to the scenario in the argument, which is also about two sets of people and how they sleep.


However, since both reading and watching television involve light, and since reading and watching television are both different from attempting to sleep, there is no clear connection between this comparison and the conclusion about reducing numbers of streetlights to help people to sleep.


So, this choice does not affect the argument.


The way to avoid choosing a choice like this one is to be careful not to come up with convoluted stories that seem to create logical connections where none really exist.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) Of the areas mentioned in the study, those with higher levels of outdoor nighttime light did not have higher numbers of noisy disturbances at night.
</b>


This choice strengthens the argument because it can be used to eliminate a possible alternative reason why people living in areas with high levels of outdoor nighttime light reported more dissatisfaction with the quality and quantity of their sleep than was reported by people living in areas with low levels of outdoor nighttime light.


By saying that, of the areas mentioned in the study, those with higher levels of outdoor nighttime light did not have higher numbers of noisy disturbances at night, this choice indicates that it’s not the case that noisy disturbances, rather than outdoor light, are causing the dissatisfaction with sleep of people in areas with relatively high levels of outdoor nighttime light. The elimination of that possibility makes it more likely that the outdoor nighttime light is indeed the cause of the sleep related problems.


> <b>(E) Residents living in areas with high levels of outdoor nighttime light tend to value quality of sleep over quantity of sleep.
</b>


This choice says that residents value quality of sleep over quantity of sleep. However, the argument is not about the effects of artificial lighting on one type of sleep or the other. Rather, both the quantity and the quality have always been mentioned together. Thus, this choice has no impact on the argument.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
