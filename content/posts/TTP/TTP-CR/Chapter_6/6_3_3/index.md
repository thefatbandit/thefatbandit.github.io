## Lesson Content
We say that two variables are correlated when a change in one variable accompanies a change in the other.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Two variables are correlated when the variables change together in a consistent manner. In a positive correlation, as one variable goes up (or down), so does the other. In a negative correlation, as one variable goes up (or down) the other does the opposite.
{{< /alert >}}

Now, the fact that two variables are correlated (i.e.

While a correlation between two variables may in some cases lend support to the conclusion that there is a causal relationship between the variables, it would be incorrect to infer that, because the variables are correlated, one must cause the other.

However, it is not uncommon for humans to make this error – that is, mistakenly to take correlation for causation.

Between 2000 and 2009, there was a very strong positive correlation between (1) the divorce rate in Maine and (2) the per capita consumption of margarine in the U.S.

Wow – look at the lines on the graph, which show an almost perfect upward and downward connected movement between the two variables.

Can we therefore conclude that eating more margarine causes an increase in the divorce rate in Maine? Should we all stop eating margarine to save the marriages of the citizens of Maine? Or, could the reverse cause and effect be at play? Is divorce making people somehow consume margarine? Should we investigate why getting divorced may make people crave margarine?

What’s happening here? Surely, we have uncovered a newsworthy cause-and-effect relationship? Right? Not exactly.

The point here is that, when two variables show an apparent correlation (either positive or negative), we cannot on the basis of that correlation alone conclude that a cause-and-effect relationship links them.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The fact that two variables show a correlation (either positive or negative), does not allow us – on the basis of that fact alone – to conclude that a cause-and-effect relationship links them.
{{< /alert >}}

Let's now practice noticing correlation in cause-and-effect arguments.

##  Example 7 

Banquet halls that want to do more business should increase their prices.

Which of the following, if true, most seriously weakens the argument?

1. [ ] (A) A banquet hall can increase its profits by carefully choosing the food and beverage items it offers so as to maximize margins.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) The more popular a month is for holding events, the less banquet halls have to offer discounts to generate sales for that month.
3. [ ] (C) A banquet hall that does more business than another banquet hall is not necessarily more profitable than the other banquet hall.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) Often people perceive a service priced higher than other similar services to be of higher quality than the other services.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) Some banquet halls are so busy that they do not want to do more business than they are already doing.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



The author has concluded the following:


<u>Conclusion</u>: Banquet halls that want to do more business should increase their prices.


The author has arrived at that conclusion after observing a correlation between the prices banquet halls charge and the number of events they host: the higher the prices, the more events they host.


In going from that observation to the conclusion, the author has assumed that the higher prices cause the greater numbers of events hosted. Hmm …


A choice that provides an alternative explanation for the correlation will weaken the argument.


> <b>(A) A banquet hall can increase its profits by carefully choosing the food and beverage items it offers so as to maximize margins.
</b>


The author has presented a strategy for banquet halls that want to “do more business.”


Now, this choice presents a strategy via the use of which a banquet hall can “increase its profits.”


While doing more business and increasing profits are related, the fact that there is a strategy that a banquet hall can use to “increase its profits” does not mean that a banquet hall won’t “do more business” by using another strategy. After all, there can be multiple strategies that work.


So, this choice does not weaken the argument.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B) The more popular a month is for holding events, the less banquet halls have to offer discounts to generate sales for that month.
</b>


 This choice provides an alternative explanation for the correlation observed by the author: the cause and effect are the reverse of what the author believes them to be.


In other words, this choice indicates that, rather than higher prices causing banquet halls to have busier months, what’s going on is that, during more popular months, banquet halls offer fewer discounts and thus charge prices that are on average higher. Simply put, this choice indicates that, rather than higher prices cause busier months, busier months cause higher prices.


By providing a plausible alternative explanation for the observed events, this choice attacks the author’s assumption that higher prices cause banquet halls to do more business and, thus, weakens the argument.


> <b>(C) A banquet hall that does more business than another banquet hall is not necessarily more profitable than the other banquet hall.
</b>


 The conclusion of the argument is specifically about a strategy that banquet halls can use if they “want to do more business.” Whether a banquet hall will become more profitable by doing more business is another matter. So, what this choice says about profitability has no effect on the support for the conclusion.


To avoid choosing a choice like this one, we have to be careful not to be fooled into perceiving two different things, such as doing more business and being more profitable, to be the same thing.


> <b>(D) Often people perceive a service priced higher than other similar services to be of higher quality than the other services.
</b>


 This choice has completely the wrong effect.


While what this choice says is related to the argument, the effect of this choice is to explain why the strategy proposed by the author would work. After all, the fact that, often, people perceive a service priced higher than other similar services to be of higher quality than the other services would explain why a banquet hall could do more business by increasing its prices: people seeing the higher prices would perceive that banquet hall’s services to be of higher quality and, therefore, choose to use that banquet hall.


Of course, a choice that explains why the strategy proposed by the author would work does not weaken the argument.


> <b>(E) Some banquet halls are so busy that they do not want to do more business than they are already doing.
</b>


 Notice that the conclusion of the argument is about a strategy that banquet halls that “want to do more business” can use. The fact that some banquet halls are so busy that they do not want to do more business than they are already doing does not indicate that the strategy that the author has proposed will not work for banquet halls that do want to do more business.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

##  Example 8 

Studies have shown that, the higher the average level of education of members of a society, the fewer children are had on average by members of that society.

Which of the following, if true, does the most to confirm the above conclusion?

1. [ ] (A) Parents who have many children often have difficulty paying for their children to be educated.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) On average, the more educated people are, the more knowledgeable they are about how to optimally rear children.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) It is not the case that the governments that do the most to promote education are also those that do the most to advocate having small families.
4. [ ] (D) Children who are better educated tend to cause their parents less trouble than children who are less well educated.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) People who have children as teenagers are more likely to drop out of school than and tend to have more children than people who start having children later in life.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



The author concludes the following:


<u>Conclusion</u>: We can slow population growth by improving access to education.


The author bases that conclusion on a negative correlation between (1) average level of education of members of a society (2) average number of children had by members of that society. In other words, the author has observed that when one variable, level of education, is higher, a second variable, number of children, is lower.


Of course, in basing that conclusion on that observation, the author has assumed that education is the cause of people’s having fewer of children. So, information that helps to confirm that education is in fact the cause of people’s having fewer children will strengthen the argument.


> <b>(A) Parents who have many children often have difficulty paying for their children to be educated.
</b>


 This choice provides reason to believe that there may be an alternative explanation for the negative correlation between level of education and number of children had by members of a society: perhaps members of a society in which people have many children cannot afford to educate their children.


In other words, this choice indicates that the cause-and-effect relationship may be the reverse of what the author has assumed it to be. Perhaps it is not the case that education causes members of a society to have fewer children but, rather, the case that having fewer children causes members of a society to be more educated, because parents who have fewer children are better able to pay for their children to be educated.


By providing a plausible alternative explanation for the observed events, this choice weakens rather than strengthens the argument.


> <b>(B) On average, the more educated people are, the more knowledgeable they are about how to optimally rear children.
</b>


This choice supports the wrong conclusion. It supports a conclusion along the lines of that education benefits the members of a society.


Meanwhile this choice does not support this argument’s conclusion, because it provides no clear reason to believe that parents’ being knowledgeable about rearing children will lead to a slowing of population growth.


Since this choice provides support for the wrong conclusion, it does not strengthen the argument.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(C) It is not the case that the governments that do the most to promote education are also those that do the most to advocate having small families.
</b>


This choice strengthens the argument by serving to eliminate a plausible alternative explanation.


Imagine if the governments that do the most to promote education are also those that do the most to advocate having small families. Wouldn’t that fact serve as an alternative explanation for the observed pattern?


Yes, it certainly would, because that fact, if true, would be a very plausible alternative reason why, the higher the average level of education of members of a society, the fewer children are had by members of that society. Both the relatively high levels of education and the relatively low numbers of children would be the results of efforts of the same governments.


So, by getting rid of this potential alternate explanation for the observed pattern, this choice strengthens the argument.


If this choice doesn’t sound like a strengthener to you, try reading it without “not.” Without “not” this choice provides an alternate explanation for the observed pattern and weakens the argument.


Thus, with “not,” this choice eliminates that alternative explanation and strengthens the argument.


> <b>(D) Children who are better educated tend to cause their parents less trouble than children who are less well educated.
</b>


This choice supports the wrong conclusion. It supports a conclusion along the lines of that education of children benefits parents.


Meanwhile this choice does not support this argument’s conclusion, because there is no clear reason to believe that children’s causing less trouble will lead to a slowing of population growth.


Since this choice provides support for the wrong conclusion, it does not strengthen the argument.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E) People who have children as teenagers are more likely to drop out of school than and tend to have more children than people who start having children later in life.
</b>


This choice provides reason to believe that there may be an alternative explanation for the negative correlation between level of education and number of children had by members of a society: perhaps what’s going on is that, in societies in which people tend to have children as teenagers, people both drop out of school and have relatively high numbers of children.


In other words, this choice indicates that the cause-and-effect relationship between education and having fewer children the author has assumed exists may not exist, and that it may instead be the case that people’s having children as teenagers both causes them to be less educated and causes them to have more children.


Since this choice provides a reason to question the author’s assumption, it weakens rather than strengthens the argument.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)
