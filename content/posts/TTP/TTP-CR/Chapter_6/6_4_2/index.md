## Lesson Content
Often, there can be an alternative explanation for an event.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

One way to weaken the support for a cause-and-effect conclusion is to show that there is an alternative explanation for the observed effect.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following argument:

> <em>In the past, almost all personal computer users used the MT operating system. Two years ago, a new operating system called AX was introduced, and, since then, AX has become increasingly popular. Over the period during which AX has become more popular, the number of emails made to support centers by people seeking help with operating their computers has increased. It must be the case that AX is more difficult to use than MT.</em>

Notice the cause-and-effect reasoning set forth in the argument.

But – could there be an alternative explanation for the observed events? Yes, there could.

> <em>W</em><em>hereas, before two years ago, computer users tended to be experts, now many computer users are novices or occasional users.</em>

This fact would weaken the argument, since it provides an alternative explanation for the increase in the number of emails from people seeking help with operating their computers.

Consider the following examples in which support for cause-and-effect conclusions is weakened by choices that suggest alternative causes.

##  Example 11 

Surfer: I read that, over the last five years, there have been, on average, 82 shark attacks on humans per year worldwide.

Which of the following, if true, most seriously calls into question the surfer’s conclusion?

1. [ ] (A) While educational programs have succeeded in raising awareness of the importance of sharks for the health of the oceans, every year large numbers of sharks are killed for their fins.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) During the most recent year, more shark attacks on humans were reported than were reported in any previous year.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) The percentage of shark attacks that are fatal has been decreasing as methods for treating the resulting wounds have improved.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) The human population and the popularity of going to beaches and swimming in the ocean have been increasing steadily.
5. [ ] (E) Occasionally, a shark attack that occurs in a remote area goes unreported.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



The surfer arrives at the following conclusion:


<u>Conclusion</u>: There are more sharks in the water now than there were 20 years ago.


The conclusion is based on the fact that the number of shark attacks per year has increased. We could weaken this argument by presenting another reason why the number of shark attacks on humans has increased.


> <b>(A) While educational programs have succeeded in raising awareness of the importance of sharks for the health of the oceans, every year large numbers of sharks are killed for their fins.
</b>


 This choice is a trap.


Because this choice says that every year sharks are killed for their fins, a test-taker might construe what it says as indicating that shark populations have not rebounded.


However, even if what this choice says is true, conservation measures could still have resulted in an increase in the number of sharks in the oceans. After all, presumably, conservation measures have had some effect even if it is still the case that every year large numbers of sharks are killed for their fins. Also, regardless of what this choice says, the fact remains that the number of shark attacks has increased. Thus, concluding that there are more sharks in the oceans still makes sense even if what this choice says is true.


So, this answer choice does not weaken the argument.


> <b>(B) During the most recent year, more shark attacks on humans were reported than were reported in any previous year.
</b>


 What this choice says basically just confirms what the argument says, that the number of attacks has increased. So, it does not at all weaken the argument.


> <b>(C) The percentage of shark attacks that are fatal has been decreasing as methods for treating the resulting wounds have improved.
</b>


 The outcomes of shark attacks do not affect the number of shark attacks or the number of sharks. So, what this choice says does not affect the argument.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) The human population and the popularity of going to beaches and swimming in the ocean have been increasing steadily.
</b>


 The information provided by this choice indicates that there are more people in the world than there were twenty years ago and that going to the beach and swimming in the ocean are more popular than they were twenty years ago. Thus, this choice provides reason to believe that there are more people in the oceans than there were twenty years ago.


An increase in the number of people in the oceans would explain an increase in shark attacks on people.


So, this choice presents an alternate reason for the increase in shark attacks and thus weakens the support for the conclusion that there are many more sharks in the oceans than there were twenty years ago.


> <b>(E) Occasionally, a shark attack that occurs in a remote area goes unreported.
</b>


 This choice is a trap, because reporting, or lack of reporting, of shark attacks could affect the number of shark attacks that the surfer is aware of, and therefore, this choice could be perceived as affecting the argument.


Notice, however, that the surfer has mentioned an increase from an average of 50 shark attacks per year to an average of 82 per year. That 32 shark attacks per year increase would not have resulted from shark attacks’ “occasionally” going unreported.


Furthermore, we are given no reason to believe that the number of shark attacks that go unreported in a typical year has changed over the past twenty years. So, presumably around the same number have gone unreported each year recently as went unreported each year twenty years ago, in which case the average number of shark attacks, both reported and unreported, per year has still increased by about 32.


So, this choice does not provide information that clearly affects the support for the surfer’s conclusion.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Example 12 

The number of elm trees in Zobrinia had been decreasing steadily for decades.

Which of the following, if true, provides information that is a reason to call into question the validity of the above conclusion?

1. [ ] (A) Some elm trees in Zobrinia have recently become infected with a virulent strain of the fungus that causes the disease.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) Leaf scorch, which is a generally non-fatal tree condition caused by environmental factors, can be mistaken for Dutch elm disease.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) While tree experts in other countries have used inoculation to cause elm trees there to develop increased resistance to Dutch elm disease, such practices have not been used in Zobrinia.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) Attempts to control the spread of the elm bark beetle, which is the carrier of the fungus that causes Dutch elm disease, have been largely unsuccessful.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) Furniture made from the wood of elm trees, which had long been popular in Zobrinia, went out of favor in Zobrinia several years ago.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



The argument arrives at the following cause-and-effect conclusion:


<u>Conclusion</u>: It must be that the elm trees in Zobrinia have somehow developed increased resistance to Dutch elm Disease.


The conclusion is based on the facts that Dutch elm disease has been killing elm trees and that, recently, the decline in the number of elm trees has slowed markedly.


We could call into question the conclusion that the cause of the slowing is increased resistance to the disease by bringing up something else that could be causing the slowing of the decline in the number of elm trees.


> <b>(A) Some elm trees in Zobrinia have recently become infected with a virulent strain of the fungus that causes the disease.
</b>


 This choice could be tempting, as it could be perceived as indicating that increased resistance to Dutch elm disease can’t be the reason for the slowing of the decline in the number of elm trees, because some elm trees have recently become infected with the disease.


However, the truth is that, even if <em>some</em> elm trees have become infected with a virulent strain of the fungus that causes the disease, it could still be the case that the slowing of the decline in the number of elm trees in Zobrinia is the result of elm trees <em>in general</em> having developed increased resistance to Dutch elm disease.


> <b>(B) Leaf scorch, which is a generally non-fatal tree condition caused by environmental factors, can be mistaken for Dutch elm disease.
</b>


 This choice could be perceived as indicating that it’s possible that the elm trees in Zobrinia were experiencing leaf scorch, rather than Dutch elm disease and as, therefore, casting doubt on the conclusion that the decline in the number of elm trees has slowed because the elm trees have developed increased resistance to Dutch elm disease.


Of course, that story doesn’t really make sense, because the passage states as fact that Dutch elm disease has been killing elm trees in Zobrinia. The fact that leaf scorch could be mistaken for Dutch elm disease does not mean that Dutch elm disease hasn’t been killing elm trees in Zobrinia or that increased resistance to the disease is not the reason for the slowing of the decline in the number of elm trees.


> <b>(C) While tree experts in other countries have used inoculation to cause elm trees there to develop increased resistance to Dutch elm disease, such practices have not been used in Zobrinia.
</b>


 This choice could be perceived to be a blatant weakener, because the fact that inoculation has not been used in Zobrinia could be perceived as indicating that the elm trees in Zobrinia must not have developed increased resistance to Dutch elm disease.


However, since the elm trees may have developed increased resistance without inoculation, this choice does not affect the strength of the argument.


> <b>(D) Attempts to control the spread of the elm bark beetle, which is the carrier of the fungus that causes Dutch elm disease, have been largely unsuccessful.
</b>


 This choice provides information that can be used to eliminate an alternative reason for the slowing of the decline in the number of elm trees: beetle control.


By serving to eliminate an alternative reason for the slowing of the decline, this choice strengthens, rather than weakens, the support for the conclusion that the slowing of the decline is the result of the elm trees’ having developed increased resistance to Dutch elm disease.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E) Furniture made from the wood of elm trees, which had long been popular in Zobrinia, went out of favor in Zobrinia several years ago.
</b>


 If furniture made from the wood of elm trees is no longer popular in Zobrinia, then it’s likely that the cutting down of elm trees for the purpose of to be making them into furniture has decreased in Zobrinia. Since such a decrease in the cutting down of elm trees could explain the slowing of the decline in the number of elm trees, this choice presents an alternative explanation for the slowing of the decline and thus serves to call into question the conclusion that increased resistance to Dutch elm disease is the cause.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
