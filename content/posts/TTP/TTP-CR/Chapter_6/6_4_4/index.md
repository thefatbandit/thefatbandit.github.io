## Lesson Content
In some cases, the correct answer to a Weaken question involving a cause-and-effect argument will weaken the support for the conclusion by showing that the supposed cause occurs without the effect occurring.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When dealing with a cause-and-effect argument, showing that the supposed cause occurs without the effect occurring will weaken the argument. After all, if there is a cause-and-effect relationship at play, when we see the cause, we should also see the effect.
{{< /alert >}}

In many situations, the fact that an effect does not occur when a supposed cause does occur is a good reason to question whether the supposed cause actually causes the effect.

For example, if someone were to say that the reason that a palm tree in Cannes died was that snow fell on the tree, the fact that snow had fallen on that palm tree many times before without it’s dying or the fact that snow had fallen on many other palm trees in Cannes without their dying would be a good reason to question the conclusion that snow caused the tree to die.

To see how this type of reasoning would appear in a Weaken question involving a cause-and-effect argument, consider the following example:

> <em>The CEO of The Health Group was astonished to notice that, during the past week, the number of employees who had arrived for work later than the company’s stated start of business was triple what it had been during any other week in the company’s history. However, when he found out that much of the subway system of the city in which The Health Group is located had been closed that week for repairs, he concluded that employees had arrived late because of issues related to the subway system closures.</em>

> <em>Which of the following, if true, provides a clear reason to question the validity of the CEO’s conclusion?</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>The same parts of the city’s subway system had been closed in that same way for six entire weeks out of the previous fifteen weeks.</em>

You can see how the correct answer weakens the CEO’s argument.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

One way to weaken the support for a cause-and-effect conclusion is to show that the supposed cause occurs without the effect occurring.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following examples in which the arguments are weakened by information indicating that the supposed cause occurs, but the effect does not.

##  Example 15 

Last spring, an algae bloom, a rapid increase in algae density, occurred in Lake Farmington, turning the water in the lake from blue to green.

Which of the following, if true, would most clearly call into question the above explanation for the algae bloom in Lake Farmington?

1. [x] (A) The reclaimed groundwater from the farm near Lake Farmington was also released in similar amounts into Lake Williamson, which is ecologically similar to Lake Farmington, yet no algae bloom occurred.
2. [ ] (B) Officials have known for some time that an algae bloom in Lake Farmington was possible, yet they did not take measures to prevent a bloom from occurring.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) After the reclaimed groundwater from the farm near Lake Farmington was stopped from entering the lake, the algae bloom immediately ceased.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) Although the algae that bloomed last spring have lived in Lake Farmington for hundreds of years, prior to last spring, Lake Farmington had never experienced an algae bloom.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) When the density of algae in a lake reaches a critical value, toxins produced by the algae can harm crops on farms that irrigate with water from that lake.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



<u>Background</u>: Last spring, an algae bloom, a rapid increase in algae density, occurred in Lake Farmington, turning the water in the lake from blue to green.


<u>Premise</u>: Just before the algae bloom occurred, a large farm near the lake began releasing into the lake reclaimed groundwater containing nitrogen and phosphorus in high concentrations.


<u>Premise</u>: Nitrogen and phosphorus are key nutrients for algae.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Therefore, it must be that the reclaimed groundwater from the farm was the cause of the algae bloom.


Notice that the author is making the cause-and-effect claim that the reclaimed groundwater from the farm was the cause of the algae bloom. As always, we must be skeptical any time we are presented with a cause-and-effect claim. In this case, we must ask ourselves, “Is the reclaimed groundwater from the farm really the cause of the algae bloom?”


What might the author have failed to consider? Could there be more to the story?


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A) The reclaimed groundwater from the farm near Lake Farmington was also released in similar amounts into Lake Williamson, which is ecologically similar to Lake Farmington, yet no algae bloom occurred.
</b>


 What this choice says weakens the argument.


After all, if it is true that the reclaimed groundwater from the farm near Lake Farmington was also released in similar amounts into Lake Williamson, which is ecologically similar to Lake Farmington, yet no algae bloom occurred, then we are a lot less sure that the reclaimed groundwater from the farm is actually the cause of the algae bloom.


We must ask ourselves, if this reclaimed groundwater from the farm is really to blame for the algae bloom, then why did an algae bloom not occur in Lake Williamson, which is ecologically similar to Lake Farmington? We certainly would have expected an algae bloom to occur in both lakes.


This answer choice shows a situation in which the supposed cause (reclaimed groundwater) is present, but the effect (an algae bloom) does not occur. Thus, the author’s cause-and-effect claim is weakened.


> <b>(B) Officials have known for some time that an algae bloom in Lake Farmington was possible, yet they did not take measures to prevent a bloom from occurring.
</b>


 While it may have been shortsighted and even negligent for officials to do nothing to prevent an algae bloom that they knew was possible, that fact does not weaken case for the cause-and-effect claim made by the author.


Does their knowing that an algae bloom in Lake Farmington was possible indicate that the reclaimed groundwater from the farm was not the cause of the algae bloom? No, not at all.


In fact, their knowing doesn’t indicate anything about the cause of the algae bloom, nor does their lack of action to prevent the algae bloom. Of course, knowing something is possible and doing nothing to prevent that thing are not the same as actually <em>causing</em> something to happen.


Thus, the reclaimed groundwater from the farm could very well still be the cause of the algae bloom, and the author’s argument is not weakened.


> <b>(C) After the reclaimed groundwater from the farm near Lake Farmington was stopped from entering the lake, the algae bloom immediately ceased.
</b>


 What this choice says strengthens the argument.


If, after the reclaimed groundwater from the farm was stopped from entering Lake Farmington, the algae bloom immediately ceased, then we have more conviction in the author’s claim that the reclaimed groundwater was the cause of the algae bloom, because when the cause (reclaimed groundwater) was removed, the effect (algae bloom) disappeared.


After all, if a cause-and-effect link exists between two variables, when the cause goes away, we expect that the effect does also.


Of course, we are not looking for information that strengthens the argument; we need information that calls into question the author’s cause-and-effect claim.


> <b>(D) Although the algae that bloomed last spring have lived in Lake Farmington for hundreds of years, prior to last spring, Lake Farmington had never experienced an algae bloom.
</b>


 What this choice says strengthens the argument.


If the algae that bloomed last spring have lived in Lake Farmington for hundreds of years, yet Lake Farmington never experienced an algae bloom before last spring, then we have more conviction in the strength of the argument. After all, just after the reclaimed groundwater from the farm went into the lake, the algae bloom occurred, even though the algae have lived in the lake for hundreds of years without blooming.


That situation would certainly make us more willing to accept that there is a cause-and-effect relationship between the release of groundwater into the lake and the algae bloom.


Of course, we are not looking for information that strengthens the argument; we need information that calls into question the author’s cause-and-effect claim.


> <b>(E) When the density of algae in a lake reaches a critical value, toxins produced by the algae can harm crops on farms that irrigate with water from that lake.
</b>


 This choice could be tempting because it mentions farms, algae, and a lake, and therefore this choice may seem relevant to the argument.


However, this answer choice is about something that occurs once the density of algae gets too high. That information does not help us understand what caused the algae level in Lake Farmington to get so high.


In other words, this choice simply states a fact about algae; it doesn’t weaken the case for the cause-and-effect claim made by the author.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

##  Example 16 

For decades, paleontologists struggled to explain why the saber-toothed cat died out approximately 10,000 years ago, after having thrived for approximately 2 million years.

Which of the following, if true, would most clearly call into question the above explanation for the saber-toothed cat’s extinction?

1. [ ] (A) The largest extinction on record, the Permian extinction, was the result of volcanic activity.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) Though recent scientific research indicates that Earth’s climate is now becoming warm enough to cause extinction of some species, populations of many species seem unaffected by the warm conditions.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) The geological record tends to confirm that 15,000 years ago, the global average temperature started increasing considerably.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) Evidence suggests that the earth quickly warmed considerably around 50,000 years ago before it started cooling off again around 30,000 years ago.
5. [ ] (E) Many other species of megafauna, such as the bison, continued to thrive during the period during which the saber-toothed cat died out.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



Since this argument concludes that a rapid shift in climate caused the saber-toothed cat to go extinct, a good way to weaken the case for this conclusion is to show that there were other, similar periods of climate change that did not cause extinction of the saber-toothed cat.


> <b>(A) The largest extinction on record, the Permian extinction, was the result of volcanic activity.
</b>


 The argument is not concerned with the reasons for other extinctions. The argument is solely concerned with the reason for the extinction of the saber-toothed cat.


The largest extinction may have been the result of volcanic activity, but we have no information that indicates that this largest extinction included the extinction of the saber-toothed cat. Therefore, this choice does not affect the argument.


> <b>(B) Though recent scientific research indicates that Earth’s climate is now becoming warm enough to cause extinction of some species, populations of many species seem unaffected by the warm conditions.
</b>


 This choice could tempting, as the fact that populations of some species seem unaffected by the current warm conditions could be perceived as providing a reason to question the conclusion that warming in the past caused the extinction of the saber-toothed cat.


However, the truth is that, unlike the species that seem unaffected by the warmer conditions existing currently, the saber-toothed cat did go extinct, and it went extinct when temperatures increased. Overall, the fact that some species don’t seem to be affected by warm conditions now does not mean that warm conditions in the past did not cause the extinction of one particular species, the saber-toothed cat.


> <b>(C) The geological record tends to confirm that 15,000 years ago, the global average temperature started increasing considerably.
</b>


 This information provides some confirmation of an aspect of the explanation put forth, the aspect that the Earth’s climate warmed up. Thus, this choice only strengthens the argument.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D) Evidence suggests that the earth quickly warmed considerably around 50,000 years ago before it started cooling off again around 30,000 years ago.
</b>


If considerable warming that quickly occurred 50,000 years ago did not result in the extinction of the saber-toothed cat (since the cat thrived for 2 million years before it went extinct around 10,000 years ago, we know that it existed 50,000 years ago), then the conclusion is weakened.


After all, if climate change was enough to cause the extinction of the cat, then the climate change that occurred around 50,000 years ago should have led to the extinction of the cat. So, given that the extinction did not happen 50,000 years ago, we have a reason to believe that climate change was not the reason for the extinction.


So, this choice casts doubt on the conclusion that climate change led to the extinction of the saber-toothed cat because similar climate change happened before but did not lead to the extinction of the cat.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E) Many other species of megafauna, such as the bison, continued to thrive during the period during which the saber-toothed cat died out.
</b>


 Notice that, regardless of what this choice says about “many other species of megafauna,” the saber-toothed cat did go extinct, and thus there must be some explanation for the extinction of the saber-toothed cat.


The passage presents an explanation for the extinction of the saber-toothed cat: the climate changed, and the saber-toothed cat could not adapt quickly enough to survive.


The fact that other megafauna thrived does not indicate that that explanation is incorrect. After all, it’s quite possible that the other megafauna were able to adapt to climate change and, thus, to thrive under the same conditions that led to the extinction of the saber-toothed cat.


Thus, this choice does not impact the argument.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Example 17 

Health Expert: The prevalence of dementia has reached epidemic proportions, with nearly one in three people over the age of 85 experiencing the issue.

Which of the following, if true, does the most to undermine the support for the above conclusion?

1. [ ] (A) The neurons of people who go on to experience dementia start to form protein plaques long before any symptoms of the disease appear.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) By the time they reach age 5, the vast majority of people become infected with herpes viruses, which then remain in the people’s nervous systems.
3. [ ] (C) To date, no known medical method has been successful in completely clearing a person’s nervous system of herpes viruses.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) Not all protein in the neurons of people experiencing dementia clumps into plaques.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) Herpes viruses that infect humans exist in several different forms, each of which is associated with different types of health issues.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



The question asks us to find the choice that does the most to undermine the support for the following conclusion:


<u>Conclusion</u>: It is clear that the cause of dementia is herpes viruses.


The conclusion is supported by the fact that neurons exposed to herpes viruses in the lab have formed protein plaques of the type associated with the development of dementia.


But are herpes viruses really the cause of the condition?


Information indicating that it is often the case that the supposed cause, herpes viruses, is present in a person, but that that person does not experience the effect, dementia, would be a reason to question the validity of the conclusion.


> <b>(A) The neurons of people who go on to experience dementia start to form protein plaques long before any symptoms of the disease appear.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, because, since it appears to disconnect the formation of the plaques from the onset of the condition, it could be perceived as indicating that, even if herpes viruses trigger the formation of plaques, the viruses don’t cause dementia.


However, the truth is that the fact that the formation of plaques begins long before symptoms appear does not affect the strength of the argument, as the plaques are still associated with the health issue. All this choice indicates is that plaques exist in a person’s system before it becomes apparent that the person is experiencing dementia. So, this choice does not really contradict the understanding of dementia that is the basis of the support for the conclusion.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B) By the time they reach age 5, the vast majority of people become infected with herpes viruses, which then remain in the people’s nervous systems.
</b>


This choice weakens the argument by indicating that the cause, herpes viruses, is often present when the effect, dementia, is not. In order to see why, we have to notice a key detail in the passage.


The passage says that <em>nearly one in three people</em> over the age of 85 experience the issue.


This choice says that <em>the vast majority of people</em> become infected with herpes viruses, which then remain in the people’s nervous systems.


What we can notice is that “the vast majority of people” is much more than half of all people, while “nearly one in three people” is less than a third of people, and much less than “the vast majority of people.” Thus, the information we have indicates that many more people harbor herpes viruses in their nervous systems than experience dementia.


By providing information indicating that it is often the case that people harbor herpes viruses in their nervous systems yet don’t experience dementia, this choice serves to call into question the conclusion that herpes viruses cause dementia.


> <b>(C) To date, no known medical method has been successful in completely clearing a person’s nervous system of herpes viruses.
</b>


This choice weakens the support for the wrong conclusion.


If the conclusion were that, because it has been discovered that herpes viruses cause dementia, people are on the verge of solving dementia, this choice would cast doubt on the conclusion. After all, if people have not found a way to clear people’s nervous systems of herpes viruses, then, even if it were known that herpes viruses cause dementia, there might not be any way to take advantage of that knowledge.


However, that conclusion is not the conclusion that we have to weaken, and the fact that no known medical method has been successful in completely clearing a person’s nervous system of herpes viruses does not contradict or weaken the support for the conclusion that herpes viruses cause dementia.


> <b>(D) Not all protein in the neurons of people experiencing dementia clumps into plaques.
</b>


This choice could be tempting since it uses words that appear in the passage, “protein,” “neurons,” “dementia,” and “plaques,” and since what this choice says could be perceived as somehow contradicting the premise that protein plaques are associated with dementia.


However, a correct answer to a weaken question will never contradict a premise, because the premises are taken to be factual, and the truth is that this choice is merely a pseudo-weakener worded to seem to conflict with what the passage says.


After all, regardless of what this choice says, the passage states as fact that protein plaques are associated with dementia. In other words, even if “not all protein in the neurons of people experiencing dementia clumps into plaques,” it is still the case that protein that does clump into plaques is associated with dementia.


So, regardless of what this choice says, protein plaques are associated with dementia, neurons exposed to herpes viruses have formed protein plaques of the type associated with the development of dementia, and therefore, there is reason to believe that herpes viruses cause dementia.


Thus, this choice does not affect the support for the conclusion.


> <b>(E) Herpes viruses that infect humans exist in several different forms, each of which is associated with different types of health issues.
</b>


This choice could be temping, because the implications of what it says could be twisted in a way along the lines of the following:


Herpes viruses are associated with different types of health issues. Thus, some types of herpes viruses cause health issues that are not dementia. So, we can’t be sure that herpes viruses cause dementia.


However, that reasoning is not really logical. Even if we were not sure that a particular herpes virus would cause dementia, it could still be the case that, when dementia occurs, it is caused by some form of herpes virus. So, this choice has no effect on the strength of the argument.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

Let’s now discuss another way to weaken the support for a cause-and-effect conclusion.
