## Lesson Content
Since one way to weaken a cause-and-effect argument is to present an alternate cause for the observed effect, one way to strengthen a cause-and-effect argument is to rule out an alternative cause for the effect.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

One way to strengthen the support for a cause-and-effect conclusion is to rule out alternative causes.
{{< /alert >}}

Remember that, whenever an author makes a cause-and-effect claim, he assumes that his claim is accurate.

Therefore, one way to strengthen the case for a cause-and-effect claim is to add more reason to believe that that assumption is a good one.

Remember, we don’t have to prove a conclusion correct to strengthen support for it.

Let’s look at a simple example:

> <em>While testing a new multivitamin on a group of research subjects with low vitamin D levels, Company X saw a significant increase in the vitamin D levels in all the subjects during the period of the study. Company X then concluded that its multivitamin had surely caused the rise in the vitamin D levels in the test subjects.</em>

These people who have low vitamin D levels are given a multivitamin.

While Company X’s conclusion may very well be valid, there could be other factors that might have led to the increase in Vitamin D levels in the subjects.

For example, the period of the study might have coincided with a very sunny period of weather, and, given that sunlight is known to boost natural production of vitamin D, the sunlight, not the multivitamin, might have caused the increase in vitamin D levels in the subjects.

Or, during the period of the study, the subjects may have increased their intakes of vitamin D rich foods.

There are many other factors that could have increased the vitamin D levels of the subjects.

So, one way to strengthen the support for the conclusion that it is the multivitamin that has caused the higher vitamin D levels is to rule out as many such alternative causes as possible.

For example, imagine that we determined that the subjects did not increase their sun exposure.

Now, what if we were also to discover that the subjects’ diets remained unchanged? Wouldn’t the original cause-and-effect conclusion be even more believable? For sure, it would.

Now, imagine that we could rule out all other possible variables that could have affected the subjects’ vitamin D levels.

Thus, one way to strengthen a cause-and-effect argument is to rule out alternative causes of the observed effect.

##  Example 19 

200 college seniors were recruited to participate in a study.

Which of the following, if true, does the most to confirm the conclusion stated above?

1. [ ] (A) When the study participants were recruited, they agreed to fill out a second survey ten years after they graduated from college.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) Many colleges could add conflict resolution classes to their curricula without hiring additional faculty.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) The relationships of a person who has strong conflict resolution skills will tend to be relatively low in stress.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) It may make sense for college students who want to earn high incomes to take conflict resolution classes.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) The participants’ survey responses showed that those given the conflict resolution training were, on average, no more motivated to have high incomes than those not given the training.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



The author of this argument observes the pattern that the average income of people who were given thirty hours of conflict resolution training was significantly higher than the average income of people not given that training. Given that information, the author arrives at the following conclusion.


<u>Conclusion</u>: It must be that conflict resolution skills help people increase their incomes.


Of course, the author may have missed another factor that could have caused the correlation between people’s being given the conflict resolution training and their having higher incomes. So, one way to strengthen the case for the author’s conclusion is ruling out an alternative cause for the observed effect.


> <b>(A) When the study participants were recruited, they agreed to fill out a second survey ten years after they graduated from college.
</b>


 This choice has the wrong effect. Rather than strengthen the case for the conclusion, this choice simply explains part of the scenario in the passage. It explains how it came to be that the study participants filled out a second survey ten years after they graduated.


Of course, information on how it came to be that they filled out the second survey does not help to confirm a conclusion based on the results of that survey. After all, information on why all 200 participants filled out the second survey does not help us to determine what caused the difference in incomes.


> <b>(B) Many colleges could add conflict resolution classes to their curricula without hiring additional faculty.
</b>


 This choice supports the wrong conclusion. Rather than support the conclusion of the argument presented by the passage, this choice supports a conclusion along the lines of that it would be easy for many colleges to add conflict resolution classes to their curricula.


To correctly answer a Strengthen question, we have to be careful to find support for the conclusion of the argument in the passage, not for a related conclusion, and this choice does not provide any confirmation for the conclusion that conflict resolution skills help people increase their incomes.


> <b>(C) The relationships of a person who has strong conflict resolution skills will tend to be relatively low in stress.
</b>


 This choice is tempting because it has an effect similar to the effect we need.


We need information that confirms that conflict resolution skills help people increase their incomes. On the other hand, this choice <em>may explain how</em> conflict resolution skills help people increase their incomes. After all, the fact that the relationships of a person who has strong conflict resolution skills will tend to be relatively low in stress may explain why the people who were given conflict resolution training had higher incomes: they somehow did better in their careers because their relationships were relatively low in stress.


Notice, however, that this choice does not really provide confirmation for the argument’s conclusion. After all, do we know for sure that having relationships that are relatively <em>low</em> in stress causes people to have relatively high incomes? Not really. It could be, for instance, that people who have relationships that are relatively <em>high</em> in stress work all the time to get away from other people and therefore have relatively high incomes.


So, this choice does not really provide additional confirmation for the conclusion.


> <b>(D) It may make sense for college students who want to earn high incomes to take conflict resolution classes.
</b>


 Notice that, rather than provide confirmation for the conclusion, this choice <em>follows from</em> the conclusion. After all, if it is true that conflict resolution skills help people increase their incomes, then it may make sense for college students who want to earn high incomes to take conflict resolution classes.


On the other hand, what this choice says about what it <em>may</em> make sense for college students to do does not confirm that conflict resolution skills do in fact help people increase their incomes.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E) The participants’ survey responses showed that those given the conflict resolution training were, on average, no more motivated to have high incomes than those not given the training.
</b>


 This choice provides additional confirmation for the conclusion by serving to rule out an alternative cause.


It could have been that motivation to have high incomes, rather than conflict resolution skills, caused the people who were given the conflict resolution training to have incomes that were higher, on average, than those of the people not given the training.


Thus, what this choice does by saying that the survey responses showed that those given the training were no more motivated to have high incomes than those not given the training is indicate that a difference in motivation was not responsible for the difference in incomes. With the ruling out of that alternative cause, the conclusion that conflict resolution skills caused the people given the training to have higher incomes becomes a bit more believable.


So, by serving to rule out a possible alternative cause, motivation, this choice helps to confirm that conflict resolution skills help people to increase their incomes.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Example 20 

Every year the students who go to Middletown Elementary School and Greeneville Elementary School are tested for the purpose of assessing their progress in a variety of academic areas, and every year the test scores reveal that, on average, the students at Middletown Elementary have made more progress than have the students at Greeneville Elementary.

Which of the following, if true, provides the most additional support to the conclusion?

1. [ ] (A) The administration at Middletown Elementary is known for being particularly effective in determining how best to use technology to facilitate learning.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) The parents of students at Greeneville Elementary are as supportive of the students’ academic progress as the parents of students at Middletown Elementary are.
3. [ ] (C) Sometimes teachers focus their efforts on getting students’ test scores to increase, to the detriment of students’ making well rounded academic progress.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) School administrators often consider student test scores in evaluating and rewarding teacher performance.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) Some teachers at Middletown Elementary have received awards for excellence in teaching.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Breakdown of the Argument:


<u>Background</u>: Every year, the students at Middletown Elementary and Greenville Elementary are tested to assess their academic progress.


<u>Premise</u>: Every year the test scores reveal that the students at Middletown Elementary have made more progress than have the students at Greenville Elementary.


<u>Conclusion</u>: The teachers at Middletown Elementary must be more effective than the teachers at Greenville Elementary.


The argument goes from the premise that every year the students at Middletown Elementary make more progress than is made by the students at Greenville Elementary to the conclusion that the teachers at Middletown Elementary are more effective than the teachers at Greenville elementary.


Of course, the reason for the difference in student progress could be something other than levels of teacher effectiveness. So, information that helps rule out another reason for the difference would make the argument stronger by increasing the likelihood that the difference in student performance is indeed due to a difference in levels of teacher effectiveness.


> <b>(A) The administration at Middletown Elementary is known for being particularly effective in determining how best to use technology to facilitate learning.
</b>


 It might be tempting to make up a story about why the fact that the administration at Middletown Elementary is known for being particularly effective in determining how best to use technology to facilitate learning means that the teachers at Middletown elementary are more effective than the teachers at Greenville Elementary.


However, the truth is that we have no clear reason to believe that there is such a connection. In fact, for all we know, effective use of technology could be making up for ineffective teaching at Middletown Elementary.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B) The parents of students at Greeneville Elementary are as supportive of the students’ academic progress as the parents of students at Middletown Elementary are.
</b>


 Differences in levels of parental support of student progress could be the reason for the difference between the academic progress of students at Middletown Elementary and that of students at Greenville Elementary. So, by saying that parents of Greenville Elementary students are as supportive as parents of Middletown Elementary students, this choice rules out the possibility that a difference in parental support underlies the difference in student performance.


Thus, by ruling out an alternative explanation for the difference in performance, this choice increases our confidence in the conclusion that a difference in teacher effectiveness underlies the difference in student performance.


> <b>(C) Sometimes teachers focus their efforts on getting students’ test scores to increase, to the detriment of students’ making well rounded academic progress.
</b>


 This answer choice could be tempting, even though it does not strengthen the argument, because it states a fact about how teachers teach that could cross a test-taker’s mind as the test-taker reads the passage.


All the same, adding the general fact that some teachers focus on getting students to score higher on tests does make any stronger the case that the teachers at Middletown are more effective than those at Greenville Elementary.


> <b>(D) School administrators often consider student test scores in evaluating and rewarding teacher performance.
</b>


 The fact that school administrators use student test scores in evaluating teacher performance could be construed as lending weight to the argument, which is based on test scores. However, the truth is that the argument has already used test scores to make its case. To strengthen the argument, we need new information that we can combine with the information on test scores.


> <b>(E) Some teachers at Middletown Elementary have received awards for excellence in teaching.
</b>


 While what this choice says may seem to indicate that teachers at Middletown Elementary are especially effective and, therefore, more effective than the teachers at Greenville Elementary, the truth is that the fact that some teachers at Middletown Elementary have received awards does not clearly indicate that teachers at Middletown are in general more effective than those at Greenville. It could even be that many teachers at Greenville Elementary have also received such awards.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
