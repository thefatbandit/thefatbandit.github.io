## Lesson Content
An inference is a conclusion that can be drawn from a set of facts.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

An inference is a conclusion that can be drawn from a set of facts.
{{< /alert >}}

On the GMAT, a Critical Reasoning Inference question is a question that presents a set of facts and then asks which of the answer choices states a conclusion that can be properly inferred from that set of facts.

As you read the above description, you may have wondered why the word “properly” was included.

Unlike the conclusions in other types of GMAT questions, which, as we have discussed, may not be fully supported by the facts given, the correct answer to an Inference question is a conclusion that is fully supported by the facts given, and, thus, must be true.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Unlike the conclusions in other types of GMAT questions, which, as we have discussed, may not be fully supported by the facts given, the correct answer to an Inference question is a conclusion that is fully supported by the facts given, and, thus, must be true.
{{< /alert >}}

If, given the facts presented in the stimulus, an answer could be true sometimes but not true at other times, that option is not the correct answer to an Inference question.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

If, given the facts presented in the stimulus, an answer could be true sometimes but not true at other times, that option is not the correct answer to an Inference question.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s say that again: In GMAT Critical Reasoning, a properly drawn inference is not just something that could be <em>reasonably</em> concluded given what is said in the stimulus.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The correct answer to an Inference question is the choice that must be true given what is stated in the passage. An answer option that makes a statement that may not be true will not be the correct answer to an Inference question.
{{< /alert >}}

Since an Inference question requires us to choose an answer that can be proven true solely on the basis of the information presented in the stimulus, correctly answering Inference questions requires determining exactly what the statements in the stimuli say and do not say.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Correctly answering an Inference question requires noting exactly what the statements in the stimulus say and do not say.
{{< /alert >}}

<u>Inference Questions Don’t Really Contain “Arguments.” </u>

While we’ve gotten into the habit of referring to the stimulus of a Critical Reasoning question as an “argument,” the stimuli in Inference questions generally do not contain arguments in the purest sense of the word “argument,” wherein conclusions based upon some premises are presented.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Inference questions generally do not contain arguments in the purest sense of the word “argument,” wherein conclusions based upon some premises are presented. Instead, the stimulus of an Inference question simply presents us with a set of facts. Then, from that set of facts, our job is to determine what can be properly inferred.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 1 

Research has shown that engaging in conversation while driving requires that a driver engage in two cognitive processes simultaneously, and, thus, that drivers talking on cell phones are not as focused on handling challenging moments in driving as are drivers who are not engaging in conversation.

If the statements above are true, which of the following can be properly inferred from them?

1. [ ] (A) Drivers who have passengers in their cars are less likely to engage in cell phone conversations than are drivers without passengers in their cars.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) It is generally the case that drivers who converse with passengers in their cars are more focused on handling challenging moments in driving than are drivers talking on cell phones.
3. [ ] (C) The driving of drivers who have extensive practice in handling multiple tasks simultaneously is not affected by those drivers’ conversing on cell phones.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) If a driver conversing on a cell phone while driving were to stop conversing in order to handle a challenging driving situation, the other person conversing might hang up.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) Driving while talking on a cell phone is comparable in dangerousness to driving while intoxicated.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

##### Question Level: medium



Information Given:


<u>Fact 1</u>: Engaging in conversation while driving requires that a driver engage in two cognitive processes simultaneously.


<u>Fact 2</u>: Drivers talking on cell phones are not as focused on handling challenging moments in driving as are drivers who are not engaging in conversation.


<u>Fact 3</u>: It is generally the case that a driver and a passenger will take breaks from conversing when the driver most needs to concentrate on driving.


To get the correct answer to this question, we have to be careful not to choose a choice that is plausible but not truly supported by the passage. The passage discusses the effect that conversing on a cell phone has on driving and provides a key fact about conversations between drivers and passengers in cars. What we can infer will have to be based on these specific facts, and no others.


> <b>(A) Drivers who have passengers in their cars are less likely to engage in cell phone conversations than are drivers without passengers in their cars.
</b>


 While what this choice says may be true in the real world, the passage does not say anything about how having a passenger in a car affects whether a driver talks on a cell phone. So, the information in the passage does not support this choice. In answering an Inference question, we need to be vigilant not to choose as correct answer choices that say things that are true in the real world yet at the same time are not valid inferences.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B) It is generally the case that drivers who converse with passengers in their cars are more focused on handling challenging moments in driving than are drivers talking on cell phones.
</b>


 The passage makes clear that drivers talking on cell phones are not as focused on handling challenging moments in driving as are drivers not engaged in conversation. It also says that drivers and passengers in the same car who are talking will take breaks from conversing when the driver most needs to concentrate on driving, meaning, effectively, that during challenging moment in driving, such a driver will not be conversing. Since drivers not conversing are more focused on driving than are drivers conversing on cell phones, we can infer from what the passage says that it is generally the case that drivers who converse with passengers in their cars are more focused on handling challenging moments in driving than are drivers talking on cell phones.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) The driving of drivers who have extensive practice in handling multiple tasks simultaneously is not affected by those drivers’ conversing on cell phones.
</b>


 The passage does not provide any information regarding how practice in simultaneously handling multiple tasks affects a driver’s ability to drive while conversing on a cell phone.


> <b>(D) If a driver conversing on a cell phone while driving were to stop conversing in order to handle a challenging driving situation, the other person conversing might hang up.
</b>


 While what this choice says seems plausible, the passage does not give us any reason to believe that it is true.


> <b>(E) Driving while talking on a cell phone is comparable in dangerousness to driving while intoxicated.
</b>


 The passage provides no information that we can use to make a comparison between the dangerousness of conversing on a cell phone while driving and that of driving while intoxicated.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

Let’s now discuss some basic guidelines for correctly answering Inference questions.
