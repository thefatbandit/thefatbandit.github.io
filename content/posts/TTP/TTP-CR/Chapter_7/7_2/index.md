## Lesson Content
There are a number of smart strategies that we can use when answering Inference questions.

<u>Never Bring in Outside Information or Knowledge </u>

We must answer Inference questions using only the facts provided to us in the stimuli; we must not bring in any outside information.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

We must answer Inference questions using only the facts provided to us in the stimulus; we must not bring in any outside information. Do not fall for answer options that state facts that are true but are not inferences supported by the facts provided in the stimulus.
{{< /alert >}}

For example, let’s again consider the following choice from the question in the previous section on driving while talking on a cell phone:

> (E) Driving while conversing on a cell phone is comparable in dangerousness to driving while intoxicated.

A test-taker might be aware that research has indeed indicated that conversing on a cell phone can be as detrimental to a person’s driving effectiveness as intoxication.

In Inference questions, trap choices like this one prey upon our natural tendency to seek answer options that sound familiar or logical to us.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The fact that an answer choice sounds familiar or logical to us does not make that answer choice correct.
{{< /alert >}}

<u>An Inference Need Not Summarize the Passage or Provide a Main Conclusion to an Argument</u>

Often, people feel that an inference must either summarize the passage or provide a logical main conclusion to an argument.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Often, people feel that an inference must either summarize the passage or provide a logical main conclusion to an argument. In fact, an inference need not do either of these things.
{{< /alert >}}

OK – wait a minute.

Given an argument, an inference is simply <em>any</em> conclusion that can be properly drawn from the set of facts that make up that argument.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Given an argument, an inference is simply any conclusion that can be properly drawn from the set of facts that make up that argument.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following passage:

> <em>No new houses have been built in Verdantville in the past eight years, and the values of houses in Verdantville have not increased. Over the same time period, the number of children living in Verdantville has increased substantially. The costs of operating the schools in Verdantville are paid for entirely by property taxes, which are based on house values.</em>

Given what the passage says about the number of houses in Verdantville, the value of those houses, the number of children living in Verdantville, and the way the costs of operating the schools are covered, the following statement could be the main conclusion of the argument:

> <em>(<u>Possible Main Conclusion of the Argument</u>) Thus, in order to cover the expenses associated with educating all the children, Verdantville will have to increase property tax rates.</em>

Notice, while this possible main conclusion is supported by the information in the passage, it does not have to be true given what the passage says.

Contrast that main conclusion with the following inferences that we can make given what the passage says.

> <em>(<u>Inference</u>)</em> <em>The aggregate value of the houses in Verdantville is no greater than it was eight years ago.</em>

Given the fact that neither the number of houses nor the values of houses in Verdantville have increased, we can infer that the aggregate value of the houses in Verdantville is no greater than it was eight years ago.

> <em>(<u>Inference</u>)</em> <em>On average, there are more children per house in Verdantville than there were eight years ago.</em>

Given the fact that the number of houses in Verdantville has not increased and the fact that the number of children living in Verdantville has increased, it must be true that the average number of children per house has increased.

Notice, while neither of the two inferences is the main conclusion of the argument, both are conclusions that one could draw from what the passage says.

So, in answering an Inference question, we don’t necessarily need to seek out what would be the author’s main conclusion to the argument.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

An inference need not be the main conclusion of an argument; if the facts presented are true – and they always will be in Critical Reasoning Questions – any conclusion that can be properly drawn from them will be a valid inference. It’s important to be flexible when looking for inferences.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 2 

In a recent survey, 1,000 professional poker players were asked whether they had any formal training in statistics.

If the statements above are true, which of the following can be properly inferred from them?

1. [ ] (A) Most professional poker players do not have time to respond to surveys.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) Having formal training in statistics is helpful for professional poker players.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) Most professional poker players opt to informally study statistics on their own.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) A person can become a professional poker player without having any formal training in statistics.
5. [ ] (E) Most professional poker players do not attend college at any time during their careers.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



To correctly answer this question, we must stick closely to the facts provided to us in the passage and resist the urge to bring in unsupported outside information. Remember, we are seeking an answer choice that says something that MUST be 100-percent true given what the passage says.


> <b>(A) Most professional poker players do not have time to respond to surveys.
</b>


 The information provided by the passage contradicts what this statement says. After all, of the 1,000 professional poker players who were asked whether they had any formal training in statistics, 900 players responded. In other words, 90 percent of those surveyed responded.


Thus, given what the passage says, it seems likely that most professional poker players <em>do</em> have time to respond to surveys.


When an answer choice is contradicted by information in the passage, that answer choice will never be an inference.


> <b>(B) Having formal training in statistics is helpful for professional poker players.
</b>


 Notice that the passage never provides us with information regarding how helpful formal training in statistics is to professional poker players.


The only information about formal training in statistics that the passage provides is that 400 of the 900 professional poker players who responded to the survey indicated that they <em>did not</em> have formal training in statistics.


The passage says nothing about whether the other 500 players who responded to the survey had formal training in statistics.


Furthermore, even if some of the players did have formal training in statistics, the passage does not say that it was useful to them.


So, the passage does not support what this choice says.


If you choose this choice as correct, you may have done so because it seemed quite plausible. After all, poker is a game of numbers and probabilities. So, what this choice says may have resonated with you. Remember, however, when answering inference questions, we are seeking something that MUST be true given what the passage says, not something that is simply plausible.


> <b>(C) Most professional poker players opt to informally study statistics on their own.
</b>


 Notice that the survey is about professional poker players who have formal training in statistics. We are not provided with any information about whether these players opt to informally study statistics on their own.


The fact that 400 players said that they do not have formal training in statistics does not mean that those 400 players have studied statistics informally.


For all we know from what the passage says, many of the players have not studied statistics at all.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) A person can become a professional poker player without having any formal training in statistics.
</b>


 Notice that 400 of the 900 professional poker players who responded to the survey did not have any formal training in statistics, and yet, they are professional poker players.


Of course, then, those 400 poker players are living proof that one can become a professional poker player without having any formal training in statistics.


Therefore, given what the passage says, we see that it must be true that a person can become a professional poker player without having any formal training in statistics.


> <b>(E) Most professional poker players do not attend college at any time during their careers.
</b>


We don’t have any information about whether professional poker players do or do not attend college at any time during their careers. For all we know from what the passage says, 100 percent of poker players attend college during their careers.


Thus, this choice is completely unsupported.


If you picked this choice as correct, you may have done so because what it says seems plausible. Perhaps you felt that a person who was a professional poker player would have avoided attending college. Remember, though, we’re seeking a choice that MUST be true given what the passage says, not a choice that just sounds plausible.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Example 3 

In nuclear fusion, the nuclei of two atoms join to form a nucleus of a different element and, in the process, release energy that could be used to produce electrical energy.

If the statements above are true, which of the following must also be true?

1. [ ] (A) Fusion will not be a viable means of solving the world’s energy related problems in the near future.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) If temperatures of 50 million degrees Celsius are reached, nuclear fusion will occur.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) If the nuclei of two atoms joined to form a nucleus of a different element, that process occurred in a situation characterized by temperatures around 50 million degrees Celsius.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) Cold fusion cannot occur at temperatures above room temperature.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) If, in an experiment, energy from a nuclear reaction was used to produce electrical energy, cold fusion did not drive that process.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



Facts Presented in the Passage:


<u>Fact 1</u>: In nuclear fusion, the nuclei of two atoms join to form a nucleus of a different element and, in the process, release energy that could be used to produce electrical energy.


<u>Fact 2</u>: Generally, temperatures around 50 million degrees Celsius are needed to produce nuclear fusion, but such temperatures are extremely difficult to reach.


<u>Fact 3</u>: Now, scientists are studying cold fusion, which could theoretically produce energy through nuclear fusion occurring at room temperature, helping to solve the world’s energy related problems.


<u>Fact 4</u>: However, to date, no one has demonstrated that cold fusion can actually occur.


To get this question correct, we have to differentiate between a main conclusion that could be true given what is said in the passage and an inference that must be true if the statements made in the passage are true.


> <b>(A) Fusion will not be a viable means of solving the world’s energy related problems in the near future.
</b>


It is true that the passage says that temperatures around 50 million degrees Celsius are needed to produce nuclear fusion, that such temperatures are extremely difficult to reach, and that, to date, no one has proven that cold fusion can actually occur. So, a reasonable main conclusion that follows from what the passage says is that fusion will not be a viable means of solving the world’s energy related problems in the near future.


However, we are not looking for a reasonable main conclusion. We are looking for a choice that says something that must be true given what the passage says, and, for all we know, even though the facts presented indicate that, currently, there is no known way to practically harness fusion to generate energy, someone will discover and apply a way to do so in the near future, whatever the “near future” even is. Is the near future next month, next year, within the next decade? So, the facts presented do not mean that it must be true that fusion will not be a viable means of solving the world’s energy related problems in the near future.


> <b>(B) If temperatures of 50 million degrees Celsius are reached, nuclear fusion will occur.
</b>


Notice that the passage does not say that temperatures of 50 million degrees Celsius alone are sufficient to cause nuclear fusion to occur. Rather it says that, generally, temperatures around 50 million degrees Celsius are needed to produce nuclear fusion. We must be careful to not confuse the necessary with the sufficient.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) If the nuclei of two atoms joined to form a nucleus of a different element, that process occurred in a situation characterized by temperatures around 50 million degrees Celsius.
</b>


The passage says, “In nuclear fusion, the nuclei of two atoms join to form a nucleus of a different element and, in the process, release energy that could be used to produce electrical energy. Generally, temperatures around 50 million degrees Celsius are needed to produce nuclear fusion.” It’s important to notice, however, that the passage does not say that nuclear fusion occurring at 50 million degrees Celsius is the only process via which nuclei of two atoms join to form a nucleus of a different element. Therefore, we can’t conclude that, if the nuclei of two atoms joined to form a nucleus of a different element, that process occurred in a situation characterized by temperatures around 50 million degrees Celsius. After all, there may be processes, such as cold fusion, that result in the nuclei of two atoms’ joining to form a nucleus of a different element at temperatures not around 50 million degrees Celsius.


> <b>(D) Cold fusion cannot occur at temperatures above room temperature.
</b>


The passage says that scientists are studying cold fusion, which could theoretically produce energy through nuclear fusion occurring at room temperature, helping to solve the world’s energy related problems. However, we don’t have evidence that cold fusion cannot occur at temperatures above room temperature. For all we know, cold fusion could occur at temperatures somewhat higher than room temperature. We must be careful to stick as closely as possible to the information provided to us in the passage.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E) If, in an experiment, energy from a nuclear reaction was used to produce electrical energy, cold fusion did not drive that process.
</b>


The passage says, “to date, no one has demonstrated that cold fusion can actually occur.” Therefore, given what the passage says, it must be true that, if, in an experiment, energy from a nuclear reaction was used to produce electrical energy, cold fusion did not drive that process. After all, cold fusion has never been accomplished to date.


Notice, what this choice says is not an obvious main conclusion that follows from what the passage says. At the same time, it is an inference that is fully supported by what the passage says.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

<u>Do Not Seek to Predict Answers to Inference Questions</u>

Given even a few facts, the number of valid inferences that can be drawn from them is large.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Given even a few facts, the number of valid inferences that can be drawn from them is large. Therefore, it’s not a good use of time to seek to predict an answer to an inference question. Instead, our job is to intimately understand the facts presented as well as their relationship to one another.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following passage:

> <em>The deer carrying capacity of an area is the number of deer that can inhabit that area long term. If an area’s deer population exceeds the area’s carrying capacity, the deer will consume plants in that area faster than the plants can replenish themselves. Two years ago, all wolves in Johnson County, which had always kept the area’s deer population below the area’s carrying capacity, were chased off, and none have returned. As a result, the area’s deer population now exceeds the area’s carrying capacity. </em>

If we were to start coming up with inferences that we could make given the information presented in the above passage, we could quickly have multiple inferences.

> <em>There are currently more deer in Johnson County than there were two years ago.</em>

> <em>The deer in Johnson County are currently consuming plants faster than the plants can replenish themselves.</em>

> <em>Currently, there are more deer in Johnson County than can inhabit the area long term.</em>

> <em>There are fewer wolves in Johnson County than there were two years ago.</em>

> <em>Wolves in an area can keep the population of deer in that area lower than it would be without the presence of the wolves.</em>

Remember, it’s not a good use of time to seek to predict an answer to an inference question.

<u>Don</u><u>’t Confuse Assumptions and Inferences</u>

Although assumptions and inferences are similar, in that an assumption is an unwritten premise that must be true in order for a conclusion to be properly drawn from a set of provided premises and an inference is an unwritten conclusion that must be true if the facts provided are true, as you can see, assumptions and inferences are also quite different; an assumption serves as a bridge between the premises and the conclusion, whereas an inference is an unstated conclusion that must be true if the premises provided are true.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

An assumption is an unwritten premise that must be true in order for a conclusion to properly stem from a set of provided premises, while an inference is an unwritten conclusion that must be true if the facts provided are true.
{{< /alert >}}

Furthermore, we’ll never need to identify an assumption in an Inference question, and we’ll never need to identify an inference in an Assumption question.

Let’s now discuss the types of Inference question stems that you’re likely to see.
