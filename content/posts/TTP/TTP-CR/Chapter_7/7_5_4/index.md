## Lesson Content
In answering Inference questions, we must be extremely careful with answer choices that bring in new information.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In an Inference question, an answer choice that depends on support from information beyond what’s presented in the stimulus can’t be correct.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example:

> <em>While surgery to treat cataracts restores vision clarity quickly, it is somewhat expensive. At the same time, there are relatively inexpensive holistic methods that some people who experience cataracts could use to clear their vision within a reasonable timeframe, just as effectively though not as quickly as surgery does. Nevertheless, even people experiencing cataracts who could clear their vision within a reasonable timeframe via use of holistic methods generally opt for surgery instead.</em>

> <em>If the statements above are true, which of the following can be properly inferred on the basis of them?</em>

> <em>(<u>Trap Choice</u>)</em> <em>People are more concerned about the time involved in dealing with cataracts than the expense involved.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>More money is spent in the process of solving problems involving cataracts than necessarily has to be spent to solve them.</em>

Notice, the trap choice and the correct answer are both related to the discussion in the passage, and both seem to be supported by the statements.

The trap choice, however, takes a step beyond what is fully supported when it makes an assertion about people’s concerns, because the passage defines only what people choose but not what motivates them.

The correct answer, on the other hand, is completely supported by the facts presented in the passage.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Watch out for answers that depend on new information not supported by what’s said in the stimuli of Inference questions.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at an example:

##  Example 9 

Even as Yrstania’s population has increased, the number of people who use public libraries in Yrstania has been declining steadily since the 1950s.

Which of the following can be properly inferred from the statements above?

1. [ ] (A) For young people in Yrstania, accessing information through home computers and mobile phones has to a large degree replaced library use.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) The money that Yrstania spends on public libraries would be better spent on infrastructure improvements.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) In Yrstania, since the 1950’s, public library use by people under age 25 has declined more than has public library use by people over age 65.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) People over 65 years of age prefer reading printed materials over using electronic media.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) In the 1950s, the percentage of people living in Yrstania who used public libraries was greater than the percentage who do so today.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



<u>Fact 1</u>: Yrstania’s population has increased.


<u>Fact 2</u>: The number of people who use public libraries in Yrstania has been declining.


<u>Fact 3</u>: In Yrstania, the people over age 65 are those most likely to use public libraries.


<u>Fact 4</u>: In Yrstania, the people under age 25 are those least likely to use public libraries.


The key to getting the right answer to this question is to stick with only the facts provided in the stimulus. The stimulus has provided us with only numerical facts about library use. The passage does not go beyond this numerical information to provide information such as why library use has been declining, how much it has been declining for each age category, or why younger people are less likely to use the library than older people are. So, to get this question right, we have to be careful not to get tempted into picking an answer choice that requires the support of information other than the specific information that the passage provides.


> <b>(A) For young people in Yrstania, accessing information through home computers and mobile phones has to a large degree replaced library use.
</b>


This option is not supported by the passage. It introduces two new concepts: home computers and mobile phones. Did we see anything about home computers and mobile phones in the passage? Not a thing – right? This wrong answer is seductive because it might seem to be a reasonable explanation for why younger people use libraries the least. However, nothing in the passage supports this explanation. Remember, we cannot bring outside information into Inference questions.


> <b>(B) The money that Yrstania spends on public libraries would be better spent on infrastructure improvements.
</b>


This answer introduces two new concepts: money and infrastructure. The passage never discusses how governments make decisions about how to spend money or whether infrastructure would be a better use of money than libraries. Therefore, this answer also goes beyond what is supported by the passage. Notice that this answer employs a common GMAT trick: comparing two things in a way that the passage never does. How do we know which use of money is “better” or “worse?”


> <b>(C) In Yrstania, since the 1950’s, public library use by people under age 25 has declined more than has public library use by people over age 65.
</b>


 This answer introduces a new concept: how much public library use has <em>changed for each age category</em>. While what this choice says may seem to be follow from what the passage says about library use by people over age 65 and people under age 25, the truth is that, even though people under age 25 are currently those who use public libraries the least, it does not have to be the case that library use by that group has declined more than has library use by those over age 65. It could be, for instance, that those under age 25 rarely used public libraries even in the 1950’s, perhaps because most of these people had access to school libraries, and that now, for some reason, people under age 25 use public libraries more than they did back then, even though they don’t use them much now either. So, for all we know from what the passage says, use of public libraries by people over age 65 decreased, while use of public libraries by people under age 25 increased.


> <b>(D) People over 65 years of age prefer reading printed materials over using electronic media.
</b>


This choice introduces an idea not addressed in the passage, the preferences of people over 65 years of age. While what this choice says may be true, the passage gives us no basis for determining whether people over 65 years of age prefer reading printed material or using electronic media.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E) In the 1950s, the percentage of people living in Yrstania who used public libraries was greater than the percentage who do so today.
</b>


If today the population today is higher than it was in the 1950s, but a smaller number people use the library today, then the percentage of people who used the library in the 1950s must have been higher than it is today. That is, today we have a smaller number of library-using people divided by a larger total population, which is clearly a smaller percentage than the original.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
