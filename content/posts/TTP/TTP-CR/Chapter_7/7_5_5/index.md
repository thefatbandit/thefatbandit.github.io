## Lesson Content
In reading Inference questions, you may encounter incorrect answer choices that say things that are true in the real world but are not completely supported by the information presented to us in the stimulus.

Consider the following example:

> <em>The cost of solar panels that can be used by homeowners to generate electricity has been decreasing. Over the past seven years, the cost per watt of power generation capacity has decreased by 50 percent, and this trend is expected to continue.</em>

> <em>Which of the following must be true if the statements above are true?</em>

> <em>(<u>Trap Choice That is True in the Real World</u>) As a result of lower panel costs, installing a solar generating system now makes sense financially for many households.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>With a certain amount of money, a homeowner can purchase twice the solar power generating capacity that that homeowner could have purchased seven years ago with that amount of money.</em>

The trap choice could be tempting, because, not only is what the trap choice says plausible given what the passage says, but also, what it says is true in the real world.

What the correct answer says, on the other hand, is clearly true.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Inference questions may include incorrect answer choices that say things that are true in the real world but are not supported by the statements in the stimuli. Be careful not to select these answer choices.
{{< /alert >}}

Now, for some practice, here are some examples that include answer choices that are true in the real world but are not supported by the stimulus.

##  Example 10 

Recently, the Fitness Factory, a health club, for the first time began promoting its memberships to professionals who work in the high-rise buildings on the city block on which it is located.

If the statements above are true, which of the following can be properly inferred from them?

1. [ ] (A) When professionals who work in high-rise buildings join health clubs, they use considerably more complimentary towels, soaps, and lotions than professionals who do not work in high-rise buildings use.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) The Fitness Factory’s expenses have increased since it began promoting its memberships to professionals who work in the high-rise buildings on the city block on which it is located.
3. [ ] (C) Much of the new revenue generated via the Fitness Factory’s promoting its memberships to professionals who work in the high-rise buildings has come from selling personal training packages.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) Recently, the management of the Fitness Factory has made a number of bad decisions, which have led to the sharp decline in its profits.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) Once it began promoting its memberships to professionals who work in the high-rise buildings on the block on which it is located, the Fitness Factory stopped all its other marketing.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A) When professionals who work in high-rise buildings join health clubs, they use considerably more complimentary towels, soaps, and lotions than professionals who do not work in high-rise buildings use.
</b>


 What this answer choice says sounds very plausible. After all, soaps, lotions, and towels are available at most high-end fitness clubs today, and these amenities might not be cheap.


However, nothing in the passage would allow us to infer what this answer choice says. There could be thousands of reasons why the Fitness Factory saw its profits decline sharply. For example, perhaps the rent that the Fitness Factory pays doubled recently.


When solving inference questions, we must resist the urge to choose as correct a choice that matches what goes on in the real world yet is not an inference based only on what the passage says.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B) The Fitness Factory’s expenses have increased since it began promoting its memberships to professionals who work in the high-rise buildings on the city block on which it is located.
</b>


The passage tells us that the Fitness Factory has seen its sales revenue increase significantly, but its profits have declined sharply.


The following is common knowledge:


Profit = Revenue – Expenses


We can see from that equation that, if revenue went up but profit went down, the Fitness Factory’s expenses must have increased.


What this choice says must be true given what the passage says.


> <b>(C) Much of the new revenue generated via the Fitness Factory’s promoting its memberships to professionals who work in the high-rise buildings has come from selling personal training packages.
</b>


 What this answer choice says sounds very plausible. After all, it could very well be that, in reality, fitness clubs generate a lot of revenue by selling personal training to their clients.


However, nothing in the passage would allow us to infer what this answer choice says. We are not provided with any information about how the new revenue has been generated.


When solving inference questions, we must resist the urge to choose as correct a choice that fits what goes on in real world yet is not an inference based only on what the passage says.


> <b>(D) Recently, the management of the Fitness Factory has made a number of bad decisions, which have led to the sharp decline in its profits.
</b>


 What this answer choice says sounds very plausible. After all, the bad decisions of a company’s management often lead to sharp declines in the company’s profitability; we see such phenomena on a regular basis in the business world.


However, the passage does not fully support what this answer choice says. We are not provided with any information that clearly indicates that the company’s management has made bad decisions.


There could be thousands of reasons why the Fitness Factory saw its profits decline sharply. For example, perhaps the insurance that the Fitness Factory pays doubled in price recently.


When solving inference questions, we must resist the urge to choose as correct an answer choice that fits what goes on in the real world yet is not an inference based only on the passage.


> <b>(E) Once it began promoting its memberships to professionals who work in the high-rise buildings on the block on which it is located, the Fitness Factory stopped all its other marketing.
</b>


 Nothing at all in the passage would lead us to what this answer choice says. All we know is that the Fitness Factory, a health club, for the first time began promoting its memberships to professionals who work in the high-rise buildings on the city block on which it is located.


We don’t know about any of the company’s other marketing efforts.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

##  Example 11 

A “Use by” date on a food item is meant to indicate the last day on which the food should be consumed.

If the statements above are true, which of the following can be properly inferred from them?

1. [ ] (A) In many cases, food marked with a “Use by” date is still good for consumption for a period of time after that date.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) On food items marked with both “Sell by” and “Use by” dates, the “Sell by” dates always precede the “Use by” dates.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) People generally pay more attention to “Use by” dates on food than to “Sell by” dates on food.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) Sometimes, marking food with “Sell by” dates results in food going to waste.
5. [ ] (E) When people are shopping, they often use “Sell by” dates as indicators of the freshness of food items that they are considering purchasing.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



<u>Fact 1</u>: A “Use by” date indicates the last day on which a food item should be consumed.


<u>Fact 2</u>: A “Sell by” date indicates the last day on which a food item should be sold.


<u>Fact 3</u>: Not understanding the distinction, people often discard food that they would otherwise have consumed, because the “Sell by” date has been passed.


The key to getting the right answer to this question is to stick with only the facts provided in the stimulus and avoid choosing trap choices that are true in the real world but not supported by the stimulus.


The stimulus provides details on meanings of “Use by” and “Sell by” dates, contrasts the two types of dates, and discusses one consequence of marking food with “Sell by” dates. The correct answer will be supported by these specific facts.


> <b>(A) In many cases, food marked with a “Use by” date is still good for consumption for a period of time after that date.
</b>


This choice could be tempting because it is true in the real world. However, the passage says nothing that clearly indicates that sometimes food items are good for consumption after their “Use by” dates.


> <b>(B) On food items marked with both “Sell by” and “Use by” dates, the “Sell by” dates always precede the “Use by” dates.
</b>


This choice could be tempting because the passage indicates that, in most cases, foods are expected to be good for consumption after their “Sell by” dates. In other words, in most cases, foods can be <em>used </em>after their “Sell by” dates, and, therefore, in most cases, “Sell by” dates would precede “Use by” dates. However, “most cases” is not all cases, and so, it’s conceivable that the last day on which a food item should be sold would be the last day on which it should be consumed, in which case it could be marked with “Sell by” and “Use by” dates that are the same date.


> <b>(C) People generally pay more attention to “Use by” dates on food than to “Sell by” dates on food.
</b>


While what this choice says may be true in the real world, and, while what the passage says about “Use by” dates and “Sell by” dates could be taken as indicating that “Use by” dates are more important than “Sell by” dates, the passage does not provide any information that clearly indicates that people pay more attention to “Use by” dates than they do to “Sell by” dates.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) Sometimes, marking food with “Sell by” dates results in food going to waste.
</b>


The passage indicates that food is expected to be good for a period of time after its “Sell by” date. It also indicates that people who do not understand the difference between “Sell by” dates and “Use by” dates often discard food that is good for consumption when they see that the food’s “Sell by” date has passed. Of course, food that is discarded even though it is good for consumption goes to waste. Thus, from what the passage says, we can infer that, sometimes, marking food with “Sell by” dates results in food going to waste.


> <b>(E) When people are shopping, they often use “Sell by” dates as indicators of the freshness of food items that they are considering purchasing.
</b>


While what this choice says is true in the real world, the passage does not tell us anything about how people use the dates other than that they often discard food upon seeing the food’s “Sell by” date. So, this choice is not supported by what the passage says.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

Now that we’ve discussed how to recognize trap answers in Inference Questions, let’s discuss types of correct answers that appear in these questions.
