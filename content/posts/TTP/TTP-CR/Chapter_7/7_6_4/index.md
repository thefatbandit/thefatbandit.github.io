## Lesson Content
One variation that can make an Inference question a little trickier is a question’s having a correct answer that is based on some but not all the mathematical information present in a passage.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When looking for the correct answer to an Inference question involving numerical information, be sure to be open to choosing any choice, not just the ones that seem to be based on all the information presented by the passage.
{{< /alert >}}

We saw this type of answer in the Verdantville example earlier.

> <em>No new houses have been built in Verdantville in the past eight years, and the values of houses in Verdantville have not increased. Over the same time period, the number of children living in Verdantville has increased substantially. The costs of operating the schools in Verdantville are paid for entirely by property taxes, which are based on house values.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>On average, there are more children per house in Verdantville than there were eight years ago.</em>

In that example, the inference about the number of children per house is based on just two facts out of several facts presented in the passage.

Since some of the information presented by the passage will not be necessary for supporting the correct answer, in order to find the correct answer to this type of question, we have to remember that any choice that is proven true by the information in the passage will be correct and not focus only on choices that incorporate all of the information present in the passage.

Here’s another example:

> <em>In Meklerton, the number of people who drive cars to work has decreased by 10 percent over the past two years, while, over the same time period, the number who ride bicycles to work has increased by 30 percent. In rush hour traffic in Meklerton, a person on a bicycle can travel 150 percent faster than can a person in a car.</em>

> <em>Which of the following can be properly concluded from the information provided in the passage?</em>

> <em>(<u>Trap Choice</u>)</em> <em>In Meklerton, the average amount of time spent by a person travelling to work each day has decreased over the past two years.</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>In Meklerton, the ratio of people who travel to work by bicycle to people who travel to work by car has increased.</em>

A test-taker looking for an answer that is based on all the information in the passage might be tempted to choose the trap choice, as the trap choice may seem to follow from the information presented in the passage.

The correct answer is based on only a portion of the information in the passage, the decrease in people using cars and the increase in people cycling.

So, the correct answer to an Inference question that includes numerical information can be a simple deduction that can be proven using just a small portion of the information presented by the passage.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The correct answer to an Inference question that includes numerical information can be a simple deduction that can be proven using just a small portion of the information presented by the passage. It’s not always the case that we must use all the information presented in the stimulus to arrive at the correct answer.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 20 

Over the past ten years, the debt of the government of Vesornia has increased by 39 percent.

Which of the following can be properly concluded from the information provided by the statements above?

1. [ ] (A) If the leading economist’s belief is correct, Vesornia will go bankrupt in a little over ten years.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) In order to avoid bankruptcy, Vesornia must achieve a higher rate of growth of gross domestic product.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) If the rate of gross domestic product growth in Vesornia increases by 25 percent, Vesornia will not go bankrupt.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) During the past ten years, the government debt of Vesornia has increased at a rate faster than the rate at which it increased during the ten years previous to the past ten.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) Government debt per capita in Vesornia is greater than it was ten years ago.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



Analysis of the Information Given:


<u>Effect of the First Statement</u>: Defines the change in size of government debt in Vesornia over a time period. Time Period: Past ten years. Direction: Increase. Numerical Value: 39 percent.


<u>Effect of Second Statement</u>: Defines change in the size of population of Vesornia. No change.


<u>Effect of Third Statement</u>: Defines change in size of gross domestic product of Vesornia. Direction: Increase. Numerical Value: 32 percent.


<u>Effect of Fourth Statement</u>: Describes the belief of leading economist – If debt increases an additional 40 percent, Vesornia will immediately go bankrupt.


This passage is one with which we have to be careful because all the numbers provided might tempt us to draw unwarranted conclusions. The truth is that the passage does not provide us with specific information on how the various numbers relate to the economist’s prediction. For instance, we are given values for percentage growth of gross domestic product and government debt, but we can’t say for sure to what degree growth in gross domestic product or growth in government debt would have to change in order for bankruptcy to be avoided.


So, to find the correct answer to this question, we have to be careful not to fall for trap answers that seem to be supported by the numerical information provided but are not really.


> <b>(A) If the leading economist’s belief is correct, Vesornia will go bankrupt in a little over ten years.
</b>


This answer could be tempting, because a test-taker might jump from the idea that the debt increased by 39 percent over the past ten years to the conclusion that the debt will increase by another 40 percent in a little over ten years. However, we really don’t know that this will occur. For all we know, the debt will not grow further, or other trends will change. So, this answer is not supported.


> <b>(B) In order to avoid bankruptcy, Vesornia must achieve a higher rate of growth of gross domestic product.
</b>


Given the information on debt and growth in gross domestic product presented in the passage, we might be tempted to believe that what this choice says is supported. It isn’t really, however. There could be ways for Vesornia to avoid bankruptcy without an increase in growth in gross domestic product.


> <b>(C) If the rate of gross domestic product growth in Vesornia increases by 25 percent, Vesornia will not go bankrupt.
</b>


Since, if the country’s gross domestic product had grown 25 percent more than it did over the past ten years, it would have grown 40 percent rather than 32 percent, and, since 40 percent is greater than the growth in government debt, which was 39 percent, one might be tempted to decide that a 25 percent increase in gross domestic product growth would save Vesornia from bankruptcy. However, we don’t really have sufficient information for making that prediction. For all we know, Vesornia would go bankrupt even if gross domestic product were to grow 100 percent per year. So, this choice is not supported.


> <b>(D) During the past ten years, the government debt of Vesornia has increased at a rate faster than the rate at which it increased during the ten years previous to the past ten.
</b>


While Vesornia’s government debt grew a lot in the past ten years, the passage does not indicate that it did not grow just as fast during the ten years that preceded the past ten years.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E) Government debt per capita in Vesornia is greater than it was ten years ago.
</b>


 With only two pieces of information out of the several pieces in the passage we can support this choice. We know that government debt increased by 39 percent. We know that size of the population was steady. Since the percentage increase in government debt was positive, while there was no increase in the number of people in Vesornia, it has to be the case that debt per capita, i.e. debt per person, increased.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Example 21 

Last month, the average interest rate charged for a commercial loan made in Mennerton was lower than the average for any month in twenty years.

Which of the following can be properly inferred from the statements above?

1. [ ] (A) In Mennerton, had only commercial equipment loans been made last month, last month’s average commercial loan rate would not have been the lowest monthly average seen in twenty years.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) 4.5 percent is the lowest commercial loan rate seen in Mennerton in twenty years.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) The month before last month, the average rate charged for a commercial loan in Mennerton was over 5 percent.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) If no commercial loans had been made last month in Mennerton, the average commercial loan rate for the past nine months would have been higher than it was.
5. [ ] (E) For each of the twelve months preceding last month, the average rate on commercial equipment loans was greater than 9 percent.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



Analysis of the Information Given:


<u>Effect of the First Statement</u>: Defines that monthly average interest rates on commercial loans in Mennerton were at a 20 year low last month.


<u>Effect of Second Statement</u>: Defines last month’s averages for rates on two types of commercial loans, 4.5 percent for real estate loans and 9 percent for equipment loans.


The passage provides limited information: a comparison between the average rate last month and averages rate for all months for the preceding 20 years, and the exact values of last month’s average levels of interest rates on two categories of loans. It does not provide any specific information on levels of interest rates in other time periods.<br/><br/>
One fact that is clear, though, is that we can compare last month’s average commercial loan rate with the average for any other month during the past 20 years and be sure that last month’s average is lower than the other month’s average.


> <b>(A) In Mennerton, had only commercial equipment loans been made last month, last month’s average commercial loan rate would not have been the lowest monthly average seen in twenty years.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, because, last month’s average commercial equipment loan rate is so much higher than the average commercial real estate loan rate that one could get the impression that, if only equipment loans had been made last month, there’s no way that last month’s average rate would have been lower than the 20 year average. However, the truth is that the passage does not provide any information from which one can properly infer that the average commercial loan rate in Mennerton was not higher than 9 percent every month for over 20 years. So, it does not have to be true that 9 percent would not be the lowest rate in over 20 years, and so, what this choice says does not <em>have to be true</em> given what the passage says.


> <b>(B) 4.5 percent is the lowest commercial loan rate seen in Mennerton in twenty years.
</b>


This answer could be tempting, because, since last month’s commercial rates were the lowest, on average, in twenty years, and since, last month, the average rate for commercial real estate loans was much lower than the average rate for commercial equipment loans, one might be tempted to decide that the average rate for commercial real estate loans was the lowest commercial loan rate seen in twenty years. The truth is, however, that another month’s average for all commercial loans could have been higher than this month’s average even though the other month’s average for commercial real estate loans was lower. So, what this choice says does not have to be true.


> <b>(C) The month before last month, the average rate charged for a commercial loan in Mennerton was over 5 percent.
</b>


Even though last month’s mix of loans included commercial equipment loans whose rates averaged 9 percent, it is still possible that last month’s average rate, which was based also on commercial real estate loans whose rates averaged 4.5 percent, was under 5 percent. So, it is not necessarily the case that the average rate for the month before last month was over 5 percent.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) If no commercial loans had been made last month in Mennerton, the average commercial loan rate for the past nine months would have been higher than it was.
</b>


 If last month’s average was lower than that of any other month of the last twenty years, then last month’s average was lower than that of any other month of the last nine months. So, last month’s average brought down the average for the past nine months. Therefore, if no commercial loans at all had been made last month, then the lowest monthly average, last month’s, would not have brought down the nine month average, which therefore would have been higher than it was. Notice, this choice is completely supported by what is said in only the first statement in the passage.


> <b>(E) For each of the twelve months preceding last month, the average rate on commercial equipment loans was greater than 9 percent.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, as the fact that the average commercial equipment loan rate was 9 percent for the month that had the lowest average commercial loan rate in over 20 years could be perceived as indicating that 9 percent is the lowest monthly average commercial equipment loan rate seen in 20 years, and thus, in 12 months as well. However, since a monthly average for all commercial loans could be higher than this month’s even with a lower average for commercial equipment loans, one of the 12 months preceding this month could have had an average commercial equipment loan rate lower than this month’s.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Example 22 

In Derask, the total number of gasoline burning automobiles on the roads doubled from 1977 to 2017.

Which of the following is a conclusion that can be properly drawn from the information in the passage?

1. [x] (A) The average number of miles travelled by an automobile burning a liter of fuel in Derask was in 2017 twice what it was in 1977.
2. [ ] (B) In Derask, the average annual cost of operating an automobile in 1977 was nearly twice the average annual cost of operating an automobile in 2017.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) In Derask, in 2017, more automobiles traveled more miles than were traveled per automobile in 1977 than traveled fewer miles than were traveled per automobile in 1977.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) No automobile used in Derask in 2017 burned more gasoline per mile traveled than did any automobile used in Derask in 1977.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) The total number of miles traveled by automobiles in Derask in 2017 was 10 percent greater than the total number of miles traveled by automobiles in Derask in 1977.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



Analysis of the Information Given:


<u>Effect of the First Statement</u>: Defines the change in number of automobiles over the time period – the number doubled.


<u>Effect of Second Statement</u>: Defines change in average fuel burned per mile – decrease by 50 percent.


<u>Effect of Third Statement</u>: Defines difference in quantity of gasoline used in one year and used in another – 10 percent more used in 2017 than in 1977.


We are presented with a few aggregate data points. Notice, we don’t know the actual number of automobiles or exactly what underlies the differences between the averages. To get the right answer, we have to be careful not to imagine that we have more information than we actually have.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(A) The average number of miles travelled by an automobile burning a liter of fuel in Derask was in 2017 twice what it was in 1977.
</b>


 What this choice says is very basic, and it happens to be completely supported by the second statement in the passage. If the average amount of fuel each automobile burned in going a mile decreased by 50 percent, then the average distance an automobile would go in burning a certain quantity, such as a liter, of fuel must have doubled.


> <b>(B) In Derask, the average annual cost of operating an automobile in 1977 was nearly twice the average annual cost of operating an automobile in 2017.
</b>


While we know that the average amount of fuel burned by an automobile going a certain distance was double in 1977 what it was in 2017, since we don’t know the cost of fuel in the two years, we don’t have a basis for saying that the cost to operate a vehicle in 1977 was nearly twice the cost to operate one in 2017. Also, there are costs involved in operating an automobile other than fuel costs, and since we are given no information on trends in these other costs, what this choice says is not supported.


> <b>(C) In Derask, in 2017, more automobiles traveled more miles than were traveled per automobile in 1977 than traveled fewer miles than were traveled per automobile in 1977.
</b>


This choice could be tempting, because it might seem to make sense that, if the number of miles traveled per car in 2017 was 10 percent greater than the number traveled per car in 1977, then it must be the case that, in 2017, more automobiles traveled more miles than were traveled per automobile in 1977 than traveled fewer miles than were traveled per automobile in 1977. There are many ways to increase an average though. It could be that a small number of automobiles traveled many miles in 2017, bringing the 2017 average up even though, in 2017, most cars traveled no more miles than were traveled per car in 1977.


> <b>(D) No automobile used in Derask in 2017 burned more gasoline per mile traveled than did any automobile used in Derask in 1977.
</b>


The fact that the average amount of fuel burned per mile traveled by an automobile was greater in 1977 than in 2017 does not indicate that in 2017 no automobile burned more fuel per mile traveled than any automobile did in 1977. For one thing, in 2017, someone could still be using an automobile from 1977. The passage gives us aggregate information on average quantities of fuel burned, but not information on how much fuel any individual automobile burned.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E) The total number of miles traveled by automobiles in Derask in 2017 was 10 percent greater than the total number of miles traveled by automobiles in Derask in 1977.
</b>


This choice could seem to fit the fact that 10 percent more fuel was burned in 2017 than in 1977. However, in 2017, on average, automobiles burned less fuel per mile than automobiles burned per mile in 1977. So, in order to burn 10 percent more fuel in 2017, automobiles would have had to travel more than 10 percent more in total than they did in 1977. We don’t need to calculate the exact difference in travel (120 percent) to eliminate this choice. We just have to notice that, since in 2017 automobiles used less fuel per mile than they did in 1977, a 10 percent increase in fuel use would require a greater than 10 percent increase in miles traveled.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

Let’s now discuss Inference questions that contain the word “Except.”
