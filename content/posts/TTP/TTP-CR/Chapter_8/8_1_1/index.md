## Lesson Content
Find the Conclusion questions will be based on passages that always contain premises (evidence), and, from time to time, include background information or an intermediate conclusion.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

An argument’s conclusion must be closely connected to its premises, assumptions, and any intermediary conclusions that may be present.
{{< /alert >}}

It’s important to keep in mind that a conclusion must follow from only the information presented in the argument and any assumptions made by the argument.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

An argument’s conclusion must follow only from the information presented in the argument and any assumptions made by the argument. The conclusion cannot be based upon outside information that was never mentioned or implied.
{{< /alert >}}

It’s also important to keep in mind that the correct answer must do the best job, among the five answers, of capturing the author’s main point.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The correct answer to a Find the Conclusion question must do the best job, among the five answers, of capturing the author’s main point, her conclusion.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example:

> <em>Peterville’s library is seeking to raise money to make up a deficit in its budget by having a book drive in which local citizens will donate their unwanted books to the library. The library staff has decided to sell off any donated book of which the library already has a copy. Since people enjoy buying books at cheap prices, the book drive will allow the library to make up its funding deficit. </em>

Here is what we have, in shorthand notation:

> <u>Premise 1</u>: Library has deficit

> <u>Premise 2</u>: Will sell duplicate book copies

> <u>Premise 3</u>: People tend to buy cheap books

> <u>Conclusion</u>: Book drive = no more deficit

But what if we were to remove the conclusion from this argument? It would look like this:

> <em>Peterville’s library is seeking to raise money to make up a deficit in its budget by having a book drive in which local citizens will donate their unwanted books to the library. The library staff has decided to sell off any donated book of which the library already has a copy. People enjoy buying books at cheap prices. </em>

Now, with the conclusion missing, rather than providing facts that lead to an opinion, this argument now lists only facts.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Don’t expect the conclusion to be provided in a Find the Conclusion question; in answering such a question, our task is to find the choice that best articulates the author’s logically justified but unstated conclusion.
{{< /alert >}}

One powerful strategy that we can use is the “therefore test.” As you know, “therefore” is a word commonly used to indicate that a conclusion is about to follow.

> <em>Peterville’s library is seeking to raise money to make up a deficit in its budget by having a book drive in which local citizens will donate their unwanted books to the library. The library staff has decided to sell off any donated book of which the library already has a copy. People enjoy buying books at cheap prices. <strong>Therefore:</strong></em>

Whatever follows the “therefore” must be the logical conclusion of the argument.

> <em>Peterville’s library is seeking to raise money to make up a deficit in its budget by having a book drive in which local citizens will donate their unwanted books to the library. The library staff has decided to sell off any donated book of which the library already has a copy. People enjoy buying books at cheap prices. <strong>Therefore, the book drive will allow the library to make up its funding deficit.</strong></em>

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In Find the Conclusion questions, consider using the “therefore test” to help you efficiently and accurately locate an argument’s conclusion.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 2 

Smithtown Roast is the best-selling brand of coffee in grocery stores.

The most reasonable conclusion based on the information above is that

1. [ ] (A) most people who buy their coffee from grocery stores buy the least expensive brand.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) most coffee drinkers buy their coffee at grocery stores, rather than from specialty markets.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) the tastes of coffee experts who judge at tasting competitions are different from the tastes of the general coffee drinking public.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) coffee tasting competitions tend to include all of the best-selling coffee brands.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) the best-selling brand of coffee is not necessarily the best tasting brand.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



This argument has two premises:


<u>Premise 1</u>: Smithtown Roast is the best-selling brand at grocery stores


<u>Premise 2</u>: Smithtown Roast ranks last in tasting competitions.


Notice that there is no conclusion given. It’s our job to find argument’s conclusion, which will logically from these two premises. One way to conceptualize this argument, and the correct answer we seek is to say:


<em>Smithtown Roast is the best-selling brand of coffee in grocery stores. However, Smithtown Roast is consistently rated poorly by coffee experts during coffee-tasting competitions. <strong>Therefore,</strong></em>


Whatever comes after “<em>therefore</em>” must be the logical conclusion to the argument.


> <b>(A) most people who buy their coffee from grocery stores buy the least expensive brand.
</b>


This answer choice provides information that could explain the mismatch between the buying patterns at grocery stores and the results of tasting competitions if we assume that Smithtown Roast is least expensive brand. However, this information on the relative price of Smithtown Roast is new, outside information that does not logically follow from the stated premises. This is not the argument’s conclusion.


> <b>(B) most coffee drinkers buy their coffee at grocery stores, rather than from specialty markets.
</b>


This answer might seem to follow from what the argument says if we were not really paying attention to what is said and we were to arrive at an erroneous association between the popularity of Smithtown Roast in grocery stores and the popularity of the stores themselves as places for buying coffee. However, the truth is that the fact that the most popular coffee in grocery stores does not taste very good does not mean that most people buy coffee in grocery stores.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) the tastes of coffee experts who judge at tasting competitions are different from the tastes of the general coffee drinking public.
</b>


This answer choice is very temping, as it seems to follow from what the passage says. The judges say the coffee doesn’t taste good but people buy it anyway. Maybe people who buy coffee don’t have the same taste in coffee that judges have. The truth is though that this conclusion is not really supported by what the argument says, as there could be many reasons other than taste for the popularity of Smithtown Roast, reasons such as price, marketing, or convenience.


> <b>(D) coffee tasting competitions tend to include all of the best-selling coffee brands.
</b>


While the passage supports the conclusion at least one best-selling brand, Smithtown Roast, has been found in coffee tasting competitions, in saying that “coffee tasting competitions tend to include <em>all</em> of the best-selling coffee brands” this answer choice goes beyond what is supported.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E) the best-selling brand of coffee is not necessarily the best tasting brand.
</b>


This answer synthesizes the information in the premises. It offers a logically justified yet unstated final point: a brand of coffee’s sales is not necessarily related to the quality of its taste. Notice that we could do the following:


<em>Smithtown Roast is the best-selling brand of coffee in grocery stores. However, Smithtown Roast is consistently rated poorly by coffee experts during coffee-tasting competitions. <strong>Therefore, t</strong></em><strong>he best-selling brand of coffee is not necessarily the best tasting brand.</strong>


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

Let’s now discuss how to recognize a Find the Conclusion question.
