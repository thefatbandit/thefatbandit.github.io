## Lesson Content
The question stem for a Find the Conclusion question will ask us to find the best conclusion to an argument among the five possible answer choices.

> <em>Which of the following conclusions is best supported by the information above?</em>

> <em>The most reasonable conclusion based on the premises above is that</em>

> <em>If the statements above are all true, they most strongly support which of the following conclusions?</em>

> <em>The information above provides the most support for which of the following hypotheses?</em>

> <em>The experimental results support which of the following hypotheses?</em>

> <em>Which of the following is most strongly supported by the statements given?</em>

> <em>Which one of the following most accurately states the main point of the argument?</em>

> <em>Which of the following most logically concludes the argument</em><em>?</em>

> <em>Which one of the following best expresses the main point of the argument?</em>

> <em>Which of the following most accurately expresses the author’s main idea in the argument?</em>

> <em>The author is arguing that</em>

Be able to quickly recognize Find the Conclusion questions.

##  Example 3 

In legislative elections in Country Z, the incumbent usually wins re-election when the economy in that legislator’s district is doing well.

The statements above are logically structured to lead to which of the following conclusions?

1. [ ] (A) In Country Z, if the economy in a district is doing poorly in an election year, the incumbent legislator in that district probably will not be reelected.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) In Country Z, the economic expertise of a legislator is the factor that has the most impact on whether that legislator will be reelected.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) In Country Z, any incumbents who have been reelected when economic conditions have been poor must have been in “safe” districts structured to make it easier for incumbents to win.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) In Country Z, when economic conditions are poor, the impact on electoral outcomes of candidates’ qualifications will tend to be greater than it will be when economic conditions are strong.
5. [ ] (E) Voter turnout in Country Z can be expected to be greater for elections held when the economy is doing poorly than for those held when the economy is doing well.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



This argument has two premises:


<u>Premise 1</u>: When the economy is doing well, the incumbent in a district usually re-elected.


<u>Premise 2</u>: When the economy is not doing well, or when people are worried about it, the incumbent does not have this advantage.


The correct answer will find some way to combine these two pieces of information to lead to a final point that logically follows from them. Remember, we can visualize this problem as:


<em>In legislative elections in Country Z, the incumbent usually wins re-election when the economy in that legislator’s district is doing well. However, when the economy is doing poorly or when a majority of citizens in a legislator’s district express concerns about the strength of the economy, simply being the incumbent no longer provides an advantage. <strong>Therefore,</strong> </em>


The correct answer must logically fit into the space after “therefore.”


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A) In Country Z, if the economy in a district is doing poorly in an election year, the incumbent legislator in that district probably will not be reelected.
</b>


This answer goes too far. The premises state that, when the economy is not doing well, an incumbent no longer has an advantage. However, lacking an advantage is not the same as losing. The passage gives us no reason to believe that when economic conditions in a district are poor, an incumbent will probably lose.


> <b>(B) In Country Z, the economic expertise of a legislator is the factor that has the most impact on whether that legislator will be reelected.
</b>


This choice goes beyond the information provided in the premises. The premises describe a relationship between reelection of legislators and the condition of the economy. The fact that that relationship exists does not mean that the economic expertise of a legislator is the factor that has the most impact on whether that legislator will be reelected. So, this statement might be true, but, since it adds “economic expertise,” which is only loosely related to what the premises discuss, and says “factor that has the most impact,” making an unsupported comparison between factors, it goes beyond what is supported by the premises.


> <b>(C) In Country Z, any incumbents who have been reelected when economic conditions have been poor must have been in “safe” districts structured to make it easier for incumbents to win.
</b>


This conclusion, essentially that a specific single factor, “safe” districts, underlies all cases of legislators being reelected in Country Z when economic conditions are poor, goes well beyond what is supported by the premises. The premises indicate that incumbency is not an advantage when economic conditions are poor, but do not indicate that “safe” districts or any other factor is the only factor that matters when incumbency is not an advantage.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D) In Country Z, when economic conditions are poor, the impact on electoral outcomes of candidates’ qualifications will tend to be greater than it will be when economic conditions are strong.
</b>


This answer synthesizes the information in the premises and offers a logically justified but unstated final point. If incumbency is a great advantage in elections when the economy of a district is doing well but provides no advantage when economic conditions are poor, then it stands to reason that, when economic conditions are poor, the impact on electoral outcomes of candidates’ qualifications will tend to be greater than it will be when economic conditions are strong, because, when economic conditions are strong, incumbency tends to override any other factor, such as candidates’ qualifications.


> <b>(E) Voter turnout in Country Z can be expected to be greater for elections held when the economy is doing poorly than for those held when the economy is doing well.
</b>


This answer might well be true, but it goes beyond the information provided in the premises. The premises never mention voter turnout or what factors might influence it, and the fact that economic factors influence election outcomes does not clearly indicate that more voters turn out when economic conditions are poor than turn out when the economy is doing well.


Notice that the full argument is as follows:


<em>In legislative elections in Country Z, the incumbent usually wins re-election when the economy in that legislator’s district is doing well. However, when the economy is doing poorly or when a majority of citizens in a legislator’s district express concerns about the strength of the economy, simply being the incumbent no longer provides an advantage. <strong>Therefore, in Country Z, when economic conditions are poor, the impact on electoral outcomes of candidates’ qualifications will tend to be greater than it will be when economic conditions are strong</strong></em><strong>.</strong>


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
