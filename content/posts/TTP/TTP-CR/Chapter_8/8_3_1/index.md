## Lesson Content
As you’ve seen, other question types may also use words like “concludes” or “conclusion” in their question stems.

For example, the question stem “<em>Which of the following best supports the conclusion above?</em>” includes the word “conclusion,” but it’s actually a Strengthen the Argument question stem.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When reading a Critical Reasoning question stem, we must take the time necessary in order to be sure we’ve correctly identified what the question is asking us to do.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s explore this idea further.

As you learned in previous chapters, Weaken the Argument, Strengthen the Argument, and Assumption questions all ask us to do something with the conclusion.

Because Find the Conclusion questions are not as common as Strengthen the Argument, Weaken the Argument, and Assumption questions, it can be easy to mistake a Find the Conclusion question for a question of one of those other three types.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The fact that the word “conclusion” appears in a question stem doesn’t necessarily mean that we’re dealing with a Find the Conclusion question. Also, the fact that the word “conclusion” fails to appear doesn’t necessarily mean that we’re not dealing with a Find the Conclusion question. We must read question stems carefully.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example.

##  Example 5 

In a recent sociological study, an actor posing as a job candidate interviewed for 100 similar entry-level positions using the same resume in each case.

Which of the following hypotheses is most strongly supported by the statements above?

1. [ ] (A) The actor used the same university transcript when applying for each job.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) On average, finding employment is almost as difficult for individuals who have been convicted of minor crimes as it is for individuals who have been convicted of major crimes.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) Some employers consider factors besides work experience when hiring.
4. [ ] (D) Governments should prohibit employers from asking job candidates about criminal records.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) The most common reason for people’s having difficulty finding employment is that they have been convicted of crimes.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



If you were to read the stem too quickly, you might see the words “strongly supported” and get the impression that you are answering a Strengthen the Argument question. However, this question is clearly of the Find the Conclusion type. Also, notice that the question stem does not use the word “conclusion.” The question is asking “which of the following hypotheses” is supported by the information. In general, the terms “hypothesis” and “conclusion” are interchangeable in Critical Reasoning questions.


<u>Statement 1</u>: An actor applied for 100 jobs.


<u>Statement 2</u>: He was less likely be called back when he admitted to having committed a minor crime.


The correct answer will find some way to combine these two pieces of information that leads to a final statement that logically follows from them.


Remember, the problem can be distilled to:


<em>In a recent sociological study, an actor posing as a job candidate interviewed for 100 similar entry-level positions using the same resume in each case. In half of the cases, the actor indicated that he had been convicted of a minor crime, and in these cases the actor was statistically less likely to be called back for a second interview than when he did not indicate that he had been convicted of a minor crime.</em> <strong><em>Therefore,</em></strong><em> </em>


The correct answer must logically fit into the space after “therefore.”


> <b>(A) The actor used the same university transcript when applying for each job.
</b>


This sentence is clearly not the conclusion of the argument. However, people might choose this answer if they somehow perceive this question to be a Strengthen the Argument question with a passage supporting a conclusion about the effects of criminal records on employment. Answer (A) could then strengthen that argument by eliminating a possible alternative reason for the statistical difference mentioned. In other words, the fact that the actor used the same transcript every time would indicate that changes in transcripts would not be the cause of his not getting called back for second interviews, making it more likely that his having indicated that he had been convicted of a minor crime was the cause. However, in answering this question, we are not seeking evidence to support a conclusion; we are seeking a conclusion.


> <b>(B) On average, finding employment is almost as difficult for individuals who have been convicted of minor crimes as it is for individuals who have been convicted of major crimes.
</b>


This choice could be tempting since what the passage says indicates that an individual’s having been convicted of even a minor crime makes finding employment more difficult than it otherwise would be for that individual.


However, the passage does not support the comparison expressed by this choice. After all, the passage provides no information at all on how difficult it is for an individual convicted of a major crime to find employment.


In other words, the fact that an individual who indicated that he was convicted of a minor crime “was statistically less likely to be called back for a second interview” does not indicate that finding employment is “almost as difficult” for individuals who have been convicted of minor crimes as it is for individuals who have been convicted of major crimes. After all, given what the passage says, it could be the case that finding employment is much less difficult for individuals who have been convicted of minor crimes than it is for individuals who have been convicted of major crimes.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) Some employers consider factors besides work experience when hiring.
</b>


This answer synthesizes the information in the statements and offers a logically justified but unstated final point. Since, in presenting the same resume in each case, the actor presumably presented the same information about work experience, it is reasonable to suggest that some factor other than work experience plays a role.


The argument reads:


In a recent sociological study, an actor posing as a job candidate interviewed for 100 similar entry-level positions using the same resume in each case. In half of the cases, the actor indicated that he had been convicted of a minor crime, and in these cases the actor was statistically less likely to be called back for a second interview than when he did not indicate that he had been convicted of a minor crime. <strong>Therefore, some employers consider factors besides work experience when hiring.</strong>


> <b>(D) Governments should prohibit employers from asking job candidates about criminal records.
</b>


This answer goes beyond what is supported by the statement to making a conclusion about the optimal role of government in regulating the interview process. This sort of conclusion could be logically drawn only if we add significantly different premises.


> <b>(E) The most common reason for people’s having difficulty finding employment is that they have been convicted of crimes.
</b>


 This answer choice takes information from the passage and extends it into an unsupported conclusion. While the information in the passage seems to indicate that a person’s having a criminal record makes finding employment more difficult for that person than it would otherwise be, extending that conclusion to the conclusion that having a criminal record is the <em>most common</em> reason for people’s having difficulty in finding employment is not supported by the passage.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)
