## Lesson Content
As you know, inferences are conclusions that must be true if the statements made are true (and the statements in passages are always considered true in Critical Reasoning questions).

One major difference between these two types of questions is the solidity of the support that the statements in the stimuli provide for the correct answers to the questions.

In an Inference question, the correct answer <em>must be true</em> given what the statements say.

For example:

> <em>Production at the local bake shop has been fairly steady over the past year. However, the mix of different cakes does vary from day to day. For example, while the total number of cakes in the shop is the same this morning as it was yesterday morning, the number of chocolate cakes in the shop is greater this morning than it was yesterday morning.</em>

> <em>If the statements above are true, which of the following can be properly inferred from them?</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) There are fewer non-chocolate cakes in the shop this morning than there were yesterday morning.</em>

In the above question, the correct answer is a typical Inference question answer, in that it is proven true by the statements in the stimulus.

In contrast, the correct answer to a conclusion question isn’t necessarily a conclusion that must be true given what the stimulus says.

For example:

> <em>The vast majority of people in Boselton who attend football games are under 35 years of age. Recently the population of Boselton has begun to decline sharply as people under 35 years of age move to other cities that they find more livable, and this outmigration is expected to continue.</em>

> <em>Which of the following is most strongly supported by the statements given?</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>Attendance at football games in Boselton can be expected to decline.</em>

The correct answer is a conclusion that follows from the statements in the stimulus, but does it have to be true? No.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

One major difference between Inference questions and Find the Conclusion questions is that the correct answers to Inference questions have to be true given what the stimuli say, while the correct answers to Find the Conclusion questions don’t necessarily have to be true given what the statements say.
{{< /alert >}}

A second major difference between Inference questions and Find the Conclusion questions is that the correct answers to Inference questions can be any conclusions that are supported by any portions of the stimuli, while the correct answers to Find the Conclusion questions are generally main conclusions that follow logically from entire stimuli.

Consider the following passage from the Inference questions Chapter:

> <em>The deer carrying capacity of an area is the number of deer that can inhabit that area long term. If an area’s deer population exceeds the area’s carrying capacity, the deer will consume plants in that area faster than the plants can replenish themselves. Two years ago, all wolves in Johnson County, which had always kept the area’s deer population below the area’s carrying capacity, were chased off, and none have returned. As a result, the area’s deer population now exceeds the area’s carrying capacity. </em>

Each of the following is a valid inference that can be properly drawn from the passage:

> <em>The deer in Johnson County are currently consuming plants faster than the plants can replenish themselves.</em>

> <em>Currently, there are more deer in Johnson County than can inhabit the area long term.</em>

> <em>There are fewer wolves in Johnson County than there were two years ago.</em>

> <em>Wolves in an area can keep the population of deer in that area lower than it would be without the presence of the wolves.</em>

Notice that, although each of the above is a perfectly valid inference, none is really the main conclusion of the argument.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In an Inference question, the correct answer can be anything that must be true, given what is said in any part of the stimulus; the correct answer doesn’t have to be the argument’s main conclusion.
{{< /alert >}}

Now, let’s look at our previous examples again, starting with the Inference example:

> <em>Production at the local bake shop has been fairly steady over the past year. However, the mix of different cakes does vary from day to day. For example, while the total number of cakes in the shop is the same this morning as it was yesterday morning, the number of chocolate cakes in the shop is greater this morning than it was yesterday morning.</em>

> <em>If the statements above are true, which of the following can be properly inferred from them?</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>There are fewer non-chocolate cakes in the shop this morning than there were yesterday morning.</em>

Notice, the correct answer is proven true by the third sentence in the stimulus and doesn’t at all rely on support from the first or second sentence.

Now, here’s the Find the Conclusion example again:

> <em>The vast majority of people in Boselton who attend football games are under 35 years of age. Recently the population of Boselton has begun to decline sharply as people under 35 years of age move to other cities that they find more livable, and this outmigration is expected to continue.</em>

> <em>Which of the following is most strongly supported by the statements given?</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>Attendance at football games in Boselton can be expected to decline.</em>

Notice, every sentence matters in this case.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A second difference between an Inference question and a Find the Conclusion question is that the correct answer to an Inference question can be any inference that is clearly supported by any part, even just a part of a sentence, of the stimulus, while the correct answer to a Find the Conclusion question will be a main conclusion that follows logically from the entire stimulus.
{{< /alert >}}

Now, sometimes it’s hard for test-takers to differentiate between an Inference question and a Find the Conclusion question because their question stems can sound very similar.

Generally, an inference question stem will ask what can be taken from the statements.

> <em>If the statements above are all true, which of the following can be properly inferred on the basis of them?</em>

That question asks, basically, what can be inferred <em>from</em> the statements.

Even this next stem, which uses the word <em>“</em><em>conclusion</em><em>”</em> rather than <em>“inference,”</em> is asking us to draw something <em>from</em> the statements, rather than asking us what is supported <em>by</em> the statements.

> <em>If the statements above are true, which of the following conclusions can be properly drawn?</em>

Also, notice, the strict tone of the wording in this question.

In contrast, generally, rather than ask what we can infer <em>from</em> the statements, a Find the Conclusion question stem will ask what is supported <em>by</em> the statements.

> <em>If the statements above are true, which of the following is most strongly supported by them?</em>

> <em>Which of the following hypotheses is best supported by the statements given?</em>

> <em>The considerations above, if true, most strongly support which of the following claims?</em>

Notice, these questions ask what is supported <em>by</em> the statements, and the wording, such as <em>“most strongly”</em> or<em> “best,”</em> implies that the correct answers will be strongly supported by the statements in the passages but don’t have to be things that must be true given the statements in the passages.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

An Inference question asks us to find a choice that can be inferred from the statements in the passage. A Find the Conclusion question asks us to find a choice that is supported by the statements in the passage.
{{< /alert >}}

Another key thing to remember is that an inference must be completely valid, so an inference cannot be dependent upon any assumptions, because an inference must be true based upon what is said in the stimulus, and by definition, an assumption is not present in the stimulus.

Let’s contrast our examples again.

In this one, the correct answer is not dependent on any assumptions:

> <em>Production at the local bake shop has been fairly steady over the past year. However, the mix of different cakes does vary from day to day. For example, while the total number of cakes in the shop is the same this morning as it was yesterday morning, the number of chocolate cakes in the shop is greater this morning than it was yesterday morning.</em>

> <em>If the statements above are true, which of the following can be properly inferred from them?</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) There are fewer non-chocolate cakes in the shop this morning than there were yesterday morning.</em>

Logic dictates that the inference stated in the correct answer has to be true, and arriving at that inference does not require making any assumptions.

In our Conclusion example, however, the support for the conclusion does rely on some assumptions.

> <em>The vast majority of people in Boselton who attend football games are under 35 years of age. Recently the population of Boselton has begun to decline sharply as people under 35 years of age move to other cities that they find more livable, and this outmigration is expected to continue.</em>

> <em>Which of the following is most strongly supported by the statements given?</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>) Attendance at football games in Boselton can be expected to decline.</em>

The argument depends on the assumptions, among others, that, in Boselton, football will not become more popular with people aged 35 and over and that people who move away from Boselton will not travel back to Boselton just to attend football games.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The correct answer to an Inference question cannot depend on any assumptions, because assumptions are not necessarily true, and the correct answer to an Inference question must be true given what is said in the stimulus.
{{< /alert >}}

Even given all of the above, in some rare cases, questions will have aspects of both Inference questions and Find the Conclusion questions.

Now that we understand how Conclusion questions work, let’s continue.
