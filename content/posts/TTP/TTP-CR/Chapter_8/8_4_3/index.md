## Lesson Content
A conclusion is a synthesis of the premises of an argument.

The creators of GMAT Critical Reasoning questions realize that, in the heat of taking the test, a test-taker can be tempted to take a shortcut and select a choice that includes subject matter similar to that of the passage without carefully comparing the implications of the statements in the passage with the conclusion stated in the choice.

Let’s take a look at how answers of this type can work.

Consider the following passage:

> <em>Many analysts have written off Magic Technology, Inc. as a serious player in its sector, as its products are way behind those of similar companies and it has been steadily losing market share. However, Wizard Industries, Inc. was in a similar position a few years ago, and, by focusing on key product categories and spending heavily on research and development, it made itself into a leader. Meanwhile, Magic Technology, Inc.’s financial position is currently stronger than Wizard Industries, Inc.’s financial position was a few years ago.</em>

> <em>If the statements above are true, which if the following conclusions do they best support?</em>

Now, here’s the type of unwarranted conclusion that could appear in an incorrect answer:

> <em>Unless Magic Technology, Inc. focuses on the product categories that Wizard Industries, Inc. focuses on, Magic Technologies will soon go out of business.</em>

While that conclusion sounds related to what the passage says, it’s really just a mashup of ideas and wording that appear in the passage and doesn’t really follow from what the passage says.

Here’s another one:

> <em>Industry analysts had incorrectly predicted that Wizard Industries, Inc. would not stage a comeback.</em>

Once again, we have a conclusion that is loosely related to the ideas presented in the passage and uses wording from the passage but doesn’t capture what is actually meant by the statements in the passage.

Of course the following statement would be a correct answer:

> <em>The analysts’ assessment of Magic Technology, Inc.’s situation could prove to be incorrect.</em>

The facts presented, that another company in a similar market position but in a weaker financial position was able to become an industry leader, support the conclusion that, contrary to the analysts’ assessment, Magic Technology may stage a comeback.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Some incorrect answer choices that appear in Find the Conclusion questions state conclusions that are loosely related to the premises in the stimuli or include wording from the stimuli but are unwarranted because they don’t actually capture the meaning of what’s presented in the stimuli.
{{< /alert >}}

To avoid choosing choices that state conclusions that sound like what the passage says but are unwarranted because they don’t actually reflect the meanings of the statements in the passage, you have to go beyond looking for a choice that contains subject matter similar to that presented in the passage, pay close attention to the points being made in the passage, and seek a choice that truly captures those points.

To improve your skill in telling the difference between trap choices that sound like what passages say and conclusions that actually follow from what passages say, as you consider the following example, consider how someone could be trapped by the answer choices that are related to the premises of the passage but don’t actually follow from what the premises say.

##  Example 8 

For decades, bicycling advocates have sponsored public awareness campaigns that encourage cyclists to wear helmets.

Which of the following statements best completes the argument above?

1. [ ] (A) Public awareness campaigns encouraging wearing of cycling helmets are more effective in increasing the safety of cyclists than are mandatory helmet laws.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) It is not clear that public awareness campaigns encouraging cyclists to wear helmets result in increased safety of cyclists.
3. [ ] (C) No cyclist should wear a helmet when riding in the streets.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) Bicycling advocates are generally in opposition to mandatory helmet laws.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) A bicycle helmet provides little protection in a serious collision with an automobile.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

##### Question Level: easy



<u>Statement 1</u>:Bicycling advocates have sponsored public awareness campaigns that encourage cyclists to wear helmets.


<u>Statement 2</u>:In cities with mandatory helmet laws, bicycle helmets give a false sense of security not only to cyclists, who ride more recklessly as a result, but also to drivers, who feel that they no longer need to watch out for cyclists.


The correct answer to this question will be not only related to the statements but also directly supported by them. Answers that include wording or ideas from the statements but are not actually supported by them will be incorrect.


> <b>(A) Public awareness campaigns encouraging wearing of cycling helmets are more effective in increasing the safety of cyclists than are mandatory helmet laws.
</b>


This answer could be tempting, because it includes wording from both the statement about public awareness campaigns and the statement about mandatory helmet laws. Also, the fact that, in the passage, the two statements are connected by the word “however” could be perceived as indicating that a conclusion comparing the two strategies is supported by the passage. However, the truth is that the passage does not tell us anything about the effects of cycling advocates’ public awareness campaigns, and thus gives us no basis for making the comparison made in this choice.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B) It is not clear that public awareness campaigns encouraging cyclists to wear helmets result in increased safety of cyclists.
</b>


If wearing helmets when they are mandatory results in a false sense of security and recklessness, then wearing them in response to public awareness campaigns may also have these safety compromising results. Therefore, one can conclude from what the passage says that it is not clear that public awareness campaigns encouraging cyclists to wear helmets result in increased safety of cyclists. Here’s the complete argument:


<em>For decades, bicycling advocates have sponsored public awareness campaigns that encourage cyclists to wear helmets. However, in cities with mandatory helmet laws, bicycle helmets have given a false sense of security not only to cyclists, who ride more recklessly as a result of wearing helmets, but also to drivers, who feel that they no longer need to watch out for cyclists. <strong>Therefore, it is not clear that public awareness campaigns encouraging cyclists to wear helmets result in increased safety of cyclists.</strong></em>


> <b>(C) No cyclist should wear a helmet when riding in the streets.
</b>


This answer is tempting, but it is not supported by the passage. The statements focus on cyclists taking unnecessary risks and drivers not looking out for cyclists. However, the conclusion that <em>no</em> cyclists should wear helmets goes beyond what is supported by the passage. Even given what the passage says, there are numerous groups for whom helmets could, reasonably, still be a good idea, groups such as professional cyclists moving at high speed.


> <b>(D) Bicycling advocates are generally in opposition to mandatory helmet laws.
</b>


 This choice takes ideas from the statements about bicycling advocates and mandatory helmet laws and spins them into a conclusion that those statements do not support. While, in the passage, a contrast is made between the presumed goal of advocates, safety, and certain results of helmet laws, false senses of security and recklessness, the statements don’t tell us anything about the advocates’ position on the laws.


> <b>(E) A bicycle helmet provides little protection in a serious collision with an automobile.
</b>


This may well be true in real life, but it is not the conclusion. Rather than integrating the statements, this answer depends on new information, the degree of protection provided by helmets, that was never mentioned in the argument.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

Let’s look at another kind of trap answer for Conclusion questions.
