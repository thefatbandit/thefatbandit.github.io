## Lesson Content
One aspect of the GMAT is that it tests a test-taker’s degree of attention to detail, and one way in which the GMAT tests attention to detail is by including in a Find the Conclusion question choices that may seem to follow from what the passage says but actually conflict with some detail of what the passage says.

Consider the following example:

> <em>The city of Danterton gets tax revenue by adding a sales tax based on a percentage of the price of items sold to the sale of all retail merchandise sold in Danterton. Even though the total retail sales revenue in Danterton last year was no greater than that of the previous year, total sales tax revenue received was greater last year than it was the previous year, and thus covered a large portion of the cost involved in undertaking a major renewal of Danterton’s shopping district. </em>

> <em>Which of the following conclusions is best supported by the information above?</em>

> <em>(A) The renewal of Danterton’s shopping district has resulted in an increase in the number of people who come to Danterton to shop.</em>

> <em>(B) Most of the costs of the renewal project had been covered during the previous year.</em>

> <em>(C) Danterton increased the percentage of sales price that it uses in calculating the amount of tax to be paid on retail sales.</em>

In order to avoid choosing a wrong answer in answering a question like this one, we have to be sure to notice the details of the passage.

> <u>Statement 1</u>: Danterton adds a sales tax based on a percentage of the price of items sold to the sale of all retail merchandise sold.

> <u>Statement 2</u>: Total retail sales revenue in Danterton last year was no greater than that of the previous year.

> <u>Statement 3</u>: Total sales tax revenue received was greater last year than it was the previous year.

> <u>Statement 4</u>: Last year’s sale tax revenue was sufficient to pay for a large portion of the renewal of the shopping district.

Notice, sales tax revenue increased, while total retail sales revenue did not.

So is this choice correct?

> <em>(A) The renewal of Danterton’s shopping district has resulted in an increase in the number of people who come to Danterton to shop.</em>

No, it actually tends to conflict with what the passage says, as the passage indicates that total retail sales revenue did not increase.

The next choice also is not fully in line with what the passage says.

> <em>(B) Most of the costs of the renewal project had been covered during the previous year.</em>

While this choice seems to imply that sales tax revenue was sufficient to cover the costs last year because there was not much cost left to cover last year, the passage says that the reason that last year’s sales tax revenue was sufficient to cover a large portion was that it was greater than the previous year’s sales tax revenue.

This final choice is supported by what the passage says.

> <em>(C) Danterton increased the percentage of sales price that it uses in calculating the amount of tax to be paid on retail sales.</em>

Given the fact that total retail sales revenue did not increase but total sales tax revenue did, one reasonable conclusion is that the town increased the sales tax rate.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Some answer choices that appear in Find the Conclusion questions state conclusions that are supported by some of the information in the passages but are incorrect because they conflict with key details found in the passages.
{{< /alert >}}

So, to avoid choosing an answer choice that conflicts with some of the information presented in the passage, you have to go beyond understanding the general idea of what the passage says to paying close attention to the details of the passage.

Notice how some of the answer choices in the next example tend to conflict with aspects of what the passage says.

##  Example 9 

When DNA evidence related to criminal proceedings is deemed inadmissible in a courtroom, it must be true that either the DNA evidence was possibly contaminated or it was tampered with by the police.

Which of the following statements best completes the argument above?

1. [ ] (A) Most defendants with inadmissible DNA evidence are guilty.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) Admissible DNA evidence indicating that a defendant is innocent can sometimes be wrong.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) Jurors should trust their feelings when they are involved in deliberations related to criminal proceedings.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) Perceptions of a defendant’s guilt are sometimes inappropriately influenced by inadmissibility of DNA evidence.
5. [ ] (E) Some jurors ignore the fact that DNA evidence is inadmissible when evaluating the guilt of a defendant.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

##### Question Level: easy



<u>Statement 1</u>: When DNA evidence of a criminal defendant is deemed inadmissible in a courtroom, it must be true that either the DNA evidence was possibly contaminated or that it was tampered with by the police.


<u>Statement 2</u>: According to the law, in no way can the inadmissibility of a criminal defendant’s DNA evidence in a courtroom be interpreted as an implication of the defendant’s guilt.


<u>Statement 3</u>: Members of the jury will occasionally develop negative feelings toward defendants when their DNA evidence is deemed inadmissible.


Remember, the correct answer will best fit after “therefore.”


<em>When DNA evidence related to criminal proceedings is deemed inadmissible in a courtroom, it must be true that either the DNA evidence was possibly contaminated or that it was tampered with by the police. According to the law, in no way can the inadmissibility of a criminal defendant’s DNA evidence in a courtroom be interpreted as an implication of the defendant’s guilt. However, members of the jury will occasionally develop negative feelings toward defendants when their DNA evidence is deemed inadmissible. <strong>Therefore,</strong></em>


> <b>(A) Most defendants with inadmissible DNA evidence are guilty.
</b>


This answer tends to conflict with the information presented in the passage. The author’s argument is actually consistent with the idea that the inadmissibility of the DNA evidence is not somehow correlated to a defendant’s guilt.


> <b>(B) Admissible DNA evidence indicating that a defendant is innocent can sometimes be wrong.
</b>


If you know a little about DNA evidence, you may be tempted to choose this answer because you know that what it says is true in reality. However, this sentence is not supported by the passage, and is certainly not the conclusion of this argument. This answer choice discusses whether admissible evidence can be “wrong.” While the passage does discuss errors that could potentially lead to evidence being deemed inadmissible, it never mentions what specific errors might occur that would make admissible evidence wrong. Remember that, any time you have to turn to information that is outside of the passage, the answer is incorrect.


> <b>(C) Jurors should trust their feelings when they are involved in deliberations related to criminal proceedings.
</b>


 If you were not reading carefully, this answer choice might sound good to you, as it sounds plausible, and jurors’ feelings are mentioned in the last sentence of the passage. However, what this choice says conflicts with information presented in the passage. The implications of the passage are that the feelings mentioned in the last sentence are feelings that the jurors should not necessarily trust, as they are negative feelings related to the inadmissibility of DNA evidence, which evidence, according to the passage, should not be interpreted as indicating that a defendant is guilty.


<strong>(D) Correct. </strong>The premises state that inadmissibility of DNA evidence does not indicate that a defendant is guilty and that jury members, nevertheless, sometimes develop negative feelings toward defendants when DNA evidence is deemed inadmissible. This answer adds the interpretation that such negative feelings are “inappropriate.” This answer logically completes the argument:


<em>When DNA evidence related to criminal proceedings is deemed inadmissible in a courtroom, it must be true that either the DNA evidence was possibly contaminated or that it was tampered with by the police. According to the law, in no way can the inadmissibility of a criminal defendant’s DNA evidence in a courtroom be interpreted as an implication of the defendant’s guilt. However, members of the jury will occasionally develop negative feelings toward defendants when their DNA evidence is deemed inadmissible. <strong>Therefore, </strong></em><strong>perceptions of a defendant’s guilt are sometimes inappropriately influenced by inadmissibility of DNA evidence.</strong>


> <b>(E) Some jurors ignore the fact that DNA evidence is inadmissible when evaluating the guilt of a defendant.
</b>


If you picked this answer, it may have been because it seemed logical to you. After all, you may have reasoned that, if jurors develop negative feeing about a defendant in response to finding out about the inadmissibility of DNA evidence, some jurors may ignore the law that the inadmissibility of a defendant’s DNA evidence cannot be interpreted as an implication of the defendant’s guilt. And, sadly, some jurors may actually ignore this law in real life. However, this answer is not supported by the passage. We’re told only that jurors may develop negative feelings. Developing negative feelings and choosing to ignore a law are two different things.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
