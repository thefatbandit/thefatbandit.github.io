## Lesson Content
Evaluate the Argument questions present a plan, a proposal, or an argument and then ask us to find an answer that highlights information that helps us to evaluate whether the plan or proposal will work or whether the conclusion of the argument is valid.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Evaluate the Argument questions present a plan, a proposal, or an argument and then ask us to find an answer that highlights information that helps us to evaluate whether the plan or proposal will work or whether the conclusion of the argument is valid.
{{< /alert >}}

So, when we solve Evaluate the Argument questions, we read someone’s plan, proposal, or argument and ask <em>“Hey – what information would help us to decide whether the plan or proposal will succeed as outlined or whether the argument as presented is sound?”</em>

Now, the good news about Evaluate the Argument questions is that most are simply Assumption questions presented in a different format.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Because of the strong similarity between Assumption questions and Evaluate the Argument questions, when we answer Evaluate the Argument questions, we must be on the lookout for missing information that must be true in order for the logic of the argument to properly work. In other words, we must be looking for assumptions made in the argument.
{{< /alert >}}

From past chapters, you’ve already learned that if an argument’s assumption is found to be true (and the argument is a good one), then the argument is sound.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

As we know, if an argument’s assumptions are found to be true (and the argument is a good one), then the argument is sound. However, if the argument’s assumptions are found to be false, then the argument falls apart. As we’ll soon see, most Evaluate the Argument questions center on this concept.
{{< /alert >}}

To get you going, let’s return to some examples that you’ve seen before, beginning with the disease x example from the Assumptions Chapter, but this time we’ll analyze the example from an Evaluate the Argument point of view.

> <em>All people in houses in which communicable diseases are present should be quarantined. Two people in the yellow house on Maple Street came down with disease x yesterday. Therefore, all people in the yellow house on Maple Street should be quarantined.</em>

> <em>Which of the following pieces of information would be most useful in evaluating the logic of the argument presented above?</em>

Let’s begin by breaking down the parts of this argument.

> <u>Premise 1</u>: All people in houses in which communicable diseases are present should be quarantined.

> <u>Premise 2</u>: Two people in the yellow house on Maple Street came down with disease x yesterday.

> <u>Conclusion</u>: People in the yellow house should be quarantined.

Notice that the author assumes that disease x is a communicable disease.

So – what would we want to know in order to effectively assess the merits of the argument presented? Of course, we’d want to know <em>whether disease x is a communicable disease</em>.

> <em>(A)</em> <em>Whether disease x is a communicable disease</em>

In other words, we’d like to know whether the author’s assumption is a good one.

In this example, we see the close similarity between Assumption questions and Evaluate the Argument questions.

What is being assumed in the following examples?

##  Example 1 

Diets Plus CEO: We recently surveyed 500 people who had used our Healthy Plus prepared meals.

In evaluating the argument, it would be most useful to establish which of the following?

1. [ ] (A) Whether, when people among the 500 people who had used the Healthy Plus meals stopped using the meals, they kept off the body fat that they had lost
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) Whether the 500 people who used the Healthy Plus prepared meals needed to lose more weight than the average person needs to lose
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) Whether, when the 500 people used the Healthy Plus prepared meals, they also began for the first time to follow rigorous exercise programs
4. [ ] (D) Whether the Healthy Plus prepared meals taste as good as other healthy prepared foods
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) Whether most of the 500 people who used the Healthy Plus prepared meals had used different diets in the past
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



<u>Background</u>: Diets Plus recently surveyed 500 people who had used its Healthy Plus prepared meals.


<u>Premise</u>: All of those people had lost some body fat, 400 of those people had lost at least 20 pounds of body fat, and 300 of those people had lost at least 25 pounds of body fat.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Clearly, our Healthy Plus prepared meals are all a person needs lose unwanted body fat.


Notice that the CEO is essentially making the cause-and-effect claim that Healthy Plus prepared meals are responsible for the results achieved by the people surveyed. As always, we must be skeptical anytime we are presented with a cause-and-effect claim.


In considering this argument, we must ask ourselves whether the Healthy Plus prepared meals are truly responsible for the results achieved by the people in the study. Might there have been another cause? What information would help us in evaluating the validity of this claim?


> <b>(A) Whether, when people among the 500 people who had used the Healthy Plus meals stopped using the meals, they kept off the body fat that they had lost
</b>


 As usual, we must pay close attention to the conclusion, which is that Healthy Plus prepared meals are all a person needs to <em>lose</em> unwanted body fat.


Notice that the CEO never claims that using the Healthy Plus prepared meals will help a person keep the body fat off after the person stops using the meals. The argument is not about keeping body fat off, and it’s certainly not about keeping body fat off after ceasing to use the Healthy Plus meals. It’s about losing body fat.


So, establishing whether, when people who had used the Healthy Plus meals stopped using the meals, they kept off the body fat that they had lost would not help us to evaluate the argument.


> <b>(B) Whether the 500 people who used the Healthy Plus prepared meals needed to lose more weight than the average person needs to lose
</b>


 We must be careful of answer choices that say things that don’t take us in a clear direction.


For example, what would it mean if the 500 people who used the Diets Plus Healthy Plus prepared meals needed to lose more weight than the average person needs to lose? Would that fact make the argument any more valid? No, right? What if they did NOT need to lose more weight than the average person needs to lose? Nothing changes, right?


After all, how much weight a person needs to lose does not affect the cause-and-effect claim made by the Diets Plus CEO. In other words, how much weight the people surveyed needed to lose does not change whether the meals helped them to lose weight.


If you chose this answer as correct, you likely did so because it sounded important and related to the argument.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) Whether, when the 500 people used the Healthy Plus prepared meals, they also began for the first time to follow rigorous exercise programs
</b>


 The CEO is essentially making the cause-and-effect claim that Healthy Plus prepared meals are responsible for the results achieved by the people surveyed. As we said, any time an author makes a cause-and-effect claim, she assumes that there could not be an alternative explanation for the observed events.


So, what if, when the 500 people used the Diets Plus Healthy Plus prepared meals, they also began for the first time to follow rigorous exercise programs? Wouldn’t that fact weaken the argument? For sure it would, because, if true, that fact brings up an alternative explanation for why the people who used Healthy Plus prepared meals lost unwanted body fat: they began exercising.


On the other hand, if the 500 people used the Diets Plus Healthy Plus prepared meals DID NOT for the first time to follow rigorous exercise programs, then the argument is stronger because we have ruled out an alternative explanation for why the people used the Diets Plus Healthy Plus prepared meals lost unwanted body fat.


So, we can see that establishing whether, when the 500 people used the Healthy Plus prepared meals, they also began for the first time to follow rigorous exercise programs can help us to evaluate validity of the conclusion.


> <b>(D) Whether the Healthy Plus prepared meals taste as good as other healthy prepared foods
</b>


 As usual, we must pay close attention to the conclusion, which is that Healthy Plus prepared meals are all a person needs lose unwanted body fat.


Notice that the CEO never claims anything about the taste of the Healthy Plus prepared meals. So, how would information on the taste of the Healthy Plus prepared meals help us in evaluating the validity of the argument?


The Diets Plus Healthy Plus prepared meals may or may not taste as good as other healthy prepared foods, and in either case, the argument could work perfectly.


> <b>(E) Whether most of the 500 people who used the Healthy Plus prepared meals had used different diets in the past
</b>


 Maybe most of the 500 people who used the Healthy Plus prepared meals had used different diets in the past. Maybe none had. Either way, their using the Healthy Plus prepared meals could have been the cause of their losing body fat.


So, establishing whether most of the 500 people surveyed had used different diets in the past would not be useful in evaluating the soundness of the argument.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

##  Example 2 

Because the Academy of Pediatrics recommends that teenagers sleep until around 8 am, claiming that their doing so is beneficial for both their academic performance and overall health, Danterton High School moved its start time from 7:30 am to 9:00 am.

In evaluating the argument, it would be most useful to establish which of the following?

1. [ ] (A) Whether most students at Danterton High School prefer the new start time or the previous start time
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) Whether parents of students at Danterton High School have difficulty getting to work on time as a result of the later start time
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) Whether most teenagers wake up later than 8 am on weekends
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) Whether most students at Danterton high school have taken advantage of the new start time by starting to go to morning programs before going to school
5. [ ] (E) Whether improving the diets of students at Danterton High School would be beneficial for both their academic performance and overall health
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



Breakdown of the Argument:


<u>Background</u>: The Academy of Pediatrics recommends that teenagers sleep until around 8 am, claiming that their doing so is beneficial for academic performance and health.


<u>Premise</u>: Danterton High School moved its start time from 7:30 am to 9 am.


<u>Premise</u>: The academic performance and health of the students did not improve.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Waking up later does not really help teenagers.


If this were an Assumption question, we would be looking for an answer that states an assumption integral to the argument. For instance, the argument depends on the assumption that moving the start time has resulted in students’ sleeping until 8 am. Since this is, however, an Evaluate the Argument question, we are instead looking for a different kind of choice, one that asks a question the answer to which is information that either shows that a key assumption is incorrect, and thus weakens the argument, or confirms something that has been assumed, and thus strengthens the argument.


> <b>(A) Whether most students at Danterton High School prefer the new start time or the previous start time
</b>


 We don’t have to assume that the students prefer one start time or another to make this argument. The conclusion is based on the lack of change in academic performance and health. Even if, for instance, students prefer the new time, changing the start time to give the students more time to sleep has not had the expected benefits.


> <b>(B) Whether parents of students at Danterton High School have difficulty getting to work on time as a result of the later start time
</b>


 No matter how we answer this question, the answer doesn’t affect the argument. Regardless of whether the parents are experiencing difficulties as a result of the school’s starting later, changing the start time has not had the expected results, and so the conclusion holds.


> <b>(C) Whether most teenagers wake up later than 8 am on weekends
</b>


 Information on what time teenagers wake up on weekends does not affect this argument. The evidence presented is the apparent lack of results of the change made to the school’s start time. A necessary assumption would link that evidence with the conclusion. Without reason to believe that there is a connection between the time the teenagers wake up on weekends and the school’s start time, there is no clear reason to assume anything about when teenagers wake up on weekends.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) Whether most students at Danterton high school have taken advantage of the new start time by starting to go to morning programs before going to school
</b>


 To choose this choice, we have to see that the answer to the question that it poses could seriously weaken the argument. The conclusion relies on the assumption that the students are sleeping later because the school’s start time has been changed. However, if the answer to this question is “Yes,” then students aren’t actually sleeping later as a result of the new start time. Rather, they are getting up early to go to morning programs. In that case, the conclusion is based on a faulty assumption and, thus, may not be valid. So, to evaluate this argument, we would want to know the answer to this question.


> <b>(E) Whether improving the diets of students at Danterton High School would be beneficial for both their academic performance and overall health
</b>


 Information regarding whether improving students’ diets would be beneficial for their academic performance and overall health does not help us to evaluate the conclusion that waking up later does not help teenagers.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

Let’s now discuss a technique that we can use to solve many evaluate the Argument questions – the “Yes/No” test.
