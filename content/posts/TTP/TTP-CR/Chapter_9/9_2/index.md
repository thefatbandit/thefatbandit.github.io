## Lesson Content
Many Evaluate the Argument questions can be solved via the use of what’s called the Yes/No test.

In many cases, the answer choices in Evaluate the Argument questions pose questions that can be answered in two ways, either “Yes” or “No.”

In such a case, the correct answer to an Evaluate the Argument Question must be a question such that, if it is answered “Yes”, the argument is affected in one way (either strengthened or weakened), and, if it is answered “No,” the argument is affected in the reverse way.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In many cases, the answer choices in Evaluate the Argument questions pose questions that can be answered in two ways, either “Yes” or “No.” In such a case, the correct answer to an Evaluate the Argument Question must be a question such that, if it is answered “Yes”, the argument is affected in one way (either strengthened or weakened), and, if it is answered “No,” the argument is affected in the reverse way.
{{< /alert >}}

For instance, if the “Yes” answer strengthens the argument, then the “No” answer will weaken the argument, and, if the “Yes” answer weakens the argument, then the “No” answer will strengthen it.

On the other hand, in the case of a wrong answer, whether the response to the question posed by the answer is “Yes” or “No,” the answer has no effect on the argument.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When using the “Yes/No Test” on Evaluate the Argument questions, in the case of a wrong answer, whether the response to the question posed by the answer is “Yes” or “No,” the answer has no effect on the argument. In other words, a “Yes” makes us say “So what?” Similarly, a “No” makes us say “So what?”
{{< /alert >}}

Considered in the context of an example, this concept will be much easier to understand.

> <em>All people in houses in which communicable diseases are present should be quarantined. Two people in the yellow house on Maple Street came down with disease x yesterday. Therefore, all people in the yellow house on Maple Street should be quarantined.</em>

> <em>Which of the following pieces of information would be most useful in evaluating the logic of the argument presented above?</em>

> (A) <em>Whether disease x is a communicable disease </em>

> (B) <em>Whether people in a neighboring house have been infected by disease x</em>

Let’s begin with answer (A).

What happens to the argument if the answer to the question is “Yes”? In that case, the argument is sound; it makes sense to quarantine the people in the yellow house on Maple Street.

For (B), what if the answer is “Yes”? Who cares? The fact that people in a neighboring house have been infected by disease x does not impact this argument.

Let’s look at another example with which you already have some experience:

> <em>Factory pollution has resulted in a reduction in the axolotl population that once thrived in Wattachen Lake. When present even in small amounts, chemical runoff from the factory causes reproductive problems for the axolotls. Experts recommend that the factory be re-located elsewhere in order to restore the ecological conditions in and surrounding Wattachen Lake. Once the factory is gone, they claim, the axolotl population will certainly return to its original pre-factory levels within two years.</em>

> <em>In evaluating the argument, it would be most useful to determine which of the following?</em>

> <em>(A) Whether axolotls ever live for longer than ten years</em>

> <em>(B) Whether the factory pollutants in the water will begin to disintegrate only several years after removal of the factory</em>

The experts’ argument is essentially the following:

> Relocate factory.

What is one assumption made by the author? One major assumption is that this chemical runoff from the factory will not persist in the water after the factory has been relocated.

What if the answer to (A) is “Yes”? Is the support for the conclusion affected? No.

Now, let’s look at answer (B).

Let’s look at another one:

> <em>A survey has found that parents who smoke are more likely than parents who don’t smoke to have children who are involved in juvenile delinquency. Therefore, it’s clear that parental smoking causes children to engage in destructive behavior.</em>

> <em>The answer to which of the following would be most useful in evaluating the argument?</em>

> (A) <em>Whether some of the children who most actively engage in destructive behavior have parents who do not smoke</em>

> (B) <em>Whether living in difficult or stressful circumstances can lead parents to smoke and children to engage in destructive behavior</em>

The author concludes that there is a cause-and-effect relationship between parental smoking and juvenile delinquency.

If the answer to (A) is “Yes,” and, thus, <em>some of the children who most actively engage in destructive behavior have parents who do not smoke, </em>is the support for the conclusion affected? No.

If the answer to (B) is “Yes,” and <em>living in difficult or stressful circumstances can lead parents to smoke and children to engage in destructive behavior, </em>then the argument is weakened, because it may be the case that living in difficult or stressful circumstances causes people to smoke and causes juvenile delinquency, but there is no cause-and-effect link between smoking and juvenile delinquency.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Consider using the Yes/No test to solve Evaluate the Argument questions.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Example 3 

Side by side tests of fruits and vegetables grown using conventional and organic methods have found no significant differences between the levels of key nutrients in the conventionally grown produce and the levels of those nutrients in the organically grown produce.

In order to evaluate the argument, it would be most useful to determine which of the following?

1. [ ] (A) Whether fruits and vegetables that are grown using organic methods taste different from those grown using conventional methods
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) Whether plants take in nutrients from the soil as a necessary aspect of their growth processes
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) Whether conventionally grown produce contains significant levels of health harming chemicals that organically grown produce does not contain
4. [ ] (D) Whether the differences between the prices of most organically grown produce and those of most conventionally grown produce are justified
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) Whether the methods used in farming organically grown produce are more labor intensive than those used in farming conventionally grown produce
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

##### Question Level: easy



Breakdown of the Argument:


<u>Premise</u>: Side by side tests have found no significant differences between levels of certain nutrients in organically grown produce and the levels of those nutrients in conventionally grown produce.


<u>Conclusion</u>: There is no reason to believe that there is any health benefit to be derived from eating organically grown produce instead of conventionally grown produce.


The argument goes from one specific fact about differences between organically grown produce and conventionally grown produce to concluding that there is no reason to believe that there is any health benefit to be derived from eating organically grown produce rather than conventionally grown produce.


Coming to that conclusion from that one fact requires assuming that the only way that eating organically grown produce could be better for health than eating conventionally grown produce is through the organically grown produce having greater levels of certain nutrients.


Information indicating that there is in fact a way in which eating organically grown produce could be better for health than eating conventionally grown produce would weaken the argument.


Information ruling out a possible way in which eating organically grown produce rather than conventionally grown produce could be beneficial for health would strengthen the argument.


> <b>(A) Whether fruits and vegetables that are grown using organic methods taste different from those grown using conventional methods
</b>


The argument is specifically about the health benefits of eating organically grown produce rather than conventionally grown produce. Information indicating that “Yes,” the two types taste different or that “No,” they don’t taste different does not affect the conclusion about health benefits.


> <b>(B) Whether plants take in nutrients from the soil as a necessary aspect of their growth processes
</b>


The conclusion is based on the levels of nutrients present in the types of produce. Regardless of whether “Yes,” plants take in nutrients from the soil as a necessary aspect of their growth processes or “No,” they don’t, the nutrients in the produce and the health benefits associated with eating the produce remain the same.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) Whether conventionally grown produce contains significant levels of health harming chemicals that organically grown produce does not contain
</b>


 Concluding that, since the levels of certain nutrients are the same in the two types of produce, eating organic produce is not better for health requires assuming that eating conventionally grown produce is not somehow harmful to health in a way that eating organically grown produce is not. So, if the answer to the question posed by this choice is “Yes, conventionally grown produce contains significant levels of health harming chemicals that organically grown produce does not contain,” then the argument is seriously weakened, as that answer indicates that there is reason to believe that, contrary to what the conclusion says, there is reason to believe that eating organically grown produce is better for health than is eating conventionally grown produce. On the other hand, if the answer is “No, conventionally grown produce does not contain significant levels of such chemicals,” then the support for the conclusion is made even stronger, as with this information we can rule out a possible way in which eating organically grown produce could be better for health than is eating conventionally grown produce.


> <b>(D) Whether the differences between the prices of most organically grown produce and those of most conventionally grown produce are justified
</b>


Information on whether differences between prices of the two types of produce are justified does not affect the conclusion, which is about the effects on health of eating the types of produce. So, neither a “Yes” answer nor a “No” answer to the question posed by this choice affects the argument.


> <b>(E) Whether the methods used in farming organically grown produce are more labor intensive than those used in farming conventionally grown produce
</b>


 Since the degree of use of labor in farming the two types of produce does not affect the health related effects of eating the produce, neither a “Yes” answer nor a “No” answer to the question posed by this choice would affect the conclusion.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)
