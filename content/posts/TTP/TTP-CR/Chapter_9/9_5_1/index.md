## Lesson Content
Many Evaluate the Argument answer choices allow us to apply the Yes/No test, because the choices pose questions that can be answered “Yes” or “No.” However, other answer choices can be structured in ways such that the Yes/No test doesn’t work if we apply the test literally.

Consider the following question:

> <em>What proportion of lower income New York City residents travel to Manhattan by subway rather than by car?</em>

That question cannot be answered “Yes” or “No.” So, we couldn’t use the Yes/No test to handle it were it to appear as an answer choice.

> - Almost all lower income New York City residents travel to Manhattan by subway rather than by car.

> - Only two percent of lower income New York City residents travel to Manhattan by subway rather than by car.

Let’s see how extreme answers like these could be useful in answering an Evaluate the Argument question:

> <em>A proposed new law would mean that anyone driving a car into the Manhattan borough of New York City would have to pay an $11 fee. The revenue raised as a result of imposing the fee would be used to improve subway service going into Manhattan. However, the mayor asserts that imposing the fee would hurt the interests of the lower income residents of New York City, for whom the new fee would represent a significant outlay.</em>

> <em>The answer to which of the following questions would be most useful for evaluating the accuracy of the mayor’s claim</em>?

> <em>(A) What percentage of New York City residents are considered “lower income”?</em>

> <em>(B) What proportion of lower income New York City residents travel to Manhattan by subway rather than by car?</em>

OK, now let’s use extreme answers to determine which answer choice highlights the most useful information.

We could answer the question posed by choice (A) in the following two extreme ways:

> - 10 percent.

> - 90 percent.

Does either extreme answer provide information that is useful for evaluating the accuracy of the mayor’s claim? No.

Now, here again are our extreme answers to the question posed by choice (B):

> - Almost all lower income New York City residents travel to Manhattan by subway rather than by car.

> - Only two percent of lower income New York City residents travel to Manhattan by subway rather than by car.

These two answers definitely provide information that we can use to evaluate the accuracy of the mayor’s claim.

So, by using extreme answers, we can determine that choice (B) is the correct answer to this question.

##  Example 7 

For years, students at Dearborn High School have scored below the national average on standardized tests of math and science.

In evaluating the argument, it would be most useful to have the answer to which of the following?

1. [ ] (A) What percentage of the students at Dearborn High School are currently enrolled in math or science courses?
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) Do teachers at the Dearborn High School who teach English and History classes offer free after school tutoring in English and History?
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) How far below the national average have the math and science test scores of students at Dearborn High School been?
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) Will the world recognized experts offering tutoring at some point also become full time teachers at Dearborn High School?
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) What percentage of the students at Dearborn High School are able to attend the free after school tutoring?

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



<u>Background</u>: For years, students at Dearborn High School have scored below the national average on standardized tests of math and science.


<u>Premise</u>: Dearborn High School recently began offering to all students enrolled in its math and science classes free after school tutoring with world recognized experts in the topics currently being taught in those classes.


<u>Conclusion</u>: Surely, this approach will help its math and science students improve their scores on standardized tests of math and science.


As we go through the answer choices that follow, we’ll see that many cannot be answered using yes/no responses. That’s ok! We will be creative in how we use each answer choice.


The author has presented a plan. Any time an author presents a plan, he assumes that the plan will work. In other words, he assumes that there are not variables that will enter into the equation that will spoil the plan.


In the TTP Course, we refer to these variables as “malicious variables. ”


Is the author’s assumption a good one?


> <b>(A) What percentage of the students at Dearborn High School are currently enrolled in math or science courses?
</b>


 As always, it is important to focus on the conclusion of the argument, which, in this case, is that the approach of offering after school tutoring will help Dearborn High School’s “math and science students improve their scores on standardized tests of math and science. ”


Notice that the conclusion is about the math and science students only. It is not about students who are not enrolled in math or science classes. So, in evaluating the argument we have to consider only the math and science students.


Now, let’s try answering the question posed by this choice in two different ways.


First let’s answer that 90 percent of Dearborn High School students are enrolled in math and science classes. Are we now any more or less convinced that offering those students after school tutoring will help them? No.


OK, what if the answer is that 10 percent of Dearborn High School students are enrolled in math and science classes. Does that information mean that the after school tutoring will or will not be helpful? No.


We can see that, regardless of what the answer to the question posed by this choice is, the answer does not indicate whether the tutoring will be helpful. So, the answer to this question would not help us to evaluate the argument.


> <b>(B) Do teachers at the Dearborn High School who teach English and History classes offer free after school tutoring in English and History?
</b>


 As always, it is important to pay attention to the conclusion of the argument, which, in this case, is that the approach of offering after school tutoring will help Dearborn High School’s “math and science students improve their scores on standardized tests of math and science. ”


Now, let’s answer this question in two different ways.


What if the answer is “Yes, teachers at the Dearborn High School who teach English and History classes offer free after school tutoring in English and History”? Does that fact mean that the physics and math tutoring will, or will not, help physics and math students to improve their standardized test scores? No.


Similarly, if the answer to this question is “No, the English and History teachers do not offer free tutoring,” that fact has no impact on whether the physics and math tutoring will help students to improve their standardized test scores.


So, the answer to the question posed by this choice does not help us to evaluate the argument.


> <b>(C) How far below the national average have the math and science test scores of students at Dearborn High School been?
</b>


 Let’s answer this question in two different ways and see whether either answer affects the support for the conclusion.


What if the answer to this question is “Far below”? Would that fact give us any more or less faith that the plan will work? Not at all, right? After all, the test scores of Dearborn High students could have been far below the national average, and the plan could still work perfectly, or not work at all.


What if the answer to this question is “Not much lower”? Would that fact give us any more or less faith that the plan will work? Not at all, right? After all, the test scores of Dearborn High students could be just a bit below the national average, and the plan could still work perfectly.


So, regardless of how we answer this question, the answer does not affect the support for the conclusion.


> <b>(D) Will the world recognized experts offering tutoring at some point also become full time teachers at Dearborn High School?
</b>


 What if, at some point, the world recognized experts who do the tutoring will become full time teachers at Dearborn High School? Would that fact make the argument any stronger or any weaker? No, because what the world recognized experts do at a future point in time has no bearing on the plan proposed by the school, which is to have these experts work as tutors. What these experts go on to do after they leave their jobs as tutors will not impact the plan.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E) What percentage of the students at Dearborn High School are able to attend the free after school tutoring?
</b>


 Let’s answer this question in two different ways.


First, what would happen to the argument if the answer to this question were “Zero percent”? The argument would fall apart, right? After all, the author’s conclusion is that “this approach will help its math and science students improve their scores on standardized tests of math and science. ” However, if none of the students at Dearborn High School are able to attend the free after school tutoring, then the plan falls apart.


Alternatively, what would happen to the argument if the answer to this question were “Almost 100 percent”? In this case, the argument would be stronger because the more students who can actually attend the after school tutoring, the more faith we have that the plan will actually work as described.


Since answers to the question posed by this choice can weaken or strengthen the argument, this choice is the correct answer.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Example 8 

In many urban areas called food deserts, there are no local stores that offer large selections of fresh, healthy to eat food.

The answer to which of the following questions would be most useful for evaluating the likelihood of success of these initiatives?

1. [ ] (A) What percentage of the population of cities that would offer the incentives is represented by people who reside in food desert type areas?
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) How many studies of the effectiveness of offering incentives to encourage the opening of large food stores with comprehensive selections in food deserts have been undertaken?
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) Do companies that operate large food stores cite lack of demand as the reason they have not previously opened stores in food desert type areas?
4. [ ] (D) Can the cities with the greatest percentages of their areas considered food deserts afford to offer incentives to encourage the opening of food stores with comprehensive selections in food deserts?
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) How often does the average person who lives in an urban area considered a food desert shop for food?
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



In order to evaluate the likelihood of success, first we must determine what constitutes success.


<u>Goal of the Plan</u>: Improve the health of residents of urban areas that are currently food deserts.


So, the correct answer choice will help us to determine whether offering incentives to encourage the opening of large food stores with comprehensive selections of food in areas that are currently considered food deserts will <em>result in improved health</em> of people living in those areas. Is the author making any assumptions? Think about this: what if these companies have not opened stores in these areas because there is not sufficient demand for the goods that they would sell? If there were a lack of demand for such goods, then even if the stores opened, residents would not shop in them. So, the author is assuming that there is a demand for the goods that these stores would sell, that people in these food desserts would actually purchase these more healthy foods.


> <b>(A) What percentage of the population of cities that would offer the incentives is represented by people who reside in food desert type areas?
</b>


 Notice that the Yes/No test cannot be applied to this choice. However, we can use extreme answers.


For example, let’s assume that a very small percentage of the population of cities that would offer the incentives is represented by people who reside in food desert type areas. Would this assumption either strengthen or weaken the claim that such programs could result in improved health of the people in those areas? No – it would not.


Now, let’s go to the other extreme. Let’s say that a very high percentage of the population of cities that would offer the incentives is represented by people who reside in food desert type areas. Would this information either strengthen or weaken the claim? No – it would not.


> <b>(B) How many studies of the effectiveness of offering incentives to encourage the opening of large food stores with comprehensive selections in food deserts have been undertaken?
</b>


 This choice is a tempting trap, as it could be perceived as basically asking whether offering such incentives has been effective in the past. To avoid choosing this choice, we have to see that the truth is that this choice is not asking whether offering such incentives has been effective. It is asking simply how many studies have been done.


We could answer:


- 0 studies have been done.


- 1000 studies have been done.


Since, either way, we have no information on what the results of any such studies are, neither answer is information that indicates whether offering the incentives will be effective.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C) Do companies that operate large food stores cite lack of demand as the reason they have not previously opened stores in food desert type areas?
</b>


 If the answer to this question is “Yes,” then, even if such stores were to open in food desert areas, there would be no demand for the healthy food that they sell. If residents of those areas would not buy healthy food even if it were available, then encouraging stores to open in those areas would not make any difference in people’s health. If, on the other hand, the answer to this question is “No,” then it is possible that encouraging such stores to open in food desert areas would result in improvement in the health of residents of those areas.


> <b>(D) Can the cities with the greatest percentages of their areas considered food deserts afford to offer incentives to encourage the opening of food stores with comprehensive selections in food deserts?
</b>


Neither a “Yes” answer nor a “No” answer to this question affects the support for the conclusion of the argument, because the conclusion is not that all cities <u>can offer</u> or <u>will offer</u> the incentives but, rather, that offering the incentives will have a particular result in cities where they <u>are</u> offered.


> <b>(E) How often does the average person who lives in an urban area considered a food desert shop for food?
</b>


 Does it really matter how often a person shops for food? Let’s try two fairly extreme answers.


- Once a day.


- Once a month.


What’s the difference? Not much. No matter how often a person shops for food, the person could benefit from having healthier choices available.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)
