## Lesson Content
In most cases, Evaluate the Argument answer choices will pose questions that can be either answered “Yes” and “No” or answered with two extreme answers.

For example:

> <em>Why did the divorce rate decrease?</em>

> <em>What other types of plants are attacked by the fungus?</em>

There is not one particular way in which to handle all such answer choices.

> - Does the reason for the divorce rate decrease affect the support for the argument?

> - Does it matter what types of plants are attacked by the fungus?

If you are stumped by such an answer choice, you can consider a few ways in which the question could be answered.

For example:

> <em>Why did the divorce rate decrease?</em>

> - Because the laws changed.

> - Because people started waiting to get married.

After coming up with these answers, you would consider whether either of these answers, would affect the support for the conclusion.

The key to not getting into trouble with answer choices that are in the form of open ended questions like the ones above is to avoid using them to make up convoluted, unsupported stories.

Let’s consider such a choice in the context of an example:

> <em>The serious air pollution problems of San Alonso are to a large degree caused by the approximately 400,000 fossil fuel powered motor vehicles that are used in the city each day. Fortunately, it is estimated that, within five years, 200,000 of the motor vehicles used in San Alonso each day will be non-air-polluting electric vehicles. Therefore, within five years, the amount of air pollution typically seen in San Alonso will be greatly reduced.</em>

> <em>The answer to which of the following questions would be most useful for evaluating the likelihood that the prediction will prove to be correct</em>?

> <em>(A) What types of motor vehicles do people use in San Alonso? </em>

> <em>(B)</em> <em>Is the number of motor vehicles used in San Alonso each day five years from now predicted to be greater than 600,000?</em>

Here’s the prediction we are evaluating:

> <u>Conclusion</u>: Within five years, the amount of air pollution typically seen in San Alonso will be greatly reduced.

Now, let’s consider choice (A).

> <em>(A) What types of motor vehicles do people use in San Alonso?</em>

Does the answer to the question posed by choice (A) matter? Let’s say that all the motor vehicles used are cars.

The key in this case is to make sure that we don’t come up with a story along the lines of that replacing fossil fuel powered cars with electric cars will reduce pollution, but replacing fossil fuel powered scooters with electric scooters would not.

Now let’s consider choice (B):

> <em>(B)</em> <em>Is the number of motor vehicles used in San Alonso each day five years from now predicted to be greater than 600,000?</em>

Choice (B) is a question that can be answered “Yes” or “No.” If the answer is “Yes,” then, in five years, there will be more than 600,000 motor vehicles used in San Alonso.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When an Evaluate the Argument answer choice is in the form of an open-ended question, carefully consider how answers to that question could affect the support for the argument, and be careful not to make up convoluted or unsupported stories that make that choice seem correct even though it isn’t really.

##  Example 9 

The Logic Truck Company has unveiled its first model, the TrucaTruk, a battery-powered electric truck meant to be used by people living in rural Atruca.

Knowing which of the following is most important for determining the likelihood that the above prediction will prove to be correct?

1. [ ] (A) Why a truck that can handle rough roads would be popular in rural Atruca
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) Whether farmers in rural Atruca currently sell agricultural goods to buyers from urban areas
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) Whether anyone currently living in rural Atruca could afford to buy a truck that sells at a price higher than the price at which the TrucaTruk is expected to sell
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) What percentage of people in rural Atruca have access to sources of electrical power with which they could charge electric vehicles
5. [ ] (E) What types of loads people in rural Atruca generally haul in trucks
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

##### Question Level: easy



Breakdown of the Argument:


<u>Premise</u>: The TrucaTruk is designed specifically to meet the transportation needs of people living in rural Atruca and can handle rough roads.


<u>Premise</u>: Unlike any other full sized truck currently available, the TrucaTruk will be priced at a level quite affordable for many residents of rural Atruca


<u>Conclusion</u>: As soon as the TrucaTruk is offered for sale, many will be bought.


The prediction that many TrucaTruks will be bought is based on the fact that the TrucaTruk is designed to perform well in rural Atruca and on the fact that it is priced affordably. Of course, there are other factors that figure into whether many of the trucks will be bought.


To correctly answer this question, we have to avoid choosing trap choices that highlight information that seems to matter but does not really and to make sure that the choice that we do choose highlights information that clearly affects the prospects for demand for TrucaTruks.


> <b>(A) Why a truck that can handle rough roads would be popular in rural Atruca
</b>


 This choice poses an open ended question that we can’t answer either with “Yes” and “No” or with two extreme answers. So, we have to carefully consider how answers to the question posed might affect the argument.


What if the answer were, “Because heavy rains often damage roads in rural Atruca”? Does that information affect the argument? Not for any clear reason.


As long as the truck is suited to the transportation needs of potential customers, demand should be strong, and so, it really doesn’t matter why a truck that can handle rough roads would be popular in rural Atruca.


> <b>(B) Whether farmers in rural Atruca currently sell agricultural goods to buyers from urban areas
</b>


 A “No” answer to this question might seem to indicate that there would not be much demand for TrucaTruks, as a test-taker could assume that, if farmers in rural Atruca do not sell agricultural goods to buyers in urban areas, they would have little use for trucks. However, this line of reasoning goes beyond what is supported by the information we have. For one thing, the trucks could be useful in ways other than for transporting agricultural good to urban areas. So, a “No” answer has no effect on the prediction.


Similarly, we have no basis for thinking that a “Yes” answer to this question would affect demand for TrucaTruks. If the answer is “Yes,” then farmers might want trucks for transporting goods to urban areas, but, on the other hand, they may already have ways of transporting goods to urban areas and have no use for trucks for this purpose.


> <b>(C) Whether anyone currently living in rural Atruca could afford to buy a truck that sells at a price higher than the price at which the TrucaTruk is expected to sell
</b>


 The passage has made clear that other trucks are not “affordable for many residents of rural Atruca.” So, regardless of whether this question is answered “Yes” or “No,” it will be the case that many people who live in rural Atruca are not able to afford other trucks and will be able to afford to buy a TrucaTruk.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D) What percentage of people in rural Atruca have access to sources of electrical power with which they could charge electric vehicles
</b>


 The TrucaTruk is described in the passage as “battery powered.” A battery powered vehicle has to be charged regularly. Therefore, if the answer to this question is “1 percent,” then most people in rural Atruca would have no way to charge the batteries in TrucaTruks, and thus the trucks would be useless to them. Thus a “1 percent” answer would weaken the argument. If, on the other hand, the answer to this question is “100 percent,” then an assumption upon which the argument relies, that there are ways in which people in rural Atruca can charge batteries, is correct, and so, that answer strengthens the support for the conclusion.


> <b>(E) What types of loads people in rural Atruca generally haul in trucks
</b>


 This is an open ended question that we cannot either answer “Yes” and “No” or answer in two extreme ways. So, let’s just consider how an answer to this question could affect the strength of the argument. While we may be able to come up with a convoluted story about why the types of loads matter, the truth is that we have no clear reason to believe that information on the types of loads that people carry in trucks in rural Atruca has implications for the demand for TrucaTruks.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
