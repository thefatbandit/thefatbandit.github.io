## Lesson Content
So far, in all of the Evaluate the Argument questions that we have considered, the answer choices have been in the form of questions.

All the same, to answer Evaluate questions of this type, we can use a method that is similar to the methods that we have been using.

Consider the following example:

> <em>In response to residents’ complaints about constant noise, the city council of Mergate is considering permanently closing Mergate Airport. However, many businesses in Mergate depend on spending by vacationers, and so, in deciding whether to close the airport, the members of the city council are concerned about the effect that doing so would have on the profitability for such businesses in Mergate.</em>

> <em>Which of the following, if true, would be most important for the city council to take into account in arriving at a decision regarding whether to close the airport?</em>

> <em>(A) Businesses in Mergate that depend on spending by vacationers have not been as profitable in recent years as they had been in the past.</em>

> <em>(B)</em> <em>When a small private airport was shut down in Boselton, some businesses in Boselton experienced increases in revenue.</em>

> <em>(C) Without the constant noise made by airplanes taking off and landing at Mergate Airport, Mergate would be a much more attractive destination for vacationers.</em>

In this example, the conclusion isn’t stated outright, but we can tell that the city council is seeking to evaluate the following conclusion:

> <u>Conclusion</u>: It makes sense to close Mergate Airport.

We could have decided the conclusion is the opposite conclusion, that it doesn’t make sense to close the airport, but since the same answer choice will affect either conclusion, we can just go with the above conclusion.

Now let’s consider the effects of the choices.

> <em>(A) Businesses in Mergate that depend on spending by vacationers have not been as profitable in recent years as they had been in the past.</em>

<strong>Incorrect.</strong> Choice (A) is just like certain trap answers that we saw in the Weaken the Argument chapter.

> <em>(B)</em> <em>When a small private airport was shut down in Boselton, some businesses in Boselton experienced increases in revenue.</em>

<strong>Incorrect.</strong><em> </em>As we discussed when we saw an example similar to this one in the Strengthen chapter, this choice is only loosely connected to the conclusion of the argument.

> <em>(C) Without the constant noise made by airplanes taking off and landing at Mergate Airport, Mergate would be a much more attractive destination for vacationers.</em>

<strong>Correct.</strong><em> </em>This choice definitely affects the support for the conclusion.

Before we found the correct answer, we didn’t know that the correct answer would be a strengthener.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

To find the answer to an Evaluate the Argument question that has answer choices that are statements, identify the conclusion of the argument, and then, find a choice that either weakens or strengthens the support for the conclusion.
{{< /alert >}}

The correct answer to the following question is the one that provides information that either weakens or strengthens the support for the conclusion.

##  Example 10 

The city of Neurtai has added dozens of miles of protected bicycle lanes over the past six years, and yet the number of cyclists involved in collisions with motor vehicles has not decreased but, rather, increased slightly over the same time period.

Which of the following, if true, is the most significant for determining whether it makes sense for Neurtai to discontinue adding protected bicycle lanes?

1. [x] (A) In recent years, as people in Neurtai have become more interested in fitness and ecology, there has been a significant increase in the popularity of cycling there.
2. [ ] (B) The number of miles of protected bicycle lanes added so far in Neurtai is not as great as the number that planners had expected to have been added by now.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) Even in areas of Neurtai that have protected bicycle lanes, cyclists are not protected when they are crossing intersections.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) As the Neurtai public works department has gained experience in adding protected bicycle lanes, the cost per mile of adding the lanes has decreased.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) Many cyclists who ride in areas of Neurtai in which protected lanes have not been added have never gotten into collisions with motor vehicles.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

##### Question Level: hard



Since the answer choices in this Evaluate question are in the form of statements, we can treat this question as a hybrid of Weaken and Strengthen and look for a choice that either weakens or strengthens the support for the conclusion.


<u>Conclusion</u>: The Neurtai city of Neurtai should save money by discontinuing the process of adding protected bicycle lanes.


The support that the passage provides for the conclusion is that, even though the city has added dozens of miles of protected bike lanes, the number of collisions between cyclists and motor vehicles has increased slightly, rather than decreased.


Now, we need information that would either undermine or strengthen the support for the conclusion.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A) In recent years, as people in Neurtai have become more interested in fitness and ecology, there has been a significant increase in the popularity of cycling there.
</b>


 At first, since this choice mentions popularity of cycling rather than something about bike lanes or collisions, it might seem to make sense to dismiss this choice. However, careful consideration of this choice reveals that its implications are that the number of cyclists in Neurtai has increased significantly. This information undermines the support for the conclusion by attacking a key assumption of the argument.


The conclusion is based on the fact that collisions between cyclists and motor vehicles increased slightly. If we assume that the number of cyclists has remained the same, then a slight increase in such collisions tends to indicate that safety has not increased. However, this choice attacks that assumption, by implying that the number of cyclists has not remained the same, but rather increased significantly. If the number of cyclists has increased significantly, but the number of collisions between cyclist and motor vehicles has increased only slightly, then it seems likely that the protected lanes are contributing to the safety of cyclists.


Since this choice thus weakens the conclusion, it is key information that can be used in determining whether to continue adding protected lanes.


> <b>(B) The number of miles of protected bicycle lanes added so far in Neurtai is not as great as the number that planners had expected to have been added by now.
</b>


 This choice is a pseudo-weakener, as it could be construed as indicating that the reason that cyclist safety has not improved is not that the lanes are not effective, but rather than not enough such lanes have been completed. However, since we are concerned with the effectiveness of the lanes that have been completed, this choice does not actually affect the support for the conclusion.


> <b>(C) Even in areas of Neurtai that have protected bicycle lanes, cyclists are not protected when they are crossing intersections.
</b>


This choice could be construed as strengthening the support for the conclusion by showing that the protected bicycle lanes don’t truly protect cyclists, or as weakening the support for the conclusion by showing that the lanes actually do protect cyclists, and that the reason for the slight increase in collisions with motor vehicles is not that the lanes don’t make cyclists safer but that, even with the lanes, cyclists remain unprotected when they cross intersections. However, those lines of thinking are not logical, and the truth is that this choice neither strengthens nor weakens the support for the conclusion. It merely indicates that, even in areas in which there are protected bicycle lanes, cyclists are not always protected. Regardless of that fact, it could be that, by protecting cyclists some of the time, the lanes make cyclists safer, and what this choice says provides no information that can be used to determine whether, by protecting cyclists when they do protect cyclists, the lanes make cyclists safer.


> <b>(D) As the Neurtai public works department has gained experience in adding protected bicycle lanes, the cost per mile of adding the lanes has decreased.
</b>


 This choice might seem to attack the rationale for discontinuing the process of adding protecting cycling lanes, because it indicates that the cost of doing so is decreasing. However, a decreasing cost is still a cost, and meanwhile, if the lanes are not preventing collisions between cyclists and motor vehicles, then, regardless of how much they cost, they are not serving their purpose. So, this choice does not really affect the support for the conclusion.


> <b>(E) Many cyclists who ride in areas of Neurtai in which protected lanes have not been added have never gotten into collisions with motor vehicles.
</b>


 This choice is another pseudo-strengthener, in that it could be construed as indicating that the lanes are not necessary for preventing collisions between cyclists and motor vehicles. The truth is however, that, even if many cyclists who are not riding in such lanes do not get into collisions with motor vehicles, the lanes could still be helping to prevent collisions between cyclists and motor vehicles. Another way to look at this scenario is to consider the following. In a city with 100,000 cyclists, if 90 percent of cyclists were to get into collisions with motor vehicles, we would still have <em>many </em>cyclists, 10,000, who didn’t. So, that fact that many cyclists don’t get into collisions with motor vehicles does not mean that there are no collisions to prevent.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)
