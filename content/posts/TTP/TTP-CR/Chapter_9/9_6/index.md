## Lesson Content
As we have discussed, the correct answer to an Evaluate the Argument question is a question or statement that highlights information that we could use to weaken or strengthen the argument.

In writing Evaluate the Argument questions, question writers come up with all kinds of ways to create trap choices that seem to highlight information that affects the arguments but don’t really do so.

> - carefully note the conclusion the validity of which is being evaluated.

> - refrain from making up convoluted stories that seem to make incorrect choices work even though the truth is that the choices don’t really highlight information that affects the arguments.

> - relentlessly use the Yes/No test or the other methods that we have discussed until you find a choice that clearly does highlight information that affects the argument.

##  Example 11 

A survey of Springfield homeowners revealed that many who were selling their homes were doing so without the help of a real estate agent because they thought agent commissions to be too high.

In evaluating the argument, it would be most useful to determine which of the following?

1. [ ] (A) Whether going to a 4 percent commission rate would have helped the real estate agents to attract more clients
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) Whether the houses that were sold with the help of an agent sold at an average price higher than that of the houses that were sold without the help of an agent
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) Whether the agents who did not reduce their commission rates experienced a reduction in the number of houses that they sold
4. [ ] (D) Whether a new survey of Springfield homeowners indicates that they feel that the new rates are more reasonable than the higher rates
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) Whether the agents who reduced their rate sold more than half of the houses sold in Springfield during the year after the agents reduced their rate
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

##### Question Level: medium



<u>Conclusion</u>: The real estate agents who reduced their rate didn’t realize any benefit from doing so.


The fact that the real estate agents sold the same number of houses after reducing their commission rate as before they did could suggest that the reduction did not benefit them. However, this conclusion relies on the assumption that the number of houses that they sold after the reduction was unaffected by the reduction in commission, an assumption that is not necessarily correct.


> <b>(A) Whether going to a 4 percent commission rate would have helped the real estate agents to attract more clients
</b>


 This answer is a trap, as the answer “Yes, they would have attracted more clients,” could be construed as indicating that reducing rates in general is beneficial, and the answer “No, they would not have attracted more clients,” could be construed as indicating that, in general, reducing rates does not result benefit agents. However, the argument claims that the agents did not benefit from the specific move, reducing their rate from 8 percent to 6 percent. Information on the results of a possible further decrease, to 4 percent, does not affect the conclusion about whether the agents benefited from the actual decrease. It could be that Springfield homeowners selling their houses are no more likely to use an agent if agents in general charge 4 percent than they are to use an agent if agents in general charge 6 percent commission.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B) Whether the houses that were sold with the help of an agent sold at an average price higher than that of the houses that were sold without the help of an agent
</b>


 The answers to this question don’t help at all with the evaluation of the argument. The correct answer should indicate something about the benefits for agents of changing rates. Comparing the price of houses sold by real estate agents with those of houses sold without the help of real estate agents might provide information useful for evaluating the benefits of working with an agent but does not help with evaluating whether reducing the commission rate was beneficial for the agents who did so.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(C) Whether the agents who did not reduce their commission rates experienced a reduction in the number of houses that they sold
</b>


 If the answer is “Yes,” and, thus, the agents who did <em>not </em>decrease their commission rate sold fewer houses than they did the year before, then the agents who did decrease their commission rate may have benefited from doing so. If the agents who did not reduce their rate sold fewer houses, and those who did reduce their rate sold the same number of houses, then the total number of houses sold must have decreased. So, those who reduced their rate sold the same number of houses they sold before they reduced their rate, even though the total number of houses sold that year was lower than the number sold the year before. The agents who reduced their rate selling the same number of houses even though the total number of houses sold decreased could be evidence that reducing their rates benefited them


Likewise, if the answer is “No,” then the agents who did not decrease their rate sold the same number of houses as they sold the year before, the agents who did reduce their rate sold the same number of houses as they sold the year before, and the total number of houses sold remained the same. In that case, the only apparent result of the commission reduction is that the agents who reduced their rate received lower commissions, while the agents who didn’t reduce their rate received just as much as they received the year before. Thus, a “No” answer tends to be a reason to doubt that the reduction in commission rate generated benefits.


> <b>(D) Whether a new survey of Springfield homeowners indicates that they feel that the new rates are more reasonable than the higher rates
</b>


 This choice could trap a test-taker who does not notice that this choice is not directed at the correct conclusion. The correct answer needs to help in the evaluation of whether there was a benefit to the real estate agents who lowered their commissions. What is relevant to evaluating the results of the real estate agents’ decision is homeowners’ behavior/decisions and the actual commissions from sales facilitated by those agents. Regardless of whether the answer to the question posed by this choice is “Yes” or “No,” the opinions of homeowners do not constitute information indicating whether the agents with lower rates actually derived any benefit from reducing their rates.


> <b>(E) Whether the agents who reduced their rate sold more than half of the houses sold in Springfield during the year after the agents reduced their rate
</b>


 The answer to this question reveals nothing, because we have nothing relevant to compare the answer to, as we don’t have the proportion of houses sold by the agents who reduced their rate before they reduced their rate. Even though half the agents reduced their rate, it could be that, even before they reduced their rate, those agents who went on to reduce their rate sold well over half the houses sold, or well under half the houses sold. Neither the passage nor this choice says what the proportion was. So, regardless of whether the answer to this question is “Yes” or “No,” we have no way of using the information provided by that answer to determine whether reducing the rate had any effect.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)
