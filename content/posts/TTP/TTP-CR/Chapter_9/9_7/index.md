## Lesson Content
There is a difference between information that is pertinent to a topic and information that affects the support for a specific conclusion about that topic.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

There is a difference between information that is pertinent to a topic and information that affects the support for a specific conclusion about that topic.
{{< /alert >}}

For instance, in a general discussion of a problem, information on how to solve the problem would be pertinent.

Consider the following example:

> <em>Doctors often determine without investigating thoroughly that people experiencing occasional trouble breathing are experiencing asthma, a health issue characterized by airway constriction, and prescribe medication that helps with keeping airways open. However, occasional trouble breathing is not always caused by airway constriction and can be caused instead by a person’s being overweight. Therefore, it must be the case that people experiencing occasional trouble breathing often take medication that does nothing to alleviate their symptoms</em>.

> <em>Determining which of the following would be most helpful in assessing the validity of the claim made?</em>

> <em>(<u>Incorrect Choice that Highlights Information Pertinent to the Discussion</u>) Whether, by simply losing weight, an overweight person can solve occasional breathing problems caused by being overweight</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>Whether using medications commonly used to keep airways open causes the person using such medications to lose weight</em>

Here’s the conclusion the validity of which we are seeking to assess:

> <u>Conclusion</u>: It must be the case that people experiencing occasional trouble breathing often end up taking medication that does nothing to alleviate their symptoms.

Notice how the incorrect choice that highlights pertinent information could trap a test-taker.

The correct answer, on the other hand, clearly highlights information that would affect the support for the conclusion.

Here’s another example in which the incorrect answer highlights information that is certainly pertinent to what’s discussed in the passage and yet does not affect the argument:

> <em>The serious air pollution problems of San Alonso are to a large degree caused by the approximately 400,000 fossil fuel powered motor vehicles that are used in the city each day. Fortunately, it is estimated that, within five years, 200,000 of the motor vehicles used in San Alonso each day will be non-air-polluting electric vehicles. Therefore, within five years, the amount of air pollution typically seen in San Alonso will be greatly reduced.</em>

> <em>The answer to which of the following questions would be most useful for evaluating the likelihood that the prediction will prove to be correct</em>?

> <em>(<u>Incorrect Choice that Highlights Information Pertinent to the Discussion</u>)</em> <em>Is there an ecologically sound method for disposal of spent batteries from electric vehicles? </em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>Is the number of motor vehicles used in San Alonso each day five years from now predicted to be more than 600,000?</em>

You can see how the incorrect choice could tempt a test-taker.

As discussed when we saw this example earlier, the answer to the question posed by the correct answer, on the other hand, could make or break the support for the conclusion.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In answering Evaluate the Argument questions, beware of incorrect choices that highlight information that is pertinent to the topics that arguments are about but does not affect the support for the arguments.
{{< /alert >}}

When answering an Evaluate question, to avoid choosing a choice that highlights information that is pertinent to the topic discussed in the passage but does not affect the conclusion of the argument, make sure that you identify the conclusion of the argument and then keep the conclusion in mind as you consider the choices.

##  Example 12 

Researchers in the United States found that the percentage of people diagnosed by physicians as having kidney stones is significantly higher in the southern states than in the northern states.

Which of the following would it be most useful to establish in order to evaluate the researchers' hypothesis?

1. [ ] (A) How much higher the rate of diagnosis of kidney stones in the southern states is than the rate of diagnosis of kidney stones in the northern states
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) Whether it would be possible for people who live in the southern states to prevent entirely the formation of kidney stones by drinking large amounts of water consistently
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) How the rate of diagnosis of kidney stones in the southern states compares with the rate of diagnosis of kidney stones worldwide
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) Whether people diagnosed by physicians as having kidney stones are ever misdiagnosed and do not actually have kidney stones
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) Whether, in years when temperatures in the southern states are unusually low, the southern states’ kidney stones diagnosis rate decreases

{{< /quizdown >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Here’s the hypothesis that we have to evaluate:


<u>Conclusion</u>: The higher incidence of kidney stones in the southern states is the result of people’s experiencing dehydration at a relatively high rate in those states as a result of the relatively high temperatures in those states.


In order to find the correct answer, we have to avoid getting sucked in by a trap answer that brings up information that is pertinent to the topic at hand but doesn’t affect the support for the argument.


> <b>(A) How much higher the rate of diagnosis of kidney stones in the southern states is than the rate of diagnosis of kidney stones in the northern states
</b>


 We could answer this question with two extreme answers:


- Ten times as high.


- Only slightly higher.


Does it matter how much higher the diagnosis rate is in the southern states? No. All we need in order to support the conclusion is information indicating that there is a significantly higher diagnosis rate in the southern states, and the passage provides that information. So, while the degree of difference in rates of diagnosis might be pertinent in general to the topic discussed in the passage, it is not information that affects the support for the conclusion.


> <b>(B) Whether it would be possible for people who live in the southern states to prevent entirely the formation of kidney stones by drinking large amounts of water consistently
</b>


 A “Yes” or “No” answer to the question posed by this choice could be pertinent to a general discussion of dehydration and kidney stones. However, the support for the conclusion that the cause of the difference between the kidney stones diagnosis rates of the northern states and the southern states is not affected by information on whether people could entirely prevent kidney stones by drinking large amounts of water. Regardless of whether yes, drinking large amounts of water would entirely prevent kidney stones or no, it wouldn’t prevent all kidney stones, the fact remains that dehydration could be contributing to the relatively high kidney stones diagnosis rate in the southern states.


> <b>(C) How the rate of diagnosis of kidney stones in the southern states compares with the rate of diagnosis of kidney stones worldwide
</b>


 The comparison brought up by this choice does not matter. Regardless of how the rate of diagnosis in the southern states compares with the worldwide rate of diagnosis, the rate of diagnosis in the southern states is higher than that in the northern states, and so, any answer to the question posed by this choice says has no effect on the scenario presented in the passage or on the support for the conclusion.


> <b>(D) Whether people diagnosed by physicians as having kidney stones are ever misdiagnosed and do not actually have kidney stones
</b>


 The information highlighted by this choice is certainly pertinent to a discussion of rates of kidney stones diagnosis and could be perceived as affecting the support for the conclusion, because the support is based on rates of diagnosis of kidney stones. So, the answer “Yes, sometimes people diagnosed by physicians as having kidney stones do not actually have kidney stones,” could seem to undermine the force of the evidence presented by indicating that a kidney stones diagnosis is not reliable. However, since, even if the answer to this question is “Yes,” we don’t have a clear reason to believe that all kidney stones diagnoses are incorrect, and there is still a difference between diagnosis rates in the northern and southern states, neither the scenario nor the support for the conclusion is really changed by the answer to this question.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E) Whether, in years when temperatures in the southern states are unusually low, the southern states’ kidney stones diagnosis rate decreases
</b>


 The answer, “Yes, in years when temperatures in the southern states are unusually low, the southern states’ kidney stones diagnosis rate decreases,” would serve to strengthen the cause-and-effect conclusion of the argument, by showing that, when the cause, relatively high temperatures in the southern states, is not present, the effect, a relatively high kidney stones diagnosis rate, is not present either. A “No” answer, on the other hand, would weaken the support for the argument, because it would indicate that, even when the cause is not present, we still have the effect.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
