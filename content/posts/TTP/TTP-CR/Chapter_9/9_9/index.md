## Lesson Content
In reviewing Weaken the Argument questions and Strengthen the Argument questions, we saw that some answer choices affect conclusions that are related to the conclusions of the arguments but are not the conclusions that we need to affect.

For example, we could have an argument the conclusion of which is that, by educating the public, avant garde artists could increase the market for their work.

Consider the following example:

> <em>Historically, Marvelo Chocolate Company sold its limited production, high end chocolate only in specialty stores, but, a year ago, Marvelo started offering its chocolate in some regular grocery stores in addition to continuing to offer it in specialty stores. After this change was made, sales of Marvelo Chocolate decreased rather than increased. Marketing experts theorized that this decrease occurred because Marvelo’s offering its chocolate in ordinary grocery stores had reduced the appeal of the brand to its traditional customer base.</em>

> <em>The answer to which of the following would be most useful for evaluating the validity of the marketing experts’ conclusion?</em>

> <em>(<u>Choice that Misses the Point</u>)</em> <em>To what degree do grocery stores look out for the interests of major chocolate companies over those of smaller, high end chocolate companies?</em>

> <em>(<u>Correct Answer</u>)</em> <em>Are the members of Marvelo’s traditional customer base aware of what brands of chocolate are offered in grocery stores?</em>

Here’s the conclusion the validity of which we need to evaluate:

> <u>Conclusion</u>: The decrease in Marvelo’s sales occurred because Marvelo’s offering its chocolate in ordinary grocery stores had reduced the appeal of the brand to its traditional customer base.

So, we are concerned with why the decrease occurred.

The incorrect choice that misses the point goes into a whole other discussion, one that could end up providing information that would explain why Marvelo Chocolate didn’t sell much chocolate in grocery stores.

The correct answer, on the other hand, poses a key question.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In answering an Evaluate the Argument question, beware of incorrect choices that bring up information that misses the point of the argument and affects the wrong conclusion.
{{< /alert >}}

So, if these answer choices highlight information that is not actually related to the points of arguments, why would test-takers ever pick such choices? Because test-takers aren’t always clear about the points of the passages.

It’s easy to read a passage and come away with a general sense of what the passage is about rather than a clear understanding of what the exact point of the passage is.

Notice the difference between the trap choices that miss the point and the correct answer as you answer the following question.

##  Example 14 

A recent study has found that people who have been consistently using mouthwash at least twice a day are more likely to experience diabetes than are people who have been using mouthwash once a day or less.

To evaluate whether the approach mentioned would be likely to achieve its goal, it would be most useful to know which of the following?

1. [ ] (A) Whether, by having a healthy diet, a person could avoid experiencing diabetes even if that person were to use mouthwash at least twice a day
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) Whether the symptoms that indicate that a person is likely to soon experience diabetes include persistent bad breath
3. [ ] (C) Whether the use of mouthwash results in the prevention of any health issues that are more detrimental than diabetes
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) Whether most people who consistently use mouthwash at least twice a day do not eventually experience diabetes
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) Whether there are products that can be substituted for mouthwash and the use of which is not associated with higher incidences of diabetes
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

##### Question Level: hard



The passage suggests the following:


<u>Conclusion</u>: Limiting use of mouthwash can help to prevent diabetes.


We have to find a choice that highlights information that would help with evaluating whether limiting use of mouthwash really helps with preventing diabetes.


The argument is based on the correlation between people’s using mouthwash at least twice a day and a higher than normal likelihood of developing diabetes. So, we need a choice that highlights information that either strengthens or weakens the link between that correlation and the conclusion that limiting mouthwash use can help to prevent diabetes.


In seeking the correct answer, we have to be careful not to get trapped by a choice that sounds right but misses the point of the argument.


> <b>(A) Whether, by having a healthy diet, a person could avoid experiencing diabetes even if that person were to use mouthwash at least twice a day
</b>


 This choice misses the point of the argument. The passage is not about whether there is a way to use mouthwash without developing diabetes. The passage’s focus is on the possibility that limiting use of mouthwash could help to prevent diabetes. Regardless of whether one could somehow use mouthwash without developing diabetes, one could still help to prevent diabetes by limiting use of mouthwash.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B) Whether the symptoms that indicate that a person is likely to soon experience diabetes include persistent bad breath
</b>


 A “Yes” answer to the question posed by this choice would seriously weaken the support for the argument, by indicating that the actual cause-and-effect relationship may be the reverse of the one that the author of the argument assumes exists. If bad breath is a symptom that typically precedes the onset of diabetes, then, rather than mouthwash is causing diabetes, maybe the cause and effect relationship is the onset of diabetes causes people to use mouthwash. Since a “Yes” answer would thus seriously undermine the support for the conclusion, this choice is the correct answer.


> <b>(C) Whether the use of mouthwash results in the prevention of any health issues that are more detrimental than diabetes
</b>


The conclusion of the argument is very specific, that one can prevent diabetes by limiting one’s use of mouthwash. The author does not claim that one <u>should limit</u> one’s use of mouthwash or that limiting one’s use of mouthwash is <u>beneficial overall</u>. So, since one may or may not be able to prevent diabetes by limiting one’s use of mouthwash regardless of whether the use of mouthwash prevents health issues more detrimental than diabetes, neither a “Yes” answer nor a “No” answer to this question has any affect on the support for the conclusion.


> <b>(D) Whether most people who consistently use mouthwash at least twice a day do not eventually experience diabetes
</b>


 This choice could be tempting, because the answer “Yes, most people who consistently use mouthwash at least twice a day do not eventually experience diabetes,” could seem to weaken the support for the conclusion that limiting one’s use of mouthwash can help to prevent diabetes.


Notice, however, regardless of whether most people who use mouthwash at least twice a day do so without developing diabetes, the fact remains that people who use mouthwash at least twice a day are more likely to experience diabetes than are people who don’t use mouthwash a least twice a day, and so, the support provided by that fact is not affected by the answer to the question posed by this choice.


> <b>(E) Whether there are products that can be substituted for mouthwash and the use of which is not associated with higher incidences of diabetes
</b>


 This choice misses the point of the argument. The focus of the argument is not on ways to get the effects of mouthwash without experiencing diabetes. It’s on preventing diabetes by limiting mouthwash use. Regardless of whether, “Yes,” there are such products or, “No,” there are not, if mouthwash does indeed cause diabetes, then one could help to prevent diabetes by limiting use of mouthwash.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
