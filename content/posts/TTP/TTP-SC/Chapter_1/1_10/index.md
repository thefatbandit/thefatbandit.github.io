## Lesson Content
There are many ways in which sentences that appear in Sentence Correction questions can be correctly written.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Sometimes, even often, we won’t be used to recognizing the ways in which sentences that appear in Sentence Correction questions can be correctly written because the sentence structures are different from what we’re used to.
{{< /alert >}}

Also, the correct versions of sentences in Sentence Correction questions are not always particularly well written.

So, because the correct versions sometimes use unfamiliar structures or are not particularly well written, if, in answering a Sentence Correction question, we only seek to find the correct version, we may not recognize it and, therefore, get the question incorrect.

Thus, instead of seeking the correct sentence directly, we will use process of elimination to remove the four versions that are most clearly flawed.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When solving Sentence Correction questions, instead of seeking the correct version directly, use process of elimination to remove the four versions that are the most clearly flawed. If we can confidently remove four answers, the one remaining must be correct.
{{< /alert >}}

For example, if a version has an error in subject verb agreement, it’s gone.

Also, it's important to know that we don’t need to find every flaw in a sentence for us to eliminate it.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When we are answering Sentence Correction questions, just one flaw in a sentence version is all it takes for us to have grounds to confidently eliminate that version. Eliminate wrong answers as quickly as possible by finding the easiest to find errors in each incorrect sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

Along these lines, we can learn a lot from the way that Sherlock Holmes, the fictional brilliant private detective, went about solving puzzles.

“<em>Once you have eliminated the impossible, whatever remains, however improbable, must be the truth.</em>”

This advice is helpful in answering Sentence Correction questions because, even if we can’t see why the correct answer is correct, if we can eliminate four sentences that we know to contain errors, then we can confidently choose the correct answer.

##  Sentence Correction Example 13  

According to numerous peer-reviewed studies, retractions of scientific work <u>does </u><u>little to amend public misconceptions that result</u> from the initial publication of erroneous information.

1. [ ] (A) does little to amend public misconceptions that result
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) do little to amend public misconceptions resulting
3. [ ] (C) doing little to amend public misconceptions that result
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) that do little to amend public misconceptions results
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) that do little to amend public misconceptions resulting
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



The key to correctly answering this question is to find at least one clear flaw in each of four of the five answer choices. Remember, just one flaw is all it takes for us to have grounds to confidently eliminate a choice. If we are able to find at least one solid reason why an answer choice is incorrect, and do that for four choices, then we know that the remaining choice must be the correct answer, even if that answer may “sound” a bit odd.


> <b>(A)</b> According to numerous peer-reviewed studies, retractions of scientific work <b>does little to amend public misconceptions that result
</b> from the initial publication of erroneous information.



The plural subject of this version, “retractions,” does not agree with the singular verb “does.” Notice that the subject of this version is not the singular “work.” We can see the error more easily by crossing off what appears between the subject and the verb:


retractions <s>of scientific work</s> does


As we can see, “retractions … does” is not grammatical. Rather, “retractions … do” would be correct.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B)</b> According to numerous peer-reviewed studies, retractions of scientific work <b>do little to amend public misconceptions resulting
</b> from the initial publication of erroneous information.



Notice that the version created with this choice correctly pairs the plural subject “retractions” with the plural verb “do.”


If you were reluctant to select this choice because you thought “resulting” does not “sound” as good as “that result,” keep in mind that correct SC sentences may not always sound the way you might initially think to write them. The truth is that “public misconceptions resulting from the initial publication …” is perfectly grammatical and logical.


Since the version created with this choice contains no grammatical errors and conveys a logical meaning, this choice is correct.


> <b>(C)</b> According to numerous peer-reviewed studies, retractions of scientific work <b>doing little to amend public misconceptions that result
</b> from the initial publication of erroneous information.



There is no main verb in the version created with this choice (i.e., no verb that names the action of the sentence subject). Thus, the sentence created is not complete. We can see the error more easily by crossing off what appears between the subject and the verb that it would pair with:


“retractions <s>of scientific work</s> doing little to amend public misconceptions that result” … <em>do/are what?</em>


“Doing” alone cannot serve as the main verb of a sentence. In this context, “doing” merely provides further description of the type of “retractions” being discussed, “retractions … doing little ….”


“To amend” is an infinitive verb, and thus cannot be the main verb in the sentence.   


“Result” provides further description of the “public misconceptions”; they are “public misconceptions that result from ….”


Since the version created with this choice does not contain a main verb to pair with the subject “retractions,” and thus is not a complete sentence, this choice is incorrect.


If you were unable to identify that the sentence created with this choice is incomplete, rest assured, we will cover in detail how to identify the main verb of a sentence in the next chapter.


> <b>(D)</b> According to numerous peer-reviewed studies, retractions of scientific work <b>that do little to amend public misconceptions results
</b> from the initial publication of erroneous information.



The plural subject of this version, “retractions,” does not agree with the singular verb “results.” We can see the error more easily by crossing off what appears between the subject and the verb:


retractions <s>of scientific work that do little to amend public misconceptions</s> results


As we can see, “retractions … results” is not grammatical. Rather, “retractions … result” would be correct.


> <b>(E)</b> According to numerous peer-reviewed studies, retractions of scientific work <b>that do little to amend public misconceptions resulting
</b> from the initial publication of erroneous information.



The sentence created with this choice is incomplete. Notice that the subject “retractions” is not followed by a main verb.


To better see the error, we can cross off the phrase beginning with “of”:


“retractions <s>of scientific work that do little to amend public misconceptions resulting from the initial publication of erroneous information</s>.” … <em>do/are what?</em>


Having crossed off the phrase that follows “retractions,” we can see that there is nothing in the sentence for “retractions” to do or be. Notice that the part of the sentence containing verbs, “that <strong>does</strong> little to amend public misconceptions <strong>resulting </strong>from …” further describes “scientific work.”


Without a main verb to pair with the sentence subject, the sentence does not convey a complete thought, and this choice is incorrect.


If you were unable to identify that the sentence created with this choice is incomplete, rest assured, we will cover in detail how to identify the main verb of a sentence in the next chapter.


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>Every sentence must have at least one subject and at least one main verb.


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>Check to be sure that singular nouns are paired with singular verbs and that plural nouns are paired with plural verbs.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
