## Lesson Content
Often, we’ll have correctly eliminated three of the five answer choices and be left with two.

We’re seeking to determine what makes them different because both sentences can’t be correct, so there must be at least one key point of difference between them that will render one more effective (correct) than the other.

Does one use the singular “is” while the other uses the plural verb “are?” Does one use the pronoun “their” while the other uses the singular “it?” Does one use a simple past tense verb, such as “walked,” while the other uses a verb in the past perfect tense, such as “had walked.” Seeking out the differences between the sentences will allow us to more precisely see what the issue in the incorrect version is.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In answering Sentence Correction questions, if we’re debating between two answers, seek to pinpoint the essential ways in which the sentences differ. There must be at least one key point of difference between them that will render one more effective (correct) than the other.
{{< /alert >}}

Of course, as you now know, the sentences could have false splits that serve to distract us from the real issues present in the sentences, so we can’t assume that all differences between the sentences matter, but we need to at least begin by considering how the two sentences are different.

Now, what happens if we can’t find something wrong with one of the two remaining sentences? If we’ve carefully and confidently evaluated the sentences and cannot find an error, we need to consider that it may be the case that, although both sentences are grammatically correct, one says something illogical.

If we can’t find an error of logic (and there are no errors of grammar), we must consider that we’ve improperly evaluated the other three sentences and return to them to reevaluate their correctness.

##  Sentence Correction Example 14  

<u>An outdoor recess period is now rare in schools, so much so that physical activity has been integrated into class assignments by some teachers as a substitute for them.</u>

1. [ ] (A) An outdoor recess period is now rare in schools, so much so that physical activity has been integrated into class assignments by some teachers as a substitute for them.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) An outdoor recess period, now rare in schools, so much that some teachers integrated physical activity into class assignments to substitute for it.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) Because they are now so rare in schools, class assignments have physical activity integrated into them as some teachers’ substitute for outdoor recess periods.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) An outdoor recess period is now so rare in schools that some teachers have integrated physical activity into class assignments as a substitute for it.
5. [ ] (E) Because they are now so rare in schools, some teachers have integrated physical activity into class assignments to substitute for an outdoor recess period.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Some of the choices in this question are relatively easy to eliminate. For instance, choice (B) is clearly incorrect because it’s not a complete sentence, and choice (E) has a clear meaning error, in that it seems to communicate that “some teachers” are “now so rare in many states.”


However, some other choices are more difficult to eliminate. So, once we have eliminated all but two or three choices, we have to carefully compare the differences between the remaining choices to determine which is correct.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>An outdoor recess period is now rare in schools, so much so that physical activity has been integrated into class assignments by some teachers as a substitute for them.
</b>


To what does the pronoun “them” at the end of the sentence refer? There is no logical antecedent of “them” in the sentence, and thus this choice can’t be correct.


Given what the sentence says, it must be that what teachers have been using physical activity integrated into class assignments as a substitute for is “an outdoor recess period.” However, the plural “them” cannot refer to the singular “an outdoor recess period.” We need the singular “it” to refer to “an outdoor recess period.”


In fact, none of the plural nouns in the sentence (schools, class assignments, teachers) makes sense as the antecedent of “them” in this context.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>An outdoor recess period, now rare in schools, so much that some teachers integrated physical activity into class assignments to substitute for it.
</b>


This choice corrects the pronoun error we saw in (A), using the singular “it” to logically refer to “an outdoor recess period.” However, we must be careful when evaluating SC answer choices not to simply look for a correction of an error we saw in another choice. We must evaluate the entire sentence on its own merits. 


If we do so, we see that the sentence created with this choice is incomplete. Notice that there is no main verb in the sentence to indicate what “an outdoor recess period” is/does at a particular point in time. In other words, whereas choice (A) included the verb “is” after “an outdoor recess period,” this choice replaces “is” with a comma, so there is no main verb to go with the sentence subject.


Additionally, the use of the phrase “so much that” does not make sense in this context. Ask yourself, “so much” what? There is nothing in the sentence that there could logically be “so much” of. It does not make sense to say that an outdoor recess period is rare in schools “so much.” The idiom “so much <em>so</em>” would make more sense for discussing the fact that recess is “rare.”


> <b>(C)</b> <b>Because they are now so rare in schools, class assignments have physical activity integrated into them as some teachers’ substitute for outdoor recess periods.
</b>


In this choice, the plural pronoun “they” seems to refer to the plural noun “class assignments,” conveying an illogical meaning:


Because [class assignments] are now so rare in schools, class assignments have physical activity integrated into them as some teachers’ substitute for outdoor recess periods.


Notice that it doesn’t make sense that class assignments would have physical activity integrated into them as a substitute for outdoor recess periods <em>because</em> class assignments “are now so rare.” After all, if class assignments are so rare, physical activity integrated into them wouldn’t serve as much of a substitute for recess. Right?


A logical meaning would be that “outdoor recess periods” are what are “now so rare.” However, because “class assignments” is the first plural noun to follow the introductory phrase, and because “outdoor recess periods” is far away from “they” at the end of the sentence, the way the sentence is constructed indicates that “they” refers (illogically) to “class assignments.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D)</b> <b>An outdoor recess period is now so rare in schools that some teachers have integrated physical activity into class assignments as a substitute for it.
</b>


This choice is the only one that conveys a clear, logical, and complete thought, with no ambiguous or illogical pronouns or other grammatical errors. 


Notice that the main verb “is” properly pairs with the singular subject “an outdoor recess period,” so the sentence is grammatically correct.


Additionally, the singular pronoun “it” clearly and logically refers to the singular “an outdoor recess period.”


> <b>(E)</b> <b>Because they are now so rare in schools, some teachers have integrated physical activity into class assignments to substitute for an outdoor recess period.
</b>


Notice that, since “teachers” is the first plural noun following the introductory phrase “Because they are now so rare in schools,” the pronoun “they” seems to refer to “some teachers,” conveying an illogical meaning.


It doesn’t make sense that “some teachers” are “rare in schools in many states.”


Also, it doesn’t make sense that, <em>because</em> some teachers are rare, those teachers would integrate physical activity into class assignments to substitute for an outdoor recess period. Why would one thing be the reason for the other? Those two facts have no logical connection to each other.


A logical meaning would be that an outdoor recess period is what is “now so rare. However, the plural pronoun “they” cannot correctly refer to the singular “an outdoor recess period.” 


<strong>Key Concept:</strong> Every sentence must have at least one subject and at least one finite verb.


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>Be sure that a pronoun clearly refers to only one antecedent in the sentence, not multiple possible antecedents.  


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>Be sure that the noun to which a pronoun refers makes sense as the person or thing performing the action associated with that pronoun.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Sentence Correction Example 15  

The harvesting of apples and peaches <u>were disrupted this year by stormy weather, and as a result, much of it</u> ended up rotting on the ground.

1. [ ] (A) were disrupted this year by stormy weather, and as a result, much of it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) was disrupted this year by stormy weather, and resultingly, many of them
3. [ ] (C) was disrupted this year by stormy weather, with the result that much of it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) were disrupted this year by stormy weather, with the result that much of that
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) was disrupted this year by stormy weather and resulted in much, which
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



Two of the answer choices in this question, the two that use the plural “were,” are relatively easy to eliminate, because we can see that “were” does not agree with the singular subject “harvesting.”


However, once we have eliminated those choices, choosing among the rest becomes a bit more challenging, because (B) uses the strange sounding “resultingly” but may be correct, (C) sounds OK, and (E) is grammatically correct but may not convey a logical meaning.


So, to arrive at the correct answer, we have to carefully analyze the differences between the choices to determine which is actually the best one.


> <b>(A)</b> The harvesting of apples and peaches <b>were disrupted this year by stormy weather, and as a result, much of it
</b> ended up rotting on the ground.



 The plural verb “were disrupted” does not agree with the subject, which is not the plural “apples and peaches” but the singular “harvesting.” Crossing off what appears between “harvesting” and “were disrupted” makes seeing the issue easier.


The harvesting <s>of apples and peaches</s> were disrupted


Also, notice that the pronoun “it” has nothing that it would logically refer to. “It” would not refer to “harvesting,” because “harvesting” would not have “ended up rotting on the ground,” and “it” would not refer to “apples” or “peaches,” because “it” is singular, while “apples” and “peaches” are plural.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B)</b> The harvesting of apples and peaches <b>was disrupted this year by stormy weather, and resultingly, many of them
</b> ended up rotting on the ground.



 This version “sounds” a little strange, because the adverb “resultingly” is not one that we see or hear used often.


Nevertheless, since “resultingly” means “consequently,” it makes sense to say “and resultingly, many of them ended up rotting.”


If we weren’t sure whether we should choose this choice over (C) or (E), we could compare the choices to see how they differ and decide which is the best.


We could compare this choice with (C) and see that (C) uses “it,” while this choice uses “them,” and see that “it” has nothing to refer to, whereas the plural “them” logically refers to the plural “apples and peaches.”


We could compare this choice with (E) and see that, whereas what “much” in (E) means is not clear, this version conveys a meaning that makes sense.


So, since this version is grammatically correct and effectively conveys a meaning that makes sense, it is the correct answer.


> <b>(C)</b> The harvesting of apples and peaches <b>was disrupted this year by stormy weather, with the result that much of it
</b> ended up rotting on the ground.



 This version correctly uses the verb “was” with the singular subject “harvesting.” So, it does not have the subject-verb agreement error that (A) and (D) have.


However, the pronoun “it” has nothing that it would logically refer to. “It” would not refer to “harvesting,” because “harvesting” would not have “ended up rotting on the ground,” and “it” would not refer to “apples” or “peaches,” because “it” is singular, while “apples” and “peaches” are plural.


> <b>(D)</b> The harvesting of apples and peaches <b>were disrupted this year by stormy weather, with the result that much of that
</b> ended up rotting on the ground.



 The plural verb “were disrupted” does not agree with the subject, which is not the plural “apples and peaches” but the singular “harvesting.” Crossing off what appears between “harvesting” and “were disrupted” makes seeing the issue easier.


The harvesting <s>of apples and peaches</s> were disrupted


Also, notice that the pronoun “that” has nothing that it would logically refer to. “That” would not refer to “harvesting,” because “harvesting” would not have “ended up rotting on the ground,” and “that” would not refer to “apples” or “peaches,” because “that” is singular, while “apples” and “peaches” are plural.


> <b>(E)</b> The harvesting of apples and peaches <b>was disrupted this year by stormy weather and resulted in much, which
</b> ended up rotting on the ground.



 This choice is not quite as easy to eliminate as (A), (C), or (D), because, unlike those three choices, this choice is grammatically correct.


Nevertheless, we can use meaning to eliminate this choice.


Notice what this choice says: “The harvesting … was disrupted … and resulted in much, which ended up rotting on the ground.


The harvesting resulted in “much,” and “much” ended up rotting on the ground? “Much” what?


Since the sentence does not indicate what “much” is, and since it’s unclear what harvesting could possibly have resulted in that would have ended up rotting on the ground, this version does not effectively convey a meaning that makes sense.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
