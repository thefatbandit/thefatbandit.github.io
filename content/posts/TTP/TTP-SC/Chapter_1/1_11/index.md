## Lesson Content
Many people believe the false notion that the original, or choice (A), version of the sentence always conveys the meaning intended by the author.

Let’s be clear about this: there is no such thing as “the intended meaning” in Sentence Correction questions.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

There is no such thing as “the intended meaning” in Sentence Correction questions; there is nothing special about the meaning expressed by the version created via the use of choice (A). We won’t know the exact meaning intended by the author until we find the correct answer, which might be (A) or might be any of the other four answers.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s clarify this idea a bit.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Any answer choice can be correct as long as its sentence is free from grammar errors and makes logical sense.
{{< /alert >}}

In other words, if the original sentence is incorrect, we don’t carry with us what we think the meaning of the correct sentence should be given what the original sentence says.

Consider the following simple mini-example:

> <em><u>Bob, the man who lives two houses down from us, walks his dog wearing a Speedo.</u></em>

> <em>(A) Bob, the man who lives two houses down from us, walks his dog wearing a Speedo.</em>

> <em>(B) While wearing a speedo, Bob, the man who lives two houses down from us, walks his dog.</em>

Notice that the original sentence is wrong because it illogically conveys that the dog is wearing a Speedo.

OK, so we know that answer choice (A) is wrong.

However, these people would be making a mistake.

What was the author’s intended meaning? Well, because answer choice (B) is correct, we see that her meaning was to convey that the man wears the Speedo.

The point to see here is that it is perfectly OK for an answer choice to change the meaning from the meaning conveyed by the original sentence.

Don’t fall into the trap of thinking that the original sentence has a monopoly on the intended meaning of the sentence.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In Sentence Correction questions, it is perfectly OK for the correct answer to change the meaning from the meaning conveyed by the original sentence. Our job is to choose the most effective sentence, even if the meaning of that most effective sentence deviates from the meaning of the original sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 16  

<u>Before leaves fall from trees, they recycle the nutrients contained</u> in autumn.

1. [ ] (A) Before leaves fall from trees, they recycle the nutrients contained
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) A leaf falling after recycling by the tree of the nutrients contained
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) A leaf falls after the nutrients they contain is recycled by the tree
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) Leaves falling after recycling of the nutrients contained in them by trees
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) Trees recycle the nutrients contained in leaves before the leaves fall

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



The original version of the sentence in this question conveys a nonsensical meaning. “They recycle <em>the nutrients contained in autumn</em>” suggests that nutrients could be contained in autumn, a season of the year.


Since the meaning conveyed by the original version is nonsensical, there is no way the correct version of the sentence will convey the meaning the original version conveys, because the correct version has to convey a meaning that makes sense.


So, in order to arrive at the correct answer, rather than look for a version that conveys the meaning conveyed by the original version, we have to look for a version that effectively conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Before leaves fall from trees, they recycle the nutrients contained
</b> in autumn.



 Notice that the pronoun “they” in this version could refer to “leaves,” to “trees,” or to both “leaves” and “trees.” Since what “they” is meant to refer to is not clear, the meaning conveyed by this version is ambiguous. Do leaves, trees, or both leaves and trees recycle nutrients? This version does not clearly indicate which of them recycle nutrients.


Also, by saying, “nutrients contained in autumn” this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that nutrients are contained in autumn, a season of the year.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>A leaf falling after recycling by the tree of the nutrients contained
</b> in autumn.



 This version describes a leaf without ever completing the thought. Notice that it says, “a leaf” and then continues by saying more about the leaf, “falling after recycling …,” but leaves the reader wondering “A leaf falling after recycling does what?”


So, this version has no verb to tell us what the leaf does and, thus, is not a complete sentence.


Also, by saying, “nutrients contained in autumn,” this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that nutrients are contained in autumn, a season of the year.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>A leaf falls after the nutrients they contain is recycled by the tree
</b> in autumn.



The pronoun “they” in this version has nothing to refer to. It seems to refer to “leaves,” but “leaves” does not appear in the sentence. Only the singular “a leaf” appears. Since, “they” is plural, “they” cannot refer to the singular “a leaf.” A plural pronoun has to have a plural noun to refer to.


Also, the plural subject “nutrients” does not agree with the singular verb “is recycled.” So, “<em>nutrients</em> they contain <em>is recycled</em>” is incorrect.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>Leaves falling after recycling of the nutrients contained in them by trees
</b> in autumn.



 This version describes leaves without ever completing the thought. Notice that it says, “leaves” and then continues by saying more about leaves, “falling after recycling …,” but leaves the reader wondering “Leaves falling after recycling do what?”


So, this version has no verb to tell us what the leaves do and, thus, is not a complete sentence.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E)</b> <b>Trees recycle the nutrients contained in leaves before the leaves fall
</b> in autumn.



 This version is grammatically correct and effectively conveys a meaning that makes sense. So, even though this version does not convey the meaning conveyed by the original version – the original version conveys that nutrients are contained in autumn, whereas this version conveys that leaves fall in autumn – this choice is the correct answer.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

Let’s now discuss why we should not eliminate an answer just because it sounds awkward.
