## Lesson Content
Many people approach sentence correction questions by using their “ears.” In other words, they choose answers based upon what “sounds” right to them or what feels right to them.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Approaching sentence correction questions by using their “ears” may help some people get some easy- and medium-level questions correct some of the time, but it certainly will not help most people get most of the questions – especially the harder questions – correct most of the time.
{{< /alert >}}

There are a number of reasons why using our “ears” is ineffective.

First, many of us, even those of us who are good writers, commit a litany of errors of logic, grammar, and style when we write.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Many of us, even those of us who are good writers, commit a litany of errors of logic, grammar, and style when we write. Therefore, if we use “what we do often” as a proxy in seeking correct answers to Sentence Correction questions, we’re bound to get questions incorrect with high frequency.
{{< /alert >}}

Second, often the correct answer to a Sentence Correction question sounds very wrong to us because we are not used to the sophisticated, “ivory tower” writing used in many Sentence Correction questions.

Third, sometimes a sentence has no grammar errors whatsoever, yet the sentence is incorrect because it says something illogical.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Our “ears” cannot help us find errors in logic in Sentence Correction questions.
{{< /alert >}}

So, we must resist the urge to use our “ears” when solving Sentence Correction questions.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

We must resist the urge to use our “ear” when solving Sentence Correction questions. We must instead actively engage our critical thinking skills to correctly answer Sentence Correction questions.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following examples.

##  Sentence Correction Example 17  

The ability of dragonflies <u>to consume the equivalent of their own body weight each day from pesky insects make them</u> a welcome sight in a garden.

1. [ ] (A) to consume the equivalent of their own body weight each day from pesky insects make them
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) to each day consume the equivalent of their own body weight in pesky insects makes them
3. [ ] (C) about consuming the equivalent of their own body weight in pesky insects each day makes it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) to consume the equivalent of its own body weight in pesky insects each day and makes it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) each day consumes the equivalent of its own body weight in pesky insects, making it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



In evaluating the answer choices, we must remember not to rely on our “ear” to determine which choices are incorrect and which choice is correct. Rather, we must identify clear, specific errors in grammar or illogical meanings in incorrect answer choices. If a choice contains no grammar or meaning errors, even if it does not “sound” how we might expect it to, then that choice will be correct.


> <b>(A)</b> The ability of dragonflies <b>to consume the equivalent of their own body weight each day from pesky insects make them
</b> a welcome sight in a garden.



If we read this choice casually, it might “sound” OK, but actually, it has multiple errors that we can notice by reading it carefully.


Notice that the subject of the sentence is the singular noun “the ability,” not the plural “dragonflies.” Thus, the plural verb “make” is incorrect, since it does not agree with the singular “ability.” We can see the error more easily by crossing off what appears between the subject and the verb:


The ability <s>of dragonflies to consume the equivalent of their own body weight each day from pesky insects</s> make


“The ability … make” is not grammatical. “The ability … makes” would be correct.


Notice also that it does not quite make sense to say that dragonflies “consume the equivalent of their own body weight each day <em>from</em> pesky insects,” as if the insects have the dragonflies’ body weight to give to the dragonflies. 


Rather, it makes sense to say that dragonflies “consume the equivalent of their own body weight” <em>in</em> insects, meaning that the quantity of insects a dragonfly consumes is equivalent to the amount the dragonfly weighs.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B)</b> The ability of dragonflies <b>to each day consume the equivalent of their own body weight in pesky insects makes them
</b> a welcome sight in a garden.



 The phrasing “ to each day consume the equivalent” may seem somewhat awkward, but we must remember not to rely on our “ear” to find correct answers and eliminate incorrect choices. “ To each day consume the equivalent” is perfectly grammatical and conveys a logical meaning. So, that phrasing is not a reason to eliminate this choice.


In fact, this choice is the only one that does not contain any errors in grammar or convey any illogical meanings. Notice that the singular verb “makes” properly pairs with the singular subject “the ability.” Furthermore, the plural pronouns “their” and “them” properly refer to the plural noun “dragonflies.”


The sentence is grammatical and the meaning it conveys is logical. So, this choice is correct.


> <b>(C)</b> The ability of dragonflies <b>about consuming the equivalent of their own body weight in pesky insects each day makes it
</b> a welcome sight in a garden.



The pronoun “it” does not have a logical antecedent. 


If “it” refers to “the ability of dragonflies,” then the sentence is saying that “the ability of dragonflies … makes <em>the ability of dragonflies </em>a welcome sight,” a nonsensical statement.


It also does not make sense for “it” to refer to “the equivalent of their own body weight,” “body weight,” or “each day.”


There is no singular noun in the sentence to which “it” could logically refer, and thus the version created with this choice does not convey a meaning that makes sense.


Notice also that it does not make sense to say “the ability … <em>about</em> consuming.” People and things can have an ability <em>to</em> do something, but it is not correct to say that they have an ability “about” doing something.


> <b>(D)</b> The ability of dragonflies <b>to consume the equivalent of its own body weight in pesky insects each day and makes it
</b> a welcome sight in a garden.



The singular pronouns “its” and “it” do not have a logical antecedent.


Given the placement of “its” in the sentence, there is only one singular noun to which these pronouns could possibly refer: “the ability.” Of course, it does not make sense to say “The ability of dragonflies  to consume the equivalent of <em>the ability’s </em>own body weight ….” That statement is completely nonsensical.


Notice also that the sentence is not even complete. We get a second action of “the ability” toward the end of the sentence, “and <em>makes</em> it a welcome sight in a garden,” but where is the first action? In other words, where is the verb that would complete the first half of “and makes”? That verb is missing from the sentence.


“To consume” is an infinitive verb, and thus it cannot be a main verb in the sentence. In other words, “to consume” does not name an action or state of being at a specific point in time. So, it does not serve to tell us what “the ability” does or is in addition to “and makes.”


So, the sentence created with this choice does not convey a complete thought or a clear and logical meaning.


> <b>(E)</b> The ability of dragonflies <b>each day consumes the equivalent of its own body weight in pesky insects, making it
</b> a welcome sight in a garden.



This choice is a good reminder that we must always read an SC answer choice in the context of the full sentence. If we do so, we see the following:


<em>The ability</em> of dragonflies each day <em>consumes</em> the equivalent of its own body weight in pesky insects, making it a welcome sight in a garden.


Notice that the subject “the ability” pairs with the verb “consumes.” Of course, the meaning conveyed by this version is completely nonsensical. It does not make sense to say that an ability consumes its own body weight.


Always read SC answer choices in the context of the full sentence!


<strong>Key Concept:</strong> Every sentence must have at least one subject and at least one finite verb.


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>Confirm that singular subjects are paired with singular verbs and that plural subjects are paired with plural verbs. 


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>Be sure that the noun to which a pronoun refers makes sense as the person or thing performing the action associated with that pronoun.


<strong>Key Concept:</strong> If a choice contains no grammar or meaning errors, even if it does not “sound” how we might expect it to, then that choice will be correct.


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>Be sure to carefully read both the underlined and the non-underlined portions of a sentence.


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>Don't select answer choices that produce sentence versions that convey illogical meanings.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

##  Sentence Correction Example 18  

<u>That hidden chambers were found by a stonemason while the house of worship was being renovated has not been</u> revealed to the public.

1. [x] (A) That hidden chambers were found by a stonemason while the house of worship was being renovated has not been
2. [ ] (B) Renovating the house of worship, hidden chambers found by a stonemason was not
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) A stonemason found hidden chambers renovating the house of worship but not being
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) Hidden chambers were found while renovating the house of worship by a stonemason but has not been
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) Renovating the house of worship, hidden chambers being found by a stonemason but not
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



Since the correct answer to this question uses a structure that most people don’t often use or see used, getting this question correct is a bit tricky. However, if we carefully assess the grammar and logic of each version, we’ll find clear flaws that we can use to eliminate the incorrect choices and, thus, arrive at the correct answer.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(A)</b> <b>That hidden chambers were found by a stonemason while the house of worship was being renovated has not been
</b> revealed to the public.



 This choice may “sound” incorrect at first, because of the way it’s constructed. So, if we use our “ears,” we may quickly decide that this choice is not correct.


The subject of this version is the noun clause “that hidden chambers were found by a stonemason while the house of worship was being renovated.” (Noun clauses are discussed in the next chapter.)


That noun clause may not “sound” like a sentence subject, but it basically means the same thing as “the fact that hidden chambers were found …,” and we can see that “the fact that hidden chambers were found” would be something that “has not been revealed to the public.”


So, this version is grammatically correct and conveys a meaning that makes sense: the fact that hidden chambers were found has not been revealed to the public.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>Renovating the house of worship, hidden chambers found by a stonemason was not
</b> revealed to the public.



This version has a clear grammar error. The plural subject “hidden chambers” does not agree with the singular verb “was,” as we can more easily see by crossing off what appears between “hidden chambers” and “was.”


hidden chambers <s>found by a stonemason</s> was not revealed


Also, notice what this version says at the beginning: “Renovating the house of worship, hidden chambers found by a stonemason was.” For reasons that we’ll discuss later in the course, that part of the sentence conveys that hidden chambers were renovating the house of worship, a nonsensical meaning.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>A stonemason found hidden chambers renovating the house of worship but not being
</b> revealed to the public.



 This choice is tricky because it’s grammatically correct and “sounds” OK, even though it’s not correct.


To eliminate this choice, we have to notice what it says, “A stonemason found <em>hidden chambers renovating the house of worship</em> but not being revealed.” That conveys the nonsensical meaning that hidden chambers were renovating the house of worship.


We can eliminate this choice because it conveys a nonsensical meaning.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>Hidden chambers were found while renovating the house of worship by a stonemason but has not been
</b> revealed to the public.



 This version has a clear grammar error. The plural subject “hidden chambers” does not agree with the verb “has,” as we can more easily see by crossing off what appears between “hidden chambers” and “has.”


Hidden chambers <s>were found while renovating the house of worship by a stonemason but</s> has not been


Also, notice what this version says at the beginning: “Hidden chambers were found while renovating the house of worship.” For reasons that we’ll discuss later in the course, that part of the sentence conveys that hidden chambers were renovating the house of worship, a nonsensical meaning.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>Renovating the house of worship, hidden chambers being found by a stonemason but not
</b> revealed to the public.



 This version may appear OK at first. However, if we look at it carefully, we can notice that it says, “hidden chambers being found … but not revealed” without ever completing the thought with a verb that says what the hidden chambers do. It leaves the reader wondering, “Hidden chambers being found … but not revealed do what?”


So, this choice can be eliminated because this version is not a complete sentence.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

Let’s now discuss being careful when later versions of a sentence fix errors that appear in earlier versions.
