## Lesson Content
Each Sentence Correction question presents us with a sentence, and all or part of the sentence is underlined.

The first answer choice, answer choice (A), offers phrasing that is a repeat of the underlined portion of the original sentence.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In Sentence Correction questions, the first answer choice offers phrasing that is a repeat of underlined portion of the original sentence. Each of the other four choices presents an alternative way of wording the underlined portion of the sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

Our task in answering a Sentence Correction question is to select the answer choice that offers wording that produces the best version of the sentence.

Generally, the best version of the sentence will have the following characteristics:

> - It will be grammatically correct.

> - It will effectively convey a meaning that makes sense.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Our task in answering a Sentence Correction question is to select the answer choice that offers wording that produces the best version of the sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 1  

Public confidence in <u>the promises made by politicians are at an all-time low, yet turnout at the polls are higher</u> than ever.

1. [ ] (A) the promises made by politicians are at an all-time low, yet turnout at the polls are higher
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) politicians who make promises are at an all-time low, yet it has higher turnout at the polls
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) promises made by politicians is at an all-time low, yet turnout at the polls is higher
4. [ ] (D) the promises politicians make are at an all-time low, yet its turnout at the polls is higher
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) promises politicians make is at an all-time low, yet with turnout at the polls, it is higher
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



While the five answer choices in this question are somewhat similar to each other, there are key differences between the choices. By carefully reading the sentence version produced with each choice, we can see that only one of the sentence versions both is grammatically correct and conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(A)</b> Public confidence in <b>the promises made by politicians are at an all-time low, yet turnout at the polls are higher
</b> than ever.



The version created with this choice is not grammatically correct because the singular subject “public confidence” is paired with the plural verb “are,” and the singular subject “turnout” is paired with the plural verb “are.”


“Public confidence … are” and “turnout … are” are not correct.


What would be correct is “Public confidence … is” and “turnout … is.”


If you have a hard time seeing this issue, one powerful strategy you can use is to cross off the phrases between the nouns and verbs:


Public confidence <s>in the promises made by politicians</s> are


turnout <s>at the polls</s> are


By crossing off the intervening phrases, we are able to more clearly see which nouns are supposed to pair with which verbs, and then assess whether the pairing is grammatical.<strong> </strong>


> <b>(B)</b> Public confidence in <b>politicians who make promises are at an all-time low, yet it has higher turnout at the polls
</b> than ever.



The version created with this choice is not grammatically correct because the singular subject “public confidence” is paired with the plural verb “are.”


If you have a hard time seeing this issue, one powerful strategy you can use is to cross off the phrase between the noun and verb:


Public confidence <s>in politicians who make promises</s> are


Additionally, the singular pronoun “it” could only possibly refer to either the singular noun “public confidence” or the singular noun “all-time low”; all of the other nouns in the sentence are plural and thus cannot be paired with a singular pronoun. After all, neither “<em>public confidence</em> has higher turnout at the polls” nor “<em>all-time low</em> has higher turnout at the polls” makes any sense. People, not a quality such as “confidence” or an amount such as “low,” go to the polls.<strong> </strong>


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> Public confidence in <b>promises made by politicians is at an all-time low, yet turnout at the polls is higher
</b> than ever.



 In this version, “Public confidence … is” and “turnout … is” are grammatically correct.


Notice also that, when we read this version, there is no question in our minds regarding what the sentence is meant to convey. Because the singular nouns are properly paired with singular verbs, we can clearly see which “action” belongs to which thing in the sentence. We see that “public confidence” is the thing that “is at an all-time low,” and that “turnout” is the thing that “is higher than ever.”  


Since this version is grammatically correct and effectively conveys a meaning that makes sense, it is the correct answer.<strong> </strong>


> <b>(D)</b> Public confidence in <b>the promises politicians make are at an all-time low, yet its turnout at the polls is higher
</b> than ever.



This version is not grammatically correct because the singular subject “public confidence” is paired with the plural verb “are.”


If you have a hard time seeing this issue, one powerful strategy you can use is to cross off the phrase between the noun and verb:


Public confidence <s>in the promises politicians make</s> are


Additionally, the singular “its” could only possibly refer to the singular “public confidence,” but saying that the “turnout” of “public confidence” is higher makes no sense. People, not a quality they have such as “confidence,” go to the polls.  


> <b>(E)</b> Public confidence in <b>promises politicians make is at an all-time low, yet with turnout at the polls, it is higher
</b> than ever.



In this version, the pronoun “it” in “<em>it</em> is higher than ever,” could refer only to either “public confidence” or “turnout at the polls.” So, this version conveys either of the following:


with turnout at the polls, <em>public confidence</em> is higher than ever


with turnout at the polls, <em>turnout</em> is higher than ever


Notice that it doesn’t make sense to say that public confidence is higher than ever <em>with</em> turnout at the polls. After all, public confidence is not “with” turnout.


Also, it certainly doesn’t make sense to say that turnout is higher than ever with turnout at the polls.


Thus, the meaning of the version created with this choice does not make sense.


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>Check to be sure that singular nouns are paired with singular verbs and that plural nouns are paired with plural verbs. 


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>When checking for subject-verb agreement, cross off any prepositional phrases that appear between the subject and finite verb.


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>Be sure that the noun to which a pronoun refers makes sense as the person or thing performing the action associated with that pronoun.


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>Don't select answer choices that produce sentence versions that convey illogical meanings.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

##  Sentence Correction Example 2  

Research has shown <u>that bananas in a container with kiwis causes the kiwis</u> to ripen quickly.

1. [ ] (A) that bananas in a container with kiwis causes the kiwis
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) that, if bananas are in a container with kiwis, they cause them
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) bananas and kiwis in a container, causing them
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) that bananas in a container with kiwis cause the kiwis
5. [ ] (E) that, when bananas are in a container and cause kiwis
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



We can see that, while the five answer choices in this question are somewhat similar to each other, there are key differences between the choices. By carefully reading the sentence version produced via the use of each choice, we can see that only one of the sentence versions both is grammatically correct and conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(A)</b> Research has shown <b>that bananas in a container with kiwis causes the kiwis
</b> to ripen quickly.



This version is not grammatically correct because the plural subject “bananas” is paired with the singular verb “causes.” “Bananas … causes” is not correct.


What would be correct is “bananas … cause.”


> <b>(B)</b> Research has shown <b>that, if bananas are in a container with kiwis, they cause them
</b> to ripen quickly.



This version has no grammatical errors. However, if we look carefully, we can see that the meaning conveyed by this version is not clear.


Notice that the sentence is about bananas and kiwis. OK. Now, notice that the sentence says, “they cause them to ripen quickly.”


The issue this version has is that whether “they” and “them” refers to “bananas” or “kiwis” is not clear. Is the sentence meant to convey that bananas cause kiwis to ripen quickly or that kiwis cause bananas to ripen quickly? The sentence does not make clear which is which.


So, this version is incorrect because it does not effectively convey a clear meaning.


> <b>(C)</b> Research has shown <b>bananas and kiwis in a container, causing them
</b> to ripen quickly.



This version is grammatically correct, but what it conveys does not make sense. Notice that this version says, “Research has shown bananas and kiwis in a container.”


So, essentially this version says, “Research has shown bananas and kiwis.”


Would research show bananas and kiwis? Of course not.


Research doesn’t show fruit. It would show something about fruit, but it would not show bananas and kiwis themselves. So, what this version conveys is nonsensical.


A version that conveys a nonsensical meaning is not correct.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D)</b> Research has shown <b>that bananas in a container with kiwis cause the kiwis
</b> to ripen quickly.



The version created via the use of this choice is grammatically correct and conveys a meaning that makes complete sense.


“bananas … cause” is grammatically correct.


Notice also that the meaning conveyed is clear. When we read this version, there’s no question in our minds regarding what the sentence is meant to convey.


Since this version is grammatically correct and effectively conveys a meaning that makes sense, it is the correct answer.


> <b>(E)</b> Research has shown <b>that, when bananas are in a container and cause kiwis
</b> to ripen quickly.



Notice what we get if we put this answer choice together with the non-underlined portions:


Research has shown that, when bananas are in a container and cause kiwis to ripen quickly.


All we have is the beginning of a sentence. When we read this version, we are left wondering, “When bananas are in a container and cause kiwis to ripen quickly, what?”


So, this version does not express a complete thought. It’s just part of a sentence, not an entire sentence.


Since this version is not a complete sentence, this version is structurally flawed and is not correct.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
