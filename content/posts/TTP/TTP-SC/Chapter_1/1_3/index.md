## Lesson Content
It would be easy to get the impression that Sentence Correction questions test only grammar knowledge, and the truth is that grammar errors do appear frequently in Sentence Correction questions.

However, while grammar knowledge may serve as a good foundation for correctly answering Sentence Correction questions, such knowledge is neither sufficient to get us to the correct answer to many questions nor what Sentence Correction really tests.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Grammar knowledge may serve as a good foundation for correctly answering Sentence Correction questions, but grammar knowledge is neither sufficient to get us to the correct answer to many Sentence Correction questions nor what Sentence Correction really tests.
{{< /alert >}}

So, if Sentence Correction is not a test of grammar knowledge, what does Sentence Correction test?

What Sentence Correction really tests is our skill in noticing issues.

Consider the following example.

> A study of the feeding behaviors of bats indicate that a bat can consume twenty mosquitos in a minute.

If we look closely, we can see that the singular noun “study” is paired with the plural verb “indicate” to form “a study … indicate,” which is incorrect.

> A <strong>study</strong> <s>of the feeding behaviors of bats</s> <strong>indicate</strong> that a bat can consume twenty mosquitos in a minute.

Notice that “study” is not next to “indicate,” as it might be in a test of basic grammar knowledge.

So, if this sentence were in a Sentence Correction question, the question would test our skill in noticing that “study” and “indicate” are incorrectly paired even though the fact that they are incorrectly paired is obscured by the presence of “of the feeding behaviors of bats” between “study” and “indicate.”

Here’s another example.

> White-tailed deer, found in Canada, the United States, Mexico, and Central and South America, they are the smallest members of the North American deer family.

The sentence above may look OK at first, but there’s an issue that we can notice: the subject of the sentence is repeated unnecessarily.

> <strong>White-tailed deer</strong><s>, found in Canada, the United States, Mexico, and Central and South America,</s> <strong>they</strong> are the smallest members of the North American deer family.

The sentence begins with a subject, “white tailed deer.” Then, “they,” which refers to “white tailed deer,” also serves as the subject of the sentence.

Of course, it’s likely that we could study English grammar fairly extensively and never run into a discussion of unnecessarily repeated subjects, since it’s fairly obvious that repeating a subject for no reason does not make sense.

The following example has another type of issue.

> Many crops have been damaged by the ongoing flooding, and they are raising food prices nationwide.

The above sentence is grammatically correct, but it has a significant issue: because the only plural noun in the sentence to which “they” could refer is “crops,” the sentence seems to convey that the crops themselves are raising prices.

GMAT Sentence Correction constantly tests our skill in noticing things by presenting us with sentences that may seem to be well constructed but are grammatically flawed or convey illogical or nonsensical meanings.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

GMAT Sentence Correction constantly tests our skill in noticing things by presenting us with sentences that may seem to be well constructed but are grammatically flawed or convey illogical or nonsensical meanings.
{{< /alert >}}

So, what does all of the above mean to us? It means that training to master GMAT Sentence correction must involve not only reviewing sentence construction basics and grammar but also learning to notice all kinds of issues in the construction of sentences and in the meanings conveyed by sentences.

That GMAT Sentence Correction would test our skill in noticing issues totally makes sense, because the GMAT is an entrance exam, not for grammar school, but for business school, where noticing the logic of and issues in business strategies, accounting methods, and other aspects of businesses is key to success.

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>Targeted Practice: 5</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

<b>Explanation:</b>


Did Adele recently borrow the tennis shoes or did the thief recently take them? Because of the placement of the adverb “recently” between the verbs “borrowed” and “were taken,” we can’t be sure. To say that differently, consider how we might read this sentence. Some of you read it as:


The tennis shoes that <strong>Adele borrowed recently </strong>were taken from her car by a thief.


However, some of you read it as:


The tennis shoes that Adele borrowed<strong> recently were taken</strong> from her car by a thief.


In other words, the meaning conveyed by the sentence is ambiguous. So, the sentence can’t be correct.


Either of the following sentences would be correct. Notice that each conveys something different:


The tennis shoes that Adele <strong>recently</strong> borrowed were taken from her car by a thief.


or


The tennis shoes that Adele borrowed were <strong>recently</strong> taken from her car by a thief.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

{{< /alert >}}
</details></h5>


   <h5><details><summary><b><u>Targeted Practice: 6</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

<b>Explanation:</b>


Notice the placement of the phrase “in 2020.” What happened when? Did the author make the statement in 2020, or was the statement made about something that would occur by 2020? In other words, some of you read the sentence as:


<strong>The author said in 2020</strong> the price of her books would need to double.


And some of you read it as:


The author said<strong> in 2020 the price of her books would need to double.</strong>


In other words, the sentence conveys an ambiguous meaning, so it can’t be correct.


Look at the following corrected sentences, each of which clearly conveys one meaning:


The author said that the price of her books would need <strong>to double</strong> <strong>in 2020</strong>.


The above version makes clear that the doubling must occur in 2020.


<strong>In 2020, the author said</strong> that the price of her books would need to double.


The above version makes clear that the author made the statement in 2020.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

{{< /alert >}}
</details></h5>

   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

<b>Explanation:</b>


Destruction of World War I makes no sense, as it seems to convey that the war itself was destroyed. How can a World War be destroyed? Europe could be destroyed, but a World War could not. Notice that, although there are no errors of grammar in the sentence, it is illogical and thus incorrect.


A corrected sentence might read:


The Treaty of Versailles set forth the terms of peace between the Allies and Germany and held Germany responsible for the destruction of Europe caused by World War I.
