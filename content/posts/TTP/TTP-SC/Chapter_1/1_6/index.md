## Lesson Content
When reading written communications (outside of Sentence Correction), we’re lenient with the writer and make efforts to understand the writer’s intended message, even if the writing has errors in grammar, word choice, style, or meaning.

In other words, we give the writer the benefit of the doubt, saying “I know what the writer meant to say,” even if the writer didn’t exactly say what was meant.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Normally, when we read, our goal is to comprehend the intended meaning, whether it be of a book, text message, or email, regardless of the writing style or quality.
{{< /alert >}}

To give the writer the benefit of the doubt, we often:

> • decipher the meaning of a sentence even when the wording makes it ambiguous

> • determine the writer's message even when it's not explicitly stated

> • use context and other hints to understand writing even when it's unclear

So, why are we discussing these things we often do when reading?

We’re discussing them because <strong>we MUST NOT do any of these things when answering GMAT Sentence Correction questions.</strong>

Here’s why.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The goal of Sentence Correction questions is to test WHETHER WE CAN PICK UP ON FLAWS IN WRITING.
{{< /alert >}}

To pick up on flaws in writing, we must be hypercritical of the writing.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

To pick up on flaws in writing, we must be hypercritical of the writing. We must not give the writer the benefit of the doubt. We must not say “I know what the writer meant.”
{{< /alert >}}

In Sentence Correction, when a sentence says something unclear, we call it out.

In Sentence Correction questions, we’re seeking out flaws in the sentences!

In other words, we’re not seeking to understand what the sentences would mean if they were well written.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In Sentence Correction questions, we’re not seeking to understand what the sentences would mean if they were well-written. We’re seeking to understand what they do mean AS WRITTEN.
{{< /alert >}}

So, when reading sentence versions in Sentence Correction questions, we don’t read them the way we would read most other things.

Rather, we read the sentences literally.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When reading sentence versions in Sentence Correction questions, we don’t read them the way we would read most other things. Rather, we read them EXACTLY AS WRITTEN.
{{< /alert >}}

For example, if someone wrote the following in an email, you would know exactly what the person meant.

> The waiter brought us bubbling glasses full of champagne.

We all know what is meant by this sentence.

> bubbling glasses

Since when can glasses be bubbling? Glasses don’t bubble.

A corrected sentence could be:

> The waiter brought us glasses full of bubbling champagne.

Ah.

What if you saw the following in a news article?

> To increase the tourists who visit it, the town has restored some historic buildings.

We know what this sentence is attempting to convey, that to cause more tourists to visit it, the town has restored some buildings.

A correct version could be:

> To increase the number of tourists who visit it, the town has restored some historic buildings.

Now, the sentence makes clear that the town restored the buildings to “increase the <em>number</em> of tourists,” rather than to increase the tourists themselves.

What about the following?

> By going there during the off season, Puerto Rico was fun for us.

If someone sent that sentence as a text message, you would know that it means that some people had fun in Puerto Rico by going there during the offseason.

However, if we read the sentence literally, we see that “By going there …, Puerto Rico was fun,” conveys that Puerto Rico was fun by going to Puerto Rico, as if Puerto Rico could go to itself.

Of course, that meaning doesn’t make sense.

A correct version is:

> By going to Puerto Rico during the offseason, we had fun.

Now the sentence says that <em>we</em> had fun by going to Puerto Rico.

OK, so, what’s the key takeaway here? The key takeaway is that, to master Sentence Correction, we must learn to read sentences in a new way.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

To master Sentence Correction, we must learn to read sentences in a new way. So, we must practice reading sentences literally to see what they convey as written.
{{< /alert >}}

In fact, we can sum up our job with regard to meaning in Sentence Correction as follows:

> • Determine what meaning a sentence version conveys when read literally.

> • Decide whether that meaning makes sense.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Our job with regard to meaning in Sentence Correction is to:
{{< /alert >}}

So, as you go through this course, when answering the Sentence Correction practice questions, be sure to practice reading sentences literally, determining what they convey, and deciding whether their meanings make sense.

##  Sentence Correction Example 5  

<u>In contrast with other novels of the time, Jane Austen’s </u><u>novels </u><u>focused</u> on everyday life and ordinary people.

1. [x] (A) In contrast with other novels of the time, Jane Austen’s novels focused
2. [ ] (B) As a contrast with other novels of the time, Jane Austen focused her novels
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) With other novels of the time that were contrasts, Jane Austen focused her novels
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) When it was contrasted with other novels of the time, Jane Austen’s novels were focused
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) To be contrasted with other novels of the time, the novels of Jane Austen focused
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: N/A



Most of the sentence versions in this question are grammatically correct. So, to determine which choice is the correct answer, we have to consider the meaning conveyed by each version and whether that meaning makes sense.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(A)</b> <b>In contrast with other novels of the time, Jane Austen’s novels focused
</b> on everyday life and ordinary people.



 “In contrast with” has a meaning similar to “unlike.” So, this version conveys the logical meaning that, unlike other novels of the time, Jane Austen’s novels focused on everyday life and ordinary people.


Since this version has no grammatical errors and conveys a meaning that makes sense, it’s correct.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>As a contrast with other novels of the time, Jane Austen focused her novels
</b> on everyday life and ordinary people.



 Notice that this version says, “As a contrast with other novels …, Jane Austen focused.” Thus, it conveys that Jane Austen was focused “as a contrast.” In other words, it suggests that Jane Austen herself was “a contrast with other novels.”


Since a person would not a be “a contrast,” much less “a contrast with other novels,” the meaning conveyed by this version doesn’t make sense.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>With other novels of the time that were contrasts, Jane Austen focused her novels
</b> on everyday life and ordinary people.



 Notice that this version says, “With other novels of the time that were contrasts, Jane Austen focused.” We see that it conveys that Jane Austen was “with other novels of the time” when she “focused.”


Since it doesn’t make sense that Jane Austen was “with other novels” or that she focused with other novels, the meaning of this version doesn’t make sense.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>When it was contrasted with other novels of the time, Jane Austen’s novels were focused
</b> on everyday life and ordinary people.



 This version is blatantly incorrect since the pronoun “it” has nothing to refer to. After all, “it” is singular, while “Jane Austen’s novels” is plural.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>To be contrasted with other novels of the time, the novels of Jane Austen focused
</b> on everyday life and ordinary people.



 Notice that this version says, “To be contrasted …, the novels of Jane Austen focused.”


Reading the sentence literally, we see that, by using “to,” it communicates that the novels focused on everyday life and ordinary people <em>so that they would be contrasted</em>, as if novels would intentionally focus on certain things for the purpose of being “contrasted.”


Since novels would not intentionally do anything “to be contrasted,” this meaning is nonsensical.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

</details></h5>

##  Sentence Correction Example 6  

Some wellness coaches claim that <u>by doing meditation and following a vegan diet, the aging of people has been reversed in its process</u>.

1. [ ] (A) by doing meditation and following a vegan diet, the aging of people has been reversed in its process
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) doing meditation and following a vegan diet has reversed people who were in the aging process
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) through doing meditation and following a vegan diet, people have reversed the process of aging
4. [ ] (D) doing meditation and following a vegan diet has been the reverse of people in the process of aging
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) through doing meditation and following a vegan diet, people have been reversed in their aging processes
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: N/A



We can tell what the sentence in this question is meant to say by reading any of the versions, but only one version actually conveys that meaning. All the others convey meanings that don’t make sense.


> <b>(A)</b> Some wellness coaches claim that <b>by doing meditation and following a vegan diet, the aging of people has been reversed in its process
</b>.



 Notice that this version says, “by doing meditation and following a vegan diet, the aging of people has been reversed.”


Reading the sentence literally, we see that it conveys that aging has been reversed “by doing meditation and following a vegan diet,” as if aging reversed itself by doing those things.


Since aging would not do those things, the meaning of this sentence doesn’t make sense.


> <b>(B)</b> Some wellness coaches claim that <b>doing meditation and following a vegan diet has reversed people who were in the aging process
</b>.



 Notice that this version says that “doing meditation and following a vegan diet has reversed people.” Since meditation and diet would not reverse people, that meaning doesn’t make sense.


What might make sense is that doing meditation and following a vegan diet have reversed <em>the aging process</em>, but the sentence doesn’t convey that meaning.


Also, notice that the singular verb “has” does not agree with the subject “doing meditation and following a vegan diet.” That subject is a list two items and therefore is plural, not singular.


So, this version is incorrect because it both conveys a nonsensical meaning and has a grammatical error.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(C)</b> Some wellness coaches claim that <b>through doing meditation and following a vegan diet, people have reversed the process of aging
</b>.



 This sentence conveys that people have reversed the process of aging through taking the actions of doing meditation and following a vegan diet.


Since that meaning makes sense and this version has no grammatical errors, this version is correct.


> <b>(D)</b> Some wellness coaches claim that <b>doing meditation and following a vegan diet has been the reverse of people in the process of aging
</b>.



 Notice that this version says, “doing meditation and following a vegan diet has been the reverse of people.”


The idea that “doing meditation and following a vegan diet,” which are actions, have been “the reverse,” i.e., the opposite, “of people” is nonsensical.


Also, notice that the singular verb “has” does not agree with the subject “doing meditation and following a vegan diet.” That subject is a list two items and therefore is plural, not singular.


So, this version is incorrect because it both conveys a nonsensical meaning and has a grammatical error.


> <b>(E)</b> Some wellness coaches claim that <b>through doing meditation and following a vegan diet, people have been reversed in their aging processes
</b>.



 Notice that this version says, “by doing …, people have been reversed in their aging processes.”


The idea that “people have been reversed” by doing meditation and following a vegan diet is nonsensical. What would have been reversed is people’s aging processes, not the people themselves.


So, the meaning conveyed by this version doesn’t make sense.





> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

Let’s now discuss the importance of the non-underlined portions of the sentences in Sentence Correction questions.
