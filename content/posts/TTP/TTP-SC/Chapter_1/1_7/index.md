## Lesson Content
Don’t for a second think that our job is to evaluate only the portion of the sentence that appears in the answer choices.

In fact, often when a person falls into a Sentence Correction trap, she does so because she failed to carefully consider the non-underlined portion of the sentence.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When answering a Sentence Correction question, we must carefully consider both the underlined and the non-underlined portions of a sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following examples.

##  Sentence Correction Example 7  

Howler monkeys, which <u>can be known for their loud howls and can be considered</u> divine patrons of the artisans by the ancient Mayans.

1. [ ] (A) can be known for their loud howls and can be considered
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) are known for their loud howls, were considered
3. [ ] (C) are known for their loud howls and were considered
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) have been known for their loud howls and for their considerable
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) are known for loud howls that are considerable
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



All of the answer choices in this question, with the possible exception of choice (B), look pretty reasonable when they are read alone, without the non-underlined portions of the sentence. So, we might be tempted to choose a choice other than (B) if we were to consider the choices without combining them with the non-underlined portions.


However, the versions that result when the choices other than (B) are combined with the non-underlined portions are not even sentences. Also, the meanings conveyed by some of the versions created via the use of the choices other than (B) are ridiculous.


So, as it turns out, choice (B), the choice that sounds the least right when read on its own, is the correct answer.


> <b>(A)</b> Howler monkeys, which <b>can be known for their loud howls and can be considered
</b> divine patrons of the artisans by the ancient Mayans.



Notice what this version is actually composed of.


It begins with a noun, “howler monkeys.” Then comes “which can be known for their loud howls and can be considered divine patrons,” which tells us something about howler monkeys but does not tell us what howler monkeys do.


So, the version created via the use of this choice is just a noun and some information about that noun that don’t add up to a complete sentence. In other words, this version lacks an independent clause, a structure we will discuss extensively in the next chapter, and without an independent clause we can’t have a sentence (you will soon be a professional with this concept).


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B)</b> Howler monkeys, which <b>are known for their loud howls, were considered
</b> divine patrons of the artisans by the ancient Mayans.



This choice sounds choppy and, since it includes two verbs, “are” and “were,” without a conjunction, such as “and,” between them, it may seem to be incorrect. Notice however, that, when we combine this choice with the non-underlined portions, we get a sentence that makes complete sense.


Howler monkeys, which are known for their loud howls, were considered divine patrons of the artisans by the ancient Mayans.


> <b>(C)</b> Howler monkeys, which <b>are known for their loud howls and were considered
</b> divine patrons of the artisans by the ancient Mayans.



This choice flows well and may appear to be correct, because it has a pattern to it, as “are known” nicely matches “were considered.”


However, when we combine this choice with the non-underlined portions, we get the following.


Howler monkeys, which are known for their loud howls and were considered divine patrons of the artisans by the ancient Mayans.


It begins with a noun, “howler monkeys.” Then comes “which are known for their loud howls and were considered divine patrons,” which tells us something about howler monkeys but does not tell us what howler monkeys do.


So, the version created via the use of this choice is just a noun and some information about that noun that don’t add up to a complete sentence. In other words, this version lacks an independent clause, a structure we will discuss extensively in the next chapter, and without an independent clause we can’t have a sentence (you will soon be a professional with this concept).


> <b>(D)</b> Howler monkeys, which <b>have been known for their loud howls and for their considerable
</b> divine patrons of the artisans by the ancient Mayans.



This choice flows well and may appear to be correct, because it has a pattern to it, as “for their loud howls” nicely matches “for their considerable.”


However, when we combine this choice with the non-underlined portions, we get the following:


Howler monkeys, which have been known for their loud howls and for their considerable divine patrons of the artisans by the ancient Mayans.


We can see that this version is just a noun, “howler monkeys,” followed by some information about that noun, “which are known for their loud howls and for ….” So, the version created via the use of this choice is not a complete sentence. In other words, this version lacks an independent clause, a structure we will discuss extensively in the next chapter, and without an independent clause we can’t have a sentence (you will soon be a professional with this concept).


Also, this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that howler monkeys have considerable divine patrons of the artisans.


> <b>(E)</b> Howler monkeys, which <b>are known for loud howls that are considerable
</b> divine patrons of the artisans by the ancient Mayans.



This choice flows well and may appear to be correct, because “that are considerable” appears to be the beginning of something that would logically provide more information about the “loud howls.” However, look at what we get when we combine this choice with the non-underlined portions:


Howler monkeys, which are known for their loud howls that are considerable divine patrons of the artisans by the ancient Mayans.


The resulting structure conveys the comically nonsensical meaning that the monkeys’ howls are considerable divine patrons of the artisans.


Also, we can see that this version is just a noun, “howler monkeys,” followed by some information about that noun, “which are known for their loud howls that ….” So, the version created via the use of this choice is not a complete sentence. In other words, this version lacks an independent clause, a structure we will discuss extensively in the next chapter, and without an independent clause we can’t have a sentence (you will soon be a professional with this concept).


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

##  Sentence Correction Example 8  

A new line for <u>trains that achieve speeds in excess of 150 miles per hour are expected</u> to be completed within the next two years.

1. [ ] (A) trains that achieve speeds in excess of 150 miles per hour are expected
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) trains, which achieve speeds that go faster than 150 miles per hour, are expected
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) trains that achieve speeds in excess of 150 miles per hour is expected
4. [ ] (D) trains that achieve a speed that is in excess of 150 miles per hour and is expected
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) trains achieving a speed that goes faster than 150 miles per hour expects
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Notice that, in the underlined portion of the sentence, we have the plural nouns “trains.” If we are not careful, the presence of this plural noun will lead us to believe that the sentence requires the plural verb “are” in “are expecting.” However, when we read the entire sentence, paying just as much attention to the non-underlined portion as to the underlined portion, we see that it is the “new line” that IS expected to be completed within the next two years.


Notice how easy it would be to not read the non-underlined portion of the sentence and, as a result, conclude that the sentence requires the plural verb “are.” Don’t do this. Be sure to read the entire sentence holistically.


Figuring out what the sentence is actually about will help us to find the right answer to this question. By reading carefully, we can see that the sentence is about a new train line that is expected to be completed within the next two years. Armed with this information, we can analyze the answer choices to determine which is the best.


> <b>(A)</b> A new line for <b>trains that achieve speeds in excess of 150 miles per hour are expected
</b> to be completed within the next two years.



If we were to consider this choice on its own, we might get the incorrect impression that this choice is the correct answer, as it could appear to convey in a grammatically correct manner that trains are expected.


However, once we consider this choice in the context of the entire sentence, we can see that it is not correct. Trains are not expected but, rather, a line is expected to be completed. So, when we combine this choice with the non-underlined portion of the sentence, we get “A new line … are expected …,” which is not grammatically correct, because the singular “a new line” does not agree with the plural “are expected.”


> <b>(B)</b> A new line for <b>trains, which achieve speeds that go faster than 150 miles per hour, are expected
</b> to be completed within the next two years.



If we were to consider this choice on its own, we might get the incorrect impression that this choice is the correct answer, as it could appear to convey in a grammatically correct manner that trains are expected.


However, once we consider this choice in the context of the entire sentence, we can see that it is not correct. Trains are not expected but, rather, a line is expected to be completed. So, when we combine this choice with the non-underlined portion of the sentence, we get “A new line … are expected …,” which is not grammatically correct, because the singular “a new line” does not agree with the plural “are expected.”


Notice also that “speeds that go faster than 150 miles per hour” conveys a nonsensical meaning. Trains might go faster than 150 miles per hour, but speeds would not “go faster.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(C)</b> A new line for <b>trains that achieve speeds in excess of 150 miles per hour is expected
</b> to be completed within the next two years.



Considered on its own, this choice could appear to be grammatically flawed, as we might get the impression that the noun “trains” is meant to be paired with “is expected,” as in “trains … is expected,” which would be incorrect because “trains,” a plural noun, would not agree with the singular “is expected.”


However, once we consider this choice in the context of the entire sentence, we can see that what the sentence is actually conveying is that a new line is expected to be completed. So, the version created via the use of this choice is “A new line … is expected …,” which is grammatically correct and conveys a logical meaning.


> <b>(D)</b> A new line for <b>trains that achieve a speed that is in excess of 150 miles per hour and is expected
</b> to be completed within the next two years.



Considering this choice on its own, we might get the impression that it is correct, as it seems to clearly and correctly outline characteristics of a speed. However, when we combine this choice with the non-underlined portion, we can see that the resulting structure does not express a complete thought and, thus, is not a complete sentence. The structure is merely a noun, “a new line,”<em> </em>followed by a lot of wording that says more about that noun.


A new line for trains that achieve … and is …


> <b>(E)</b> A new line for <b>trains achieving a speed that goes faster than 150 miles per hour expects
</b> to be completed within the next two years.



Even without considering this choice in the context of the entire sentence, we can see that this choice is very unlikely to be the correct answer, because it conveys the nonsensical meaning that a speed goes faster than 150 miles per hour. A train might go faster than 150 miles per hour, but a speed itself would not “go faster.”


To confirm that this choice is incorrect, we can consider it in the context of the entire sentence. When we do so, we see that the sentence created via the use of this choice not only conveys the nonsensical meaning that a speed “goes faster” but also conveys the nonsensical meaning that the line itself “expects to be completed.” Someone might expect a train line to be completed, and a train line might be expected to be completed, but a train line would not expect itself to be completed.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)
