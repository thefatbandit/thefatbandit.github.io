## Lesson Content
At some point, you may have heard of the idea of the Sentence Correction answer choices having “splits.” The idea of a split is simple.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

We have a split when some of the answer choices utilize one structure while the other answer choices utilize a different, dueling structure.
{{< /alert >}}

For example, maybe some choices use “their” while others use “its.” Maybe some choices use “because” while others use “due to.” Now, in some, but not all, circumstances, the splits can be helpful for us since they can clue us into what is being tested.

For example, in the example that follows, notice that we have a split.

##  Sentence Correction Example 9  

After analyzing the data, <u>if the fog would persist was uncertain so the meteorologist did what she felt was most conservative, she alerted</u> her viewers to be prepared for rain.

1. [ ] (A) if the fog would persist was uncertain so the meteorologist did what she felt was most conservative, she alerted
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) there was uncertainty regarding if the fog was going to persist, and so the meteorologist did what she felt was most conservative; alerting
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) the uncertainty about if the fog was going to persist caused the meteorologist to do what she felt was most conservative; the alerting of
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) the meteorologist was uncertain whether the fog would persist, so she did what she felt was most conservative; she alerted
5. [ ] (E) the meteorologist was uncertain whether or not the fog would persist so she did what she felt was most conservative, alerting
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



This question has a 3-2 split. Three choices include “if” while two choices include “whether.” By noticing this split, we can direct our attention to one of the key decision points that we’ll use in choosing among the choices.


“Whether” is used when a person is deciding between alternatives or when we make a statement involving uncertainty about two possible alternatives. So, we use “whether” in statements like “Bianca was wondering whether to x,” or “It was unclear whether x or y.”


On the other hand, “if” is used only for statements of conditionality. In other words, we use if in situations such as “If x, then y.”


The words “whether” and “if” are not interchangeable.


For example:


<u>Correct</u>: I don’t know whether I will go skiing this weekend. (uncertainty)


<u>Incorrect</u>: I don’t know if I will go skiing this weekend.


<u>Correct</u>: If you don’t turn in your homework, you will earn a low grade. (conditionality)


<u>Incorrect</u>: Whether you don’t turn in your homework, you will earn a low grade.


Also, the expression “whether or not” is redundant. What is correct is to say simply, “whether.”


> <b>(A)</b> After analyzing the data, <b>if the fog would persist was uncertain so the meteorologist did what she felt was most conservative, she alerted
</b> her viewers to be prepared for rain.



The first issue we see in this choice is that it uses “if” in expressing what the meteorologist was uncertain about. While people often use “if” in this way, the GMAT does not consider using “if” in this way correct. The GMAT reserves “if” for statements of conditionality. In scanning the answer choices, we can see the (B) and (C) also make the same mistake.


Also, notice what this version says at the beginning: “After analyzing the data, if the fog would persist was uncertain.” For reasons that we’ll discuss later in the course, that part of the sentence conveys that “if the fog would persist” was analyzing the data, a nonsensical meaning. After “after analyzing the data,” we need a noun that names something capable of analyzing data. Of course, “if the fog would persist” does not have that capability.


Also, just for those of you who may have noticed, yes, this version is a run-on sentence and, thus, has a major error of a type that we will study extensively in the next chapter.


> <b>(B)</b> After analyzing the data, <b>there was uncertainty regarding if the fog was going to persist, and so the meteorologist did what she felt was most conservative; alerting
</b> her viewers to be prepared for rain.



 The first issue we see in this choice is that it uses “if” in expressing what the meteorologist was uncertain about. While people often use “if” in this way, the GMAT does not consider using “if” in this way correct. The GMAT reserves “if” for statements of conditionality.


Also, notice what this version says at the beginning: “After analyzing the data, there was uncertainty regarding if the fog was going to persist.” For reasons that we’ll discuss later in the course, that part of the sentence conveys that “there” was analyzing the data, a nonsensical meaning. After “after analyzing the data” we need a noun that names something capable of analyzing data. Of course, the word “there” does not have that capability.


> <b>(C)</b> After analyzing the data, <b>the uncertainty about if the fog was going to persist caused the meteorologist to do what she felt was most conservative; the alerting of
</b> her viewers to be prepared for rain.



 The first issue we see in this choice is that it uses “if” in expressing what the meteorologist was uncertain about. While people often use “if” in this way, the GMAT does not consider using “if” in this way correct. The GMAT reserves “if” for statements of conditionality.


Also, notice what this version says at the beginning: “After analyzing the data, the uncertainty about if the fog was going to persist.” For reasons that we’ll discuss later in the course, that part of the sentence conveys that “the uncertainty” was analyzing the data, a nonsensical meaning. After “after analyzing the data” we need a noun that names something capable of analyzing data. Of course, “the uncertainty” does not have that capability.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D)</b> After analyzing the data, <b>the meteorologist was uncertain whether the fog would persist, so she did what she felt was most conservative; she alerted
</b> her viewers to be prepared for rain.



This version correctly uses “whether” in making a statement about the uncertainty of the meteorologist (regarding whether the fog would persist).


Furthermore, notice that this version logically places the noun “meteorologist” directly after “After analyzing the data” and, thus, conveys the logical meaning that it was the meteorologist who was analyzing the data.


> <b>(E)</b> After analyzing the data, <b>the meteorologist was uncertain whether or not the fog would persist so she did what she felt was most conservative, alerting
</b> her viewers to be prepared for rain.



 This version uses the unidiomatic expression “whether or not.” The correct expression is “whether.”


Also, just for those of you who may have noticed, yes, this version is a run-on sentence and, thus, has a major error of a type that we will study extensively in the next chapter.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

{{< /alert >}}
</details></h5>


Ok, great.

However, sometimes the splits are present to distract us from the real issues in the sentences and don’t actually affect the choices.

For example, consider the following sentences:

> The people creating the new marketing strategy is meeting with the product design team next week.

> The people who are creating the new marketing strategy are meeting with the product design team next week.

> The people creating the new marketing strategy, they are meeting with the product design team next week.

Notice the split?

Two sentences use “the people creating” while one sentence uses “the people who are creating.” Guess what? This difference is immaterial.

##  Sentence Correction Example 10  

Surprising to many is that, while <u>Manhattan still has a high population density, the number of people living there is lower than it was in</u> 1910.

1. [x] (A) Manhattan still has a high population density, the number of people living there is lower than it was in
2. [ ] (B) Manhattan still has a high population density, the people living there are lower than they were in
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) Manhattan’s population density is still high, the people living there is a lower number than
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) Manhattan’s population density is still high, the people living there have been lowered from what they were in
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) Manhattan still has a high population density, the people living there are lower than the number were in
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Three of the choices in this question begin with “Manhattan still has a high population density,” and the other two choices begin with “Manhattan’s population density is still high.” So, this question has a 3-2 split.


However, both “Manhattan still has a high population density” and “Manhattan’s population density is still high” are correct, and they mean essentially the same thing. Thus, the 3-2 split in this question does not highlight decision points that we can use to answer the question and is just a distraction.


So, to arrive at the correct answer, we have to find other differences between the choices, differences that do serve as decision points.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(A)</b> Surprising to many is that, while <b>Manhattan still has a high population density, the number of people living there is lower than it was in
</b> 1910.



This version is grammatically correct and effectively conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(B)</b> Surprising to many is that, while <b>Manhattan still has a high population density, the people living there are lower than they were in
</b> 1910.



This version is grammatically correct. Notice what it says though. It says that the people living in Manhattan are “lower than they were in 1910.”


Of course, that statement does not make sense. The point to be made is not that the people themselves are somehow lower than they were in 1910; the point to be made is that the number of people living in Manhattan is lower than it was in 1910, but that’s not what this version says.


> <b>(C)</b> Surprising to many is that, while <b>Manhattan’s population density is still high, the people living there is a lower number than
</b> 1910.



This version has an error in grammar. The plural subject “people” does not agree with the singular verb “is.”


Furthermore, this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that people “is” a number.


> <b>(D)</b> Surprising to many is that, while <b>Manhattan’s population density is still high, the people living there have been lowered from what they were in
</b> 1910.



This version is grammatically correct. Notice what it says though. It says that the people living in Manhattan “have been lowered from what they were in 1910.”


So, this version seems to convey that some outside force somehow “lowered” the people living in Manhattan “from what they were.” Since, of course, nothing has lowered the people living in Manhattan, and since lowering people from “what they were” does not make sense at all, this version is nonsensical.


> <b>(E)</b> Surprising to many is that, while <b>Manhattan still has a high population density, the people living there are lower than the number were in
</b> 1910.



This version has an error in grammar. The singular subject “the number” does not agree with the plural verb “were.”


Furthermore, this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that people are lower than a number.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

{{< /alert >}}
</details></h5>

##  Sentence Correction Example 11  

In the early 1900s, in order to travel from place to place, <u>freight trains would be hopped onto by tramps even though entering freight cars without authorization is now extremely difficult</u>.

1. [ ] (A) freight trains would be hopped onto by tramps even though entering freight cars without authorization is now extremely difficult
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) tramps would hop onto freight trains, though it is now extremely difficult to enter freight cars without authorization
3. [ ] (C) hopping onto freight trains would be done though it is now extremely difficult to enter freight cars without authorization by tramps
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) tramps, hopping onto freight trains, though entering freight cars without authorization being extremely difficult now
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) there were tramps, and they hopped onto freight trains even though now it is extremely difficult to enter freight cars without authorization
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Since each choice in this question is unique, this question has no splits that we can use to identify decision points. So, in order to find the correct answer to this question, we have to carefully analyze each version on its own to determine whether it is grammatically correct and effectively conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(A)</b> In the early 1900s, in order to travel from place to place, <b>freight trains would be hopped onto by tramps even though entering freight cars without authorization is now extremely difficult
</b>.



This version is grammatically correct. Notice what it conveys though. For reasons that will be discussed in a later chapter, since “in order to travel from place to place” is directly followed by “freight trains would be hopped onto by tramps,” this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that trains traveled by being hopped onto by tramps.


As if that meaning were not ridiculous enough, this version goes on to say that, in the early 1900s, “trains would be hopped onto by tramps even though entering freight cars without authorization is now extremely difficult.”


Since “even though” means “despite the fact that,” this version conveys the illogical meaning that, in the early 1900s, trains were hopped onto by tramps despite the fact that entering freight cars is difficult now, as if the fact that entering freight cars is difficult now would have somehow affected tramps hopping onto freight trains in the past.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B)</b> In the early 1900s, in order to travel from place to place, <b>tramps would hop onto freight trains, though it is now extremely difficult to enter freight cars without authorization
</b>.



This version is grammatically correct.


Furthermore, since “in order to travel from place to place” is followed by “tramps would hop onto freight trains,” this version conveys the logical meaning that tramps traveled by hopping onto trains.


Since this version is grammatically correct and conveys a meaning that makes sense, this choice is the correct answer.


> <b>(C)</b> In the early 1900s, in order to travel from place to place, <b>hopping onto freight trains would be done though it is now extremely difficult to enter freight cars without authorization by tramps
</b>.



This version is grammatically correct. Notice what it conveys though. For reasons that will be discussed in a later chapter, since “in order to travel from place to place” is directly followed by “hopping onto freight trains would be done,” this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that hopping onto freight trains traveled.


As if that meaning were not ridiculous enough, since “by tramps” does not come right after “hopping” and instead appears all the way at the end of this version, right after “authorization,” this version seems to convey that entering a freight car now requires authorization by tramps.


> <b>(D)</b> In the early 1900s, in order to travel from place to place, <b>tramps, hopping onto freight trains, though entering freight cars without authorization being extremely difficult now
</b>.



Notice what we get when we combine this answer choice with the non-underlined portion.


in order to travel from place to place, tramps, hopping onto freight trains,


We can see that that thought is not complete: there’s no verb that says what “tramps, hopping onto freight trains” do.


So, this version can be eliminated because it is not a complete sentence.


> <b>(E)</b> In the early 1900s, in order to travel from place to place, <b>there were tramps, and they hopped onto freight trains even though now it is extremely difficult to enter freight cars without authorization
</b>.



This version is grammatically correct. Notice what it says though: “in order to travel from place to place, there were tramps.” What exactly that statement means is not clear. Who or what was traveling? What did tramps have to do with traveling? Read literally, this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that tramps existed so that someone or something unidentified could travel.


As if that meaning were not ridiculous enough, this version goes on to say that, in the early 1900s, tramps “hopped onto freight trains even though now it is extremely difficult to enter freight cars.”


Since “even though” means “despite the fact that,” this version conveys the illogical meaning that, in the early 1900s, tramps hopped onto trains despite the fact that entering freight cars is difficult now, as if the fact that entering freight cars is difficult now would have somehow affected tramps hopping onto freight trains in the past.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

We have seen that sometimes splits can be useful to us, sometimes splits are present to distract us, and sometimes there are no splits in the answer choices.

When there are splits, there’s a good possibility that the splits are associated with issues in the sentence versions.

At the same time, regardless of whether there are splits in a question, we must carefully evaluate each sentence version holistically to determine which choice is the best.

Let’s now discuss using strong decision points in choosing among Sentence Correction answer choices.
