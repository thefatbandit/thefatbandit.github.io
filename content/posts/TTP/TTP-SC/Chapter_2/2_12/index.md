## Lesson Content
A complex sentence is composed of one independent clause and at least one subordinate clause (SC).

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A complex sentence is composed of one independent clause and at least one subordinate clause.
{{< /alert >}}

The following are examples of complex sentences:

>                      SC                                              IC

> Because Lucy loves wine, I brought her a pinot noir from Burgundy.

>                      IC                                                        SC

> I brought Lucy a pinot noir from Burgundy because she loves wine.

>                      SC                                                        IC

> Although Sebastian loved the flying dog, he loved the princess more.

Notice that each of these sentences contains at least one subordinate clause and an independent clause.

It is important to understand that a subordinate clause actually plays a specific modifying role in a sentence.

> I left yesterday.

In this sentence, it should be clear that “yesterday” tells us when the action “left” took place.

> I left <strong>after eating</strong>.

> I left <strong>after we finished eating</strong>.

Notice that the subordinate clause in the above version is an adverb clause; it expresses when “I left.” In other words, this subordinate clause functions as an adverb by telling us when the action took place.

The natural place for an adverb to appear is somewhere after the verb.

> I hit a home run <strong>on the third pitch</strong>.

> I jumped up and down <strong>while you were trying to study. </strong>

However, in English we have the flexibility to move things around; we just have to let the reader know when something is out of its normal place.

Consider the following sentences:

> On the third pitch, I hit a home run.

> I, on the third pitch, hit a home run.

Notice that a complex sentence can take three different forms.

> I jostled you while you were sleeping.

> While you were sleeping, I jostled you.

> I, while you were sleeping, jostled you.

It’s important to understand that all three of these sentences are perfectly grammatical.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When the subordinate clause is out of place in a sentence, a comma or commas are required to indicate the departure from the expected sentence arrangement.
{{< /alert >}}

As we have seen, when a subordinate clause precedes an independent clause, the subordinate clause should be followed by a comma.

Consider the following sentences:

>                     SC                                        IC

> <u>Because I love the ocean,</u> <u>I spent the summer in Portofino.</u> (mandatory comma)

Notice that, when the subordinate clause comes first, the comma between the subordinate clause and the independent clause is mandatory.

However, both of the following sentences are correct:

> I spent the summer in Portofino, because I love the ocean.

> I spent the summer in Portofino because I love the ocean.

We see that, when a subordinate clause comes after an independent clause, the comma between the two clauses is optional.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When a subordinate precedes an independent clause, the subordinate clause must be followed by a comma. When a subordinate clause comes after an independent clause, the comma between the two clauses is optional.
{{< /alert >}}

The test-writers can use complex sentences to create false splits.

> Because I wanted to work out, I went to the gym.

> I went to the gym, because I wanted to work out.

> I went to the gym because I wanted to work out.

Actually, as you know from what we just learned, all of these sentences are correct! Remember when a subordinate clause begins a complex sentence, it must be followed by a comma.

Be careful to avoid the following incorrect variation:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: <u>Although I was quite hungry</u><strong>, so</strong> <u>I chose something more nutritious</u>.

Here, a coordinating conjunction is incorrectly joining a subordinate clause to an independent clause.

Notice that the conjunction “although” already connects the two clauses.
