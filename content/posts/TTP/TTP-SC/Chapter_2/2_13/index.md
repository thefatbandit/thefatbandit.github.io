## Lesson Content
A compound-complex sentence is a sentence with more than one independent clause and at least one subordinate clause.For example:

>                         SC                                   IC                                       IC

> Because we went to the beach, I ran in the soft sand, <strong>so</strong> I could build my endurance.

This example features one subordinate clause followed by two independent clauses, making this a compound-complex sentence.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A compound-complex sentence is a sentence with more than one independent clause and at least one subordinate clause.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at a few more examples:

>                       SC                                   IC                                      IC

> While you were at the beach, I ran a few errands<strong>,</strong> <strong>and</strong> I bought groceries for dinner.

This example features one subordinate clause followed by two independent clauses and, thus, is a compound-complex sentence.

When we join two independent clauses within a compound-complex sentence, the same rules apply as do in a compound sentence: we must link the independent clauses either with a comma and a coordinating conjunction or with a semicolon.

>                      SC                                                    IC                                                         IC

> Although I was quite hungry<strong>,</strong> I decided to wait until I got home to eat<strong>, but</strong> all the apples had been eaten.

Note that, in this example, the opening subordinate clause is followed by a comma, which alerts the reader that the independent clause is starting.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When joining two independent clauses within a compound-complex sentence, we must link the independent clauses either with a comma and a coordinating conjunction or with a semicolon.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s consider another compound-complex sentence.

>                      SC                                                              IC                                            IC

> Because Sara loves to read, she often spends her afternoons at the bookstore, or she curls up in her favorite reading chair at home.

Note that this example begins with a subordinate clause followed by a comma.

Whenever a subordinating conjunction immediately follows a coordinating conjunction, there must be at least three clauses in the sentence.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Whenever a subordinating conjunction immediately follows a coordinating conjunction, there must be at least three clauses in the sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example:

> I wanted to go scuba diving, <strong>but because</strong> I was tired from our long run, I read a book instead.

Notice that we have a subordinating conjunction “because” immediately after a coordinating conjunction “but”.

Since the subordinate clause “because I was tired from our long run” correctly modifies the second independent clause, “I read a book instead,” it is properly placed and properly punctuated, with a comma before the coordinating conjunction and a comma at the end of the subordinate clause.

Be on the lookout for incorrectly constructed sentences.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: I wanted to go scuba diving, but because I was tired from our long run.

This sentence incorrectly drops the third clause, which would be modified by the subordinate clause.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When a subordinating conjunction follows a coordinating conjunction in a compound-complex sentence, make sure all necessary clauses appear in the sentence.
{{< /alert >}}
