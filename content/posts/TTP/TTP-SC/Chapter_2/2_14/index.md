## Lesson Content
Earlier in the chapter, we said that there are three types of dependent clauses: subordinate clauses, relative clauses, and noun clauses.

Relative clauses begin with relative pronouns.

> that, which, who, whom, whose, where, when

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Memorize the relative pronouns:
{{< /alert >}}

A relative clause is a type of dependent clause, and as such, a relative clause is unable to stand alone as a sentence.

Relative clauses always appear embedded within other clauses.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Relative clauses always appear embedded within other clauses. They are unable to stand alone as sentences.
{{< /alert >}}

Like a subordinate clause, a relative clause begins with a word that takes away the clause’s independence: a relative pronoun.

Consider the following sentence:

> The baboon had an angry look about it.

Of course, the above simple sentence is composed of an independent clause containing a subject, “baboon,” a finite verb, “had,” and no word to take away the clause’s independence.

At the same time, an English sentence is like a modular couch: another section can always be added.

> The baboon <strong>that grasped its baby</strong> had an angry look about it.

Having more to say about the baboon, we have added a relative clause.

Notice that the relative clause begins with a relative pronoun, “that.”

As we have discussed, every clause must have its own subject (and a finite verb, which answers the question “When did the action occur?”).

> My boat, <strong>which has an electric motor</strong>, is faster than most boats with gasoline engines.

The subject of the relative clause is “which.” The finite verb is “has.”

> The man <strong>who solved the problem</strong> is smiling happily.

The subject of the relative clause is “who.” The finite verb is “solved.”

> The star <strong>whose supply of hydrogen is running out</strong> will soon collapse.

The subject of the relative clause is “supply of hydrogen.” The finite verb is “is.”

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Every relative clause must have a subject and a finite verb.
{{< /alert >}}

In some relative clauses, the relative pronoun is the subject of the relative clause.

<u>When the Relative Pronoun is the Subject of the Relative Clause</u>

Let’s again consider our example:

> The baboon <strong>that grasped its baby</strong> had an angry look about it.

In this example, the relative pronoun “that,” like a personal pronoun, stands for a noun already mentioned, “the baboon.”

The relative clause “that grasped its baby” contains a finite verb in the past tense: “grasped.”

The finite verb has a subject, but the grammatical subject of the relative clause is not “baboon.” The boundaries of the relative clause are “that” and “baby.” So, the subject must be found within those boundaries.

When the relative pronoun is the subject of the relative clause, it may not be omitted from the clause.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When the relative pronoun is the subject of the relative clause, it may not be omitted from the clause.
{{< /alert >}}

Notice how we create an ungrammatical, and illogical, structure if we remove the relative pronoun “that”:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The baboon <strong>grasped its baby</strong> had an angry look about it.

Baboon is the subject of the independent clause.

Let’s consider another example.

> John lives on a beach <strong>that becomes populated with sea turtles between May and November</strong>.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: John lives on a beach <strong>becomes populated with sea turtles between May and November</strong>.

Have a look at each of the following sentences in which the relative clauses are bolded.

> I lost the sapphire <strong>that was given to me.</strong>

“That” is the subject of the relative clause; “was” is the finite verb

> The man <strong>who lives next to me</strong> plays the guitar.

“Who” is the subject of the relative clause; “lives” is the finite verb.

> Target Test Prep, <strong>which provides awesome GMAT prep</strong>, helped me to score 770.

“Which” is the subject of the relative clause; “provides” is the finite verb.

<u>When the Relative Pronoun is Not the Subject of the Relative Clause</u>

In other cases, the relative pronoun is not the subject of the relative clause.

When the relative pronoun is not the subject of the clause, the relative pronoun can be dropped.

Consider the following example:

> The baboon <strong>that Irving was feeding</strong> had an angry look about it.

Notice that, in this case, the relative pronoun “that” is not the subject of the relative clause; “Irving” is.

> The baboon <strong>Irving was feeding</strong> had an angry look about it.

> The baboon <strong>that Irving was feeding</strong> had an angry look about it.

If the GMAT were to present us with two sentence versions like the above two, the difference between the relative clauses would be only something to distract us from seeing the real issues elsewhere in the sentence versions.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When a relative pronoun is not the subject of the relative clause, it can be omitted from the sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

Which of the following two sentences is correct?

> Valerio is at the coffee shop <strong>that he mentioned yesterday</strong>.

> Valerio is at the coffee shop <strong>he mentioned yesterday</strong>.

Both sentences are correct as written.

Notice that both of the following sentences are correct because “that” is not the subject of the relative clause.

> These are the books <strong>that the librarian has marked to be donated to the local school.</strong>

> These are the books <strong>the librarian has marked to be donated to the local school.</strong>

So then the structure of a relative clause is simple:

relative clause = [relative pronoun acting as the subject] + [finite verb]

> or

relative clause = [relative pronoun not acting as the subject] + [subject] + [finite verb]

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

relative clause = [relative pronoun acting as the subject] + [finite verb]
{{< /alert >}}
