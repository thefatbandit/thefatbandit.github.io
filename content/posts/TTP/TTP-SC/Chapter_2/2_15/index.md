## Lesson Content
Remember that a relative clause is a dependent clause, and therefore it cannot stand alone as a sentence.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A relative clause is a dependent clause, and therefore it cannot stand alone as a sentence. In other words, a relative clause must be combined with an independent clause if we are to have a sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

However, in Sentence Correction questions, we’ll often encounter sentence versions that are just relative clauses attached to nouns.

Consider the following well-constructed sentence.

> <u>Correct</u>: Max Planck is considered one of the most important figures in modern physics.

Notice what happens when we add a relative pronoun immediately after the subject:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Max Planck, who is considered one of the most important figures in modern physics.

We now have a fragment, because there is no longer an independent clause or a main verb.

In fact, the longer and the more complex the fragment, the more apt we are to accept it as a sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Max Planck, who is considered one of the most important figures in modern physics because of his work in the field of quantum theory, which earned him the Nobel Prize in physics in 1918.

If we’re not looking carefully, we may accept that fragment as a sentence.

One simple way to turn that fragment into a sentence is to remove the relative pronoun “who.”

> Max Planck <strong>is</strong> considered one of the most important figures in modern physics because of his work in the field of quantum theory, which earned him the Nobel Prize in physics in 1918.

Now we have a sentence.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Max Planck, <s>who is considered one of the most important figures in modern physics because of his work in the field of quantum theory, which earned him the Nobel Prize in physics in 1918</s>.

Notice how easy it now is to see that that structure lacks an independent clause.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A good strategy that we can use to determine whether we actually have a sentence (or just a fragment) is to cross off any relative clauses.
{{< /alert >}}

In the previous example, we used the relative pronoun “who,” but any of the relative pronouns can be used to create fragments.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The polymerase chain reaction, <strong>which</strong> allows scientists to quickly make tens of millions of copies of a specific sample of DNA and has aided in the discovery of many new diagnostic tests, vaccines, and medical therapeutics.

Notice that all we have is a noun, “polymerase chain reaction,” followed a long relative clause, “which … therapeutics.” The structure therefore lacks a main verb; there is no independent clause.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The polymerase chain reaction, <strong><s>which</s></strong><s> allows scientists to quickly make tens of millions of copies of a specific sample of DNA and has aided in the discovery of many new diagnostic tests, vaccines, and medical therapeutics</s>.

We could fix the fragment by removing the relative pronoun (“which”):

> The polymerase chain reaction <strong>allows</strong> scientists to quickly make tens of millions of copies of a specific sample of DNA and has aided in the discovery of many new diagnostic tests, vaccines, and medical therapeutics.

Let’s look at another example sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Gettysburg, where, between July 1, 1863 and July 3, 1863, the Union army under the command of General George Meade engaged the Confederate army under the command of General Robert E.

Notice that the noun “Gettysburg” is followed by a long relative clause, “where … North.” The structure therefore lacks a main verb; there is no independent clause.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Gettysburg, <s>where, between July 1, 1863 and July 3, 1863, the Union army under the command of General George Meade engaged the Confederate army under the command of General Robert E. Lee, stopping the second, and most ambitious, push by the Confederate army into the North.</s>

We could fix the fragment by adding a finite verb:

> Gettysburg <strong>is</strong> where, between July 1, 1863 and July 3, 1863, the Union army under the command of General George Meade engaged the Confederate army under the command of General Robert E.

Let’s consider one more example sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Found in the latest model, the autopilot feature that autonomously performs almost all of the tasks that a driver would need to perform and that uses forward-looking radar and eight cameras, in addition to 360-degree sonar, which detects obstacles in an eight-meter radius around the car.

Notice that this structure lacks a main verb.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Found in the latest model, the autopilot feature <s>that autonomously performs almost all of the tasks that a driver would need to perform and that uses forward-looking radar and eight cameras, in addition to 360-degree sonar, which detects obstacles in an eight-meter radius around the car.</s>

We could fix the fragment by adding a finite verb:

> Found in the latest model, the autopilot <strong>is</strong> a feature that autonomously performs almost all of the tasks that a driver would need to perform and that uses forward-looking radar and eight cameras, in addition to 360-degree sonar, which detects obstacles in an eight-meter radius around the car.
