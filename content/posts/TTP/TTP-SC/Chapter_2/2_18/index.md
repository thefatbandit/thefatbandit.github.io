## Lesson Content
We already know that a noun clause can serve as the subject of a sentence.

For example:

> Dayo picked <strong>apples</strong>.

The noun “apples” is the direct object.

For example:

> The deer ate <strong>the green grass</strong>.

The noun phrase “the green grass” is the direct object.

Direct objects don’t have to be single nouns though.

> I knew <strong>why the deer was eating my flowers. </strong>

> I felt <strong>that it was time to go swimming. </strong>

> Rin Tin Tin wondered <strong>whether the grass was greener on the other side. </strong>

> The court mandated <strong>that deer be allowed to swim and eat green grass.</strong>

> Penelope has yet to determine <strong>how green grass tastes.</strong>

Notice that, in noun clauses, a word such as “that,” “which,” or “where” that introduces the clause does <em>not</em> refer to a noun.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In a noun clause, a word such as “that,” “which,” or “where” that introduces the clause does not relate the clause back to a noun as the relative pronouns in relative clauses do.
{{< /alert >}}

In noun clauses that serve as direct objects, the word “that” is often dropped:

> I knew <strong>that dinner would be served soon</strong>.

> I knew <strong>dinner would be served soon</strong>.

The above two sentences are equally acceptable, and in any Sentence Correction question with such a difference between sentence versions, the difference can be ignored.
