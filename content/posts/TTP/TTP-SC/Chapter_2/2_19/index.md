## Lesson Content
A noun clause can also serve as the object of a preposition.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: I was unhappy about.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The bill was limited by.

Those sets of words do not express complete thoughts.

For example, a preposition can be followed by a single noun:

> I was unhappy about <strong>the weather</strong>.

> I was unhappy about <strong>the oranges</strong>.

> I was unhappy about <strong>market conditions</strong>.

> The bill was limited by <strong>greed</strong>.

> The bill was limited by <strong>time</strong>.

> The bill was limited by <strong>money</strong>.

However, a preposition can also be followed by a noun clause.

> I was unhappy about <strong>what the auditors found</strong>.

> I was unhappy about<strong> how the matter was handled</strong>.

> I was unhappy about <strong>what was going on in El Salvador</strong>.

> The bill was limited by <strong>what the super PACs were willing to accept</strong>.

> The bill was limited by <strong>how it had been written</strong>.

> The bill was limited by <strong>what was available to spend</strong>.

Notice that, in each of these example sentences, we have a noun clause acting as the object of a preposition.
