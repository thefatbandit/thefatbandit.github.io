## Lesson Content
In language, we put words together to form two elementary types of structures: phrases and clauses.

A phrase is simply a collection of words that cannot stand alone as a sentence because there is no subject-verb relationship.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A phrase is a collection of words without a subject-verb relationship. A phrase can never stand alone as a sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

The following are examples of phrases:

Notice that none of these phrases can stand alone as a sentence.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: For the national good.

Notice also that none of the phrases has a sense of a subject performing an action.

To form any sentence in English one must have a clause.

In order for a string of words to qualify as a clause, it must have a subject-verb relationship.

For example:

> Tom ate his lunch.

> Vivek is the happiest guy in town.

> that the fog rolled in

> Because the volcano was spurting

> Although you love eating pistachios

> whichever path you choose

Notice that, in each of the above strings of words, there is a subject doing something or being something.

This subject-verb relationship is a good start.

> Tom to eat his lunch

> Because the volcano spurting

> that the fog rolling in

> Although you eating pistachios

> Vivek to be the happiest guy in town

Likely you can immediately tell that the above strings of words are not clauses.

None of the above utterances have a verb that indicates when the actions they mention took place.

When did any of the actions mentioned by these collections of words occur? We don’t know, right? For example, when did Tom “to eat” his lunch? Past? Present? Future? Who knows! There is not a verb that answers the question “When did the action occur?”

So, the reason why the above collections of words are not clauses is that they don’t have verbs that indicate when actions took place.

We have a name for a verb form that answers that question in a clause: “finite verb.”

A finite verb is a verb that answers the question “When did the action occur?”

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A finite verb is a verb that answers the question “When did the action occur?”
{{< /alert >}}

Looking back, notice that each of those five phrases above lacks a finite verb.

> Tom <strong>ate</strong> his lunch.

> Tom <strong>had</strong> eaten his lunch.

> Because the volcano <strong>is</strong> spurting

> Because the volcano <strong>was</strong> spurting

> that the fog <strong>will</strong> roll in

> that the fog <strong>can</strong> roll in

> Although you <strong>would</strong> eat pistachios

> Although you <strong>do</strong> eat pistachios

So then, simply put, a clause is not just any set of words that contains both a noun and a verb but rather a set of words that contains a subject and, specifically, a finite verb.

> <u>clause</u> = [subject] + [finite verb]

The clause may contain many other things, but it must have those two elements.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A clause has both a subject and a finite verb. A phrase lacks a subject, a finite verb, or both.
{{< /alert >}}

In order to determine whether a group of words is a phrase or a clause, check for the presence of a subject and a finite verb.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In order to determine whether a group of words is a phrase or a clause, check for a subject and a finite verb. If both are present, we have a clause. If either is missing, we have a phrase.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following set of words:

> the weather is pleasant

Notice that we have a subject “weather” and a finite verb “is,” a verb that indicates that the event mentioned is occurring in the present.

Consider the following examples of clauses.

> because <strong>you loved</strong> me

> <strong>Sara is</strong> at the beach

> although <strong>you are</strong> going skiing next weekend

> that the <strong>cats had been</strong> jumping up and down on the bed

> <strong>Estella wears</strong> a bright pink scarf

> Because the <strong>snow has</strong> freshly fallen

At this point, you know that a clause contains a subject and a finite verb.

> the boy <strong>to run</strong> down the hill

> all the pickles <strong>eaten</strong> by the children

> the students <strong>looking</strong> to better their futures

It’s important to see that the verb forms in these examples are not sufficient for creating clauses.

Why are we belaboring this point about the finite verb? Because most, if not all students, when asked what constitutes a clause (or a sentence for that matter) will answer “a subject and a verb.” However, it’s crucial to understand that it’s not just any verb that makes a clause (or a sentence); it’s a finite verb.

> My parents <strong>gave</strong> me a car for graduation.

> I <strong>will</strong> finish my homework tomorrow.

> Esterhazy <strong>was</strong> drinking from your cup!

> Sonia <strong>would</strong> have been leading if not for her sloppy habits.

> That approach <strong>is</strong> sure to work.

> I <strong>have</strong> eaten in the cafeteria.

> Zala <strong>had</strong> contradicted everything.

> They <strong>are</strong> eating all the avocados.

> They <strong>were</strong> eating all the avocados.

> The entire team <strong>would </strong>have gone.

> I <strong>can </strong>explain myself.

> Algernon <strong>must </strong>be older than I.

We now understand the difference between a phrase and a clause.
