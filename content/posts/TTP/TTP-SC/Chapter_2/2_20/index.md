## Lesson Content
The proper use of relative clauses and noun clauses is important in GMAT Sentence Correction.

Since relative clauses and noun clauses look similar, when we see a clause that begins with a word such as “that,” “which,” “who,” or “where,” we need to decide whether it is a relative clause or a noun clause.

It will help to remember that a relative clause must follow the noun that the clause is modifying.

Consider the sentence you read a moment ago:

> It will help to remember <u>that a relative clause must follow the noun that the clause is modifying</u>.

The underlined clause begins with “that” and is not following a noun.

> A clause <u>that is not following a noun</u> cannot be a relative clause.

You should also be able to recognize the clause underlined above, which again begins with “that.” This clause is following a noun, the word “clause.” The meaning of the sentence indicates that the relative pronoun “that” stands for the word “clause” and relates the information in the clause to “clause.” Therefore, this underlined clause must be a relative clause.

Figuring out which of the two types of clause you have does not have to be complicated.

A clause that follows a noun and modifies that noun is a relative clause.

A clause that acts as a noun is a noun clause.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

It is important to recognize and to distinguish between relative clauses and noun clauses.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s consider some more examples:

> The elephants <strong>that live in the area</strong> generally avoid humans.

The clause “that live in the area” follows the noun “elephants” and modifies “elephants.” So, it is a relative clause.

> Drawings of the old train station, <strong>which was located on South Street</strong>, can be seen in the lobby of Town Hall.

The clause “which was located on South Street” follows the noun “station” and modifies “station.” So, it is a relative clause.

<strong>That Shaniqua had won the election</strong> surprised nobody.

The clause “that Shaniqua had won the election” does not follow a noun and serves as the subject of the sentence.

> Eddie sat there and wondered <strong>which came first</strong>, the chicken or the egg.

The clause “which came first” does not follow a noun and serves as the direct object of “wondered.” So, it must be a noun clause.
