## Lesson Content
As we know, a semicolon can be used between two independent clauses.

Consider the following example:

> When Monica is in Europe, she’ll visit London, England; Paris, France; Rome, Italy; and Vienna, Austria.

Notice that the sentence uses semicolons to more clearly separate items in a list that could be confusing if commas were used instead.

Imagine the sentence without the semicolons:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: When Monica is in Europe, she’ll visit London, England, Paris, France, Rome, Italy, and Vienna, Austria.

Yikes! What’s going on there? Is Monica going to visit London, England or both London and England? Is she going to visit Paris, France or both Paris and France?

We can see that what the list is meant to convey is much clearer when semicolons are used to separate the items than when only commas are used.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Semicolons can be used to separate items in a list when using commas would make the list confusing.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at another example:

> At the dinner will be Fidel Castro, the Prime Minister of Cuba; John F.

We can see that use of semicolons keeps the list from being confusing.

Imagine the sentence without the semicolons:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: At the dinner will be Fidel Castro, the Prime Minister of Cuba, John F.

Again, yikes! What’s going on there?

Let’s look at one more example:

> I need to purchase a dry-erase board, a notebook, and a calculator to study for math; an iPad for English; and a leather-bound journal for history.

Notice how semicolons are used to make the list clear when the list would be unclear without the semicolons:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: I need to purchase a dry-erase board, a notebook, and a calculator to study for math, an iPad for English, and a leather-bound journal for history.

Who knows what the sentence is trying to say.
