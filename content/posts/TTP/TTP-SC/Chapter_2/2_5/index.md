## Lesson Content
Consider one of our last examples of an independent clause:

> The crowd laughed.

The clause has a subject and a finite verb - all that is needed, in this instance, for a complete sentence.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Because the crowd laughed.

Although you may not know exactly what is missing here, it’s almost certainly clear to you that we don’t have a sentence.

“Because” is a member of a class of words called subordinating conjunctions, words that can take away the independence of a clause when placed at the head of the clause.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When a subordinating conjunction is placed at the head of a clause, that clause becomes a subordinate clause, a type of dependent clause.
{{< /alert >}}

The following are examples of subordinate clauses headed by subordinating conjunctions:

> <strong>since</strong> John was at the beach

> <strong>although</strong> Scott is typing fast

> <strong>whereas </strong>Douglas may complain about the strict rules

> <strong>because</strong> you and I are best friends

> <strong>unless</strong> she arrives at the airport by midnight

> <strong>once</strong> the mountain lion puts its cubs to bed

> <strong>if</strong> you want to become a pilot

Two things are important to notice.

Now, we need to be able to recognize subordinate conjunctions when we see them.

> after, before, until, once, while, although, whereas, because, if, unless, though, even though, as much as, just as, whenever

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

If a clause begins with a subordinating conjunction, it is a subordinate clause.
{{< /alert >}}

We can visualize the structure of a subordinate clause as follows:

> subordinate clause = [subordinating conjunction] + [subject] + [finite verb]

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

subordinate clause = [subordinating conjunction] + [subject] + [finite verb]
{{< /alert >}}
