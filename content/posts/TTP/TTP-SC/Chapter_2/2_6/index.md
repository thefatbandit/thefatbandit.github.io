## Lesson Content
In the simplest sense, a sentence is a collection of words that contains at least one independent clause.

Let’s continue by talking about subjects.

Most of you are familiar with the concept of the subject of a sentence.

For example:

> <strong>Whales</strong> are highly intelligent animals.

> The blue<strong> ball</strong> was rolling down the hill.

> <strong>Doug</strong> will ask Martha to come to his house for dinner.

In the sentences above, “whales,” “ball,” and “Doug” are the subjects performing the actions.

<u>Incorrect</u>: are highly intelligent animals.

<u>Incorrect</u>: was rolling down the hill.

<u>Incorrect</u>: will ask Martha to come to his house for dinner.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The subject of a sentence is the person or thing performing the action in the sentence. Without at least one subject, there can be no sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

In addition to needing at least one subject, because every sentence must contain at least one independent clause, every sentence must contain at least one main verb.

> Whales <strong>are</strong> highly intelligent animals.

> The blue ball <strong>was </strong>rolling down the hill.

> Doug <strong>will </strong>ask Martha to come to his house for dinner.

In the sentences above, “are,” “was,” and “will” are the main verbs.

Without at least one subject and at least one main verb, we don’t have a sentence.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Every English sentence must contain at least one main verb.
{{< /alert >}}

Notice that, if we remove the main verbs from the sentences above, we no longer have sentences:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Whales highly intelligent animals.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The blue ball rolling down the hill.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Doug ask Martha to come to his house for dinner.

Some of you are questioning the second example, saying, “Wait a minute – ‘rolling’ is a verb.”

Yes, technically, “rolling” is a verb (it’s a present participle, and we will talk more about present participles later), but present participles like “rolling” can’t serve as the main verb in a sentence.

In fact, we don’t need to have ever heard of the term present participle to see that “rolling” can’t be a main verb because it does not tell us when the action occurred; it is not a finite verb.

When we don’t have at least one subject and at least one main verb in a sentence, we have a sentence fragment.

Any answer choice that produces a sentence fragment will not be a correct answer on the GMAT.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When we lack either a subject or a main verb in a sentence, we have a sentence fragment. Any answer choice that produces a sentence fragment will not be a correct answer on the GMAT.
{{< /alert >}}

This concept seems simple enough, but if we’re not careful, we can run into trouble in answering Sentence Correction questions.

Consider the following:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: A love story serving as a source of deep insights into human relations.

Notice that this sentence fragment almost seems to be a sentence, but when we look closely, we see that the structure lacks a main verb.

Notice that the following versions, however, are sentences, because they do contain main verbs:

> A love story serving as a source of deep insights into human relations <strong>is</strong> being written by Martha.

The finite verb “is” tells us that the action is occurring now and serves as the main verb in the sentence.

> A love story serving as a source of deep insights into human relations <strong>was</strong> being written by Martha.

The finite verb “was” tells us that the action occurred in the past, and serves as the main verb in the sentence.

> A love story serving as a source of deep insights into human relations <strong>will</strong> be written by Martha.

The finite verb “will” tells us that the action will occur in the future and serves as the main verb in the sentence.

Let’s look at a couple more sentence fragments:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Parents driving to the grocery store with plenty of gas in the tank.

Imagine that someone spoke those words to you.

We appear to have the verb “driving” and the subject “parents.” However, let’s try to answer our question, “When did the action take place?” In the past? In the present? In the future?

In taking a closer look at the sentence fragment, we see that the present participle “driving” does not provide enough information to answer the question “When?”

If the action were currently taking place, a complete sentence would convey that “Parents <strong>are</strong> driving to the grocery store ….” If the action had already occurred, the sentence would tell us “Parents <strong>were</strong> driving” or perhaps “Parents <strong>drove.</strong>” We don’t get such information from the participle “driving,” and thus we have a fragment, rather than a complete sentence.

Here’s another fragment:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: With donations to the climate-change charity from numerous benefactors at the gala, including local businesses, national corporations, community organizations, and private families.

The fragment above provides a lot of information, saying so much that we may think we have a complete sentence.

So, when answering Sentence Correction questions, we must be sure to choose answers that produce versions with subjects and main verbs.
