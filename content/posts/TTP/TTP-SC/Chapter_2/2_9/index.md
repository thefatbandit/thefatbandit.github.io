## Lesson Content
A compound sentence is composed of at least two independent clauses.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A compound sentence is composed of at least two independent clauses.
{{< /alert >}}

We must connect these independent clauses in specific ways.

One way is to use what’s referred to as a coordinating conjunction.

There are seven coordinating conjunctions:

> for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so

We can use the mnemonic device “FANBOYS” to help us distinguish these conjunctions from other types of transition words.

> <strong>F</strong>or <strong>A</strong>nd <strong>N</strong>or <strong>B</strong>ut <strong>O</strong>r <strong>Y</strong>et <strong>S</strong>o

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The coordinating conjunctions (FANBOYS): For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So.
{{< /alert >}}

When independent clauses are connected by a coordinating conjunction, a comma must precede the conjunction.

Consider the following example sentences:

> I ran<strong>, and</strong> I fell.

> She jumped on the bed<strong>, and</strong> she screamed at the top of her lungs.

> I went to the store<strong>, yet</strong> I bought nothing.

> John went hiking<strong>, but</strong> Sara went to the beach.

> I wrote her the letter<strong>, but</strong> I chose not to mail it.

> Blaze and Molly will go to the park<strong>, or</strong> they’ll take a nap instead.

> The walrus and the seal were eating lunch<strong>, and</strong> they were also taking in some sun.

Notice that, in each of these example sentences, two independent clauses are connected by a comma and a coordinating conjunction.

So, we have an important rule for creating compound sentences: a compound sentence can be created by connecting two independent clauses using a comma and a coordinating conjunction.

In fact, we can create a structural formula for the compound sentence:

<strong>Compound Sentence</strong> = [independent clause] + [,] + [coordinating conjunction] + [independent clause] + [.]

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Compound Sentence = [independent clause] + [,] + [coordinating conjunction] + [independent clause] + [.]
{{< /alert >}}

It is important to note that the comma is <strong>not</strong> optional.

At some point, the test writers will likely tempt us to accept invalid ways of forming a compound sentence.

Consider two simple independent clauses: “I ran” and “I fell.” Would the following be correct?

> <u>Incorrect</u>: I ran I fell.

No – of course not.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Linking two independent clauses with no intermediary word or punctuation is always incorrect.
{{< /alert >}}

Now, our ear won’t help us in this situation because we can’t hear a period or its absence.

The following are all run-on sentences:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: I went to the gym I love to work out.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: She was on a long flight she is tired now.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The dog jumped over the fence he has so much energy.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: I went to the movies I ate a large bucket of popcorn.

What about the following? Would the following be correct?

> <u>Incorrect</u>: I ran, I fell.

No – this sentence also has a major error.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: I went swimming, the water felt amazing.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Peter runs track, he’s a great athlete.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Johanna loves eating mangoes, she can eat more than anyone I know.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Linking two independent clauses with only a comma is always incorrect. The error is known as a “comma splice.”
{{< /alert >}}

What about the following sentence? Is it correct?

> <u>Incorrect</u>: I ran and I fell.

Although this sentence includes the coordinating conjunction “and,” this sentence is also incorrectly written.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Linking two independent clauses with only a coordinating conjunction is always incorrect in a GMAT Sentence Correction question.
{{< /alert >}}

Each of the incorrect versions below is a run-on sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: I went to the gym and I lifted weights.

> <u>Correct</u>: I went to the gym<strong>, and</strong> I lifted weights.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: She was on a long flight yesterday so she is taking the day off today.

> <u>Correct</u>: She was on a long flight yesterday<strong>, so</strong> she is taking the day off today.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: I was not hungry but I ate a large bucket of popcorn.

> <u>Correct</u>: I was not hungry<strong>, but</strong> I ate a large bucket of popcorn.

As sentences grow more complicated (like the ones you’ll likely encounter in Sentence Correction questions), this small, but important, flaw becomes easy to miss.

Consider the following example sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Responding to critics, the committee refused to admit any culpability in the devastation caused by cost overruns and it specifically diverted blame to the change in bureaucratic oversight.

Can you spot the error? Let’s break down the sentence:

> Independent clause: “the committee refused to admit any culpability in the devastation caused by cost overruns”

> Independent clause: “it specifically diverted blame to the change in bureaucratic oversight.”

The grammatical error is the lack of a comma before the coordinating conjunction “and.” This point is not a minor one.

> Responding to critics, the committee refused to admit any culpability in the devastation caused by cost overruns<strong>, and</strong> it specifically diverted blame to the change in bureaucratic oversight

Sentence correction questions may also attempt to trick us into choosing as correct sentences that seem well-written but actually violate the rules we have discussed.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: I ran, consequently, I fell.

This sentence seems ok, but it’s a comma splice.

> <u>Correct</u>: I ran, and, consequently, I fell.

So, we can see that the absence of a comma before a coordinating conjunction forbids having an independent clause follow.

Make sure you fully understand the following structures:

> The majority of voters showed their displeasure, yet _____________.

> The majority of voters showed their displeasure, yet they still failed to cast dissenting votes.

> The majority of voters showed their displeasure yet<em> _____________.</em> (This forbids the filling of the blank with an independent clause because there is no comma before the coordinating conjunction “yet.”)

> The majority of voters showed their displeasure yet still failed to cast dissenting votes.

Your ear cannot help you in these contexts.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The absence of a comma before a coordinating conjunction forbids having an independent clause follow because the result would be a run-on sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Some of the versions created via the use of the choices in this question are blatantly incorrect because they lack main verbs. Others include main verbs but do not convey meanings that make sense.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>The career of James Marshall Hendrix, the singer, songwriter, and guitarist known to most as Jimi Hendrix, lasting only four years in mainstream American culture, but he is regarded
</b> as one of the greatest instrumentalists in Rock and Roll history.



 This version incorrectly uses a comma and the conjunction “but” to connect a long phrase to a clause.


Notice that “lasting” is not a finite verb, and thus there is no finite verb before “but.” So, everything that appears before “but” is just a long phrase.


We can see the issue more easily by simplifying the sentence:


The career of James Marshall Hendrix … lasting only four years …, but he is regarded ….


> <b>(B)</b> <b>James Marshall Hendrix’s career, the singer, songwriter, and guitarist known to most as Jimi Hendrix, lasting only four years in mainstream American culture but regarded
</b> as one of the greatest instrumentalists in Rock and Roll history.



Since “lasting” and “regarded” are not finite verbs, this version has no main verb.


Also, this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that Hendrix’s career, rather than Hendrix himself, was a singer, songwriter, and guitarist, and that his career was regarded as one of the greatest instrumentalists.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>James Marshall Hendrix, the singer, songwriter, and guitarist known to most as Jimi Hendrix, had a career lasting only four years in mainstream American culture, but it is regarded
</b> as one of the greatest instrumentalists in Rock and Roll history.



This version has finite verbs “had” and “is.” However, by reading this choice along with the non-underlined portion, we can see that this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that Hendrix’s career, rather than Hendrix himself, is regarded as one of the greatest instrumentalists.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>James Marshall Hendrix’s career, the singer, songwriter, and guitarist known to most as Jimi Hendrix, lasted only four years in mainstream American culture but is regarded
</b> as one of the greatest instrumentalists in Rock and Roll history.



This version has finite verbs “lasted” and “is.” However, this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that Hendrix’s career, rather than Hendrix himself, was a singer, songwriter, and guitarist, and that his career is regarded as one of the greatest instrumentalists.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E)</b> <b>James Marshall Hendrix, the singer, songwriter, and guitarist known to most as Jimi Hendrix, had a career that lasted only four years in mainstream American culture, but he is regarded
</b> as one of the greatest instrumentalists in Rock and Roll history.



This version correctly uses a comma and the conjunction “but” to connect two independent clauses.


The first clause has the subject “James Marshall Hendrix” and the main verb “had.” The second clause has the subject “he” and the main verb “is.”


This version is grammatically correct and conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
