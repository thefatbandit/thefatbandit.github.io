## Lesson Content
Leave it to the GMAT test-makers to turn sentences inside out.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In an inverted sentence, the main verb precedes the subject.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at some examples of inverted sentences.

The first version of the sentence below is the version that most of us would write; we tend to place the subject before its finite verb.

> <strong>Coyotes</strong>,medium-sized, dog-like animals,<strong> are </strong>found in many suburban areas.

> Found in many suburban areas <strong>are coyotes</strong>, medium-sized, dog-like animals.

Notice that, in the inverted version, the finite verb “are” precedes the subject “coyotes.” Although we are more comfortable reading and writing the first version, both sentences are correctly constructed.

Below is another set of examples in which the first version of the sentence is the version that most of us would write; we tend to place the subject before its finite verb.

> <strong>The captain of the team and the coach</strong> <strong>were</strong> able to clearly see what factors had contributed to the team’s loss only after seeing the highlights of the game on film.

> Only after seeing the highlights of the game on film <strong>were the captain of the team and the coach</strong> able to clearly see what factors had contributed to the team’s loss.

Notice that, in the inverted version, the finite verb “were” precedes the compound subject “the captain of the team and the coach.” Although we are more comfortable reading and writing the first version, the two sentences are equally effective.

Even in an inverted sentence, the subject and its finite verb must agree.

For example:

> Through those trees <strong>(lives/live) </strong>a creepy old man.

Which is correct? “Live” or “lives”?

Using our strategy of identifying the verb first and then determining who or what is performing the action, we can adeptly handle this challenge.

> Through those trees <strong>lives</strong> a creepy old <strong>man</strong>.

Let’s look at a more advanced example:

> Although there are many sites of interest to visit in the Scottish capital, Edinburgh, on top of the Castle Rock <strong>(sits/sit)</strong> possibly the most famous of them, the historic fortress that is also the city’s namesake.

There are many descriptive elements in the long, inverted sentence above, and the phrase “possibly the most famous of them” further obscures whether we need a singular or plural verb.

> <s>Although there are many sites of interest to visit in the Scottish capital, Edinburgh,</s> on top of the Castle Rock (sits/sit) <s>possibly the most famous of them, </s>the historic fortress<s> that is also the city’s namesake.</s>

The “historic fortress” is sitting on top of the Castle Rock.

> Although there are many sites of interest to visit in the Scottish capital, Edinburgh, on top of the Castle Rock <strong>sits</strong> possibly the most famous of them, the <strong>historic fortress</strong> that is also the city’s namesake.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In an inverted sentence, the verb precedes the subject, but the subject and verb must still agree.
{{< /alert >}}

Often, inverted sentences begin with the words “here” or “there,” as in the following example:

> <u>There <strong>(goes/go)</strong> the ladies from the church</u>, visiting the sick people in the hospital<em>. </em>

Using the same technique we employed for the previous example, we can identify the verb in the underlined clause and then determine who or what is performing the action.

> <u>There <strong>go</strong> <strong>the ladies</strong> from the church</u>, visiting the sick people in the hospital.

Let’s look at another example:

> Here <strong>(is/are)</strong> the agreement terms, which you should read through carefully before making a decision.

The word “agreement” is singular.

> The agreement terms <strong>are</strong> here …

Thus, the correct sentence should read:

> Here <strong>are</strong> the agreement <strong>terms</strong>, which you should read through carefully before making a decision.

##  Sentence Correction Example 9  

Used for mining copper ores located at shallow <u>depths is open pit mining, which</u> is less expensive than underground mining.

1. [x] (A) depths is open pit mining, which
2. [ ] (B) depths are open pit mining, this
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) depths, open pit mining, which
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) depths are open pit mining, which
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) depths, open pit mining that
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



This question involves a classic inverted sentence structure: the subject of the sentence, “open pit mining,” appears after, rather than before, the main verb.


We need an answer choice that correctly pairs a singular verb with the singular subject “open pit mining.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A)</b> Used for mining copper ores located at shallow <b>depths is open pit mining, which
</b> is less expensive than underground mining.



This version correctly pairs the singular verb “is … used” with the singular subject “open pit mining.”


The sentence contains no grammatical errors and effectively conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(B)</b> Used for mining copper ores located at shallow <b>depths are open pit mining, this
</b> is less expensive than underground mining.



This version incorrectly pairs the plural verb “are … used” with the singular subject “open pit mining.”


Notice that the sentence is inverted: “Used for mining copper ores located at shallow depths” describes the subject “open pit mining,” which appears after the main verb rather than before the main verb.


Thus, we need the main verb to agree, not with the plural noun “depths,” but with the singular “open pit mining,” which follows the verb.


Notice also that this version connects two clauses, “used … are opening pit mining” and “this is less expensive,” using only a comma. So, this version is an improperly constructed comma splice.


> <b>(C)</b> Used for mining copper ores located at shallow <b>depths, open pit mining, which
</b> is less expensive than underground mining.



This version does not contain a main verb and, thus, does not contain an independent clause. Therefore, this version is not a complete sentence.


Notice that, in this version, “used” in “<em>Used</em> for mining copper located at shallow depths” is not a finite verb. Rather the phrase “used for mining copper …” describes “open pit mining,” as we can more easily see by considering this simplified version:


“Used for mining …, open pit mining ….”


Additionally, “is” in “<em>which is</em> less expensive …” is part of a relative clause describing “open pit mining,” and thus “is” is not a main verb.


> <b>(D)</b> Used for mining copper ores located at shallow <b>depths are open pit mining, which
</b> is less expensive than underground mining.



This version incorrectly pairs the plural verb “are” with the singular subject “open pit mining.”


Notice that the sentence is inverted: “Used for mining copper ores located at shallow depths” describes the subject “open pit mining,” which appears after the main verb rather than before the main verb.


Thus, we need the main verb to agree, not with the plural noun “depths,” which immediately precedes the main verb, but with the singular “open pit mining,” which follows the verb.


> <b>(E)</b> Used for mining copper ores located at shallow <b>depths, open pit mining that
</b> is less expensive than underground mining.



 This version does not contain a main verb and, thus, does not contain an independent clause. Therefore, this version is not a complete sentence.


Notice that, in this version, “used” in “<em>Used</em> for mining copper located at shallow depths” is not a finite verb. Rather the phrase “used for mining copper …” describes “open pit mining,” as we can more easily see by considering this simplified version:


“Used for mining …, open pit mining ….”


Additionally, “is” in “<em>that is</em> less expensive …” is part of a relative clause describing “open pit mining,” and thus “is” is not a main verb.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

##  Sentence Correction Example 10  

So dangerous for ships <u>navigating the waters off the Outer Banks of North Carolina, the area that</u> became known as the Graveyard of the Atlantic.

1. [ ] (A) navigating the waters off the Outer Banks of North Carolina, the area that
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) was navigating the waters off the Outer Banks of North Carolina that the area
3. [ ] (C) were navigating the waters off the Outer Banks of North Carolina, and so, the area
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) that were navigating the waters off the Outer Banks of North Carolina that the area
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) that navigate the waters off the Outer Banks of North Carolina, an area that
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



Correctly answering this question takes noticing that the correct version is an inverted sentence and that the subject of the sentence is the singular “navigating.”


Since the subject is the singular “navigating, the main verb must also be singular.


> <b>(A)</b> So dangerous for ships <b>navigating the waters off the Outer Banks of North Carolina, the area that
</b> became known as the Graveyard of the Atlantic.



This version has no main verb. “Navigating” is not a finite verb, and “became” appears within the relative clause “that became known ….” So, this version is not a complete sentence.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B)</b> So dangerous for ships <b>was navigating the waters off the Outer Banks of North Carolina that the area
</b> became known as the Graveyard of the Atlantic.



In this version, we have the finite verb “was.”


The subject could not be “so dangerous for ships,” because it does not make sense to say that “so dangerous for ships” was navigating. In other words, “so dangerous for ships” would not navigate waters.


There is another possible subject for “was,” however. That possible subject is “navigating the waters,” which comes after, rather than before, “was.”


With “navigating the waters” as the subject, the sentence conveys that navigating the waters off the Outer Banks was so dangerous for ships that the area became known as the Graveyard of the Atlantic. So, the use of this choice results in the creation of a well-constructed inverted sentence, in which “navigating the waters” is the subject and “was” is the main verb.


> <b>(C)</b> So dangerous for ships <b>were navigating the waters off the Outer Banks of North Carolina, and so, the area
</b> became known as the Graveyard of the Atlantic.



The use of “were” may seem to be correct, because “were” follows “ships.” However, as discussed in the explanation for choice (B), the verb does not go with “ships” but rather with the singular “navigating.” So, we need the singular “was” rather than the plural “were.”


Also, this version is not logical. With “so dangerous” we need “that,” as in “navigating was so dangerous <em>that</em> …,” but this version combines “so dangerous” with “and so,” to create the illogical “So dangerous were navigating the waters …, and so, the area became known as the graveyard.”


> <b>(D)</b> So dangerous for ships <b>that were navigating the waters off the Outer Banks of North Carolina that the area
</b> became known as the Graveyard of the Atlantic.



This version has no subject and no main verb. In other words, this version does not say that something was so challenging and dangerous that it became known as the Graveyard of the Atlantic. It includes only a phrase that begins with “so challenging and dangerous that …” without including a subject and a main verb.


The only finite verbs included in this version are “were” and “became.” Since “were” appears within the relative clause, “that were navigating the waters …,” and since “became” appears within the subordinate clause “that the area became known,” neither “were” nor “became” can be the main verb of a sentence.


> <b>(E)</b> So dangerous for ships <b>that navigate the waters off the Outer Banks of North Carolina, an area that
</b> became known as the Graveyard of the Atlantic.



This version is merely a long phrase that begins with “so dangerous for ships that.” It has neither a subject nor a main verb.


The only finite verbs included in this version are “navigate” and “became.” Since “navigate” appears within the relative clause, “that navigate the waters …,” and since “became” appears within the relative clause “that became known,” neither “navigate” nor “became” can be the main verb of a sentence.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
