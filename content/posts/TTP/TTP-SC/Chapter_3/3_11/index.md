## Lesson Content
Collective nouns are nouns that name groups of people or things.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Collective nouns are nouns that refer to groups of people or things. When collective nouns appear as subjects on GMAT Sentence Correction questions, they are almost always considered singular.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following sentence:

> The <strong>team is</strong> at the lake this weekend, for a workshop on conflict resolution skills.

Notice that the collective noun “team” is singular since in this context it refers to a single collection of people.

Thus, we need the singular verb “is.”

It would be incorrect to use the plural verb “are.”

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The <strong>team are</strong> at the lake this weekend, for a workshop on conflict resolution skills.

Again, the correct verb is “is” because nowhere in the sentence is there evidence that the sentence is referring to any individual members of the team.

Sometimes, however, a collective noun will require a plural verb, although this situation is less likely.

Notice the difference between the above example sentence and the following sentence:

> After the conflict resolution workshop had ended, the <strong>team were</strong> practicing speaking articulately to each other and using emotional intelligence to diffuse problems among members of the team.

Notice that the above sentence is about the individual members of the team.

Only individual people could be “practicing speaking articulately to each other and using emotional intelligence to diffuse problems among members of the team.” The team itself as a single entity would not have been “practicing speaking articulately to each other.” So, in this case, “the team” refers to a set of individuals and is considered a plural subject.

Let’s consider some more examples:

> The crew sometimes <strong>argue </strong>among themselves over who should handle each of the tasks.

> The crew <strong>is</strong> scheduled to arrive in the afternoon.

In each of the sentences above, the subject is the same, “crew.” The context indicates whether to use a singular or plural verb.

In the case of the first sentence, it is clear that a crew would not argue with itself.

In the second sentence, the “crew” acts as a single unit, as the entire crew is scheduled to arrive in the afternoon.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When the context of a sentence makes it clear that the members of a group are not acting as a single unit but rather as individuals, a plural verb is required.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at a couple of additional examples:

> The board <strong>needs</strong> a quorum before it <strong>holds</strong> a vote on amendments to the bylaws.

> The panel always <strong>starts</strong> off with an orderly discussion but quickly <strong>devolves</strong> into shouting and name-calling.

In the first example, the sentence is focused on the collective requirements and actions of the board.

Similarly, in the second example, we are concerned not with the individual statements of members of the panel, but with the conduct of the panel as a whole.

<u>Collective Nouns Followed by Prepositional Phrases</u>

Another way in which collective nouns can appear is followed by prepositional phrases.

> pack of wolves

> group of protesters

> family of bears

Subject-verb agreement in sentence with structures of this type works similarly to subject-verb agreement in sentences with collective nouns alone.

If the focus of the sentence is on the group as a whole rather than on the members of the group, the verb should be singular, as it is in the following example:

> The <strong>flock of geese</strong> flying overhead <strong>was</strong> the largest that I have ever seen.

In the above example, the focus of the sentence is on the size of the entire flock of geese.

Here’s another.

> The <strong>crowd of people was dispersed</strong> by the manager of the hotel.

In the example above, the manager did not disperse individuals.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When the context of a sentence containing a subject made up of a collective noun followed by a prepositional phrase makes it clear that the group is acting as a single unit, a singular verb is required.
{{< /alert >}}

On the other hand, if the focus of the sentence is on the individual members within the group, then the verb should be plural.

> A <strong>group of dogs were</strong> chasing each other in the park earlier.

In the above example, it is not the group that was chasing but, rather, the dogs that were chasing each other.

Here’s another example in which a plural verb is required.

> Only a <strong>couple of the experts are</strong> aware that they have been chosen to restore the artwork.

In the sentence above, it is not the couple that is aware but rather the experts who are aware.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When the context of a sentence containing a collective noun followed by a prepositional phrase makes clear that the members of the group are not acting as a single unit but rather as individuals, a plural verb is required.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 11  

As the divers in the cage <u>were lowering into the water, a horde of sharks that were</u> coming at them from all directions, some eyeing the cage from a distance, others swimming right up to the cage and poking at it with their noses.

1. [ ] (A) were lowering into the water, a horde of sharks that were
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) lowered into the water with a horde of sharks that was
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) were lowered into the water, a horde of sharks were
4. [ ] (D) was lowering into the water with a horde of sharks
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) was lowered into the water, a horde of sharks was
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



This sentence contains two noun + prepositional phrase structures: “divers in the cage” and “horde of sharks.”


The main verb of the first clause of the sentence has to agree with the plural “divers,” as “cage” is not the subject of the clause. “Divers” is the subject.


In the correct version of the sentence, the version created via the use of choice (C), “horde of sharks” is the subject of the main clause. The collective noun, “horde,” could be considered singular or considered plural depending on the context. In this case, “horde” must be considered plural, since individual sharks, rather than the horde as a unit, would be “coming at them from all directions, some eyeing the cage from a distance, others swimming right up to the cage and poking at it with their noses.”


> <b>(A)</b> As the divers in the cage <b>were lowering into the water, a horde of sharks that were
</b> coming at them from all directions, some eyeing the cage from a distance, others swimming right up to the cage and poking at it with their noses.



In this version, the plural “were” is correctly used with the plural subject “divers” and “that were” is correctly used with “sharks.”


However, this version has no independent clause. It starts with a dependent clause, “as the divers …,” and then has a second part that has only a subject, “horde of sharks” and no main verb. Notice that everything that follows “horde of sharks” is a long relative clause, “that were coming ….” The main verb of a sentence cannot be contained in a relative clause.


Also, the expression “the divers … were lowering into the water” does not really make sense. “Lowering” is an action that someone has to perform on something, but the sentence does not say that the divers lowered something. It’s says only “the divers … were lowering.” What would be correct is “the divers … were lowered into the water,” “the divers … were lowering themselves into the water,” or “the divers … were descending into the water.”


> <b>(B)</b> As the divers in the cage <b>lowered into the water with a horde of sharks that was
</b> coming at them from all directions, some eyeing the cage from a distance, others swimming right up to the cage and poking at it with their noses.



This version contains only a dependent clause, “As the divers in the cage lowered into the water,” and a very long prepositional phrase that begins with “with” and continues to the end of this version. So, this version has no independent clause and, thus, is not a complete sentence.


Also, the expression “the divers … lowered into the water” does not really make sense. “Lowered” is an action that someone has to perform on something, but the sentence does not say that the divers lowered something. It’s says only “the divers … lowered.” What would be correct is “the divers … were lowered into the water,” “the divers … lowered themselves into the water,” or “the divers … descended into the water.”


Also, since the sentence conveys that the sharks were coming from all directions and acting in various ways, “a horde of sharks that was” is incorrect. The point is not that the horde was coming from all directions. The sharks were coming from all directions. So, the sentence is focused on the individual sharks within the horde, rather than on the horde as a unit. Thus, “horde of sharks” is considered plural, and “that,” which has nothing singular to refer to, must also be considered plural. So, rather than use “was,” the sentence has to use the plural “were,” as in “a horde of sharks that <em>were</em> coming at them from all directions.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> As the divers in the cage <b>were lowered into the water, a horde of sharks were
</b> coming at them from all directions, some eyeing the cage from a distance, others swimming right up to the cage and poking at it with their noses.



In the first clause, the plural “divers” and the plural “were” agree.


Also, the sentence says that the sharks were coming from all directions and acting in various ways. So, the point is not that the horde was coming from all directions. The sharks were coming from all directions. So, the sentence is focused on the individual sharks within the horde, rather than on the horde as a unit. Thus, “horde of sharks” is considered a plural subject, and it makes sense to use the plural “were,” in “a horde of sharks <em>were</em> coming at them from all directions.”


> <b>(D)</b> As the divers in the cage <b>was lowering into the water with a horde of sharks
</b> coming at them from all directions, some eyeing the cage from a distance, others swimming right up to the cage and poking at it with their noses.



In this version, the singular verb “was” does not agree with the plural subject “divers.” “In the cage” is a prepositional phrase providing information about the divers, and “cage” is not the subject of the sentence.


Also, this version has no independent clause. It is made up of only the dependent clause “As the divers in the cage was lowering …” and a very long prepositional phrase that begins with “with” and continues to the end of this version.


> <b>(E)</b> As the divers in the cage <b>was lowered into the water, a horde of sharks was
</b> coming at them from all directions, some eyeing the cage from a distance, others swimming right up to the cage and poking at it with their noses.



In the first clause of this version, “was” does not agree with the plural subject, “divers.”


Also, since the sentence says that the sharks were coming from all directions and acting in various ways, “a horde of sharks was” is incorrect. The point is not that the horde was coming from all directions. The sharks as individuals were coming from all directions. So, in this case, the collective noun “horde” is considered a plural subject and, thus, requires the plural “were,” as in “a horde of sharks <em>were</em> coming at them from all directions.”


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)
