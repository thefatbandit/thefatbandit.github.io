## Lesson Content
Of course, since a collective noun subject can be correctly paired with a singular verb or plural verb, we could see a Sentence Correction question in which it is not entirely clear whether a collective noun would most logically be considered a singular subject or plural subject.

In such a case, our best move may be to choose an answer choice by looking for differences between choices other than agreement between that collective noun and its associated verb.

Let’s use this method to choose among the answer choices in the following example.

##  Sentence Correction Example 14  

<u>Since a group of high school students was expected to attend the introductory economics class the next day, the professor, along with his assistants, was racing</u> to put together a lesson that would both fit the curriculum and capture the interest of novices.

1. [x] (A) Since a group of high school students was expected to attend the introductory economics class the next day, the professor, along with his assistants, was racing
2. [ ] (B) Since a group of high school students were expected to attend the introductory economics class the next day, the professor, along with his assistants, were racing
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) Since a group of high school students were expected to attend the introductory economics class the next day, the professor and his assistants was in a race
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) With a group of high school students, who was expected to attend the introductory economics class the next day, the professor, along with his assistants, were in a race
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) A group of high school students expecting to attend the introductory economics class the next day; the professor and his assistants were in a race
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

##### Question Level: medium



Even though we have discussed that the GMAT leans toward using singular verbs with collective nouns, we also know that, when the focus of a clause is on the individuals in a group, the verb used with a collective noun should be plural. So, in seeing this question, we might wonder whether the “group of high school students” should be considered one group that will be attending the class, in which case the singular “was expected” would be preferred, or the sentence is focused on the individuals in the group, each of whom will be attending the class, in which case using the plural “were expected” would make more sense.


Fortunately, the question does not force us to make that decision, because there are other differences among the choices that we can use to find the correct answer. So, we can put aside the question of whether the “group of students” is best considered a singular or plural subject and base our choice on things we are more confident about.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A)</b> <b>Since a group of high school students was expected to attend the introductory economics class the next day, the professor, along with his assistants, was racing
</b> to put together a lesson that would both fit the curriculum and capture the interest of novices.



We may not be entirely sure whether “group of students” should be considered a singular subject or a plural subject. So, we may not be certain that “was expected” is correct.


However, we know that, even though the additive phrase, “along with his assistants,” follows “professor,” a singular subject like “professor” remains singular even if it is followed by an additive phrase. So, “was racing” agrees with “professor,” making this choice the only one in which there is no flaw in the clause about the professor and his assistants.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>Since a group of high school students were expected to attend the introductory economics class the next day, the professor, along with his assistants, were racing
</b> to put together a lesson that would both fit the curriculum and capture the interest of novices.



We may not be entirely sure whether “group of students” should be considered a singular subject or a plural subject. So, we may not be certain that “were expected” is incorrect.


However, we know that, even though the additive phrase, “along with his assistants,” follows “professor,” a singular subject like “professor” remains singular even if it is followed by an additive phrase. Thus, “were racing” disagrees with the subject of the main clause, “professor,” making this choice incorrect.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>Since a group of high school students were expected to attend the introductory economics class the next day, the professor and his assistants was in a race
</b> to put together a lesson that would both fit the curriculum and capture the interest of novices.



We may not be entirely sure whether “group of students” should be considered a singular subject or a plural subject. So, we may not be certain that “were expected” is incorrect. However, the singular “was” does not agree with the compound subject, “the professor and his assistants.”


Also, it does not really make sense to say that the professor and his assistants “were in a race to put together a lesson.”


> <b>(D)</b> <b>With a group of high school students, who was expected to attend the introductory economics class the next day, the professor, along with his assistants, were in a race
</b> to put together a lesson that would both fit the curriculum and capture the interest of novices.



The pronoun “who” can refer only to people, and not to things. So, “who” would not refer to “group” but rather to “students,” which is plural. So, we need “who were” and, thus, “who was” is incorrect.


Also, we know that, even though the additive phrase, “along with his assistants,” follows “professor,” a singular subject like “professor” remains singular, even if it is followed by an additive phrase. Thus, in the main clause, “were” disagrees with the subject, “professor.”


Also, this version nonsensically conveys that the professor and his assistants were in a race with a group of high school students.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>A group of high school students expecting to attend the introductory economics class the next day; the professor and his assistants were in a race
</b> to put together a lesson that would both fit the curriculum and capture the interest of novices.



When a semicolon connects two parts of a sentence that express ideas, both of the parts of the sentence that are connected by the semicolon must be complete clauses. In this case, however, the part that precedes the semicolon has no main verb. Rather, it consists only of the noun “group” along with multiple modifiers. So, the part that precedes the semicolon is not a complete clause.


Also, this version conveys the illogical meaning that the professor and his assistants were in a race to put together a lesson.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)
