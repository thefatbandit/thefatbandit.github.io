## Lesson Content
Some preceding elements in a subject can require that the subject take a singular verb even if the subject seems to be plural.

For instance, a subject composed of a noun preceded by “every,” “each,” or “many a” always takes a singular verb.

Consider the following examples:

> The <strong>guys</strong> at the prom <strong>look</strong> amazing.

> <strong>Every guy</strong> at the prom <strong>looks</strong> amazing.

In the first sentence, the subject, “guys,” is plural and therefore requires a plural verb.

However, when the subject begins with “every,” as is the case in the second sentence, the subject is understood to be singular.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When a subject begins with the word(s) “every,” “each,” “many a,” or “many an,” the subject always takes a singular verb.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at another example.

> Each of us <strong>(prefers/prefer)</strong> a different style of cooking, and so, it has been difficult to reach a consensus about the menu.

Although the pronoun “us” seems to signal the need for a plural verb, in this case, “us” is part of an expression that begins with “each.” So, the singular verb “prefers” is correct.

> Each of us <strong>prefers</strong> a different style of cooking, and so, it has been difficult to reach a consensus about the menu.

Let’s look at one more example.

> Clarisse’s inbox is filled with more than a thousand emails, and many an email <strong>(has/have)</strong> gone unanswered.

The sentence above is made up of two independent clauses, the second of which begins with the phrase “many an.” Since any subject that begins with “many an” is singular, we know that the verb that follows must be singular, despite the fact that we can tell that “many an email” is, in a sense, referring to multiple emails.

> Clarisse’s inbox is filled with more than a thousand emails, and many an email <strong>has</strong> gone unanswered.

Note that we would still require the singular “has” following “many an” even if the preceding clause were a dependent clause.

> Though there are more than a thousand emails in Clarisse’s inbox, many an email <strong>has</strong> gone unanswered.

##  Sentence Correction Example 15  

Multiverse theory posits that there are a <u>multitude, or even an infinite number, of universes in existence, each with its</u> own laws of physics and potential for intelligent life.

1. [x] (A) multitude, or even an infinite number, of universes in existence, each with its
2. [ ] (B)  multitude as well as an even infinite number of universes in existence and that every one of them have their
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) multitude, along with even an infinite number, of universes in existence, each has its
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) multitude, or even an infinite number, of universes in existence, so that each have their
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) multitude, and even an infinite number, of universes in existence, every one of which have its
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

##### Question Level: easy



> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A)</b> Multiverse theory posits that there are a <b>multitude, or even an infinite number, of universes in existence, each with its
</b> own laws of physics and potential for intelligent life.



 The version created with this choice conveys a logical meaning and is grammatically correct.


Notice that the singular “each,” which in this context conveys the meaning “each universe,” is correctly paired with the singular “its.” 


This version also correctly uses “or” to separate the two possibilities that multiverse theory posits: that there are either a “multitude” of universes <em>or</em> an “infinite number” of them, not both. Thus, this version avoids conveying the illogical meaning conveyed by some others.


> <b>(B)</b> Multiverse theory posits that there are a <b> multitude as well as an even infinite number of universes in existence and that every one of them have their
</b> own laws of physics and potential for intelligent life.



Since the plural pronoun “them” is preceded by the phrase “every one of,” the verb and pronoun following them must be in singular form. In other words, the subject becomes the singular “every <em>one</em>,” not the plural “them.”


So, the plural “have their” is incorrect. “<em>Every one</em> of them <em>has its</em> own laws …” would be correct.


Additionally, it is illogical to say that “there are a multitude <em>as well as</em> an even infinite number of universes,” because if there are an infinite number of universes, then by definition, there are a multitude.


> <b>(C)</b> Multiverse theory posits that there are a <b>multitude, along with even an infinite number, of universes in existence, each has its
</b> own laws of physics and potential for intelligent life.



The version created by this choice contains a comma splice, meaning that two independent clauses are joined by only a comma. Notice that the following two clauses are joined by nothing more than a comma:


Multiverse theory posits that there are a multitude, along with even an infinite number, of universes in existence<strong>,</strong>


each has its own laws of physics and potential for intelligent life.


Those are two complete sentences, right? In other words, each of those expresses a complete thought and can stand on its own. We cannot join two complete sentences with a comma alone. Rather, we need to use a semicolon to join them or place a conjunction such as “and” after the comma. 


Comma splices can be difficult to spot if we don’t read an answer choice in the context of the full sentence. So, we must always make sure to consider the full sentence created, not just the answer choice in isolation.


Additionally, it is illogical to say that “there are a multitude, <em>along with</em> even an infinite number, of universes,” because if there are an infinite number of universes, then by definition, there are a multitude.


> <b>(D)</b> Multiverse theory posits that there are a <b>multitude, or even an infinite number, of universes in existence, so that each have their
</b> own laws of physics and potential for intelligent life.



 We must pair “each” with a singular verb and pronoun. Thus, the plural “have their” is incorrect.


Notice that the implied meaning of “each” in this context is “each universe,” which is singular. It is not grammatical to say that “each [universe] have their own laws.” We must say that each “has its” own laws.


There is also an illogical meaning conveyed by the version created with this choice. It does not quite make sense to say that multiverse theory posits that there are a multitude of universes “so that” the universes have certain qualities. The nonsensical meaning conveyed is that the theory posits something <em>for the purpose of </em>giving universes certain qualities. The theory either accurately or inaccurately describes universes, but a theory cannot actually affect the nature of universes.


> <b>(E)</b> Multiverse theory posits that there are a <b>multitude, and even an infinite number, of universes in existence, every one of which have its
</b> own laws of physics and potential for intelligent life.



 The phrase “every one of” should be paired with the singular verb “has,” not the plural “have.” So, “every one of which <em>have</em> its own laws” is incorrect.


Additionally, it is illogical to say that “there are a multitude, <em>and</em> even an infinite number, of universes,” because if there are an infinite number of universes, then by definition, there are a multitude. So, the sentence should say that there are either a “multitude” or “an infinite number,” not both.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

##  Sentence Correction Example 16  

<u>Believed by many leaders is that, in order for our nation to achieve financial solvency, each of our millions of citizens has</u> to seek financial solvency, because a nation can be only as solid as its citizens.

1. [x] (A) Believed by many leaders is that, in order for our nation to achieve financial solvency, each of our millions of citizens has
2. [ ] (B) So that our nation achieves financial solvency, many leaders believe that all of our millions of citizens has
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) It is believed by many leaders that, for our nation to achieve financial solvency, each of our millions of citizens who has
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) Many leaders believe that, in order for our nation to achieve financial solvency, every one of our millions of citizens have
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) So that our nation will achieve financial solvency, many leaders believe that each of our millions of citizens has
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

##### Question Level: hard



Correctly answering this question requires noticing subject-verb agreement issues and paying careful attention to the meanings conveyed by the basically grammatically correct choices. The correct answer is written to seem a little off. So, if we’re not careful, we’ll be tempted to choose one of the more correct sounding incorrect choices.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A)</b> <b>Believed by many leaders is that, in order for our nation to achieve financial solvency, each of our millions of citizens has
</b> to seek financial solvency, because a nation can be only as solid as its citizens.



This choice employs an inverted sentence structure to obscure the fact that it is the correct answer to this question. Notice, the subject of the sentence is expressed by the noun clause that follows “is.” So, we could reverse the sentence to make it the following:


That, in order for our nation to achieve financial solvency, each of our millions of citizens has to seek financial solvency, because a nation can be only as solid as its citizens is believed by many leaders.


Also, this version correctly uses the singular verb “has” with the singular subject “each of our millions of citizens.”


> <b>(B)</b> <b>So that our nation achieves financial solvency, many leaders believe that all of our millions of citizens has
</b> to seek financial solvency, because a nation can be only as solid as its citizens.



This choice can be eliminated because the version created via the use of this choice does not make sense, since it does not make sense to say that “many leaders believe” something in order for the nation to achieve financial solvency.


Also, this version incorrectly uses a singular verb, “has,” with subject “all of our millions of citizens,” which does not start with “each” or “every” but, rather, with “all,” and so this subject is plural, not singular.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>It is believed by many leaders that, for our nation to achieve financial solvency, each of our millions of citizens who has
</b> to seek financial solvency, because a nation can be only as solid as its citizens.



While this version starts off correctly, when we combine this answer choice with the non-underlined portion, we see that what may at first appear to be a noun clause beginning with “that” actually has no main verb. As a result, we have the following illogical structure: “that … each of our millions of citizens who has to seek financial solvency.” We can see even more clearly why this structure does not work if we read it along with the rest of the sentence.


It is believed by many leaders that … each of our millions of citizens who has to achieve financial solvency ….


> <b>(D)</b> <b>Many leaders believe that, in order for our nation to achieve financial solvency, every one of our millions of citizens have
</b> to seek financial solvency, because a nation can be only as solid as its citizens.



This version is correct except for one word, the plural “have,” which does not agree with the singular subject “every one of our millions of citizens.”


> <b>(E)</b> <b>So that our nation will achieve financial solvency, many leaders believe that each of our millions of citizens has
</b> to seek financial solvency, because a nation can be only as solid as its citizens.



All subjects and verbs in this version agree, and this version is basically grammatically correct.


However, this version expresses an illogical meaning, that many leaders believe something so that the nation will achieve financial solvency. It doesn’t make sense that leaders would believe something so that the nation will achieve financial solvency. We have to be careful to notice such meaning issues, so that we don’t choose an illogical choice like this one over one like the inversely structured, somewhat odd sounding choice (A).


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)
