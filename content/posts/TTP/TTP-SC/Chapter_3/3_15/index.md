## Lesson Content
A pronoun is a word that takes the place of a noun.

> <strong>Robert </strong>said that <strong>he</strong> will be able to attend the party.

In this sentence, the pronoun “he” refers to the antecedent “Robert.” Notice that, by using the pronoun, we don’t have to repeat the noun “Robert.”

> The <strong>zebra and the hippo</strong> decided that <strong>they</strong> were tired, so they looked for a comfortable place to sleep.

In this sentence, the pronoun “they” refers to the antecedents “the zebra and the hippo.” Notice that, by using the pronoun, we don’t have to repeat the nouns “zebra and hippo.”

Pronouns follow the same rules of subject-verb agreement that nouns follow.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Pronouns follow the same rules of subject-verb agreement that nouns follow. If the subject of a clause is a pronoun rather than a noun, it still must agree in number with its verb.
{{< /alert >}}

We’ll cover pronouns in greater detail in the Pronouns chapter, but for now, we’ll discuss two types of pronouns that must agree with their verbs: relative pronouns and indefinite pronouns.
