## Lesson Content
Relative pronouns, such as “which,” “that,” and “who,” can be singular or plural depending on their referents.

> The <strong>dresser that is</strong> on sale is still expensive.

> The <strong>curtains that are</strong> on sale are beautiful.

As demonstrated in the sentences above, the relative pronoun “that” can take a singular or plural verb depending on whether the noun to which “that” refers is singular or plural.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When a relative pronoun refers to a singular noun, it too is singular and takes a singular verb. On the other hand, when a relative pronoun refers to a plural noun, it is likewise plural and requires a plural verb.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s examine some more examples:

> Jurors are sometimes sympathetic toward a <strong>defendant who</strong> <strong>claims</strong> to have committed a “crime of passion.”

In the sentence above, the relative pronoun “who” refers to the singular “defendant” and, thus, requires the singular verb “claims.”

> The proposed <strong>upgrades </strong>to the community center, <strong>which include</strong> the addition of a basketball court and a reading room, will be voted on at the community board meeting later this month.

The subject of the sentence above is “the proposed upgrades,” and the relative clause beginning with “which” further describes those upgrades.

> The only <strong>restaurant </strong>within two miles of here <strong>that serves </strong>burritos is Coyote Flaco.

The relative pronoun “that” refers to the singular “restaurant,” and so, we use the singular verb “serves.”

##  Sentence Correction Example 17  

Ironically, the plan to build carbon-neutral <u>factories that has the capacity to  manufacture thousands of emissions-free trucks annually are being opposed by locals citing</u> negative environmental impacts.

1. [ ] (A) factories that has the capacity to annually manufacture thousands of emissions-free trucks are being opposed by locals citing
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) factories, which has the capacity to manufacture thousands of emissions-free trucks annually, is opposed by locals who cite
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) factories that have the capacity to annually manufacture thousands of emissions-free trucks are opposed by locals citing
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) factories that have the capacity to manufacture thousands of emissions-free trucks annually and are opposed by locals who cite
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) factories that have the capacity to manufacture annually thousands of emissions-free trucks is opposed by locals citing

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A)</b> Ironically, the plan to build carbon-neutral <b>factories that has the capacity to annually manufacture thousands of emissions-free trucks are being opposed by locals citing
</b> negative environmental impacts.



Notice that the relative pronoun “that” in this choice refers to the plural noun phrase “carbon-neutral factories.” Thus, the singular verb “has” is incorrect, since a singular verb does not properly pair with a plural noun. “Carbon-neutral factories that <em>have</em> …” would be correct.


Also, the plural verb “are” does not agree with the singular subject “the plan.”


If you have a hard time seeing this issue, one powerful strategy you can use is to cross off everything between the noun and verb.


Ironically, the plan <s>to build carbon-neutral factories that has the capacity to  manufacture thousands of emissions-free trucks annually</s> are being opposed


> <b>(B)</b> Ironically, the plan to build carbon-neutral <b>factories, which has the capacity to manufacture thousands of emissions-free trucks annually, is opposed by locals who cite
</b> negative environmental impacts.



Notice that the relative pronoun “which” in this choice refers to the plural noun phrase “carbon-neutral factories.” Thus, the singular verb “has” is incorrect, since a singular verb does not properly pair with a plural noun. “Carbon-neutral factories, which <em>have</em> …” would be correct.


> <b>(C)</b> Ironically, the plan to build carbon-neutral <b>factories that have the capacity to annually manufacture thousands of emissions-free trucks are opposed by locals citing
</b> negative environmental impacts.



In this version, the plural verb “are” does not agree with the singular subject “the plan.”


If you have a hard time seeing this issue, one powerful strategy you can use is to cross off everything between the noun and verb.


Ironically, the plan <s>to build carbon-neutral factories that have the capacity to annually manufacture thousands of emissions-free trucks</s> are opposed


> <b>(D)</b> Ironically, the plan to build carbon-neutral <b>factories that have the capacity to manufacture thousands of emissions-free trucks annually and are opposed by locals who cite
</b> negative environmental impacts.



This version is composed of only the adverb “ironically;” a noun phrase, “the plan to build carbon-neutral factories;” and a long relative clause that begins with “that” and runs all the way to the period. So, even though this version doesn’t have any subject-verb agreement issues, it’s incorrect because it has no independent clause and thus isn’t a complete sentence.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E)</b> Ironically, the plan to build carbon-neutral <b>factories that have the capacity to manufacture annually thousands of emissions-free trucks is opposed by locals citing
</b> negative environmental impacts.



 In this version, the verb “have” works logically with the noun phrase that precedes “that,” the plural “carbon-neutral factories.”


Also, the singular verb “is” agrees with the singular subject “the plan,” as we can more easily see by crossing off what appears between the subject and the verb.


Ironically, the plan <s>to build carbon-neutral factories that have the capacity to  manufacture annually thousands of emissions-free trucks</s> is opposed


This choice is the only one that produces a grammatically correct complete sentence.


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>Confirm that singular subjects are paired with singular verbs and that plural subjects are paired with plural verbs. 


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>When a relative pronoun refers to a singular noun, the pronoun too is singular and takes a singular verb. When a relative pronoun refers to a plural noun, the pronoun is likewise plural and takes a plural verb.


<strong>Key Concept:</strong> When checking for subject-verb agreement, cross off any noun modifiers that come between the subject and finite verb.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Sentence Correction Example 18  

The startup bike company’s first model, which, though not <u>well known as some other bikes, were less expensive, quickly became</u> popular with cyclists worldwide.

1. [ ] (A) well known as some other bikes, were less expensive, quickly became
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) as well known as some other bikes, were less expensive, and they quickly became
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) as well known as some other bikes, was less expensive, quickly became
4. [ ] (D) well known as some other bike, was less expensive and quickly became
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) well known, as were some other bikes, was less expensive, quickly becoming
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



In every version of the sentence, there is a relative clause beginning with “which” that modifies “model.” Since “model” is singular, “which” must be singular and, therefore, has to be paired with the singular “was,” rather than the plural “were.”


Once we have eliminated the choices in which “which” is incorrectly paired with “were,” we have to find other decision points in order to choose the correct answer from the remaining choices.


> <b>(A)</b> The startup bike company’s first model, which, though not <b>well known as some other bikes, were less expensive, quickly became
</b> popular with cyclists worldwide.



Since “which” refers to “model,” “which” must be singular. However, in this version, “which” is incorrectly paired with the plural “were.” To see this pairing more clearly, we could cross off what comes between “which” and “were.”


The startup bike company’s first model, which<s>, though not well known as some other bikes,</s> were less expensive


Also, “though not well known as some other bikes” is missing “as” before “well known.” As a result, it conveys that the first model was not well known to be some other bikes.


> <b>(B)</b> The startup bike company’s first model, which, though not <b>as well known as some other bikes, were less expensive, and they quickly became
</b> popular with cyclists worldwide.



Since “which” refers to “model,” “which” must be singular, but, in this version, “which” is incorrectly paired with the plural “were.” To see this pairing more clearly, we could cross off the what comes between “which” and “were.”


The startup bike company’s first model, which<s>, though not as well known as some other bikes,</s> were less expensive


Also, the inclusion of “and they” in this version renders the sentence illogically constructed, as we can more clearly see if we leave out the relative clause that begins with “which.”


The startup bike company’s first model … and they quickly became popular with cyclists worldwide.


Notice also that the pronoun “they” does not agree in number with what it seems to refer to, “model.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> The startup bike company’s first model, which, though not <b>as well known as some other bikes, was less expensive, quickly became
</b> popular with cyclists worldwide.



This choice may seem incorrect because the ending section, “was less expensive, quickly became,” appears to be illogical.


However, by carefully evaluating this choice in the context of the non-underlined portions of the sentence, we can see that it’s actually the correct answer.


First, since “which” refers to “model,” “which” must be singular. So, pairing “which” with “was” in the relative clause is correct, as we can more easily see by crossing off what appears between “which” and “was.”


The startup bike company’s first model, which<s>, though not as well known as some other bikes,</s> was less expensive


Next, we can confirm that the rest of this version works by crossing off the relative clause.


The startup bike company’s first model<s>, which, though not as well known as some other bikes, was less expensive,</s> quickly became popular with cyclists worldwide.


We can see that the main clause of this version is “The startup bike company’s first model … quickly became popular with cyclists worldwide,” which is logical.


So, this version is grammatically correct and effectively conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(D)</b> The startup bike company’s first model, which, though not <b>well known as some other bike, was less expensive and quickly became
</b> popular with cyclists worldwide.



This choice is a trap written to look correct even though it has some significant flaws.


Since “which” refers to “model,” “which” must be singular. So, pairing “which” with “was” is correct.


However, “well known as some other bike” is missing “as” before “well known.” Thus, as written, “not well known as some other bike” conveys that the company’s first model was not well known to be some other bike.


Also, the addition of “and” before “quickly became” turns everything that follows “model” into a relative clause that modifies “model,” as we can more easily see by crossing off the relative clause.


The startup bike company’s first model<s>, which, though not well known as some other bike, was less expensive and quickly become popular with cyclists worldwide</s>.


So this version has no main verb or independent clause and is not a complete sentence.


> <b>(E)</b> The startup bike company’s first model, which, though not <b>well known, as were some other bikes, was less expensive, quickly becoming
</b> popular with cyclists worldwide.



Since “which” refers to “model,” “which” must be singular. So, pairing “which” with “was” is correct. So, though somewhat awkward, the modifier beginning with “which” works in this version.


However, the use of “quickly becoming” rather than “quickly became” makes everything that follows “model” into a relative clause that modifies “model,” as we can more easily see by crossing off the relative clause.


The startup bike company’s first model<s>, which, though not well known, as were some other companies’ bikes, was less expensive, quickly becoming popular with cyclists worldwide</s>.


Thus, this version has no main verb or independent clause and is not a complete sentence.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)
