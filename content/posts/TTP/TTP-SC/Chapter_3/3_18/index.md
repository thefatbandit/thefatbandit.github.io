## Lesson Content
Some indefinite pronouns are always singular and, therefore, always require a singular verb.

These pronouns are:

> anybody, anyone, anything, each, either<sup>1</sup>, everybody, everyone, everything, neither<sup>1</sup>, nobody, no one, nothing, one, somebody, someone, something, whatever, whoever<sup>2</sup>

Consider the following examples in which singular indefinite pronouns are paired with singular verbs:

> After the concert,<strong> everyone was</strong> ready to go home.

> If <strong>someone fixes</strong> up this house, it will be lovely.

> Once Andrea addresses <strong>whatever has been bothering</strong> her, she will feel better.

> <strong>Whoever wins</strong> the election will inherit quite a mess.

Be sure to commit this list of singular indefinite pronouns to memory.

When “either” is used in an “either … or” construction, we must follow the rules concerning “either … or.” The same applies to “neither” in a “neither … nor” construction. In these cases, we follow the rule that the verb in a clause or sentence containing a compound subject joined by the conjunctions “or,” “nor,” “either … or,” or “neither … nor” must agree with the subject noun to which it is closest.
{{< /alert >}}

<sup>2</sup>Note that, when “whoever” precedes a plural noun, as in, for example, “whoever the owners,” the plural noun may be the subject of the sentence and take a plural verb, as in:

> Whoever <strong>the owners</strong> <strong>are</strong>, they are doing a great job of preserving the wetlands on the property.

> Whoever <strong>the officers</strong> of the company <strong>are</strong>, they are letting the earnings per share slide.

In Sentence Correction questions, singular indefinite pronouns often will be followed by prepositional phrases.

Consider the following example:

> Neither of the girls (likes/like) doing chores on the weekends.

The intervening prepositional phrase “of the girls” may tempt us to choose the plural verb “like,” because our ears have a tendency to determine whether we need a singular or a plural verb by considering the noun closest to that verb.

However, “like” is incorrect in this sentence, because the indefinite pronoun “neither” functions as the subject of the sentence and is always singular.

> <strong>Neither</strong> <s>of the girls</s> <strong>likes </strong>doing chores on the weekends.

Let’s look at another example:

> Nothing about the meetings (makes/make) the board feel confident that the executives will produce a balanced budget for the upcoming fiscal year.

It may sound natural to your ear to follow the prepositional phrase “about the meetings” with the plural verb “make.” However, because the phrase is preceded by the singular indefinite pronoun “nothing,” we must use the singular verb “makes.”

> <strong>Nothing</strong> <s>about the meetings</s> <strong>makes</strong> the board feel confident that the executives will produce a balanced budget for the upcoming fiscal year.

Let’s try another example:

> Everything about his actions (suggests/suggest) that he isn’t concerned with other people’s opinions of him.

While “his actions” would certainly “suggest,” “Everything” “suggests.” Thus, the correct sentence is:

> <strong>Everything</strong> about his actions <strong>suggests</strong> that he isn’t concerned with other people’s opinions of him.

Let’s try a different type of example:

> No one participating in these early morning gym classes (has/have) completely woken up at the beginning, but the adrenaline starts to kick in after about fifteen minutes of exercise.

Here we have an example of a sentence in which the indefinite pronoun is immediately followed by a participle, “participating ,” rather than a preposition.

> <strong>No one</strong> participating in these early morning gym classes <strong>has</strong> completely woken up at the beginning, but the adrenaline starts to kick in after about fifteen minutes of exercise.

Let’s look at another example.

> Although we advertised the job opening on a variety of employment and social networking sites, nobody among the numerous applicants (qualifies/qualify) to lead such a large team.

Words like “numerous” and “applicants” may tempt you to choose the plural form of the verb that follows them, but “nobody,” even “among numerous applicants,” remains singular, and the verb that goes with “nobody” must be singular as well.

> Although we advertised the job opening on a variety of employment and social networking sites, <strong>nobody</strong> among the numerous applicants <strong>qualifies</strong> to lead such a large team.

Let’s try a slight variation on the above example:

> Although we advertised the job opening on a variety of employment and social networking sites, not one of the numerous applicants (qualifies/qualify) to lead such a large team.

Would we still use a singular verb with “not one”? The resulting structure may sound a bit strange to the ear, but the answer is yes.

> Although we advertised the job opening on a variety of employment and social networking sites, <strong>not one</strong> of the numerous applicants <strong>qualifies</strong> to lead such a large team.

##  Sentence Correction Example 19  

While most researchers subscribe to one of two main schools of thought on the formation of Earth’s moon, neither of <u>them completely explaining</u> all aspects of the composition of the two bodies.

1. [ ] (A) them completely explaining
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) them complete their explanations of
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) them completely explain
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) the two completely explains
5. [ ] (E) the two explain completely
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

##### Question Level: easy



The subject of the second clause of the sentence begins with the singular indefinite pronoun “neither.” So, to agree with the subject, the main verb of that clause will have to be singular.


> <b>(A)</b> While most researchers subscribe to one of two main schools of thought on the formation of Earth’s moon, neither of <b>them completely explaining
</b> all aspects of the composition of the two bodies.



This version does not include an independent clause.


Notice that “explaining” in “neither of them completely explaining” is not a finite verb and, therefore, cannot on its own serve as a main verb.


Thus, this version includes only a subordinate clause, “while most researchers subscribe …,” and a phrase “neither of them completely explaining ….”


Without an independent clause, this version cannot be a complete sentence.


> <b>(B)</b> While most researchers subscribe to one of two main schools of thought on the formation of Earth’s moon, neither of <b>them complete their explanations of
</b> all aspects of the composition of the two bodies.



The subject of the second clause, “neither of them” is singular and requires a singular verb. Therefore, the plural “complete” does not agree with the subject.


Also, this version conveys that the schools of thought did not complete their explanations, a meaning that is not completely logical.


> <b>(C)</b> While most researchers subscribe to one of two main schools of thought on the formation of Earth’s moon, neither of <b>them completely explain
</b> all aspects of the composition of the two bodies.



The subject of the second clause, “neither of them” is singular and requires a singular verb. So, the plural “explain” does not agree with the subject.


(Notice that, if we’re not careful, we could incorrectly perceive the presence of “them” as indicating that we need the plural verb “explain.” In answering a Sentence Correction question, we have to remember to consider the non-underlined portion of the sentence, which, in this case, contains the singular subject “neither.”)


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D)</b> While most researchers subscribe to one of two main schools of thought on the formation of Earth’s moon, neither of <b>the two completely explains
</b> all aspects of the composition of the two bodies.



The subject of the second clause, “neither of the two” is singular and requires a singular verb. So, the singular “explains” agrees with the subject, and this version concisely conveys a logical meaning.


(Notice that this choice is created to look incorrect on its own, without the non-underlined portions of the sentence, as, if one perceives “two” to be the subject of a clause and “explains” to be the verb, then the subject and verb appear to disagree.)


> <b>(E)</b> While most researchers subscribe to one of two main schools of thought on the formation of Earth’s moon, neither of <b>the two explain completely
</b> all aspects of the composition of the two bodies.



The subject of the second clause, “neither of the two” is singular and requires a singular verb. So, the plural “explain” does not agree with the subject.


(Notice that this choice is a trap choice created to make “two” seem to be a subject that agrees with the plural verb “explain.”)


Also, the placement of “completely” just before “all” makes the meaning conveyed less clear and may make the wording seem to be redundant, as one could perceive the sentence to be conveying that neither of the two schools of thought explains “completely all” aspects.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
