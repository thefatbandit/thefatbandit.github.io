## Lesson Content
Some indefinite pronouns are always plural and therefore always take a plural verb.

These indefinite pronouns include the following: “both,” “few,” “many,” and “several.”

Be sure to memorize these plural indefinite pronouns.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A plural indefinite pronoun always takes a plural verb. These pronouns include the following: “both,” “few,” “many,” and “several.”
{{< /alert >}}

In most cases, it is intuitive to pair a plural verb with a plural indefinite pronoun.

Consider the following examples:

> <strong>Both</strong> of my sisters <strong>like</strong> to watch cartoons on the weekends.

> <strong>Several </strong>of the students <strong>prefer</strong> to read silently as opposed to aloud.

> <strong>Few</strong> of the companies in the community <strong>provide</strong> annual donations for the fundraiser.

> <strong>Many</strong> of the animals up for adoption <strong>are</strong> abandoned pets.

##### Question Level: easy



The sentence is composed of multiple clauses.


In most versions, the subject of one of the clauses is “many of the deer.” Since “many” is always plural, “many of the deer” has to be paired with a plural verb.


Since the question has multiple answer choices that are grammatically correct, in order to arrive at the correct answer, we have to first eliminate the choices that are clearly grammatically incorrect and then choose, out of the remaining choices, the choice that expresses a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Since many of the deer in the southeastern section of the state has had young, officials at the wildlife department have confidence in the area that it
</b> is sufficiently populated with deer.



In the first clause of the sentence, the singular main verb, “has had,” does not agree with the plural subject, “many of the deer.”


Also, while “officials … have confidence in the area that it is sufficiently populated” may not blatantly violate any rules of grammar, it is awkward and expresses a somewhat muddled meaning. It would make sense to say that officials “are confident that the area is sufficiently populated.” However, this version mashes together the idea that “officials … <em>have confidence</em> in the area” and the idea that officials <em>are confident that</em> “it is sufficiently populated” in a way that doesn’t make sense.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>With many of the deer in the southeastern section of the state that have had young, officials at the wildlife department have confidence that the area
</b> is sufficiently populated with deer.



In this version, the plural subject of the first clause, “many of the deer,” agrees with the main verb of that clause, “have had,” and this version is basically grammatically correct.


However, this version expresses the nonsensical meaning that the officials have confidence “with” many of the deer that have had young,” as if officials are somehow “with” many of the deer in having confidence that the area is sufficiently populated. We could say that this version seems to convey that both the officials and the deer have confidence.


Notice also that the wording “officials … have confidence that the area” is somewhat idiomatically incorrect. The ideal phrasing to use in this context is “are confident that.”


> <b>(C)</b> <b>Because of many of the deer in the southeastern section of the state that has had young, officials at the wildlife department, confident that the area
</b> is sufficiently populated with deer.



This version starts off illogically, as, rather than express that the officials are confident because many deer have had young, it appears to convey that “Because of many of the deer” the officials are confident, providing no clear logical connection between the existence of the deer and the confidence of the officials.


Also, the singular “that has” could be perceived as referring to and modifying the singular “state.” However, since logic dictates that it is the deer, rather than the state, that have had young, “that has had young” must modify “many of the deer,” which is plural, and, therefore, doesn’t agree with “that has had young.”


Also, this version has no main verb.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>Many of the deer in the southeastern section of the state has had young; therefore, officials at the wildlife department have confidence in the area that
</b> is sufficiently populated with deer.



In the first clause, the plural subject, “many of the deer,” does not agree with the singular main verb, “has had.”


Also, when this answer choice is combined with the non-underlined portion, the second clause, “officials have confidence in the area that is sufficiently populated with deer,” does not work logically with the first clause, since which area is “the area” is not made clear. Is “the area” the southwestern portion of the state?


Also, the idea that officials “have confidence in” the area is not fully logical, as “to have confidence in” generally means to somehow trust someone or something. While it’s possible that officials could somehow “have confidence in” an area, how that idea would apply in this context is not clear.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E)</b> <b>As many of the deer in the southeastern section of the state have had young, officials at the wildlife department are confident that that area
</b> is sufficiently populated with deer.



While the use of “as” in this context is not as good as the use of “since” or “because,” the use of “as” is acceptable. In fact, “as” is used in this correct version to make us question whether this version is correct.


Also, the use of the odd sounding “that that,” although correct, is meant to make this version seem possibly incorrect.


All the same, this version is correct.


The plural subject “many of the deer” agrees with the plural “have had,” and this version effectively expresses a meaning that makes sense.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
