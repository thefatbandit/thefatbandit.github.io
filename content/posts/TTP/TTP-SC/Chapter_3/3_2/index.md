## Lesson Content
In addition to the rule that each sentence must have at least one subject and at least one main verb, when a sentence has more than one clause, each of the clauses must contain a subject that agrees in number with the finite verb in that clause.

In other words, if the subject of a clause is singular, the subject’s finite verb in that clause must be singular, and if the subject of a clause is plural, the subject’s finite verb in that clause must be plural.

There are no exceptions to this rule.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In any clause in a sentence, the subject of that clause must agree in number with that subject’s finite verb in that clause.
{{< /alert >}}

Why is this information important? Because sentences can be, and almost always are, composed of multiple clauses.

> Because <strong>she has</strong> a lot of energy, <strong>Estella loves</strong> walking with her owner, but <strong>she prefers</strong> to walk off-leash.

Notice that in each clause, the subject and subject’s finite verb agree in number.

Clause 1: Because <strong>she has</strong> a lot of energy

Clause 2: <strong>Estella loves</strong> walking with her owner

Clause 3: <strong>she prefers</strong> to walk off-leash

So, in a correct version of a sentence in a Sentence Correction question, if the subject of a clause is singular, the subject’s finite verb is singular, and, if the subject is plural, the subject’s finite verb is plural.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In a correct version of a sentence in a Sentence Correction question, if the subject of a clause is singular, the subject’s finite verb is singular, and, if the subject is plural, the subject’s finite verb is plural.
{{< /alert >}}

For example:

> Since <strong>predators are</strong> prevalent in the rain forest, <strong>birds fly</strong> north to have young.

In this example, there is an independent clause in which the subject, “birds,” and the main verb, “fly,” agree.

For example:

> Although the current <strong>president was</strong> unpopular among younger voters, <strong>he was</strong> reelected.

In this example, there is an independent clause in which the subject, “he,” and the main verb, “was,” agree.

For example:

> <strong>Molly and Blaze were</strong> swimming at the beach, and <strong>Estella and Frisky were</strong> sleeping on their beds.

In this sentence we have two independent clauses.

So, to make sure that we are choosing the correct answer to a Sentence Correction question, we have to notice whether all subjects and verbs in the version created via using that choice agree.

Let’s now discuss how to find the subject and finite verb in a clause.
