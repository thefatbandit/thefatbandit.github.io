## Lesson Content
Some indefinite pronouns can be singular or plural depending on their referents.

Such indefinite pronouns include “some,” “any,” “none,” “all,” “more,” and “most.”

You can remember this list by using the acronym SANAMM, which consists of the first letters of the pronouns in the list, “<strong>S</strong>ome,” “<strong>A</strong>ny,” “<strong>N</strong>one,” “<strong>A</strong>ll,” “<strong>M</strong>ore,” and “<strong>M</strong>ost.” These indefinite pronouns are often referred to as the SANAMM pronouns.

The rules of usage of these pronouns are simple:

> - When a SANAMM pronoun refers to a singular noun, it takes a singular verb.

> - When a SANAMM pronoun refers to a plural noun, it takes a plural verb.

Let’s consider some example sentences:

> All of the class <strong>was</strong> present yesterday.

> All of the students <strong>want</strong> the teacher to extend the deadline.

Both of the above sentences are correct.

In the second sentence, the same indefinite pronoun, “all,” refers to the plural noun “students.” Therefore, “all” takes the plural verb “want.”

💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The SANAMM indefinite pronouns, “some,” “any,” “none,” “all,” “more,” and “most,” can be singular or plural depending on their referents.
{{< /alert >}}

In the section titled “Placing a Prepositional Phrase Between the Subject and the Verb,” you learned the following:

> Although we’ll see a few important exceptions, a good general strategy when checking for subject-verb agreement is to ignore prepositional phrases found between a subject and its verb.

Notice that, when one of the indefinite pronouns “some,” “any,” “none,” “all,” “more,” or “most” appears at the beginning of a subject, we can’t ignore a prepositional phrase that comes between the pronoun and the clause’s main verb.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When a subject begins with one of the indefinite pronouns “some,” “any,” “none,” “all,” “more,” or “most,” we can’t ignore the prepositional phrase that comes between the subject and the verb.
{{< /alert >}}

For example:

> <strong>Some of the birds</strong> were already sleeping in their nests.

> <strong>Some</strong> <strong>of the water</strong> is still in the lake.

Notice that in the first sentence, the plural “were” is correct, since the subject is “some of the birds.” In other words, since we have the plural noun “birds” in the prepositional phrase following “some,” we need a plural verb.

In the second sentence, notice that we have “some of the water,” and since the noun “water” is singular, we need the singular verb “is.”

Now consider the following two sentences:

> Most of the time that they had for working on their projects was instead spent socializing with one another.

> Most of the projects that the class had neglected while socializing were turned in late.

Unlike in the sentences in the previous example, in these sentences, the noun and verb are not next to each other.

In the first example, the plural noun “projects” is followed by the singular verb “was,” which wording may sound strange.

To more easily see the relationship between the subject and verb, let’s cross off the relative clause:

> <strong>Most of the time</strong> <s>that they had for working on their projects</s> <strong>was </strong>instead spent socializing with one another.

Notice that, with the relative clause crossed off, we can more clearly see that the indefinite pronoun “most” goes with the noun “time,” and that, since “time” is a singular noun, we need the singular verb “was.”

In the second sentence, on the other hand, the subject, “most of the projects,” requires the plural verb “were,” since the noun “projects” is plural.

> <strong>Most of the projects</strong> <s>that the class had neglected while socializing</s> <strong>were </strong>turned in late.

Let’s look at a final sentence:

> None of the members of the organization care about securing increased funding for school sports, because funding for arts programs has yet to return to previous levels.

Although the indefinite pronoun “none” is followed by two prepositional phrases, “of the members” and “of the organization,” for the purposes of subject-verb agreement, we are concerned only with the noun that is closest to the indefinite pronoun, <em>the noun to which the indefinite pronoun refers</em>.

> None of the organization’s <strong>members care</strong> about securing increased funding for school sports, because funding for arts programs has yet to return to previous levels.

The meaning is unchanged, but we can see that the verb “care” clearly goes with “members” and not with “organization’s.” Remember that we must read Sentence Correction questions carefully and holistically, paying close attention to what’s said.

##  Sentence Correction Example 21  

Because of the extreme difference between the pressure deep underwater and the pressure at high elevation, the leader of the <u>expeditions are ensuring that none of the people who dove this morning goes</u> mountain climbing this afternoon.

1. [ ] (A) expeditions are ensuring that none of the people who dove this morning goes
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) expeditions is ensuring that none of the people who dove this morning go
3. [ ] (C) expeditions, who is ensuring that none of the people who dove this morning go
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) expeditions, ensuring that none of the people diving this morning and going
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) expeditions is ensuring that none of the people dove this morning while going
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

##### Question Level: easy



The underlined portion of the sentence contains finite verbs of multiple clauses.


One of those clauses is the main clause of the sentence, which, in the correct version, is the following:


“the <strong>leader </strong>of the expeditions <strong>is </strong>ensuring that …”


The subject of the above clause is the singular “leader,” which requires the singular verb “is.”


Another clause is the noun clause that is the object of the first clause, which in the correct version is:


that <strong>none of the people</strong> who dove this morning <strong>go </strong>mountain climbing …


The subject of the above clause is “none of the people.” “None” can be plural or singular, depending on what it refers to. In this case, “none” refers to the plural “people.” So, the main verb of the clause must also be plural. So, the plural “go” is correct.


> <b>(A)</b> Because of the extreme difference between the pressure deep underwater and the pressure at high elevation, the leader of the <b>expeditions are ensuring that none of the people who dove this morning goes
</b> mountain climbing this afternoon.



This answer is a trap, because it is created to look as if “expeditions” is the subject and “are” is the main verb of a clause. The truth is that the subject of the clause is the singular “leader,” and so “are” is incorrect.


Also, the subject of the noun clause “that none of the people who dove this morning goes …” is “none of the people,” which requires a plural verb. Therefore, the singular verb “goes” is incorrect.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B)</b> Because of the extreme difference between the pressure deep underwater and the pressure at high elevation, the leader of the <b>expeditions is ensuring that none of the people who dove this morning go
</b> mountain climbing this afternoon.



In this version, the singular subject “leader” is correctly paired with the singular verb “is,” and the plural subject “none of the people” is correctly paired with the plural “go.”


> <b>(C)</b> Because of the extreme difference between the pressure deep underwater and the pressure at high elevation, the leader of the <b>expeditions, who is ensuring that none of the people who dove this morning go
</b> mountain climbing this afternoon.



This version does not include a main verb. It begins with a phrase, “Because of the extreme difference …,” which is followed by the noun phrase “the leader of the expedition” and then a relative clause, “who is ensuring ….” So, this version does not include an independent clause and, thus, is not a complete sentence.


> <b>(D)</b> Because of the extreme difference between the pressure deep underwater and the pressure at high elevation, the leader of the <b>expeditions, ensuring that none of the people diving this morning and going
</b> mountain climbing this afternoon.



This version does not include a main verb. It begins with a phrase, “Because of the extreme difference …,” which is followed by the noun phrase “the leader of the expedition” and then another phrase, “ensuring ….” In other words, since “ensuring” cannot on its own serve as a main verb, this version has no main verb and, thus, is not a complete sentence.


> <b>(E)</b> Because of the extreme difference between the pressure deep underwater and the pressure at high elevation, the leader of the <b>expeditions is ensuring that none of the people dove this morning while going
</b> mountain climbing this afternoon.



This version is grammatically correct. The singular subject “leader” is correctly paired with the singular verb “is,” and the plural subject “none of the people” is correctly paired with the plural “dove.”


However, this version conveys a nonsensical meaning, as it does not make sense that the leader of the expeditions would ensure that people didn’t dive in the morning while they were going mountain climbing in the afternoon. That people would dive while mountain climbing does not seem to make sense, and that people would engage in activities in the morning and in the afternoon simultaneously does not make sense at all.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
