## Lesson Content
Let’s begin our discussion of subject-verb agreement involving numbers, fractions, percentages by discussing subjects that include the word “number.”

In a sentence subject, the word “number” can be preceded by either “the” or “a.”

When, in a subject, “number” is preceded by “the,” the sentence is about the number itself.

> <strong>The number</strong> of students who apply for summer internships <strong>has</strong> been increasing.

> An official count revealed that <strong>the number</strong> of people who attended the show <strong>was</strong> 3521.

In each of the examples above, “the number of” is used, because each sentence is about a particular number.

In contrast, when, in a subject, “number” is preceded by “a,” the sentence is about a number of things, and so the subject is plural, as are the subjects in the following examples.

> <strong>A number</strong> of students <strong>have</strong> applied for summer internships at the Rainforest Alliance.

> We noticed that <strong>a small number</strong> of the people seeking admission <strong>were</strong> using counterfeit tickets.

In each of the examples above, “number” is preceded by “a.” So, in these examples “number” acts like a collective noun that is followed by “of” + a plural noun, which is a plural subject.

Notice that the above two sentences are not focused on the numbers themselves.

Now, let’s consider a more challenging example:

> <strong>The number</strong> of convicted felons who return to prison after completing their sentences, whether re-incarcerated only months after being released or several years later, <strong>has</strong> not declined in decades despite increasingly harsh sentencing practices and the implementation of mandatory minimums.

In this rather long sentence, a long relative clause separates the phrase “the number of convicted felons” from the singular verb that goes with it, “has.” Notice that, although we use the plural verb “return” with the plural noun “felons” within the relative clause “who return to prison …,” we still use the singular verb “has” with the singular subject “the number of convicted felons.”

Of course, crossing off the prepositional phrase "of convicted felons" and the relative clause can help us see the correct subject-verb agreement:

> <strong>The number</strong> <s>of convicted felons</s> <s>who return to prison after completing their sentences, whether re-incarcerated only months after being released or several years later,</s> <strong>has</strong> not declined in decades despite increasingly harsh sentencing practices and the implementation of mandatory minimums.

Let’s consider a final example:

> <strong>A number of simple, cost-effective upgrades</strong> to the building, including the installation of new windows and the use of compact fluorescent lighting in large common spaces as well as individual offices, all but <strong>ensure</strong> that the company’s energy costs will be lower this year.

Once again, we have a series of phrases that separate the subject, “a number of … upgrades,” from the plural verb that goes with it, “ensure.” In considering subject-verb agreement in a sentence like this, it helps to first identify any dependent phrases that can be ignored.

> <strong>A number of simple, cost-effective upgrades</strong> <s>to the building</s>, <s>including the installation of new windows and the use of compact fluorescent lighting in large common spaces as well as individual offices</s>, all but <strong>ensure</strong> that the company’s energy costs will be lower this year.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Subjects that begin with “the number of” take singular verbs. Subjects that begin with “a number of” take plural verbs.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s now discuss subject-verb agreement involving the word “percentage.”
