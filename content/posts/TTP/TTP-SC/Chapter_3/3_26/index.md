## Lesson Content
In some cases, the subjects of sentences aren’t simple nouns.

Let’s take a look at some examples:

> <u>To catch trains</u> <strong>is</strong> difficult at certain times of the day.

> <u>Swimming</u> <strong>is</strong> good exercise.

> <u>What I want</u> <strong>is</strong> for you to be healthy.

In the first sentence, the subject is “to catch trains,” which is an infinitive.

In the second sentence, the subject is “Swimming,” a gerund, which is a verb acting as a noun and ending in -ing.

In the third sentence, the noun clause “what I want” acts as the subject.

Note that, in all of these sentences, the nontraditional subjects take singular verbs.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Subjects in the forms of infinitives, gerunds, and noun phrases/clauses always take singular verbs.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at a few more examples:

> <u>Riding</u> in a car without a seatbelt on <strong>increases</strong> the likelihood of suffering injuries during a crash.

The nontraditional subject is the gerund “riding,” which takes the singular verb “increases.”

> <u>Failing</u> one test <strong>is</strong> not the end of the world.

The subject is the gerund “failing,” which takes the singular “is.”

> <u>Where I spend my afternoons</u> <strong>is</strong> none of your business.

The subject is “Where I spend my afternoons,” a noun clause, which takes the singular “is.”

> <u>To know you</u> <strong>is</strong> to love you.

The subject is the infinitive “To know you,” which takes the singular verb “is.”

> <u>That you have two dogs and four cats</u> <strong>is</strong> not a problem for me.

The noun clause “that you have two dogs and four cats” is the subject and takes the singular verb “is.”

> <u>That the Gentle Barn had gained the respect of animal lovers across the United States</u> <strong>was</strong> no surprise.

The noun clause “that the Gentle Barn had gained the respect of animal lovers across the United States” is the subject of the sentence and takes the singular verb “was.”

> <u>Whether the Holy Grail actually exists</u> <strong>has</strong> yet to be determined.

The noun clause “whether the Holy Grail actually exists” is the subject and takes the singular verb “has.”

> <u>To train for ultra-marathons during the winter months</u> <strong>is</strong> to spend many hours running alone in the cold, but the rewards can be sweet.

The infinitive “to train for ultra-marathons during the winter months” is the subject and requires the singular verb “is.”

##  Sentence Correction Example 26  

<u>While foraging for plant-based foods such as berries and kelp is not the primary means by which polar bears feed themselves, consuming these foods, which, though not calorie rich, contain key nutrients, is</u> beneficial to the bears’ health.

##### Question Level: medium



In all of the versions of the sentence in this question, there are multiple clauses.


The subject of the first clause in most versions is “foraging,” which is a nontraditional subject that requires a singular verb. We have to be careful not to be confused by the presence of the prepositional phrase “for plant-based foods such as berries and kelp” into thinking that the verb that goes with “foraging” should be plural.


Some of the other clauses that appear are the following:


the bears’ consuming these foods … is


by which polar bears feed themselves


bears consuming these foods … are


which, though not calorie rich, contain …”


To answer this question correctly, we have to notice subject-verb agreement issues in any of the clauses.


We also have to pay careful attention to meaning conveyed.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A)</b> <b>While foraging for plant-based foods such as berries and kelp is not the primary means by which polar bears feed themselves, consuming these foods, which, though not calorie rich, contain key nutrients, is
</b> beneficial to the bears’ health.



The verb in the first clause, “is,” agrees with the nontraditional subject “foraging for plant-based foods such as berries and kelp.”


Also, the verb in the main clause, “is,” agrees with the subject, “consuming these foods.”


So, this version is grammatically correct, and it effectively conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>With foraging for plant-based foods such as berries and kelp not being primarily a means by which polar bears feed themselves, consuming these foods, though not calorie rich, containing key nutrients
</b> beneficial to the bears’ health.



In this version, “not being primarily a means” conveys an illogical meaning. The point to be made is not that foraging for plant-based foods is not <em>primarily a means for polar bears to feed themselves</em> but, rather, primarily something else. The point is that foraging for plant-based foods is not <em>the primary means by which polar bears feed themselves</em>.


Meanwhile, since the only finite verb in this version, “feed,” is within the relative clause “by which polar bears feed themselves,” this version has no main verb and, thus, no independent clause. So, this version is not a complete sentence.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>Although foraging for plant-based foods such as berries and kelp are not the primary means by which polar bears feed themselves, consuming these foods, which, though not calorie rich, contain key nutrients
</b> beneficial to the bears’ health.



In this version, the plural “are” is incorrectly used with “foraging.”


Notice also that this version includes only a subordinate clause, “although foraging for plant-based foods such as berries and kelp are not the primary means by which polar bears feed themselves,” a phrase, “consuming these foods,” and a relative clause, “which, though not calorie rich, contain key nutrients beneficial to the bears’ health.” So, this version does not include an independent clause and, thus, is not a complete sentence.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>Even though foraging for plant-based foods such as berries and kelp is not primarily the means by which polar bears feed themselves, consuming these foods, though not calorie rich, contains key nutrients that are
</b> beneficial to the bears’ health.



This version is grammatically correct but does not convey a meaning that makes sense.


In this version, the verb in the first clause, “is,” agrees with “foraging.”


However, “is not primarily the means” conveys an illogical meaning. The point to be made is not that foraging for plant-based foods is not <em>primarily the means for polar bears to feed themselves</em> but, rather, primarily something else. The point is that foraging for plant-based foods is not <em>the primary means by which polar bears feed themselves</em>.


Furthermore, “consuming these foods, though not calorie rich, contains key nutrients” conveys the nonsensical meaning that “consuming these foods” is not calorie rich but contains key nutrients. Of course, the foods themselves, not consuming the foods, would be not calorie rich but contain nutrients.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>Though foraging for plant based-foods such as berries and kelp are not a primary means by which polar bears feed themselves, consuming these foods, which, though not calorie rich, provide key nutrients, are
</b> beneficial to the bears’ health.



In the first clause of this version, the plural “are” does not agree with the singular subject “foraging for plant-based foods.”


Also, in the main clause, the plural verb “are” does not agree with the singular subject “consuming.”


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)
