## Lesson Content
In order to ensure that the subject and finite verb of a clause agree, we must first identify these two parts of the clause.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The subject of a clause is the noun that is doing or being what the main verb expresses.
{{< /alert >}}

In order to determine which noun is doing or being what the finite verb expresses, and is thus the subject, first identify the action word in the clause, which is the main verb.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

To determine which noun is doing or being what the finite verb expresses, and is thus the subject, first identify the action word in the clause, which is the main verb.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s put our strategy to use to identify the subject in the following underlined clause:

> <u>If the boy wearing the green shirt wins the spelling bee,</u> his parents will be excited.

The action in the underlined clause is that someone or something wins.

For regular verbs, the rule is that we place an s at the end of a singular verb, and we drop the s at the end of a plural verb.

Since the subject of the clause, “boy,” is singular, the verb must also be singular, meaning it must end in s.

Let’s look at what happens when we make the subject plural:

> <u>If the boys wearing the green shirts win the spelling bee,</u> their parents will be excited.

Now, the subject of the clause is “boys.” Since the subject is plural, it must take a plural verb.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

For regular verbs, the rule is that we place an s at the end of a singular verb, and we drop the s at the end of a plural verb.
{{< /alert >}}

Here’s an example in which the subject of the clause is the noun that is being, rather than doing, what the main verb expresses:

> As the wind picked up, <u>the sailboats in the harbor on the south side of the island were rocked by waves</u>.

The main verb of the underlined independent clause is “were rocked.” To find the subject of the clause, we need to find the noun that names what was rocked, which is “sailboats.”

Remember, for a sentence to be correct, the subject and verb of each clause found in the sentence must agree.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

For a sentence to be correct, the subject and verb of each clause found in the sentence must agree.
{{< /alert >}}

### Targeted Practice Solutions

In many sentences and clauses, subjects and their verbs are very close together.

<u>Incorrect</u>: The kindergarten teacher had the children gather snowflakes to demonstrate that, like people, no two <strong>snowflakes is</strong> similar enough to justify labeling them exact replicas.

Notice that the noun “snowflakes” is plural, but the finite verb “is” is singular.

The kindergarten teacher had the children gather snowflakes to demonstrate that, like people, no two <strong>snowflakes</strong> <strong>are</strong> similar enough to justify labeling them exact replicas.

However, although fundamentally this concept of subject-verb agreement is simple, there are many sentence constructions that can appear in Sentence Correction questions that make noticing subject-verb agreement issues more challenging.

Let’s begin discussing these ways by looking at sentences in which prepositional phrases are placed between subjects and their verbs.
