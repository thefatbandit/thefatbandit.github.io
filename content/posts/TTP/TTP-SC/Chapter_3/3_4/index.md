## Lesson Content
Our ears have been trained to pair verbs with the nouns immediately preceding them, so we may make a mistake when a prepositional phrase separates a subject and its verb.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Our ears have been trained to pair verbs with the nouns immediately preceding them, so we may make a mistake when a prepositional phrase separates a subject and its verb.
{{< /alert >}}

As a quick refresher, remember that a prepositional phrase is a type of modifying phrase that begins with a preposition, such as “by,” “for,” or “in.”

> The hippopotamus is swimming <strong>in the lake</strong>.

The preposition “in” begins the prepositional phrase “in the lake,” which tells us where the hippo is swimming.

> The president is eating lunch <strong>with the vice president.</strong>

The preposition “with” begins the prepositional phrase “with the vice president,” which tells us with whom the president is eating lunch.

> <strong>In December</strong>, I always spend time walking<strong> along the river bank. </strong>

The preposition “in” begins the prepositional phrase “in December,” which tells us when the action in the sentence occurs.

> <strong>During the summer months</strong>, I partake <strong>in the sports of volleyball, paddle boarding, and surfing. </strong>

The preposition “during” begins the prepositional phrase “during the summer months,” which tells us when the action in the sentence occurs.

> The bear was running <strong>to the woods</strong>, where his favorite blueberry bushes are located.

The preposition “to” begins the prepositional phrase “to the woods,” which tells us to where the bear was running.

Ok – back to subject-verb agreement.

> The dispute about whether to get rid of the old books <strong>(has/have)</strong> the family in an uproar.

Notice the prepositional phrase “about whether to get rid of the old books.”

In this sentence, the noun that directly precedes the verb (has/have) is the plural “books.” Since our ears have been trained to pair verbs with the nouns immediately preceding them, it may sound correct to say:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The dispute about whether to get rid of the old books <strong>have</strong> the family in an uproar.

However, the books are not performing the action in this sentence; what is causing the uproar is “the dispute.” If we ignore the prepositional phrase that separates the subject from the finite verb, we can see that there is a subject-verb agreement problem when we use the plural verb “have”:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The <strong>dispute</strong> <s>about whether to get rid of the old books</s> <strong>have</strong> the family in an uproar.

The singular subject “dispute” does not agree with the plural finite verb “have.” In order for the subject and verb to agree, we must use the singular verb “has,” as demonstrated below:

> The <strong>dispute</strong> about whether to get rid of the old books <strong>has</strong> the family in an uproar.

Although we’ll see a few important exceptions shortly, a very good general strategy for checking for subject-verb agreement is to ignore a prepositional phrase found between a subject and its finite verb.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In checking for subject-verb agreement, a very good general strategy is to ignore a prepositional phrase that comes between a subject and its finite verb. We’ll discuss a few important exceptions a bit later in the chapter.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at another example sentence:

> The once beautiful house with the black shutters and the grey, sloping roof made of marble and numerous composites <strong>(has/have)</strong> been neglected for some time, but the right buyer could return the house to its previous splendor.

Because a list of nouns ending with the plural noun “composites” immediately precedes the finite verb (has/have), our ear may tell us that the correct sentence is:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The once beautiful house with the black shutters and the grey, sloping roof made of marble and numerous composites <strong>have</strong> been neglected for some time, but the right buyer could return the house to its previous splendor.

However, the intention of the sentence is not to say that shutters and composites have been neglected.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The once beautiful <strong>house</strong> <s>with the black shutters and the grey, sloping roof made of marble and numerous composites</s> <strong>have</strong> been neglected for some time, but the right buyer could return the house to its previous splendor.

To be grammatically correct, the sentence must read:

> The once beautiful <strong>house </strong>with the black shutters and the grey, sloping roof made of marble and numerous composites <strong>has </strong>been neglected for some time, but the right buyer could return the house to its previous splendor.

Students often ask, “What if the subject of a clause is found within a prepositional phrase and we cross off that prepositional phrase?” The good news is that, with the exception of some special situations that we’ll discuss later on, the subject of a clause will not be found in a prepositional phrase.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

With the exception of some special situations that we’ll discuss later on, the subject of a clause will not be found in a prepositional phrase.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 1  

Including lots of evidence supporting their claims, the <u>scientists’ presentation on the issues related to microbes that survive on the exteriors of space vehicles are expected that they will</u> catalyze changes in standard operating procedures.

1. [ ] (A) scientists’ presentation on the issues related to microbes that survive on the exteriors of space vehicles are expected that they will
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) scientists presented on the issues related to microbes that survive on the exteriors of space vehicles are expected that they will
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) scientists, in their presentation on the issues related to microbes that survive on the exteriors of space vehicles, expecting to
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) scientists’ presentation on the issues related to microbes that survive on the exteriors of space vehicles is expected to
5. [ ] (E) scientists presenting on the issues related to microbes that survive on the exteriors of space vehicles and expecting to
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



To answer this question correctly, we have to make sure that we don’t get locked into looking for a version that expresses a particular meaning, because the versions convey a variety of meanings. So, we have to simply find the one choice that effectively expresses a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(A)</b> Including lots of evidence supporting their claims, the <b>scientists’ presentation on the issues related to microbes that survive on the exteriors of space vehicles are expected that they will
</b> catalyze changes in standard operating procedures.



In this version, the subject of the sentence is the singular “presentation,” which does not agree with the main verb, the plural “are.”


Also, “are expected that they will” is an illogical jumble of two common expressions, “are expected to” and “it is expected that.”


> <b>(B)</b> Including lots of evidence supporting their claims, the <b>scientists presented on the issues related to microbes that survive on the exteriors of space vehicles are expected that they will
</b> catalyze changes in standard operating procedures.



In this version, the plural subject, “scientists” agrees with the plural verb “are.”


However, notice what we have if we remove the prepositional phrases in the middle of the sentence:


the scientists presented <s>on the issues related to microbes that survive on the exteriors of space vehicles</s> are expected that they will catalyze changes


we are left with,


the scientists presented … are expected that they will catalyze changes


The meaning conveyed is that the scientists themselves were presented, which does not make sense.


Also, “are expected that they will” is an illogical jumble of two common expressions, “are expected to” and “it is expected that.”


> <b>(C)</b> Including lots of evidence supporting their claims, the <b>scientists, in their presentation on the issues related to microbes that survive on the exteriors of space vehicles, expecting to
</b> catalyze changes in standard operating procedures.



In this version, “scientists” is followed by a prepositional phrase, “in their presentation …” and then a non-finite verb “expecting.” Thus, this version has no main verb. In other words, this version lacks an independent clause; without an independent clause, there can be no sentence.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D)</b> Including lots of evidence supporting their claims, the <b>scientists’ presentation on the issues related to microbes that survive on the exteriors of space vehicles is expected to
</b> catalyze changes in standard operating procedures.



In this version, the subject, “presentation,” agrees with the main verb, “is expected.”


Also, “is expected to catalyze” is logical and is idiomatically correct.


(Some people wonder whether the opening wording of this version, “Including lots of evidence supporting their claims,” works correctly with “the scientists’ presentation.” It does, and if you think about it, you can see why. It’s logical that, “the <strong>scientists’</strong> presentation” would be “including lots of evidence supporting <strong>their</strong> claims.”)


> <b>(E)</b> Including lots of evidence supporting their claims, the <b>scientists presenting on the issues related to microbes that survive on the exteriors of space vehicles and expecting to
</b> catalyze changes in standard operating procedures.



In this version, “presenting” and “expecting” are not finite verbs. Thus, there is no main verb to go with the sentence subject “scientists.” In other words, this version lacks an independent clause; without an independent clause, there can be no sentence.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Sentence Correction Example 2  

The range of the ideas <u>for solving the problems of the native peoples continue to expand as more people with relevant expertise has become</u> involved in the matter.

1. [ ] (A) for solving the problems of the native peoples continue to expand as more people with relevant expertise has become
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) to solve the problems of the native peoples are continuing to expand, with more people with relevant expertise becoming
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) to solve the problems of the native peoples has continued an expansion because of more people whose relevant expertise is becoming
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) for solving the problems of the native peoples, expanding continuously because of more people with relevant expertise becoming
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) for solving the problems of the native peoples continues to expand as more people with relevant expertise become

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



There are two clauses in the correct version of this sentence.


In this first clause of the sentence, one of the plural nouns “ideas” and “problems” could seem to be the subject. However, the subject of the first clause is actually the singular “range.”


Notice the placement of the prepositional phrases “of the ideas for solving the problems of the native peoples” between the subject of the first clause and its finite verb. For the purposes of determining subject-verb agreement, it will be helpful to cross off these prepositional phrases:


The range <s>of the ideas for solving the problems of the native peoples</s> continue


In doing so, we can easily see that the plural verb “continue” does not agree with the singular subject “range.”


The subject of the second clause of the sentence is the plural “people.”


> <b>(A)</b> The range of the ideas <b>for solving the problems of the native peoples continue to expand as more people with relevant expertise has become
</b> involved in the matter.



The main verb of the first clause in this version is “continue,” which is plural and, therefore, does not agree with the subject of the first clause, “range.”


Also, in the second clause, the plural “people” does not agree with the singular “has.”


> <b>(B)</b> The range of the ideas <b>to solve the problems of the native peoples are continuing to expand, with more people with relevant expertise becoming
</b> involved in the matter.



The main verb of the first clause in the sentence is “are continuing,” which is plural and, therefore, does not agree with the subject of the first clause, “range.”


> <b>(C)</b> The range of the ideas <b>to solve the problems of the native peoples has continued an expansion because of more people whose relevant expertise is becoming
</b> involved in the matter.



In this version, the subject, “range,’’ and the main verb “has continued,” agree. However, “The range of ideas … has continued an expansion” does not really make sense, as it seems to convey that the range itself is somehow actively continuing an expansion, and it does not clearly convey that it is the range that is expanding.


Also, this version does not actually convey that more people are becoming involved. Rather it conveys the nonsensical meaning that people’s expertise is becoming involved.


> <b>(D)</b> The range of the ideas <b>for solving the problems of the native peoples, expanding continuously because of more people with relevant expertise becoming
</b> involved in the matter.



This version has no main verb. Instead it has “solving,” “expanding,” and “becoming,” none of which is a finite verb that can serve as a main verb. In other words, this version lacks an independent clause; without an independent clause, there can be no sentence.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E)</b> The range of the ideas <b>for solving the problems of the native peoples continues to expand as more people with relevant expertise become
</b> involved in the matter.



In this version, the subject of the first clause, “range,” agrees with the main verb, “continues.”


Also, in the second clause, “people” agrees with “become,” and the second clause conveys a logical meaning, that more people are becoming involved in the matter.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
