## Lesson Content
An appositive is an element of a sentence that modifies or further describes another element of a sentence by renaming it.

> My brother <strong>John</strong> loves hiking in the Ozark mountains.

In the above example, the noun “brother” is a general noun.

Let’s consider another example that uses an appositive:

> The uninvited guest, <strong>a large spider</strong>, is creating a web above my television.

In this example, “a large spider” is an appositive that further describes the noun “uninvited guest.” Notice that “a large spider” is simply a renaming of “uninvited guest.” Both name the same thing.

> Cyrus the Great, <strong>founder of the Achaemenid empire and creator of the first bill of rights</strong>, was married to Cassandane Shahbanu.

The appositive “founder of the Achaemenid empire and creator of the first bill of rights” further describes the noun “Cyrus the Great.” Notice that “founder of the Achaemenid empire and creator of the first bill of rights” is simply a renaming of “Cyrus the Great.” Both name the same person.

> Inflation, <strong>the increase over time in the costs of an economy’s goods and services</strong>, is the most damaging of all taxes, intended or unintended, that a government can burden the people of a nation with.

The appositive “the increase over time in the cost of an economy’s goods and services” further describes the noun “inflation.” Notice that “the increase over time in the cost of an economy’s goods and services” is simply a renaming of “inflation.” Both name the same thing.

We’ll talk in much more detail about appositives in the Modifiers chapter, but for now, we’ll examine how appositives can be used to create confusion about subject-verb agreement.

Consider the following sentence:

> Cyrus the Great, <strong>founder of the Achaemenid empire and creator of the first bill of rights</strong>, (was/were) married to Cassandane Shahbanu.

Notice that the appositive “founder of the Achaemenid empire and creator of the first bill of rights” separates the sentence’s subject, “Cyrus the Great,” from its finite verb (was/were).

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Cyrus the Great, founder of the Achaemenid empire and creator of the first bill of rights, <strong>were</strong> married to Cassandane Shahbanu.

However, by crossing off the appositive, we can see that “were” would not agree in number with the sentence’s actual subject, “Cyrus the Great,” which is singular:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Cyrus the Great<s>, founder of the Achaemenid empire and creator of the first bill of rights,</s> <strong>were</strong> married to Cassandane Shahbanu.

Clearly, the sentence should be:

> <strong>Cyrus the Great</strong>, founder of the Achaemenid empire and creator of the first bill of rights, <strong>was</strong> married to Cassandane Shahbanu.

Let’s also examine the following sentence:

> Inflation<strong>, the increase over time in the costs of an economy’s goods and services,</strong> (is/are) the most damaging of all taxes, intended or unintended, that a government can burden the people of a nation with.

Notice that the appositive “the increase over time in the cost of an economy’s goods and services” separates the sentence’s subject, “inflation,” from its finite verb (is/are).

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Inflation, the increase over time in the costs of an economy’s goods and services, <strong>are </strong>the most damaging of all taxes, intended or unintended, that a government can burden the people of a nation with.

However, by crossing off the appositive, we can see that “are” would not agree in number with the sentence’s subject, “inflation,” which is singular:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Inflation<s>, the increase over time in the costs of an economy’s goods and services,</s> <strong>are </strong>the most damaging of all taxes, intended or unintended, that a government can burden the people of a nation with.

Clearly, the sentence should be:

> <strong>Inflation</strong>, the increase over time in the costs of an economy’s goods and services, <strong>is </strong>the most damaging of all taxes, intended or unintended, that a government can burden the people of a nation with.

Consider the following sentence:

> The new candles, delicate creations made of a berry scented wax, <strong>(adds/add)</strong> a nice bit of color to the décor of the dining room.

Notice the appositive:

> delicate creations made of a berry scented wax

This appositive renames the noun “candles.” In other words, “candles” and “delicate creations made of a berry scented wax” name the same thing.

Also, notice that the appositive separates the noun “candles” from its finite verb (adds/add).

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The new candles, delicate creations made of a berry scented wax, <strong>adds</strong> a nice bit of color to the décor of the dining room.

However, by crossing off the appositive, we can see that “adds” would not agree in number with the sentence’s subject, “candles,” which is plural:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The new candles<s>, delicate creations made of a berry scented wax,</s> <strong>adds</strong> a nice bit of color to the décor of the dining room.

Clearly, the sentence should be:

> The new <strong>candles</strong>, delicate creations made of a berry scented wax, <strong>add</strong> a nice bit of color to the décor of the dining room.

When checking for subject-verb agreement, we can more easily identify errors by ignoring any appositives that separate the subject and its finite verb.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When checking for subject-verb agreement, ignore any appositives that separate a subject and its finite verb.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at a more complicated sentence:

> The Tesla Model S, the only car currently on the road equipped with features that create a positive pressure environment inside the passenger cabin, keeping dust and other harmful airborne particles, such as viruses and bacteria, from reaching the respiratory passages of the occupants, (is/are) receiving rave reviews from leading car enthusiasts.

Notice that we have a long appositive that contains a number of plural nouns between “Tesla Model S,” the subject, from (is/are), the finite verb:

> the only car currently on the road equipped with features that create a positive pressure environment inside the passenger cabin, keeping dust and other harmful airborne particles, such as viruses and bacteria, from reaching the respiratory passages of the occupants

Because the noun just before the finite verb is the plural “occupants,” and because there are other plural nouns in the appositive, our ears may tell us that the correct verb should be “are.” To avoid making such a mistake, we cross off the appositive that comes between the subject and verb:

> The <strong>Tesla Model S</strong><s>, the only car currently on the road equipped with features that create a positive pressure environment inside the passenger cabin, keeping dust and other harmful airborne particles, such as viruses and bacteria, from reaching the respiratory passages of the occupants,</s> (<strong>is/are</strong>) receiving rave reviews from leading car enthusiasts.

We can clearly see that the correct verb must be “is.”

Thus, the correct sentence is:

> The <strong>Tesla Model S</strong>, the only car currently on the road equipped with features that create a positive pressure environment inside the passenger cabin, keeping dust and other harmful airborne particles, such as viruses and bacteria, from reaching the respiratory passages of the occupants, <strong>is</strong> receiving rave reviews from leading car enthusiasts.

##  Sentence Correction Example 3  

The passage of new tax laws, a comprehensive overhaul to the code involving reductions in corporate rates and new deductions for individuals and <u>couples, has made many citizens, who are</u> scrambling to determine whether they should form pass-through entities to take advantage of the changes.

1. [ ] (A) couples, has made many citizens, who are
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) couples, has many citizens
3. [ ] (C) couples, have many citizens, who are
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) couples and having many citizens
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) couples, have many citizens
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

##### Question Level: medium



The subject of the sentence is the singular “passage.”


Between “passage” and the main verb of the sentence are, first, a prepositional phrase, “of new tax laws,” and then, an appositive, “a comprehensive overhaul … individuals and couples.”


Since the prepositional phrase and appositive include several plural nouns, “laws,” “reductions,” “rates,” “deductions,” “individuals,” and “couples,” we could be confused into thinking that the main verb of the sentence should be plural.


However, since none of those plural nouns is the subject, the main verb of the sentence must be singular in order to agree with the sentence’s subject, “passage.<em>”</em>


To make our lives easier, we can cross off the prepositional phrase and the appositive:


The passage <s>of new tax laws, a comprehensive overhaul to the code involving reductions in corporate rates and new deductions for individuals and <u>couples</u></s><u>, has made many citizens, who are</u> scrambling to determine whether they should form pass-through entities to take advantage of the changes.


> <b>(A)</b> The passage of new tax laws, a comprehensive overhaul to the code involving reductions in corporate rates and new deductions for individuals and <b>couples, has made many citizens, who are
</b> scrambling to determine whether they should form pass-through entities to take advantage of the changes.



 This version uses the singular “has,” which agrees with the subject, “passage.” However, this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that the passage of laws has “made many citizens.” Passage of laws would not have made citizens.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B)</b> The passage of new tax laws, a comprehensive overhaul to the code involving reductions in corporate rates and new deductions for individuals and <b>couples, has many citizens
</b> scrambling to determine whether they should form pass-through entities to take advantage of the changes.



This version uses the singular “has,” which agrees with the subject, “passage.”


It also clearly conveys the logical meaning that the passage of the laws has many citizens scrambling to determine whether they should form pass-through entities.


> <b>(C)</b> The passage of new tax laws, a comprehensive overhaul to the code involving reductions in corporate rates and new deductions for individuals and <b>couples, have many citizens, who are
</b> scrambling to determine whether they should form pass-through entities to take advantage of the changes.



The plural verb “have” does not agree with the singular subject “passage.”


Furthermore, this version nonsensically conveys that the passage of the laws “have many citizens,” as if the passage of the laws possesses citizens who are scrambling to determine whether they should form pass-through entities.


> <b>(D)</b> The passage of new tax laws, a comprehensive overhaul to the code involving reductions in corporate rates and new deductions for individuals and <b>couples and having many citizens
</b> scrambling to determine whether they should form pass-through entities to take advantage of the changes.



This choice simply extends the appositive that begins with “a comprehensive” to create a long appositive, “a comprehensive overhaul to the code involving reductions in corporate rates and new deductions for individuals and couples and having many citizens scrambling to determine ….”


So, this version is composed of only “the passage of new tax laws” and an appositive that renames “the passage of new tax laws.”


Thus, this version has no main verb and or independent clause; without an independent clause, there can be no sentence.


> <b>(E)</b> The passage of new tax laws, a comprehensive overhaul to the code involving reductions in corporate rates and new deductions for individuals and <b>couples, have many citizens
</b> scrambling to determine whether they should form pass-through entities to take advantage of the changes.



In this version, the use of the plural verb “have” could seem to be correct, since one of the plural nouns between “passage” and the verb “have” could seem to be the subject of “have.” However, since the subject is actually the singular “passage,” the plural “have” does not agree with the subject.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
