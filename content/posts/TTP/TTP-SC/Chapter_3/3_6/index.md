## Lesson Content
We already know that a relative clause is a clause that begins with a relative pronoun, such as “that,” “which,” “when,” “where,” “who,” or “whom.” The following are examples of relative clauses:

> that will be fun for everyone

> which is the longest river in Asia

> who is the nicest woman we know

> where the black rhinoceros prefers to sleep

> when the music stops playing

A relative clause can be placed between a subject and its finite verb to make catching subject-verb agreement issues more challenging.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A relative clause can be placed between a subject and its finite verb to make catching subject-verb agreement issues more challenging.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following sentence:

The horse that has a spot on its chest and long, muscular legs (is/are) moving quickly.

Notice that the relative clause “that has a spot on its chest and long, muscular legs” is separating the subject, “horse,” from its finite verb, (is/are).

However, the legs are not moving quickly; the horse is.

> The <strong>horse</strong> <s>that has a spot on its chest and long, muscular legs</s> <strong>is</strong> moving quickly.

Let’s consider another one:

> The woman who is talking with the senators and the staffers (seems/seem) quite knowledgeable.

The relative clause “who is talking with the senators and the staffers” is separating the subject, “woman,” from its finite verb, (seems/seem).

However, the senators and staffers are not who seems quite knowledgeable.

> The <strong>woman</strong> <s>who is talking with the senators and the staffers</s> <strong>seems</strong> quite knowledgeable.

Thus, for checking for subject-verb agreement, a good strategy is to ignore any relative clauses that separate a subject and its finite verb.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When checking for subject-verb agreement, ignore any relative clauses that separate a subject and its finite verb.

##  Sentence Correction Example 4  

The Baroque style that dominated European art and architecture in the 17th and 18th centuries <u>and exemplified the work of composers such as Bach, Vivaldi, and Handel, who produced some of the most admired classical pieces, remaining</u> an important development in music for scholars and enthusiasts alike.

1. [ ] (A) and exemplified the work of composers such as Bach, Vivaldi, and Handel, who produced some of the most admired classical pieces, remaining
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) are exemplified by the work of composers such as Bach, Vivaldi, and Handel, who produced some of the most admired classical pieces, and remain
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) and was exemplified by the work of composers such as Bach, Vivaldi, and Handel, who produced some of the most admired classical pieces, remaining
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) is exemplified by the work of composers such as Bach, Vivaldi, and Handel, who produced some of the most admired classical pieces, and remains
5. [ ] (E) was exemplified by the work of composers such as Bach, Vivaldi, and Handel, who produced some of the most admired classical pieces, and remain
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

##### Question Level: easy



The subject of this sentence, “Baroque style,” is separated from the main verb by a very long relative clause, which begins with “that” and runs all the way to “pieces.”


So, correctly answering this question requires correctly identifying the subject-main verb pair and avoiding being thrown off by multiple aspects of the choices that obscure this subject-verb relationship and make incorrect choices seem correct.


> <b>(A)</b> The Baroque style that dominated European art and architecture in the 17th and 18th centuries <b>and exemplified the work of composers such as Bach, Vivaldi, and Handel, who produced some of the most admired classical pieces, remaining
</b> an important development in music for scholars and enthusiasts alike.



In this version, everything that follows “style” is part of a long relative clause, “that dominated …,” that modifies “style.” So, there is no main verb. In other words, this version lacks an independent clause; without an independent clause, there can be no sentence.


> <b>(B)</b> The Baroque style that dominated European art and architecture in the 17th and 18th centuries <b>are exemplified by the work of composers such as Bach, Vivaldi, and Handel, who produced some of the most admired classical pieces, and remain
</b> an important development in music for scholars and enthusiasts alike.



In this version, the logical subject is the singular “Baroque style.” The verbs, “are exemplified,” and “remain” do not agree with the singular subject, as we can more easily see by crossing off the long relative clauses and the prepositional phrase that separate the subject from the main verbs:


The Baroque <strong>style</strong> <s>that dominated European art and architecture in the 17th and 18th centuries</s> <strong>are exemplified</strong> <s>by the work of composers such as Bach, Vivaldi, and Handel, who produced some of the most admired classical pieces,</s> and <strong>remain</strong> an important development in music for scholars and enthusiasts alike.


While “remain” may seem to be part of the relative clause, “who produced …,” modifying “composers such as Bach, Vivaldi, and Handel,” it does not make sense that composers would “remain an important development in music.” Thus, it must be that “The Baroque style” is the “development in music,” but the plural “remain” does not agree with the singular “Baroque style.”


> <b>(C)</b> The Baroque style that dominated European art and architecture in the 17th and 18th centuries <b>and was exemplified by the work of composers such as Bach, Vivaldi, and Handel, who produced some of the most admired classical pieces, remaining
</b> an important development in music for scholars and enthusiasts alike.



In this version, everything that follows “style” is part of a long relative clause that begins with “that.” So, this version has no main verb to go with the subject “style” and, thus, is not a complete sentence, as we can easily see if we cross off the relative clause:


The Baroque <strong>style</strong> <s>that dominated European art and architecture in the 17th and 18th centuries and was exemplified by the work of composers such as Bach, Vivaldi, and Handel, who produced some of the most admired classical pieces, remaining an important development in music for scholars and enthusiasts alike</s>.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D)</b> The Baroque style that dominated European art and architecture in the 17th and 18th centuries <b>is exemplified by the work of composers such as Bach, Vivaldi, and Handel, who produced some of the most admired classical pieces, and remains
</b> an important development in music for scholars and enthusiasts alike.



In this version, “is exemplified” agrees with the singular subject “Baroque style.”


Also, further along in the sentence, “remains” agrees with “Baroque style.”


Thus, the sentence is grammatically correct and clearly conveys that the Baroque style is exemplified by the work of the composers mentioned and remains an important musical development.


> <b>(E)</b> The Baroque style that dominated European art and architecture in the 17th and 18th centuries <b>was exemplified by the work of composers such as Bach, Vivaldi, and Handel, who produced some of the most admired classical pieces, and remain
</b> an important development in music for scholars and enthusiasts alike.



In this version, “was exemplified” agrees with the singular subject, “Baroque style.”


At the same time, the past tense “was exemplified” could be read as suggesting that the work of the composers mentioned no longer exemplifies the Baroque style, which meaning does not make complete sense. Of course, that rather minor meaning issue would not constitute a clear reason to eliminate this choice.


At the same time, this version is clearly incorrect for the following reason.


While “remain” may seem to be part of the relative clause “who produced …,” it does not make sense that “composers, who produced …” would “remain an important development in music.” Thus, it must be that it is “the Baroque style” that remains “an important development in music,” but the plural “remain” does not agree with the singular “Baroque style.”


In order to catch this key flaw, we have to be careful to consider the non-underlined portion of the sentence.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Sentence Correction Example 5  

In keeping with the company’s multiple missions, the design of the <u>buildings, which house the product development and design departments, intending to both support sustainability and inspire</u> creativity.

1. [ ] (A) buildings, which house the product development and design departments, intending to both support sustainability and inspire
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) buildings that house the product development and design departments are intended for both the support of sustainability and the inspiration of
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) buildings that house the product development and design departments is intended to both support sustainability and inspire
4. [ ] (D) buildings, which house the product development and design departments, are intended to both support sustainability and inspire
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) buildings, which are houses to the product development and design departments, intends to both support sustainability and inspire
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



In order to answer this question, we have to clearly see that the subject of the sentence is “design” and that, therefore, the main verb of the sentence has to be singular.<br/><br/>
Identifying this subject-verb relationship is made challenging by multiple moves made by the question writer:


- The original version has no main verb and is constructed to leave the test-taker without a clear sense of which noun and verb will serve as the subject and main verb of the correct version.


- The subject, “design,” is separated from the main verb by a prepositional phrase, “of the buildings,” and a relative clause, “that house the product development and design departments.” Furthermore, both of these structures end with plural nouns, “buildings” and “departments,” tempting us perceive that the subject of the sentence is plural, rather than singular.


- The positioning of the cutoff between the non-underlined portion and the underlined portion of the sentence is such that “buildings” appears at the beginning of the answer choices, making “buildings” appear to be the subject of the sentence.


By seeing through these moves meant to obscure the relationship between the subject of the sentence and the main verb, we can eliminate the choices that have subject-verb agreement issues.


> <b>(A)</b> In keeping with the company’s multiple missions, the design of the <b>buildings, which house the product development and design departments, intending to both support sustainability and inspire
</b> creativity.



A relative clause beginning with “which” follows what could be the subject of the sentence, “the design of the buildings.”


Then, after the relative clause is a verb, “intending.” Since “intending” is not a finite verb, it cannot on its own serve as the main verb of the sentence. Thus, this version has no main verb to go with the sentence subject “the design” and is not a complete sentence. In other words, this version lacks an independent clause; without an independent clause, there can be no sentence.


Also, this version does not clearly convey whether it is the design of the buildings or the buildings themselves that are “intending to both support sustainability and inspire creativity.”


> <b>(B)</b> In keeping with the company’s multiple missions, the design of the <b>buildings that house the product development and design departments are intended for both the support of sustainability and the inspiration of
</b> creativity.



The singular subject “design” does not agree with the plural verb “are,” as we can more easily see by crossing off the prepositional phrase, “of the buildings,” and relative clause, “that house …,” that appear between “design” and “are”:


the <strong>design</strong> <s>of the buildings that house the product development and design departments</s> <strong>are</strong> intended for both the support of sustainability and the inspiration of creativity


Notice that this choice is written to seem to be correct, as, if we read this choice without considering the non-underlined portion, the plural “buildings” seems to be the subject of the plural “are.”


To correctly answer Sentence Correction questions, we have to be careful to keep the non-underlined portions of the sentences in mind.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> In keeping with the company’s multiple missions, the design of the <b>buildings that house the product development and design departments is intended to both support sustainability and inspire
</b> creativity.



In this version, the singular verb “is” agrees with the singular “design,” and this version is otherwise grammatically correct and conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(D)</b> In keeping with the company’s multiple missions, the design of the <b>buildings, which house the product development and design departments, are intended to both support sustainability and inspire
</b> creativity.



The plural verb “are” does not agree with the singular subject “design.”<br/><br/>
Notice that this choice is written to seem to be correct, as, if we read this choice without considering the non-underlined portion, the plural “buildings” seems to be the subject of the plural “are.” Also, the relative clause “which house … departments” is constructed to look blatantly correct.


To correctly answer Sentence Correction questions, we have to be careful to keep the non-underlined portions of the sentences in mind.


> <b>(E)</b> In keeping with the company’s multiple missions, the design of the <b>buildings, which are houses to the product development and design departments, intends to both support sustainability and inspire
</b> creativity.



Because the verb “intends” agrees with the subject “design,” this version is grammatically correct. However, this version conveys ideas that don’t make sense.


“Which are houses to the product development and design departments,” conveys the somewhat nonsensical meaning that, to these departments, but perhaps not to other departments, the buildings are houses.


Also, “the design ... intends to” conveys the illogical idea that the design itself is, basically, planning to do something.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)
