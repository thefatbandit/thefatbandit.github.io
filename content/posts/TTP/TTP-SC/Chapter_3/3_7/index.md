## Lesson Content
A compound subject is a subject composed of multiple nouns joined by the coordinating conjunction “and” or “or.”

> The following examples have compound subjects (bolded):

> <strong>The sidewalk and the street</strong> were damaged by the earthquake.

The largest of the existing land animals, <strong>elephants and rhinoceroses</strong> are herbivores.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A compound subject is a subject composed of multiple nouns joined by the coordinating conjunction “and” or “or.”
{{< /alert >}}

Sometimes compound subjects require plural verbs, but other times they do not.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Sometimes compound subjects require plural verbs, but other times they do not.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s explore this idea further.

> The <strong>boy</strong> <strong>and</strong> the<strong> girl</strong> <strong>walk </strong>hand in hand to school every day.

In the above sentence, we have two singular nouns: “boy” and “girl.” Because they are joined by “and,” we know that there are two people who are performing the action in the sentence.

Compound subjects consisting of plural nouns follow the same rule, as illustrated below:

> The <strong>boys</strong> <strong>and</strong> the <strong>girls</strong> <strong>walk</strong> hand in hand to school every day.

Notice that “boys” and “girls” are both plural nouns.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Normally, when two singular nouns are joined by “and,” they form a plural subject and therefore demand a plural verb. Normally, compound subjects consisting of plural nouns follow the same rule.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at a few more examples.

> At this university, <strong>professors and research assistants</strong> work as a close-knit team on government-funded scientific research.

The sentence contains a compound subject composed of two plural nouns, “professors” and “research assistants.” The plural verb “work” agrees with the compound subject.

> The <strong>style and color</strong> that the bride chose for the bridesmaids’ dresses were not my first choices, but this is her big day, so ultimately, the decisions are hers.

The sentence contains a compound subject composed of two singular nouns, “style” and “color.” The plural verb “were” agrees with the compound subject.

> Although <strong>math knowledge and science knowledge</strong> often lead to lucrative careers, <strong>reading literature and studying philosophy</strong> help students to develop important critical-thinking skills, which employers also value.

The example above contains two compound subjects: “math knowledge and science knowledge” and “reading literature and studying philosophy.” Each requires a plural verb.

Sometimes, however, two nouns joined by “and” may seem to form a compound subject but actually name one entity.

Take, for instance, the following sentence:

> The <strong>bed and breakfast is</strong> completely booked for the holiday weekend.

In this case, there are two nouns in the subject: “bed” and “breakfast.” However, the expression “bed and breakfast” refers to a single entity, a type of place to stay, and therefore requires a singular verb (is).

It would not be correct to say:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The <strong>bed and breakfast are</strong> completely booked for the holiday weekend.

We can see from the context of the sentence that we are not referring to one bed being booked and one breakfast being booked (i.e., two separate things), but rather we are referring to a single place: “the bed and breakfast.”

Let’s look at some other examples of this type of subject.

> While <strong>rice and beans is</strong> a popular dish in many parts of the world, some people prefer to combine beans with noodles.

The sentence refers to the popular combination “rice and beans” – in other words, one dish.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Subjects composed of two nouns joined by the coordinating conjunction “and” take a singular verb when the two nouns name a single entity, such as “bed and breakfast,” or “rice and beans.”
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 6  

Stop and frisk, which involved unprompted searches and questioning of civilians on the street by police officers, <u>ruled unconstitutional by a U.S. District Court judge; this decision effectively put</u> an end to this controversial law enforcement practice.

1. [ ] (A) ruled unconstitutional by a U.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) were ruled unconstitutional by a U.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) were ruled unconstitutional by a U.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) was ruled unconstitutional by a U.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) was ruled unconstitutional by a U.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



The subject of the sentence is “stop and frisk,” which is later in the sentence termed a “practice.” Since a practice is a singular entity, a singular verb is necessary.


Notice that the subject is separated from the main verb by a long relative clause that contains multiple plural nouns: “searches,” “civilians,” and “police officers.” So, we have to be careful not to get fooled by the presence of those plural nouns into choosing a plural verb.


> <b>(A)</b> Stop and frisk, which involved unprompted searches and questioning of civilians on the street by police officers, <b>ruled unconstitutional by a U. S. District Court judge; this decision effectively put
</b> an end to this controversial law enforcement practice.



When a semicolon connects two parts of a sentence that express ideas, both of the parts of the sentence that are connected by the semicolon must be complete clauses.


In this case, however, the part of the sentence that precedes the semicolon has no main verb, since “ruled” cannot be the main verb. Stop and frisk would have been ruled unconstitutional. Stop and frisk did not somehow rule itself unconstitutional by a judge. So, the part that precedes the semicolon is not a complete clause.


> <b>(B)</b> Stop and frisk, which involved unprompted searches and questioning of civilians on the street by police officers, <b>were ruled unconstitutional by a U. S. District Court judge and were a decision that effectively put
</b> an end to this controversial law enforcement practice.



This choice can be eliminated because it uses the plural “were,” which does not agree with the singular subject, “stop and frisk.” Also, this version nonsensically conveys that stop and frisk “were a decision.”


> <b>(C)</b> Stop and frisk, which involved unprompted searches and questioning of civilians on the street by police officers, <b>were ruled unconstitutional by a U. S. District Court judge, and effectively, by this decision, put
</b> an end to this controversial law enforcement practice.



This choice can be eliminated because it uses the plural “were,” which does not agree with the singular subject, “stop and frisk.” Also, this version nonsensically conveys that stop and frisk effectively put an end to itself.


> <b>(D)</b> Stop and frisk, which involved unprompted searches and questioning of civilians on the street by police officers, <b>was ruled unconstitutional by a U. S. District Court judge and was a decision effectively putting
</b> an end to this controversial law enforcement practice.



This choice correctly uses the singular “was,” which agrees with the singular subject, “stop and frisk.” However, this version nonsensically conveys that stop and frisk was a decision that put an end to itself.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E)</b> Stop and frisk, which involved unprompted searches and questioning of civilians on the street by police officers, <b>was ruled unconstitutional by a U. S. District Court judge, a decision effectively putting
</b> an end to this controversial law enforcement practice.



This choice correctly uses the singular “was,” which agrees with the singular subject, “stop and frisk,” and this version clearly conveys that the judge’s decision effectively put an end to the practice.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
