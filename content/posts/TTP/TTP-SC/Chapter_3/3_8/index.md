## Lesson Content
In the last section, we saw that the use of the word “and” can result in the creation of a compound plural subject that requires a plural verb.

In this section, we’re going to discuss additive phrases.

What we’ll see as we discuss additive phrases is that, even though expressions such as “along with” or “as well as” resemble “and,” additive phrases don’t work the way “and” works with sentence subjects.

For example, the following sentence contains an additive phrase that begins with “in addition to.”

> Estella, <strong>in addition to Frisky and Molly</strong>, was sleeping on the soft couch.

Some of you are thinking that that sentence contains an error, that the main verb should be “were sleeping.”

However, the key thing to understand about additive phrases is that, unlike the word “and,” additive phrases do not create plural subjects.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: <strong>Estella</strong>, in addition to Frisky and Molly, <strong>were</strong> sleeping on the soft couch.

Even though “Estella” is followed by “in addition to Frisky and Molly,” “Estella” is a singular subject that requires the singular verb “was,” not the plural verb “were.

Let’s look at another:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The <strong>mayor</strong>, together with all of the members of the city council, <strong>are</strong> out to lunch at the moment.

The singular subject “mayor” does not become a plural subject when used in conjunction with the additive phrase “together with all of the members of the city council.” The correct sentence is:

> The <strong>mayor</strong>, together with all of the members of the city council, <strong>is</strong> out to lunch at the moment.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Additive phrases, which are phrases that begin with structures such as “along with,” “as well as,” “in addition to,” “accompanied by,” or “together with,” do not create plural subjects. A singular subject followed by an additive phrase remains singular.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at an another example:

> Sally, <strong>along with</strong> Joe, Bobby, and Sue, (is/are) going to the fair tonight.

This sentence lets us know that there is more than one person going to the fair, and so, we may feel that a plural verb should be used.

Let’s see what we have when we remove everything between the two commas that set the additive phrase off from the rest of the sentence:

> Sally<s>, along with Joe, Bobby, and Sue,</s> (is/are) going to the fair tonight.

Once we eliminate the intervening phrase, we can see that the subject, Sally, requires the singular verb “is,” as in:

> <strong>Sally</strong>, along with Joe, Bobby, and Sue, <strong>is</strong> going to the fair tonight.

Let’s look at another example of how additive phrases fit into sentences.

> Ringo, <strong>as well as</strong> the other members of the band, (has/have) requested that there be no flash photography at the press conference.

The subject, “Ringo,” is one person.

> Ringo<s>, as well as the other members of the band,</s> (has/have) requested that there be no flash photography at the press conference.

As we can see when we remove the additive phrase, we need the singular verb “has” with our singular subject, “Ringo.”

> <strong>Ringo</strong>, as well as the other members of the band, <strong>has</strong> requested that there be no flash photography at the press conference.

It’s also helpful to remember that the subject of a sentence will never be found within an additive phrase.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The subject of a sentence will never be found within an additive phrase.
{{< /alert >}}

For example:

> <strong>Together with</strong> her coworkers, Lily (has/have) been planning a surprise birthday party for her boss all week.

In this case, an additive phrase comes at the beginning of the sentence and contains a plural noun, “coworkers.” Regardless of the placement of the additive phrase, it does not transform a singular subject into a plural subject.

The subject of the sentence will not appear within the additive phrase.

> <s>Together with her coworkers,</s> Lily (has/have) been planning a surprise birthday party for her boss all week.

So, the correct sentence must use the singular verb “has” with “Lily”:

> Together with her coworkers, <strong>Lily has</strong> been planning a surprise birthday party for her boss all week.

##  Sentence Correction Example 7  

<u>The Adventure Cycling Association, together with various other organizations, work</u> to make the United States a nation where everyone can enjoy the many benefits of bicycling.

1. [ ] (A) The Adventure Cycling Association, together with various other organizations, work
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) Together with various other organizations, the Adventure Cycling Association, working
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) Together with various other organizations, the Adventure Cycling Association works
4. [ ] (D) The Adventure Cycling Association, together with various other organizations to work
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) The Adventure Cycling Association and various other organizations works
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



The subject of most versions of the sentence is “the Adventure Cycling Association.”


Since the Adventure Cycling Association is a single entity, “the Adventure Cycling Association” is a singular subject that must be paired with a singular main verb.


Also, even when the additive phrase “together with various other organizations” appears with “the Adventure Cycling Association,” the subject is still singular.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>The Adventure Cycling Association, together with various other organizations, work
</b> to make the United States a nation where everyone can enjoy the many benefits of bicycling.



The main verb “work” in this version does not agree with the subject, “the Adventure Cycling Association.” “The Adventure Cycling Association” is a singular noun, and the presence of the additive phrase “together with various other organizations” does not make the subject plural.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>Together with various other organizations, the Adventure Cycling Association, working
</b> to make the United States a nation where everyone can enjoy the many benefits of bicycling.



Notice that neither “working” nor “to make” is a finite verb and that there is no finite verb to go with what appears to be this version’s subject, “the Adventure Cycling Association” and serve as the main verb of the sentence. So, this version has no main verb and is not a complete sentence. In other words, this version lacks an independent clause; without an independent clause, there can be no sentence.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> <b>Together with various other organizations, the Adventure Cycling Association works
</b> to make the United States a nation where everyone can enjoy the many benefits of bicycling.



In this version, the singular main verb, “works,” agrees with the singular subject, “the Adventure Cycling Association,” and this version concisely conveys a clear meaning.


Notice that the presence of the additive phrase “together with various other organizations” does not make the singular subject plural.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>The Adventure Cycling Association, together with various other organizations to work
</b> to make the United States a nation where everyone can enjoy the many benefits of bicycling.



Notice that neither “to work” nor “to make” is a finite verb and that there is no finite verb to go with what appears to be this version’s subject, “the Adventure Cycling Association” and serve as the main verb of the sentence. So, this version has no main verb and is not a complete sentence. In other words, this version lacks an independent clause; without an independent clause, there can be no sentence.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>The Adventure Cycling Association and various other organizations works
</b> to make the United States a nation where everyone can enjoy the many benefits of bicycling.



In this version, “the Adventure Cycling Association” and “various other local organizations” are connected by “and” to create a compound subject, “the Adventure Cycling Association and various other local organizations.” Of course, a compound subject is a plural subject. So, the singular main verb “works” does not agree with the plural subject “the Adventure Cycling Association and various other local organizations.”


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)
