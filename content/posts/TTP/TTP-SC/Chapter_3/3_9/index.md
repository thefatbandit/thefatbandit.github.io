## Lesson Content
Sentences and clauses containing compound subjects joined by the conjunctions “or,” “either … or,” or “neither … nor” abide by their own special subject-verb agreement rule.

In these cases, the verb must agree with the subject noun that is closest to the verb.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The verb in a clause or sentence containing a compound subject joined by the conjunctions “or,” “either … or,” or “neither … nor” must agree with the subject noun to which it is closest.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at an example of this rule in action:

> Neither the bread nor the potatoes <strong>(looks/look)</strong> very appealing.

In this sentence, the compound subject is formed by the two nouns “bread” and “potatoes.” Since these nouns are joined by “neither … nor,” the verb must agree with the subject noun to which it is closest.

> Neither the bread nor the potatoes <strong>look</strong> very appealing.

If we were to reverse the order of the nouns in the compound subject, so that the verb was closest to the singular noun “bread,” then we would need a singular verb:

> Neither the potatoes nor the bread <strong>looks</strong> very appealing.

Let’s consider another example:

> Neither Astrid nor her dogs <strong>(enjoys/enjoy)</strong> walking in the rain

In this sentence, the compound subject is formed by the two nouns “Astrid” and “her dogs.” Since these nouns are joined by “neither … nor,” the verb must agree with the subject noun to which it is closest.

> Neither Astrid nor her dogs <strong>enjoy</strong> walking in the rain

If we were to reverse the order of the nouns in the compound subject, so that the verb was closest to the singular noun “Astrid,” then we would need a singular verb:

> Neither her dogs nor Astrid <strong>enjoys</strong> walking in the rain

Consider the following example sentences:

> The prime minister or her assistants <strong>are</strong> in the meeting.

> Her assistants or the prime minister <strong>is</strong> in the meeting.

> Either the rabbits or the horse <strong>likes</strong> the dinner today.

> Either the horse or the rabbits <strong>like</strong> the dinner today.

> Neither the pilot nor the flight attendants <strong>were </strong>expecting such a beautiful sunrise.

> Neither the flight attendants nor the pilot <strong>was </strong>expecting such a beautiful sunrise.

> The investigators determined that either multiple software malfunctions or operator error <strong>was </strong>the cause of the accident.

> The investigators determined that either operator error or multiple software malfunctions <strong>were </strong>the cause of the accident.

Remember, the verb in a clause or sentence containing a compound subject joined by the conjunctions “or,” “either … or,” or “neither … nor” must agree with the subject noun to which it is closest.

Let’s look at a more challenging example:

> It is unknown whether stagnant wages or the recent cut to medical benefits <strong>(was/were)</strong> the cause of the strike.

The sentence above is tricky for the following reason.

There are two possible causes of the strike: “stagnant wages” (plural) and “the recent cut” (singular).

To more easily see the subject-verb relationship, we can cross off the prepositional phrase “to medical benefits.”

> It is unknown whether <strong>stagnant wages or the recent</strong> <strong>cut</strong> <s>to medical benefits</s> <strong>(was/were)</strong> the cause of the strike.

Since “recent cut” is the subject noun closest to the verb, it’s clear that the singular verb “was” is required:

> It is unknown whether <strong>stagnant wages or the recent cut</strong> to medical benefits <strong>was</strong> the cause of the strike.

Let’s reverse the two subject nouns to create the following sentence:

> It is unknown whether the recent cut to medical benefits or stagnant wages <strong>(was/were)</strong> the cause of the strike.

Now, the plural noun “wages” is closest to the verb.

> It is unknown whether <strong>the recent cut</strong> to medical benefits <strong>or stagnant</strong> <strong>wages</strong> <strong>were</strong> the cause of the strike.

Let’s look at another example.

> Either the manager in charge of the project or the members of the team <strong>(has/have)</strong> to claim responsibility for this oversight.

Here we have a lengthy compound subject featuring prepositional phrases and connected by “either … or.” To start, let’s see how the sentence looks without the intervening prepositional phrases.

> Either the manager <s>in charge of the project</s> or the members <s>of the team</s> (has/have) to claim responsibility for this oversight.

Removing those phrases leaves us with “either the manager or the members” as our compound subject.

> <strong>Either the manager</strong> in charge of the project <strong>or the</strong> <strong>members</strong> of the team <strong>have</strong> to claim responsibility for this oversight.

##### Question Level: easy



In order for the sentence to be correct, the subject and verb in each clause in the sentence have to agree, and the meaning conveyed has to make sense.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Though neither the nation’s military leaders nor the president were in favor of continuing the war, the vice president was reluctantly along with most other citizens,
</b> to acknowledge that the conflict was pointless.



In “neither the nation’s military leaders nor the president were,” “the president” is the subject noun closest to the main verb, “were.” However, the plural “were” does not agree with the singular “president.”


In the second clause of this version, the subject, “vice president,” agrees with the main verb, “was.” However, the meaning conveyed by the second clause is muddled.


The second clause seems to convey, though not clearly, that the vice president was reluctantly along with most other citizens, in order to acknowledge that the conflict was pointless.


Even if this meaning were clearly conveyed, it would not make sense.


Notice that, by using “though,” the sentence indicates that there is a contrast between the position of the military leaders and the president and the position of the vice president. However, if the vice president was acknowledging that the conflict was pointless, then there was no clear contrast between the position of the vice president and that of the military leaders and president, who were not in favor of continuing the war. So, the meaning conveyed is not coherent.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>With neither the nation’s military leaders nor the president in favor of continuing the war, the vice president, along with most other citizens, were reluctant
</b> to acknowledge that the conflict was pointless.



There is only one independent clause in this version, and the subject of that clause is “the vice president,” which is singular. Although the additive phrase “along with most other citizens,” follows “the vice president,” the presence of that phrase does not make the subject plural. So, the plural main verb of the clause, “were,” does not agree with the subject.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>While neither the nation’s military leaders nor the president favored continuing the war, the vice president was along with most other citizens; they were reluctant
</b> to acknowledge that the conflict was pointless.



In the first clause in this version, there can’t be a subject-verb agreement issue, because the finite verb, “favored,” would agree with a singular subject or a plural subject.


Meanwhile, the subject of the second clause, “vice president” agrees with the finite verb, “was.”


However, this version does not effectively convey a logical meaning, as it says that the vice president was along with most other citizens, but it does not clearly indicate in what way the vice president was along with other citizens, since the clause that follows the semicolon is not clearly connected to the statement about the vice president.


In fact, since “they” could refer to various combinations of the nouns that precede “they,” whom the clause that follows the semicolon is about is not clear. Is that clause about the military leaders and the president? Is it about the vice president and other citizens? Who knows? Right?


> <b>(D)</b> <b>With neither the nation’s military leaders nor the president in favor of continuing the war, the vice president, reluctantly, along with most other citizens,
</b> to acknowledge that the conflict was pointless.



This version has no main verb. Neither “continuing” nor “to acknowledge” is a finite verb, and “was” can’t be the main verb, because “was” is within the noun clause “that the conflict was pointless.” Without a main verb, we can’t have a sentence.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E)</b> <b>While neither the nation’s military leaders nor the president was in favor of continuing the war, the vice president, along with most other citizens, was reluctant
</b> to acknowledge that the conflict was pointless.



In “neither the nation’s military leaders nor the president was,” “president” is the subject noun closest to the finite verb, “was,” and the singular “was” correctly agrees with the singular “president.”


The subject of the second clause in this version is “the vice president,” which is singular. Although the additive phrase “along with most other citizens,” follows “vice president,” the presence of that phase does not make the subject plural. So, the singular verb, “was,” is correctly used.


Also, this version effectively conveys a logical meaning.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
