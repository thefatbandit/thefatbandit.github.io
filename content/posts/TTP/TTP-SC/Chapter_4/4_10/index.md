## Lesson Content
A demonstrative pronoun is a pronoun that points out or “demonstrates” the noun in the sentence.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A demonstrative pronoun takes the place of a noun and always stands alone. The major demonstrative pronouns are “this,” “that,” “these,” and “those.”
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example:

> The <strong>tires</strong> on most sports cars are wider than <strong>those</strong> on most mid-size sedans.

In the sentence above, “those” is a demonstrative pronoun.

Now, demonstrative pronouns often work differently from how personal pronouns work.

> <strong>The </strong><strong>students in the room</strong> rejoiced because class had been canceled, and <strong>they</strong> began making plans to go to the beach.

The personal pronoun “they” refers to “the students in the room.”

> Once <strong>the restored </strong><strong>plane </strong>reached 180 knots, <strong>it</strong> gracefully lifted off the runway.

The personal pronoun “it” refers to “the restored plane.”

On the other hand, demonstrative pronouns often do not refer directly to their antecedents:

> <strong>The speed</strong> of today’s cars well exceeds <strong>that</strong> of cars of the 1950s.

In the sentence above, the demonstrative pronoun “that” replaces the antecedent “speed.” However, the speed referred to by “that” is not the same as the speed that is mentioned earlier in the sentence.

While repeating “the speed” to make the comparison would not be grammatically incorrect, such repetition isn’t necessary because we can use a demonstrative pronoun.

Let’s consider some more example sentences:

> <strong>The </strong><strong>range</strong> of the new model is greater than <strong>that </strong>of the previous model.

> <strong>The </strong><strong>books</strong> in my library are older than <strong>those </strong>in your library.

> <strong>The </strong><strong>GDP</strong> of the United States is larger than<strong> that </strong>of Monaco.

In these three examples, “that” and “those” are used as demonstrative pronouns.

The words “this,” “that,” “these,” and “those” can also function as demonstrative adjectives.

Consider the usage of “that” in the following sentence:

> <strong>That</strong> book is long.

In this case, “that” does not stand alone as a pronoun.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A demonstrative adjective is always immediately followed by a noun because adjectives modify nouns.
{{< /alert >}}

It’s important to be able to tell when “this,” “that,” “these,” or “those” is being used as a demonstrative pronoun and when it is being used as a demonstrative adjective.

Let’s look at some examples.

> <strong>This</strong> bakery has a better selection of breads, but <strong>that</strong> bakery has more exotic pastries.

> The <strong>pastries</strong> at the bakery on the corner are more exotic than <strong>those</strong> at the bakery across the street.

In the first example, we are distinguishing between two bakeries: this bakery and that bakery.

In the second example, the focus shifts, and we’re comparing a particular item at each of the bakeries, pastries.

Here’s another pair:

> Even with all of the recent improvements in CGI technology, <strong>this</strong> film still does not look realistic.

> <strong>Superhero</strong> <strong>films</strong> of today rely on CGI much more than <strong>those</strong> of the early 2000s did.

In the first sentence, a demonstrative adjective tells us which film does not look realistic (this film).

<u>Use of Demonstrative Pronouns Where Personal Pronouns Would Make More Sense</u>

As we have discussed, demonstrative pronouns not only stand in for nouns but also point out or “demonstrate.” Personal pronouns, on the other hand, simply stand in for nouns.

Consider the following example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: While pandas may seem cuddly or endearing, <strong>these</strong> have exceedingly strong bites and large dexterous claws.

Since, in this example, pandas are not compared to other animals or pointed out in some way, it does not make sense to use the demonstrative pronoun “these,” and it would make more sense to simply use the personal pronoun “they,” as in:

> While pandas may seem cuddly or endearing, <strong>they</strong> have exceedingly strong bites and large dexterous claws.

Here’s another example in which the use of a demonstrative pronoun doesn’t make sense:

> While the schooner America was considered a fast sailing yacht in its time, <strong>that</strong> would be no match for the high-tech sailboats that compete for the America’s cup today.

In this example, the use of the pronoun “that” doesn’t make sense, as conveying the sentence’s intended meaning does not require the use of a pronoun that points out the schooner America.

> While the schooner America was considered a fast sailing yacht in its time, <strong>it</strong> would be no match for the high-tech sailboats that compete for the America’s cup today.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Sentence Correction questions may include choices that are incorrect because they use demonstrative pronouns in situations in which using personal pronouns would make more sense.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 24  

Although Prince Edward Island is the smallest of the Canadian provinces, <u>there is a robust agricultural industry in which they produce</u> one quarter of Canada’s potatoes.

1. [ ] (A) there is a robust agricultural industry in which they produce
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) the agricultural industry of that is robust, producing
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) that has a robust agricultural industry that produces
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) its agricultural industry is robust, producing
5. [ ] (E) this has a robust agricultural industry, which produces
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A)</b> Although Prince Edward Island is the smallest of the Canadian provinces, <b>there is a robust agricultural industry in which they produce
</b> one quarter of Canada’s potatoes.



The pronoun “they” does not have a logical antecedent. The only plural noun to which “they” could refer is “provinces,” but from the context “Although Prince Edward Island is the smallest of the Canadian provinces,” we can see that the logical meaning is that Prince Edward Island in particular, not all of the provinces, produces one quarter of Canada’s potatoes. Of course, the plural pronoun “they” cannot refer to the singular noun “Prince Edward Island.”


Additionally, “there is a robust agricultural industry” conveys a vague meaning because “there is” simply conveys that “a robust agricultural industry” exists in general but does not link that industry to Prince Edward Island specifically.


> <b>(B)</b> Although Prince Edward Island is the smallest of the Canadian provinces, <b>the agricultural industry of that is robust, producing
</b> one quarter of Canada’s potatoes.



It’s unclear to what “that” refers in “the agricultural industry of <em>that</em> is robust.” This version is a good example of a sentence in which the use of a personal pronoun, rather than a demonstrative pronoun, would clarify the meaning.


Since no comparison is expressed by the sentence, the use of the demonstrative pronoun does not make sense. In other words, although we may assume that “that” refers to “Prince Edward Island,” in this context, “that” could be anything. In this case, the personal pronoun “it” would directly refer to the singular noun “Prince Edward Island,” conveying a clear and logical meaning.


> <b>(C)</b> Although Prince Edward Island is the smallest of the Canadian provinces, <b>that has a robust agricultural industry that produces
</b> one quarter of Canada’s potatoes.



 It’s unclear to what “that” refers in “<em>that</em> has a robust agricultural industry.” This version is a good example of a sentence in which the use of a personal pronoun, rather than a demonstrative pronoun, would clarify the meaning.


Since no comparison is expressed by the sentence, the use of the demonstrative pronoun does not make sense. In other words, although we may assume that “that” refers to “Prince Edward Island,” in this context, “that” could be anything. In this case, the personal pronoun “it” would directly refer to the singular noun “Prince Edward Island,” conveying a clear and logical meaning.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D)</b> Although Prince Edward Island is the smallest of the Canadian provinces, <b>its agricultural industry is robust, producing
</b> one quarter of Canada’s potatoes.



This version uses the possessive personal pronoun “its” to directly refer to the singular noun “Prince Edward Island,” conveying a clear and logical meaning.


In fact, this is the only version that uses a personal pronoun to refer directly to the subject “Prince Edward Island.”


Notice that there is no other singular noun to which “its” could possibly refer.


The meaning is unambiguous, and the sentence contains no grammatical errors.


> <b>(E)</b> Although Prince Edward Island is the smallest of the Canadian provinces, <b>this has a robust agricultural industry, which produces
</b> one quarter of Canada’s potatoes.



 It’s unclear to what “this” refers in “<em>this</em> has a robust agricultural industry.” Notice that the sentence does not use “this” as a demonstrative adjective to specify that “<em>this</em> <em>island</em> has a robust agricultural industry.” Doing so would convey a clearer meaning.


As the sentence stands, although we may assume that “this” refers to “Prince Edward Island,” in fact “this” could be anything because “this” is not pointing out a specific noun in this context.


Notice that this choice is essentially the same grammatically as the incorrect choice (C). In other words, “<em>that</em> has” and “<em>this</em> has” are no different in this context; they are two versions of the same error. [Notice also that “industry <em>that </em>produces” in choice (C) and “industry, <em>which</em> produces” here also are simply two (in this case, valid) ways to say the same thing, that the industry produces the potatoes.]


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Sentence Correction Example 25  

In 2000, 52 Oscar award <u>statuettes that were supposed to be delivered to a location in Los Angeles but were instead thrown</u> into a dumpster, where they were found by a man looking for some packing boxes.

1. [ ] (A) statuettes that were supposed to be delivered to a location in Los Angeles but were instead thrown
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) statuettes, these were supposed to be delivered to a location in Los Angeles but were instead thrown
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) statuettes supposed to be delivered to a location in Los Angeles were instead thrown
4. [ ] (D) statuettes were supposed to be delivered to a location in Los Angeles; instead they threw these
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) statuettes, they were supposed to deliver them to a location in Los Angeles, but they instead threw them
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



Eliminating most of the incorrect choices in this question requires noticing pronoun use issues. These issues include the use of demonstrative pronouns where personal pronouns would make more sense and the presence of pronouns that have no clear antecedents.


> <b>(A)</b> In 2000, 52 Oscar award <b>statuettes that were supposed to be delivered to a location in Los Angeles but were instead thrown
</b> into a dumpster, where they were found by a man looking for some packing boxes.



Since, in this version, everything that follows the noun “statuettes” is merely a long relative clause that modifies “statuettes,” this version has no main verb and, thus, is not a complete sentence.


> <b>(B)</b> In 2000, 52 Oscar award <b>statuettes, these were supposed to be delivered to a location in Los Angeles but were instead thrown
</b> into a dumpster, where they were found by a man looking for some packing boxes.



This version starts off awkwardly as the role of subject is filled twice, first by the noun phrase “52 Oscar statuettes,” and then by the pronoun “these.”


Another issue that this version has is that the demonstrative pronoun “these” is used in a situation in which the use of a demonstrative pronoun does not make sense, as the sentence is not set up to point out any particular set of Oscar statuettes.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> In 2000, 52 Oscar award <b>statuettes supposed to be delivered to a location in Los Angeles were instead thrown
</b> into a dumpster, where they were found by a man looking for some packing boxes.



In this well structured version, the subject, “52 Oscar award statuettes supposed to be delivered to a location in Los Angeles” is followed by the main verb, “were thrown.”


The resulting sentence is grammatically sound and clearly conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(D)</b> In 2000, 52 Oscar award <b>statuettes were supposed to be delivered to a location in Los Angeles; instead they threw these
</b> into a dumpster, where they were found by a man looking for some packing boxes.



One issue that this version has is the use of “they” with no clear referent, as the sentence does not seem to be referring to any particular group of people with its use of “they,” and such use of “they” does not convey a clear meaning and is not preferred by the GMAT.


Also, “these” is a demonstrative pronoun, but it does not make sense to use such a pronoun in this situation, since the sentence is not set up to point out any particular items.


> <b>(E)</b> In 2000, 52 Oscar award <b>statuettes, they were supposed to deliver them to a location in Los Angeles, but they instead threw them
</b> into a dumpster, where they were found by a man looking for some packing boxes.



This version starts off awkwardly, as the phrase “52 Oscar statuettes” stands alone with no verb and is not well connected to the rest of the sentence.


Also, the pronoun “they,” which appears twice in this version, has no clear antecedent and does not appear to refer to any particular group of people. Such use of “they” does not convey a clear meaning and is not preferred by the GMAT.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

##  Sentence Correction Example 26  

While the arms of the Tyrannosaurus rex seem puny and useless, there is reason to believe <u>the Tyrannosaurus, using these and, thus, grasping prey</u>.

1. [ ] (A) the Tyrannosaurus, using these and, thus, grasping prey
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) the Tyrannosaurus to have used them to grasp prey
3. [ ] (C) that the Tyrannosaurus could have used those and grasped prey
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) in the Tyrannosaurus that would have used those to grasp prey
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) in the Tyrannosaurus using them and grasping prey
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



The original version has multiple issues, among them that the demonstrative pronoun “these” is used in a situation in which the use of a personal pronoun would make more sense, as the sentence is not set up to point out any particular set of arms.


The correct version uses a personal pronoun, rather than a demonstrative pronoun, and concisely conveys a clear meaning.


> <b>(A)</b> While the arms of the Tyrannosaurus rex seem puny and useless, there is reason to believe <b>the Tyrannosaurus, using these and, thus, grasping prey
</b>.



In this version the demonstrative pronoun “these” is used inappropriately, as the sentence is not set up to point out any particular set of arms.


Also, this version uses “to believe” in a way that does not make sense, seeming to convey that there is reason to believe something that the Tyrannosaurus has claimed, rather than that there is reason to believe something <em>about </em>the Tyrannosaurus.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B)</b> While the arms of the Tyrannosaurus rex seem puny and useless, there is reason to believe <b>the Tyrannosaurus to have used them to grasp prey
</b>.



This version appropriately uses the personal pronoun “them” to refer to the arms of the Tyrannosaurus and clearly conveys that there is reason to believe the Tyrannosaurus to have used its arms in a certain way.


Note that the idiom “believe X to Y” is used in this choice for the purpose of making this correct answer “sound funny” and, thus, seem to be incorrect.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> While the arms of the Tyrannosaurus rex seem puny and useless, there is reason to believe <b>that the Tyrannosaurus could have used those and grasped prey
</b>.



In this version, the demonstrative pronoun “those” is used inappropriately, as the sentence is not set up to point out any particular set of arms.


Also, in this version, the use of the arms and the grasping of prey are illogically separated. The point to be made is not that the Tyrannosaurus used its arms <em>and </em>grasped prey, but rather, that the Tyrannosaurus used its arms <em>to </em>grasp prey.


> <b>(D)</b> While the arms of the Tyrannosaurus rex seem puny and useless, there is reason to believe <b>in the Tyrannosaurus that would have used those to grasp prey
</b>.



In this version the demonstrative pronoun “those” is used inappropriately, as the sentence is not set up to point out any particular set of arms.


Also, this version uses “to believe in” in a way that does not make sense. The point to be made is not that there is reason to believe <em>in </em>the Tyrannosaurus, but rather that there is reason to believe something <em>about </em>the Tyrannosaurus.


> <b>(E)</b> While the arms of the Tyrannosaurus rex seem puny and useless, there is reason to believe <b>in the Tyrannosaurus using them and grasping prey
</b>.



This version uses “to believe in” in a way that does not make sense. The point to be made is not that there is reason to believe <em>in </em>the Tyrannosaurus, but rather that there is reason to believe something <em>about </em>the Tyrannosaurus.


Overall, this version conveys a nonsensical meaning, that there is a reason to believe in a certain Tyrannosaurus that is using its arms and grasping prey.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

Let’s now make sure that we won’t be confused by what’s called “the expletive ‘it.’”
