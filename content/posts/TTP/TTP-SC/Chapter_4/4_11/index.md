## Lesson Content
As we look out for pronouns without logical referents, we have to be careful not to be confused by what is called “the expletive ‘it.’”

Consider the following examples:

> <strong>It</strong> is apparent that Milan is a skilled writer.

> Because he had been training, <strong>it</strong> was easy for Michael to swim across the lake.

In each of the above examples, the pronoun “it” has no referent and thus may seem to be incorrectly used.

However, we have heard and read sentences like the above examples.

When the pronoun “it” is used as it is in the above sentences, it is called “the expletive ‘it.’”

An expletive “it” in a sentence does not refer to any noun and is considered a “placeholder subject” that does not express any meaning.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

An expletive “it” in a sentence does not refer to any noun and is considered a “placeholder subject” that does not express any meaning.
{{< /alert >}}

To better understand how an expletive “it” works, let’s consider our first example again:

> <strong>It</strong> is apparent that Milan is a skilled writer.

In that sentence, “it” does not express any meaning.

How can we tell? We can tell by seeing that, if we replace “it” with “that Milan is a skilled writer,” the sentence works and conveys the same meaning:

> <strong>That Milan is a skilled writer</strong> is apparent.

The above sentence works and conveys the same meaning that the version that uses “it” conveys.

We can do the same with the second example:

> Because he had been training, <strong>it</strong> was easy for Michael to swim across the lake.

> Because he had been training, <strong>for Michael to swim across the lake</strong> was easy.

Once again, when we replace “it” with something that appears later in the sentence, the sentence works and conveys the same meaning that the version that uses “it” conveys.

So, we see that an expletive “it” can stand in for something that appears later in a sentence.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

An expletive “it” often stands in for something that appears later in a sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

Now, notice that, in the sentence “<strong>That Milan is a skilled writer</strong> is apparent,” the noun clause “that Milan is a skilled writer” is the subject.

In other words, what is apparent? That Milan is a skilled writer is apparent.

So then, what we have done by using an expletive “it” in “<strong>It</strong> is apparent <strong>that Milan is a skilled writer</strong>,” is delayed the subject.

In fact, a subject that is delayed in this way is called a “delayed subject.”

So, an expletive “it” often appears in combination with a delayed subject.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

An expletive “it” often appears in combination with a delayed subject.
{{< /alert >}}

OK, great.

> In the past, people commonly burned leaves, but <strong>it</strong> is rare now.

In this example, like in the previous examples, we see the pronoun “it.” At the same time, unlike in the previous examples, in this example, “it” is not used correctly.

In this example, “it” seems to refer to “burning leaves,” but burning leaves is not directly mentioned in the sentence.

Now, likely you are wondering, “But wait a minute.

Yes, we did.

A pronoun that has no logical referent is not correctly used unless that pronoun is used as an expletive.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A pronoun that has no logical referent is not correctly used unless that pronoun is used as an expletive.
{{< /alert >}}

So, we can see that a key aspect of deciding whether “it” is being used correctly is checking to see whether “it” is being used as an expletive.

We can check to see whether “it” is being used as an expletive by looking for a delayed subject because, in most cases, where there is an expletive “it,” there is also a delayed subject.

Let’s consider our example again:

> In the past, people commonly burned leaves, but <strong>it</strong> is rare now.

Notice that there is no delayed subject that we can logically substitute for “it.” If, for instance, we replace “it” with “rare now,” we get the following nonsensical sentence:

> In the past, people commonly burned leaves, but <strong>rare now</strong> is.

So, in this case, we have the pronoun “it” without a referent, and since there is no delayed subject, we have no reason to believe that “it” is an expletive.

Now, let’s consider another example.

> The young violinist realized that <strong>it</strong> is fun to learn challenging new pieces.

In this example, see the pronoun “it,” and looking through the sentence, we see that the pronoun “it” has no referent.

In this case, there is a delayed subject: “to learn challenging new pieces.”

We can tell because, when we replace “it” with “to learn challenging new pieces,” the sentence works and conveys the same meaning:

> The young violinist realized that <strong>to learn challenging new pieces</strong> is fun.

So, since we have found a delayed subject, we now know that “it” is not used incorrectly in this case.

OK, now what about this one?

> The senators were hoping to achieve great things, but <strong>it</strong> did not work out.

Once again, we see the pronoun “it” without a referent, since there’s no singular noun to which “it” would logically refer.

Looking over the sentence, we see that there is no delayed subject that we could logically substitute for “it.” If, for instance, we put “work out” where “it” is, we get the following nonsensical sentence:

> The senators were hoping to achieve great things, but <strong>work out</strong> did not.

So, in this case, there’s no delayed subject.

Thus, in this case, “it” does not have a referent, and also there is no reason to believe that “it” is an expletive.

Now, there are some exceptions in which “it” is used as an expletive without a delayed subject, such as the following:

> <strong>It</strong> is raining.

> <strong>It</strong> is two o’clock.

However, since these exceptions are familiar expressions, we don’t have to worry about being confused by them.

So, to confirm that the pronoun “it” without a referent is not being used correctly, we can check for the presence of a delayed subject.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

To confirm that the pronoun “it” without a referent is not being used correctly, we can check for the presence of a delayed subject. If there is a delayed subject that we can logically substitute for “it,” “it” is an expletive. If there is no delayed subject that we can logically substitute for “it,” we can be confident that “it” is not correctly used.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 27  

According to researchers, <u>it is common for generally upbeat people to feel depressed during the winter months</u>.

1. [x] (A) it is common for generally upbeat people to feel depressed during the winter months
2. [ ] (B) that generally upbeat people feel depressed are common during the winter months
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) people who are generally upbeat commonly feel it during the winter months, in which they are depressed
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) during the winter months, generally upbeat people feel it commonly as being depressed
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) feeling depressed during the winter months are common for generally upbeat people
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

Notice that if we substitute the delayed subject for “it,” the meaning makes sense:


<strong>for generally upbeat people to feel depressed during the winter months</strong> is common


This is the only choice that creates a sentence that both is grammatically correct and conveys a clear meaning.


> <b>(B)</b> According to researchers, <b>that generally upbeat people feel depressed are common during the winter months
</b>.



This version contains a subject-verb agreement error. The subject “that generally upbeat people feel depressed” is singular, and thus the plural verb “are” is incorrect.


> <b>(C)</b> According to researchers, <b>people who are generally upbeat commonly feel it during the winter months, in which they are depressed
</b>.



Notice that this version does not use an expletive “it”; rather, it contains a pronoun that has no antecedent.


The sentence does not mention anything that people who are generally upbeat commonly feel during the winter months.


Also, there is no delayed subject that could be substituted for “it.”


> <b>(D)</b> According to researchers, <b>during the winter months, generally upbeat people feel it commonly as being depressed
</b>.



Notice that this version does not use an expletive “it”; rather, it contains a pronoun without any logical antecedent.


The sentence does not mention anything that people who are generally upbeat commonly feel during the winter months.


Also, there is no delayed subject that could be substituted for “it.”


So, what “it” refers to is unclear.


> <b>(E)</b> According to researchers, <b>feeling depressed during the winter months are common for generally upbeat people
</b>.



 This version contains a subject-verb agreement error. The subject “feeling depressed” is singular, and thus the plural verb “are” is incorrect.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

##  Sentence Correction Example 28  

The results of sleep research indicate that, if you can fall asleep readily at any time of day, <u>it probably means that</u> you are sleep deprived.

1. [ ] (A) it probably means that
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) it is probable that it means that
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) that probably means
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) it is probable that
5. [ ] (E) what that probably means is that
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



There is no way for a dependent clause starting with “if” to serve as the antecedent of a pronoun, because, even if we were to go with the idea that a fact can be the antecedent of a pronoun, a dependent clause that begins with “if” does not state a fact. It expresses a condition upon which the validity of a fact depends.


So, the pronoun “it” or “that” cannot refer to “if you can fall asleep readily at any time of day.”


Therefore, there is nothing for “it,” in some choices, or “that,” in other choices, to refer to.


Thus, only in the correct version, in which there is a delayed subject, is “it” correctly used.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A)</b> The results of sleep research indicate that, if you can fall asleep readily at any time of day, <b>it probably means that
</b> you are sleep deprived.



There is no referent for “it” in this version, since the sentence mentions nothing that would mean that you are sleep deprived.


Also, “it” is not an expletive because “that you are sleep deprived” is not a delayed subject that we can logically substitute for the second “it.” If we do the substitution, we get the following incomplete thought:


The results of sleep research indicate that … that you are sleep deprived probably means.


That you are sleep deprived probably means what?


> <b>(B)</b> The results of sleep research indicate that, if you can fall asleep readily at any time of day, <b>it is probable that it means that
</b> you are sleep deprived.



The first “it” in this version serves as an expletive “it” with the delayed subject “that it means that you are probably sleep deprived.”


However, the second “it” has no referent since the sentence mentions nothing that would mean that you are sleep deprived, and also, the second “it” is not an expletive because “that you are sleep deprived” is not a delayed subject that we can logically substitute for the second “it.” If we do the substitution, we get the following incomplete thought:


The results of sleep research indicate that … it is probable that that you are sleep deprived means.


That you are sleep deprived means what?


> <b>(C)</b> The results of sleep research indicate that, if you can fall asleep readily at any time of day, <b>that probably means
</b> you are sleep deprived.



There is no referent for “that” in this version, since the sentence mentions nothing that would mean you are sleep deprived.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D)</b> The results of sleep research indicate that, if you can fall asleep readily at any time of day, <b>it is probable that
</b> you are sleep deprived.



In this version, “it” serves as an expletive “it” with the delayed subject “that you are sleep deprived.” We can confirm that this version is correct by replacing “it” with the delayed subject and seeing that the sentence still works:


The results of sleep research indicate that, if you can fall asleep readily at any time of day, <strong>that you are sleep deprived</strong> is probable.


> <b>(E)</b> The results of sleep research indicate that, if you can fall asleep readily at any time of day, <b>what that probably means is that
</b> you are sleep deprived.



There is no referent for the first “that” in this version, since the sentence mentions nothing that would mean that you are sleep deprived.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
