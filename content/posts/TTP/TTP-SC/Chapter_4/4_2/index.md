## Lesson Content
As we now know, an antecedent, or referent,<em> </em>is the noun to which a pronoun refers.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

An antecedent, or referent, is the noun to which a pronoun refers. A pronoun can refer to a noun that comes either before or after it in a sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

Of course, in order for a sentence to effectively convey a meaning, it must be clear which noun is the antecedent of each pronoun in the sentence.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In order for a sentence to effectively convey a meaning, it must be clear which noun is the antecedent of each pronoun in the sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at a sentence featuring a pronoun with multiple possible antecedents:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: In a fit of anger, Jane poured the water from the vase and threw <strong>it</strong>.

To check for any errors involving a pronoun, we first identify the pronoun and then identify any possible nouns (antecedents) to which the pronoun could refer.

“It” is the only pronoun in the sentence.

To clear up this issue, we could eliminate the pronoun or one of the possible antecedents.

> In a fit of anger, Jane poured the water from the vase and threw <strong>the vase</strong>.

Replacing the pronoun “it” with its intended antecedent, “the vase,” eliminates any confusion as to what Jane threw.

Alternatively, we could rephrase the sentence and, thus, clear up the pronoun-antecedent issue without using repetitious language:

> In a fit of anger, Jane poured the water from and threw the vase.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

To check for pronoun-antecedent errors, when there is a pronoun in a sentence, identify its possible antecedents.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at another example sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Maria texted Marisol many times while she was in Los Angeles, .

Let’s identify the possible referents of the pronoun “she.” There are two possible referents: “Maria” and “Marisol.” Thus, the sentence does not clearly convey who was in Los Angeles.

A correct version of the sentence could read:

> While <strong>Maria</strong> was in Los Angeles, <strong>she</strong> texted Marisol many times.

Now the sentence makes clear who was in Los Angeles.

You may be worried about the repetition of nouns in sentences.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

If repetition of a noun is required to clarify the meaning of a sentence, the repetition is not considered redundancy on the GMAT.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at some more challenging examples.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The collapse of the country’s economy has resulted in a tightening of the military’s grip on key resources such as gold and oil, although the extraction and export of <strong>it</strong> has slowed to a trickle.

We might guess, given the wording “slowed to a trickle,” that the pronoun “it” refers to “oil.” However, the truth is that “it” could refer to “gold” or “oil,” because the extraction and export of either gold or oil could “slow to a trickle.” So, we must eliminate the problem of multiple possible antecedents, possibly by repeating the word “oil.”

> The collapse of the county’s economy has resulted in a tightening of the military’s grip on key resources such as gold and oil, although the extraction and export of <strong>oil</strong> has slowed to a trickle.

Now the sentence makes clear that the extraction and export of oil, not gold, is what has slowed.

Here’s another one:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The foundation was set up to fund expeditions for researching the lifestyles of gorillas, as <strong>they</strong> have been becoming rather rare.

Are “expeditions” becoming rather rare, or are “gorillas”? While we might be tempted to decide that “they” refers to “gorillas,” in a Sentence Correction question, a version like this sentence would be considered incorrect, since which noun “they” refers to isn’t one-hundred percent clear.

A possible correction is:

> The foundation was set up to fund expeditions for researching the lifestyles of gorillas, as <strong>such expeditions</strong> have been becoming rather rare.

By replacing the pronoun with a noun, we have created a sentence that conveys a clear meaning.

Let’s consider one more:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Recently, anthropologists found people of a nomadic tribe living in an area that had been considered uninhabitable by many of <strong>them</strong>.

In this example, the pronoun “them” could conceivably refer to either of two plural nouns, “anthropologists” or “people.” Since it’s not clear which noun is the antecedent, the GMAT would prefer another version of the sentence.

A possible preferred version is:

> Recently, anthropologists found people of a nomadic tribe living in an area that had been widely considered uninhabitable.

In this version, there is no pronoun-antecedent issue because the pronoun “them” is not even used.

Notice that the two versions in this last example don’t convey the exact same meaning, and that what makes the preferred version preferred is simply that it effectively conveys a clear meaning.

##  Sentence Correction Example 1  

Some state governors have suggested <u>that federal regulators are using excessive enforcement to punish anyone not in favor of certain</u> policies.

1. [x] (A) that federal regulators are using excessive enforcement to punish anyone not in favor of certain
2. [ ] (B) that federal regulators are using excessive enforcement to punish, because they are not in favor of certain of their
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) that federal regulators use excessive enforcement as punishment for anyone not in favor of certain of their
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) federal regulators, using excessive enforcement as punishment for not being in favor of certain
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) that federal regulators are using excessive enforcement, thus punishing them for not being in favor of certain
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



In this question, the lack of clear relationships between the pronouns “they,” “them,” and “their” and their antecedents results in sentence versions that don’t convey clear meanings. It’s not clear whose policies the policies are or who is punishing whom.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A)</b> Some state governors have suggested <b>that federal regulators are using excessive enforcement to punish anyone not in favor of certain
</b> policies.



This version clearly conveys that what the governors have suggested is that the regulators are punishing anyone not in favor of certain policies.


> <b>(B)</b> Some state governors have suggested <b>that federal regulators are using excessive enforcement to punish, because they are not in favor of certain of their
</b> policies.



This version starts off OK, but, since it’s not clear whether “they” and “their” refer to “governors” or “regulators,” the sentence does not clearly convey who is not in favor of the policies or whose policies they are.


> <b>(C)</b> Some state governors have suggested <b>that federal regulators use excessive enforcement as punishment for anyone not in favor of certain of their
</b> policies.



This version starts off unclearly, as it’s not quite clear whether the governors are suggesting that the regulators are using enforcement as punishment or that the regulators should use enforcement as punishment. Then, since “their” could refer to either “governors” or “regulators,” the meaning conveyed by this version becomes even more unclear.


> <b>(D)</b> Some state governors have suggested <b>federal regulators, using excessive enforcement as punishment for not being in favor of certain
</b> policies.



This choice might look OK in isolation. However, when we combine this choice with the non-underlined portions, we see that this version conveys the illogical meaning that the governors have suggested federal regulators, whatever that even means.


Furthermore, this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that, when they have “suggested federal regulators,” the governors have been using excessive enforcement as punishment.


So, even though this version contains no pronouns, it’s definitely not the correct answer.


> <b>(E)</b> Some state governors have suggested <b>that federal regulators are using excessive enforcement, thus punishing them for not being in favor of certain
</b> policies.



Since the antecedent of “them” is not clear, this version could convey either that the governors are punishing the regulators or that the regulators are punishing the governors.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

##  Sentence Correction Example 2  

<u>The heat shedding design of the “cool roof” of this house makes it</u> better suited for warmer climates than for cooler ones.

1. [ ] (A) The heat shedding design of the “cool roof” of this house makes it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) This house, with its “cool roof,” designed to shed heat, making it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) On this house is a “cool roof,” which is designed to shed heat, and it is
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) Since the roof of this house is a “cool roof,” designed to shed heat,
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) Because it has a “cool roof,” designed to shed heat, this house is

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



In the original version of the sentence, the pronoun “it” could refer to either “roof” or “house.” The correct version more clearly indicates which of these two things is meant to be described as better suited for warmer climates than for cooler ones.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>The heat shedding design of the “cool roof” of this house makes it
</b> better suited for warmer climates than for cooler ones.



 The meaning conveyed by this version is unclear, as the pronoun “it” could refer to either “roof” or “house.”


> <b>(B)</b> <b>This house, with its “cool roof,” designed to shed heat, making it
</b> better suited for warmer climates than for cooler ones.



The meaning of this version is rather muddled, and the wording is awkward. It is not clear whether “it” refers to “house” or “roof,” so what makes what better suited for warmer climates than cooler ones is not clear.


Also, this version contains no main verb. Without a main verb, there can be no independent clause, and we can’t have a sentence without an independent clause.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>On this house is a “cool roof,” which is designed to shed heat, and it is
</b> better suited for warmer climates than for cooler ones.



The meaning conveyed by this version is unclear, as the pronoun “it” could refer to either “roof” or “house.”


> <b>(D)</b> <b>Since the roof of this house is a “cool roof,” designed to shed heat,
</b> better suited for warmer climates than for cooler ones.



Since this version contains no pronouns, it cannot have any pronoun related issues. However, it also has no independent clause and, thus, is not a complete sentence.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E)</b> <b>Because it has a “cool roof,” designed to shed heat, this house is
</b> better suited for warmer climates than for cooler ones.



In this version, “it” clearly refers to “house,” as the sentence mentions nothing else that would have a “cool roof,” and this version is otherwise correct and effectively conveys a clear meaning.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

To complete our discussion of pronouns that have multiple possible referents, let’s address a concept that sometimes creates confusion: meaning can indicate the referent of a pronoun.
