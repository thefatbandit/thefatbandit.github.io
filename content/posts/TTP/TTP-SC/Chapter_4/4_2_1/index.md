## Lesson Content
Sometimes, even when there are multiple nouns to which a pronoun in a sentence could conceivably refer, which noun is the referent of the pronoun may be clear because meaning indicates which noun the pronoun refers to.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Sometimes, even when there are multiple nouns to which a pronoun in a sentence could conceivably refer, which noun is the referent of the pronoun may be clear because meaning indicates which noun the pronoun refers to.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example:

> After the antique clock that had been sitting on the shelf collecting dust was cleaned and adjusted, <strong>it</strong> worked well, telling the time perfectly and chiming every half hour.

This sentence includes the pronoun “it,” which could conceivably refer to “clock,” “shelf,” or “dust.” However, is the meaning of the above sentence ambiguous? Let’s consider the sentence logically.

Neither a shelf nor dust would tell the time or chime every half hour.

Here’s another example:

> Since the musicians have gotten great reviews and have been invited to perform at multiple festivals, <strong>they</strong> are quite happy with the progress that they have made.

The pronoun “they” appears in the above example, and there are in the sentence three nouns, “musicians,” “reviews,” and “festivals,” to which “they” could conceivably refer.

So, before we decide that a pronoun lacks a clear antecedent, we must take care to holistically evaluate the sentence, checking to determine whether the logic and meaning of the sentence make clear to which noun the pronoun refers.
