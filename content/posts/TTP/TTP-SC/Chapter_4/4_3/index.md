## Lesson Content
Whereas some sentences will feature a pronoun with multiple possible antecedents, as we saw in the previous section, others will lack an antecedent altogether.

Of course, as we know, each pronoun in a sentence must have one clear antecedent.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

We may encounter sentences that feature a pronoun but lack an antecedent for that pronoun. Answer choices offering such sentences will be incorrect.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The orchard’s production is high, but selling <strong>them</strong> is easy.

In this sentence, there is no noun for the pronoun “them” to replace.

The sentence could be made more effective via the addition of an antecedent:

> The orchard’s production of <strong>apples</strong> is high, but selling <strong>them</strong> is easy.

Including the noun “apples” provides an antecedent for “them” and results in a sentence that conveys a clear meaning.

Alternatively, we could improve the sentence by replacing the pronoun with a noun:

> The orchard’s production is high, but selling <strong>the apples</strong> is easy.

Let’s look at another example sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Unlike some other schools in the area, the public high school actively encourages <strong>its</strong> students to seriously pursue extracurricular activities, even requiring students to choose <strong>it</strong> at the beginning of each semester.

There are two pronouns in the sentence above: “its” and “it.” The first pronoun, “its,” refers to “high school,” but the pronoun “it” has no noun that it replaces.

> Unlike some other schools in the area, the public high school actively encourages its students to seriously pursue extracurricular activities, even requiring students to choose <strong>an activity</strong> at the beginning of each semester.

We have all heard of the infamous “they.” The infamous “they” do all kinds of things.

In each of these cases, who exactly “they” are is not made clear.

Consider the following example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: After <strong>they</strong> discovered gene expression, great progress was made in developing understanding of how to prevent and solve health issues.

While one could make the case that perhaps “they” actually does refer to some specific group of people, the truth is that, in this context, “they” doesn’t seem to refer to anyone in particular.

> After the discovery of gene expression, great progress was made in developing understanding of how to prevent and solve health issues.

##  Sentence Correction Example 3  

Skeptics contend <u>that, though it often has a mundane cause, seemingly supernatural phenomena are</u> easily explained in scientific terms.

1. [ ] (A) that, though it often has a mundane cause, seemingly supernatural phenomena are
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) that any phenomenon that is seemingly supernatural often have mundane causes and are
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) that seemingly supernatural phenomena often have mundane causes that can be
4. [ ] (D) that it often has a mundane cause if it is seemingly supernatural phenomena, as those are
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E)  that they often have mundane causes, but a seemingly supernatural phenomenon is
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A)</b> Skeptics contend <b>that, though it often has a mundane cause, seemingly supernatural phenomena are
</b> easily explained in scientific terms.



The singular pronoun “it” has no antecedent in the version created with this choice. To what does “it” refer in the sentence? There is no singular noun in the sentence to which “it” could possibly refer. The two contenders, “supernatural phenomena” and “skeptics,” are both plural nouns (and we would never refer to “skeptics,” who are people, with the pronoun “it”).


Since this version has a pronoun that is missing an antecedent, this choice cannot possibly be correct. 


Notice also that the use of the contrast word “though” does not make sense in this context because there is no contrast between having a mundane cause and being easily explained in scientific terms. Those two things are perfectly in line with each other. 


> <b>(B)</b> Skeptics contend <b>that any phenomenon that is seemingly supernatural often have mundane causes and are
</b> easily explained in scientific terms.



 The singular noun “phenomenon” is incorrectly paired with the plural verbs “have” and “are” in the version created with this choice.


To more clearly see this error, we can cross off the relative clause that appears between the noun and verbs:


Skeptics contend that any <em>phenomenon</em> <s>that is seemingly supernatural often </s><em>have</em> mundane causes and <em>are</em> easily explained


“Phenomenon have … and are” is not grammatically correct. “Phenomenon has … as is” would be correct.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> Skeptics contend <b>that seemingly supernatural phenomena often have mundane causes that can be
</b> easily explained in scientific terms.



 This choice is the only one that creates a sentence version that is grammatically correct and conveys a clear meaning. 


Notice that this version eliminates the mismatched pronoun issues we see in some of the other choices. 


Furthermore, this version correctly pairs the plural noun “phenomena” with the plural verb “have.”


> <b>(D)</b> Skeptics contend <b>that it often has a mundane cause if it is seemingly supernatural phenomena, as those are
</b> easily explained in scientific terms.



The singular pronoun “it” and singular verbs “has” and “is” do not properly pair with the plural noun “phenomena.” We need the plural pronoun “they” and the plural verbs “have” and “are” to pair with “phenomena.” Notice that there is no singular antecedent in the sentence to which “it” and the associated verbs could refer.


Notice that, if you weren’t sure whether “phenomena” is singular or plural, you could still eliminate this choice because, if “phenomena” is plural, then “it,” “has,” and “is” do not agree with “phenomena,” and if “phenomena” were singular, then “those” would not agree with it.


Additionally, even with the correct pronouns and verbs to refer to “phenomena,” the sentence would convey a circular and illogical meaning. It does not make sense to say that seemingly supernatural phenomena often have a mundane cause <em>if</em> they are seemingly supernatural phenomena. The phenomena either are or aren’t “seemingly supernatural.” 


> <b>(E)</b> Skeptics contend <b> that they often have mundane causes, but a seemingly supernatural phenomenon is
</b> easily explained in scientific terms.



The plural pronoun “they” does not have a logical antecedent in the sentence. 


“They” cannot refer to the singular “phenomenon.” We would need the plural “phenomena” to refer to “they.”


If we were to believe that “they” is supposed to refer to the plural noun “skeptics,” the sentence would be saying that “skeptics often have mundane causes.” However, while it could make sense to say that skepticism or skeptical beliefs “have mundane causes,” it doesn’t make sense to say that <em>people</em> are caused by something, mundane or otherwise.


Furthermore, the conjunction “but” is used to connect contrasting statements. However, neither meaning of “they” would create a statement that contrasts with the fact that “a seemingly supernatural phenomenon is easily explained in scientific terms.” So, the use of “but” does not make sense in this context.


With respect to both grammar and meaning, there is no way to make heads or tails of the version created with this choice.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

##  Sentence Correction Example 4  

While the shares have been valued at all-time highs in recent weeks, <u>they expect it</u> to take a dip before reaching a new peak around the holidays.

1. [ ] (A) they expect it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) it is expected
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) they expect the stock
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) investors expect it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) the stock is expected

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



> <b>(A)</b> While the shares have been valued at all-time highs in recent weeks, <b>they expect it
</b> to take a dip before reaching a new peak around the holidays.



The pronoun “they” has no clear and logical antecedent. Given the context “they expect it,” “they” conveys a nonsensical meaning if it refers to the plural noun “shares,” “highs,” or “weeks.”


Even if we assume that “they” refers to “the infamous ‘they’” – some general “they” outside the sentence – we are still left with another problem: the antecedent of the pronoun “it” is missing from the sentence. Notice that the singular pronoun “it” cannot possibly refer to the plural noun “shares.” Logically, the “it” that would “take a dip” would be the stock whose “shares have been valued at all-time highs in recent weeks,” but the singular noun “stock” (or another appropriate singular noun) does not appear anywhere in the sentence.


Since the GMAT does not prefer sentences that use “the infamous ‘they’,” and since the pronoun “it” has no antecedent, this choice cannot be correct.


> <b>(B)</b> While the shares have been valued at all-time highs in recent weeks, <b>it is expected
</b> to take a dip before reaching a new peak around the holidays.



The antecedent of the pronoun “it” is missing from the sentence. Logically, the “it” that would “take a dip” would be the stock whose “shares have been valued at all-time highs in recent weeks,” but the singular noun “stock” (or another appropriate singular noun) does not appear anywhere in the sentence.


Since the sentence contains a pronoun that is missing an antecedent, this choice is not correct.


> <b>(C)</b> While the shares have been valued at all-time highs in recent weeks, <b>they expect the stock
</b> to take a dip before reaching a new peak around the holidays.



The pronoun “they” has no clear and logical antecedent. Given the context “they expect the stock,” “they” conveys a nonsensical meaning if it refers to the plural noun “shares,” “highs,” or “weeks.”


Even if we assume that “they” refers to “the infamous ‘they’” – some general “they” outside the sentence – we know that the GMAT does not prefer sentences that use “the infamous ‘they’.” Thus, we should look for a version of the sentence that conveys a clearer meaning.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D)</b> While the shares have been valued at all-time highs in recent weeks, <b>investors expect it
</b> to take a dip before reaching a new peak around the holidays.



The antecedent of the pronoun “it” is missing from the sentence. Notice that the singular pronoun “it” cannot possibly refer to the plural noun “shares.” Logically, the “it” that would “take a dip” would be the stock whose “shares have been valued at all-time highs in recent weeks,” but the singular noun “stock” (or another appropriate singular noun) does not appear anywhere in the sentence.


Since the sentence contains a pronoun that is missing an antecedent, this choice is not correct.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E)</b> While the shares have been valued at all-time highs in recent weeks, <b>the stock is expected
</b> to take a dip before reaching a new peak around the holidays.



This version is the only one that eliminates pronouns with unclear or no antecedents and conveys a clear, logical meaning.


Instead of using the pronoun “it,” which has no singular noun to replace in the sentence, this version uses the noun “the stock.”


Notice that it does not really matter <em>who</em> is expecting the stock to take a dip, only that “the stock is expected to take a dip.” Thus, this version avoids the problem that other versions have of using “they” with no clear and logical antecedent.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Sentence Correction Example 5  

Numerous anecdotal reports indicate <u>that squirrels that would attempt to enter an attic by chewing through eaves can be prevented from doing so via use of a humane repellent</u>.

1. [x] (A) that squirrels that would attempt to enter an attic by chewing through eaves can be prevented from doing so via use of a humane repellent
2. [ ] (B) that, when squirrels attempt to enter an attic by chewing through eaves, it can be prevented through the use of a humane repellent
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) that, if squirrels attempted to enter an attic by chewing through eaves, the use of a humane repellent can prevent them from doing that
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) that squirrels that attempt to enter an attic by chewing through eaves can be prevented by using a humane repellent
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) that, while squirrels may attempt to enter an attic by chewing through eaves, this can be prevented if a humane repellent is used
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A)</b> Numerous anecdotal reports indicate <b>that squirrels that would attempt to enter an attic by chewing through eaves can be prevented from doing so via use of a humane repellent
</b>.



This version conveys the logical meaning that, via the use of a humane repellant, squirrels that would attempt to enter an attic can be prevented from attempting to enter that attic.


Notice that the use of “doing so” allows the sentence to avoid using a pronoun such as “that” in an expression such as “doing that.”


Since there is no pronoun in this version, there is no need for there to be a referent for a pronoun.


> <b>(B)</b> Numerous anecdotal reports indicate <b>that, when squirrels attempt to enter an attic by chewing through eaves, it can be prevented through the use of a humane repellent
</b>.



“When squirrels attempt to enter …” is a description of a point in time “when” something happens. So, this sentence conveys the illogical meaning that, at a point in time, “it” can be prevented. Since, obviously, a point in time would not be prevented, “it” has no logical antecedent. Notice that nothing that would be prevented is directly mentioned before “it.”


> <b>(C)</b> Numerous anecdotal reports indicate <b>that, if squirrels attempted to enter an attic by chewing through eaves, the use of a humane repellent can prevent them from doing that
</b>.



This version conveys the illogical meaning that, if an event, “squirrels attempted to enter,” occurred in the past, the use of repellent can “prevent them from doing that,” as if, had squirrels not attempted to enter, the use of repellent would not “prevent them from doing that” in the future.


Also, since something that squirrels would do in the future is not directly mentioned, the sentence includes no logical referent for “that” in “prevent them from doing that.”


> <b>(D)</b> Numerous anecdotal reports indicate <b>that squirrels that attempt to enter an attic by chewing through eaves can be prevented by using a humane repellent
</b>.



This version conveys the illogical meaning that squirrels themselves can be prevented. It’s the actions of squirrels, such as squirrels’ attempts to enter an attic, that can be prevented, not squirrels.


Also, the meaning conveyed by “can be prevented by using a humane repellent” does not quite make sense. Something would be “prevented by a repellent” or “prevented through the use of a repellent,” but it wouldn’t be “prevented by <em>using</em> a repellent.” “Using X” conveys <em>how</em> someone does something, not <em>what</em> does it.


> <b>(E)</b> Numerous anecdotal reports indicate <b>that, while squirrels may attempt to enter an attic by chewing through eaves, this can be prevented if a humane repellent is used
</b>.



There is no antecedent for the pronoun “this.” Notice that the sentence does not directly mention something that “can be prevented.” “While squirrels may attempt X” is not something that would be prevented.


Also, “this can be prevented if a humane repellent is used” does not clearly convey that the repellent would prevent the occurrence in question. Rather, it conveys that the occurrence can be prevented “if a humane repellent is used,” as if something else would prevent the occurrence if the repellent were used. This meaning is not quite logical.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

##  Sentence Correction Example 6  

The company reported that it had received many inquiries about the new pea protein product, <u>but few of them followed up their inquiries by</u> making purchases.

1. [ ] (A) but few of them followed up their inquiries by
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) but few of those inquiries by people were followed up by
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) but, of those, few were followed up by people
4. [ ] (D) but few of them followed it up by
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) but few people who inquired followed it up by
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



> <b>(A)</b> The company reported that it had received many inquiries about the new pea protein product, <b>but few of them followed up their inquiries by
</b> making purchases.



The pronoun “them” has no logical antecedent. The only plural noun to which “them” could conceivably refer is “inquiries,” and inquiries would not have “followed up their inquiries.”


We can tell that “them” is supposed to refer to the people who made the inquiries, but the people who made the inquiries are not mentioned.


> <b>(B)</b> The company reported that it had received many inquiries about the new pea protein product, <b>but few of those inquiries by people were followed up by
</b> making purchases.



While this choice itself may appear to be correct, when we combine this choice with the non-underlined portion “making purchases,” we see that this version conveys a nonsensical meaning.


“Few of those inquiries … were followed up by making purchases” suggests that inquiries would be followed up by making purchases, as if somehow, inquiries have the ability to make purchases, and by making purchases, inquiries would be followed up.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(C)</b> The company reported that it had received many inquiries about the new pea protein product, <b>but, of those, few were followed up by people
</b> making purchases.



Both “those” and “few” logically refer to “inquiries,” and this version is grammatically correct and conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(D)</b> The company reported that it had received many inquiries about the new pea protein product, <b>but few of them followed it up by
</b> making purchases.



The pronoun “them” has no logical antecedent. The only plural noun to which “them” could conceivably refer is “inquiries,” and inquiries would not have “followed … up by making purchases.” We can tell that “them” is supposed to refer to the people who made the inquiries, but the people who made the inquiries are not mentioned.


Also, this version includes no logical referent for the pronoun “it.” The sentence says only, “few of them inquired” and does not mention an “inquiry” or something else that few of them followed up and that “it” would refer to. Since it does not make sense that someone would have followed up “the company” or “the new pea protein product,” “it” doesn’t logically refer to either of those singular nouns.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E)</b> The company reported that it had received many inquiries about the new pea protein product, <b>but few people who inquired followed it up by
</b> making purchases.



This version includes no logical referent for the pronoun “it.” The sentence says only that people “inquired” and does not mention an “inquiry” or something else that people would follow up and that “it” would refer to. Since people would not follow up “the company” or “the new pea protein product,” “it” doesn’t logically refer to either of those singular nouns.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

{{< /alert >}}
</details></h5>


Let’s now discuss recognizing that a sentence is flawed because a pronoun has an adjective as its antecedent.
