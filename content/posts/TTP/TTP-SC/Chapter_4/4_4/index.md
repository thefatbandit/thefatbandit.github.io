## Lesson Content
Pronouns cannot take adjectives as their antecedents.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

An adjective can’t be the antecedent of a pronoun.
{{< /alert >}}

Sometimes in a sentence, a word that could serve as the antecedent of a pronoun is present but acting, not as a noun, but as an adjective and, therefore, cannot function logically as the antecedent of a pronoun in that sentence.

Consider the following example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The number of grams of <strong>Colombian</strong> gold sold annually has increased each year since 2005, a fact demonstrating buyer confidence in the quality of the gold produced <strong>there</strong>.

The pronoun in this sentence is “there.” Does the pronoun have an antecedent? If we don’t read carefully enough, it may appear that “Colombian” is the antecedent.

The following version of the sentence conveys a clear meaning:

> The number of grams of gold from <strong>Colombia </strong>sold annually has increased each year since 2005, a fact demonstrating buyer confidence in the quality of the gold produced <strong>there</strong>.

Now the pronoun “there”<em> </em>has the antecedent “Colombia.”

Consider the following sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: I wish the <strong>bread section</strong> were at the front of the store, so that I could purchase <strong>it</strong> more conveniently.

Clearly, the intended antecedent of the pronoun “it” is “bread.” In other words, the speaker used the pronoun “it” to avoid repeating “bread.” However, the speaker made a small yet crucial error.

So then, is the word “section” the antecedent of the pronoun “it?”<em> </em>No, the word “section” cannot be the antecedent because shoppers do not purchase sections of the grocery store (particularly not conveniently).

> I wish the bread section were at the front of the store, so that I could purchase <strong>bread</strong> more conveniently.

Alternatively, we could remove the noun that the hidden antecedent is modifying so that the antecedent is no longer hidden:

> I wish the <strong>bread</strong> <s>section</s> were at the front of the store, so that I could purchase <strong>it</strong> more conveniently.

Removing the noun “section” from the sentence allows “bread” to function as a noun rather than as an adjective, making “bread” the clear and logical antecedent of the pronoun “it.”

Let’s look at some more examples and seek to pinpoint the errors in logic and meaning that reveal faulty antecedents because pronouns are attempting to take adjectives as their antecedents.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Jason was nervous about the <strong>chemistry exam</strong> because <strong>it</strong> was not his best subject.

A chemistry exam is not a subject in school.

> Jason was nervous about the chemistry exam because <strong>chemistry</strong> was not his best subject.

Here’s another:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: When Lena visits New York City, she always attends a <strong>Broadway show</strong>, even though <strong>it’s</strong> a touristy section of Manhattan.

Is it logical to call a Broadway show a “section of Manhattan?” The touristy section to which<em> </em>“it”<em> </em>is supposed to refer is Broadway itself.

In the following version, the problem is solved:

> When Lena visits New York City, she always attends a Broadway show, even though <strong>Broadway</strong> is a touristy section of Manhattan.

Now Broadway, not a show, is correctly identified as the section of Manhattan.

##  Sentence Correction Example 7  

<u>Since the restaurant’s investment in a state-of-the-art juice press, they have been selling it</u> much more, alone and as ingredients in healthy drinks.

1. [ ] (A) Since the restaurant’s investment in a state-of-the-art juice press, they have been selling it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) The restaurant, having invested in a state-of-the-art juice press, it has been selling them
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) Since the restaurant invested in a state-of-the-art juice press, it has been selling juices
4. [ ] (D) With the restaurant’s investment in a state-of-the-art juice press, they sell
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) Having invested in a state-of-the-art juice press, the restaurant sells it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



While all versions of the sentence mention a juice press, a juice press is not juice. So, when versions of the sentence use pronouns to say that the restaurant is “selling it” or “selling them,” the pronouns “it” or “them” seem either to illogically refer to the juice press or to have no referents. The correct version of the sentence uses nouns and a pronoun effectively, clearly conveying a logical meaning.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Since the restaurant’s investment in a state-of-the-art juice press, they have been selling it
</b> much more, alone and as ingredients in healthy drinks.



The pronoun “they” has no clear referent. While one might reasonably assume that “they” refers to people who own or work at the restaurant, the sentence is not effective, because it leaves the reader wondering who exactly “they” are.


Also, the pronoun “it” has no clear referent. It could refer to “juice press,” but that people involved with the restaurant would be selling the juice press does not make sense. Alternatively, “it” could be juice, but “juice” does not appear as a noun in the sentence, only as an adjective modifying “press.”


Meanwhile, there is also an issue with agreement in number, as the sentence seems to be saying that “it,” which is singular, is being used as “ingredients,” which is plural.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>The restaurant, having invested in a state-of-the-art juice press, it has been selling them
</b> much more, alone and as ingredients in healthy drinks.



This sentence is awkwardly constructed, in that the role of subject is filled twice, first by the noun “restaurant,” and then by the pronoun “it.”


Also, the pronoun “them” has no antecedent, as the word “juice” in “juice press” is not serving as a noun, but rather as an adjective. So, while the reader may be able to deduce that “them” refers to juices, the sentence does not clearly indicate that it does.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> <b>Since the restaurant invested in a state-of-the-art juice press, it has been selling juices
</b> much more, alone and as ingredients in healthy drinks.



While conceivably “it” could refer to “juice press,” the truth is that meaning clearly dictates that “it” refers to “restaurant.” So, “it” has a clear referent.


Also, by using the noun “juices,” this version, unlike the versions that use pronouns in that spot, clearly conveys what the restaurant is selling.


Thus, this version clearly and concisely conveys its meaning.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>With the restaurant’s investment in a state-of-the-art juice press, they sell
</b> much more, alone and as ingredients in healthy drinks.



This version starts off with a meaning issue, as the use of “with” results in this version’s conveying a vague and somewhat illogical meaning. In what sense exactly are “they” “with” the investment in the juice press?


Also, “they” has no clear referent. While one might reasonably assume that “they” refers to people who own or work at the restaurant, the sentence is not effective, because it leaves the reader wondering who exactly “they” are.


Also, notice that, when this choice is combined with the non-underlined portion, the wording that results is “they sell much more, alone and as ingredients ….” What “they” sell much more of is not made clear, and the sentence could be interpreted as conveying that “they” sell, alone or as ingredients, in other words, that whoever is selling acts as ingredients.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>Having invested in a state-of-the-art juice press, the restaurant sells it
</b> much more, alone and as ingredients in healthy drinks.



While this version is concise, since juice itself is not mentioned, the apparent referent of “it” is “juice press,” and thus, this version seems to convey the illogical meaning that the restaurant sells the juice press much more.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

##  Sentence Correction Example 8  

Driving in a sandstorm can result in harm to a car’s engine, <u>as, while airborne, it</u> can clog the car’s air filter or even enter the fuel line.

1. [ ] (A) as, while airborne, it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) while it is airborne, since sand
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) as sand, while airborne,
4. [ ] (D) as, while airborne, some
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) since sand, while airborne, some
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



In the original version, there is no logical antecedent for the pronoun “it,” as the word “sand” is, in this case, part of the word “sandstorm,” and a sandstorm itself would not be airborne and, thus, clog an air filter or enter a fuel line. What makes more sense is that sand, airborne during the storm, would clog the air filter or enter the fuel line, and the correct version of the sentence clearly conveys that it is sand that does so.


> <b>(A)</b> Driving in a sandstorm can result in harm to a car’s engine, <b>as, while airborne, it
</b> can clog the car’s air filter or even enter the fuel line.



Since a sandstorm would not be airborne and, thus, clog an air filter or enter a fuel line, there is no logical antecedent for “it” in this version.


> <b>(B)</b> Driving in a sandstorm can result in harm to a car’s engine, <b>while it is airborne, since sand
</b> can clog the car’s air filter or even enter the fuel line.



In this version, “while it is airborne” seems to modify “engine,” resulting in the sentence’s conveying the nonsensical meaning that driving in a sandstorm can result in harm to a car’s engine, while that engine is airborne, since sand can clog the car’s air filter or even enter the fuel line.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> Driving in a sandstorm can result in harm to a car’s engine, <b>as sand, while airborne,
</b> can clog the car’s air filter or even enter the fuel line.



The use of “sand,” rather than the pronoun “it,” results in this version’s clearly conveying that sand, while airborne, can clog a car’s air filter or enter the fuel line.


> <b>(D)</b> Driving in a sandstorm can result in harm to a car’s engine, <b>as, while airborne, some
</b> can clog the car’s air filter or even enter the fuel line.



While we may be able to discern that the pronoun “some” refers to “sand,” this version does not provide a logical referent for “some.” Thus, this version does not clearly convey what it is that, while airborne, can clog a car’s air filter or enter the fuel line.


> <b>(E)</b> Driving in a sandstorm can result in harm to a car’s engine, <b>since sand, while airborne, some
</b> can clog the car’s air filter or even enter the fuel line.



While this version clearly conveys that it is sand that, while airborne, can clog a car’s air filter or enter the fuel line, this version includes an unnecessary pronoun, “some,” which needlessly fills the role of the subject of the second clause of the sentence, which role is already filled by the noun “sand.”


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

Now, there is one important exception to this rule about pronouns not referring to adjectives.
