## Lesson Content
As we already know, indefinite pronouns are pronouns that do not refer to a specific person, place, or thing.

<a href="https://gmat.targettestprep.com/lesson/1403"><u>Singular Indefinite Pronouns </u></a>

<a href="https://gmat.targettestprep.com/lesson/1404"><u>Plural Indefinite Pronouns</u></a>

<a href="https://gmat.targettestprep.com/lesson/1405"><u>Other Indefinite Pronouns – Sometimes Singular, Sometimes Plural</u></a>

Some of the most common pronoun-antecedent errors on the GMAT include two pronouns – an indefinite pronoun and a possessive pronoun.

Take the following example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Everyone has their own problems.

In this sentence, we have two pronouns: the indefinite pronoun “everyone” and the possessive pronoun “their.” In this case, the indefinite pronoun “everyone” acts as the antecedent for the possessive pronoun “their.”

Since English-language speakers often use possessive pronouns incorrectly in speech, this sentence may sound correct to you.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The indefinite pronoun “everyone” is always singular. That it is singular makes sense, as “everyone” is really just “every” and “one” combined, and pronouns that correctly refer to phrases that begin with “every” are always singular. Other common singular indefinite pronouns are “anyone,” “someone,” “no one,” and “nobody.”
{{< /alert >}}

Thus, we have an error in pronoun-antecedent agreement.

> <strong>Everyone</strong> has <strong>his</strong> own problems.

> <strong>Everyone</strong> has <strong>her</strong> own problems.

> <strong>Everyone</strong> has <strong>his or her</strong> own problems.

Note that, in the sentences above, we must also deal with the issue of gender agreement.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Possessive pronouns and indefinite pronouns that appear together in a sentence must agree in number.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at some other examples.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: <strong>Anyone</strong> can make a difference in <strong>their</strong> community when working alone, but effecting change is often easier when one is working as part of a like-minded collective.

The possessive pronoun “their” does not agree with its singular antecedent, the indefinite pronoun “anyone.” We must use a singular possessive pronoun.

The following is a correct version of the sentence:

> <strong>Anyone</strong> can make a difference in <strong>her or his</strong> community when working alone, but effecting change is often easier when one is working as part of a like-minded collective.

Here’s another:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: <strong>No one</strong> who genuinely cares about earning a high grade in this class would wait until the last week of the semester to start writing <strong>their</strong> term paper, particularly since the term paper is weighted even more heavily than the final exam.

“No one” is a singular antecedent, which requires singular pronouns to refer to it.

The following is a correct version of the sentence:

> <strong>No one </strong>who genuinely cares about earning a high grade in this class would wait until the last week of the semester to start writing <strong>her</strong> term paper, particularly since the term paper is weighted even more heavily than the final exam.

Let’s look at one more:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Throughout much of the country, the immigration debate is dominated by claims that current law allows <strong>everyone</strong> to cross the border unimpeded and that <strong>their</strong> motives for entering the country are usually nefarious.

At first glance, the sentence above appears to make sense, but upon closer reading, we see that the plural possessive pronoun “their” has no antecedent, since the indefinite pronoun “everyone” is singular.

The following is a correct version of the sentence:

> Throughout much of the country, the immigration debate is dominated by claims that current law allows everyone to cross the border unimpeded and that <strong>people’s</strong> motives for entering the country are usually nefarious.

In this case, it makes sense to replace “their” with the possessive noun “people’s” in order to clarify whose motives we’re talking about and avoid the pronoun-antecedent disagreement with “everyone.”

##### Question Level: easy



This sentence contrasts two different activities, parkour and freerunning. In order for the sentence to be effective, it has to convey the difference between the two activities in a clear, organized manner, and any pronoun-referent relationships must be clear.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A)</b> <b>Parkour differs from freerunning in that parkour involves simply taking an efficient line over, under, or through obstacles, whereas people freerunning add
</b> flips and other stylistic moves.



The way in which this version is constructed – one clause begins with “parkour involves,” and the next begins with “people freerunning add” – may cause this version to sound a little off.


However, since the subjects of two clauses that work together to present a contrast do not have to match, this sentence is actually well constructed and conveys its meaning effectively.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>What makes parkour and freerunning different is that, in doing parkour, people simply take an efficient line over, under, or through obstacles, while, in freerunning, it adds
</b> flips and other stylistic moves.



This sentence starts off with a meaning issue, as “what makes parkour and freerunning different” does not clearly convey that a contrast between the two is being made. One could interpret that wording as conveying that the rest of the sentence will convey how the two activities are different from something else.


Also, the use of the pronoun “it” is flawed. Since it does not make sense that “it” would refer to any of the singular nouns in the sentence, “it” must refer to “people.” Of course, this reference is flawed because “it” does not agree in number with “people.”


> <b>(C)</b> <b>Parkour is different from freerunning in that, when they do parkour, they simply take an efficient line over, under, or through obstacles, unlike freerunning, when they add
</b> flips and other stylistic moves.



The pronoun “they” lacks a referent, as it does not clearly refer to any particular group of people.


Also, the contrast made is faulty, because the sentence attempts to compare “when they do parkour” with freerunning itself.


Also, what the wording “when they add flips and other stylistic moves” is meant to convey is not clear, since that wording does not clearly indicate that flips and other stylistic moves are characteristics of freerunning.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>Parkour and freerunning differ because everyone doing parkour simply take an efficient line over, under, or through obstacles, but not when they are freerunning, adding
</b> flips and other stylistic moves.



This version begins with a meaning issue, as “parkour and freerunning differ because” seems to convey that what follows will convey <em>why</em> they differ rather than <em>how</em> they differ.


Also, the singular indefinite pronoun “everyone” does not agree with the plural “take.”


Also, “they” does not agree with its antecedent, “everyone.”


Also, the purpose of the phrase “adding flips …” is not clear.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>Whereas, in parkour, it means taking an efficient line over, under, or through obstacles, in freerunning, it is different, because of adding
</b> flips and other stylistic moves.



Both times the pronoun “it” appears in the sentence there is no clear referent.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)
