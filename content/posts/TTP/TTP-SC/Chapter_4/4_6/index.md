## Lesson Content
Some sentences contain unnecessary pronouns.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

If a sentence contains a noun and a pronoun that play the same role or two pronouns that play the same role, then the sentence contains a useless word and, thus, is illogically constructed.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: <strong>Rich Roll</strong>, once an unhealthy corporate lawyer, <strong>he</strong> decided to change his life at age 40.

While the above sentence may sound basically fine, if we consider it carefully, we can see that the noun “Rich Roll” and the pronoun “he” both fill the role of the subject of the sentence.

> Incorrect: <strong>Rich Roll</strong><s>, once an unhealthy corporate lawyer,</s> <strong>he</strong> decided to change his life at age 40.

Once the phrase is removed, it becomes obvious that the pronoun “he” is unnecessary.

> Rich Roll, once an unhealthy corporate lawyer, decided to change his life at age 40.

or

> Once an unhealthy corporate lawyer, Rich Roll decided to change his life at age 40.

Let’s consider another example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: <strong>The San Francisco garter snake</strong>, which is a subspecies of the common garter snake and is considered one of the most colorful snakes inhabiting Earth today, <strong>it</strong> has been designated an endangered species since 1967.

Once again, we have a sentence in which the subject, “the San Francisco Garter snake,” is unnecessarily repeated by a pronoun, “it.” We can make the repetition more obvious by crossing off the long relative clause that follows “snake”:

> Incorrect: <strong>The San Francisco garter snake</strong><s>, which is a subspecies of the common garter snake and is considered one of the most colorful snakes inhabiting Earth today,</s> <strong>it</strong> has been designated an endangered species since 1967.

The fact that the clause that appears between “The San Francisco garter snake” and “it” is quite long does not change the fact that there is no need to repeat the subject.

> The San Francisco garter snake, which is a subspecies of the common garter snake and is considered one of the most colorful snakes inhabiting Earth today, has been designated an endangered species since 1967.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Sentence Correction question writers may add unnecessary pronouns to incorrect answer choices to test test-takers’ attention to detail.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 11  

Atheists contend that atheism, which is often depicted as <u>anti-religious, it</u> is simply non-religious.

1. [ ] (A) anti-religious, it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) anti-religious,
3. [ ] (C) anti-religious, but it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) anti-religious, that it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) anti-religious but
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A)</b> Atheists contend that atheism, which is often depicted as <b>anti-religious, it
</b> is simply non-religious.



The pronoun “it” in “<em>it</em> is simply non-religious” is redundant. To better see this error, we can eliminate the relative clause that separates the first part of the independent clause from the second part:


Atheists contend that <strong>atheism</strong><s>, which is often depicted as anti-religious,</s> <strong>it</strong> is simply non-religious.


Notice that the pronoun “it” refers to the noun “atheism.” So, the meaning conveyed is “Atheists contend that <em>atheism atheism</em> is simply non-religious.” Thus, the pronoun “it” is unnecessary in this context.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B)</b> Atheists contend that atheism, which is often depicted as <b>anti-religious,
</b> is simply non-religious.



 This is the only choice that creates a complete, grammatical sentence with no redundant pronouns. To confirm that the sentence is complete and that it makes sense, we can eliminate the relative clause that breaks up the independent clause:


Atheists contend that atheism<s>, which is often depicted as anti-religious,</s> is simply non-religious.


“Atheists contend that atheism is simply non-religious” is a logical and complete independent clause. The relative pronoun “which” properly modifies the noun “atheism.”


The sentence meaning makes sense and the sentence contains no redundancies.


> <b>(C)</b> Atheists contend that atheism, which is often depicted as <b>anti-religious, but it
</b> is simply non-religious.



This version contains an incomplete clause.


Since “but it is simply non-religious” begins a new thought, we are left asking the question, “Atheists contend that atheism <em>what</em>?”


To better see this error, we can eliminate the relative clause that separates the first part of the sentence from the final clause:


Atheists contend that atheism<s>, which is often depicted as anti-religious,</s> but it is simply non-religious.


“Atheists contend that atheism” is not a complete thought. Thus, this choice is incorrect.


> <b>(D)</b> Atheists contend that atheism, which is often depicted as <b>anti-religious, that it
</b> is simply non-religious.



 The pronouns “that” and “it” in “<em>that it</em> is simply non-religious” are redundant. To better see this error, we can eliminate the relative clause that separates the first part of the independent clause from the second part:


Atheists contend <strong>that atheism</strong><s>, which is often depicted as anti-religious,</s> <strong>that it</strong> is simply non-religious.


“Atheists contend that atheism that it is …” does not make sense. Notice that, since the pronoun “it” refers to the noun “atheism,” the meaning conveyed is, “Atheists contend that atheism <em>that atheism</em> is ….”


> <b>(E)</b> Atheists contend that atheism, which is often depicted as <b>anti-religious but
</b> is simply non-religious.



This version contains an incomplete clause.


Since everything from “which” to the end of this version is one long relative clause, we are left asking the question, “Atheists contend that atheism <em>what</em>?”


To better see this error, we can eliminate the relative clause:


Atheists contend that atheism<s>, which is often depicted as anti-religious but is simply non-religious</s>.


“Atheists contend that atheism” is not a complete thought. Thus, this choice is incorrect.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

##  Sentence Correction Example 12  

<u>The analysts on the call noticed the CEO, when pressed for more information on the inventory growth, that he steered</u> the conversation toward a related, but less controversial, topic.

1. [ ] (A) The analysts on the call noticed the CEO, when pressed for more information on the inventory growth, that he steered
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) The CEO, the analysts on the call noticed, when pressed for more information on the inventory growth, steering
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) The analysts on the call noticed that the CEO, when pressed for more information on the inventory growth, he steered
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) As the analysts on the call noticed, the CEO, pressed for more information on the inventory growth, steered
5. [ ] (E) The CEO, when pressed for more information on the inventory growth, and the analysts on the call noticed this, he steered
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



In all of the versions of the sentence, “the CEO” serves as either the subject of a modifying clause or the subject of an independent clause, and in some versions, the same role is also played by the pronoun “he.” Thus, in each of some versions there are two words that simultaneous fill the role of the subject, and therefore those versions are poorly constructed.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>The analysts on the call noticed the CEO, when pressed for more information on the inventory growth, that he steered
</b> the conversation toward a related, but less controversial, topic.



This version uses “that” illogically. “That” should either be directly after “noticed” or not be present at all.


Also, in this version, two words, “CEO” and “he,” fill the role of subject, a structural issue that makes the sentence wordy and awkward.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>The CEO, the analysts on the call noticed, when pressed for more information on the inventory growth, steering
</b> the conversation toward a related, but less controversial, topic.



Since the participle “steering” cannot on its own serve as the main verb of a sentence, this version contains no main verb and, therefore, is not a complete sentence.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>The analysts on the call noticed that the CEO, when pressed for more information on the inventory growth, he steered
</b> the conversation toward a related, but less controversial, topic.



This version is poorly constructed, in that it uses both “the CEO” and the pronoun “he” as the subject of a clause. In other words, this version would be fine if the pronoun “he” were removed, but, as it is, it’s redundant.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D)</b> <b>As the analysts on the call noticed, the CEO, pressed for more information on the inventory growth, steered
</b> the conversation toward a related, but less controversial, topic.



This version clearly and effectively conveys that, as the analysts noticed, the CEO steered the conversation toward a related topic.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>The CEO, when pressed for more information on the inventory growth, and the analysts on the call noticed this, he steered
</b> the conversation toward a related, but less controversial, topic.



The version is illogically constructed in that, two words, “CEO” and “he,” simultaneously fill the role of the subject. Also, the overall structure, with the clause “the analysts on the call noticed this” dropped into the middle of the sentence, is awkward.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
