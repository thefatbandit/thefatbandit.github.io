## Lesson Content
Some pronoun-antecedent errors on the GMAT involve pronouns and antecedents that don’t work logically with each other.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The term “social media” is often used to describe platforms such as Facebook and Twitter, but it is actually any form of internet communication, including blogs, wikis, and more.

In this sentence, the pronoun “it” refers to the antecedent ‘the term “social media,”’ not “social media” itself.

> The <strong>term </strong>“social media” is often used to describe platforms such as Facebook and Twitter, but <strong>it</strong> actually <strong>refers to</strong> any form of internet communication, including blogs, wikis, and more.

Another way to fix the sentence is to replace the pronoun with the noun to which it is intended to refer, as in the sentence below:

> The term “social media” is often used to describe platforms such as Facebook and Twitter, but <strong>social media </strong>is actually any form of internet communication, including blogs, wikis, and more.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When the word “term” is used in reference to the name of something, make sure that any pronouns meant to refer to that thing refer to the thing itself and not to the word “term.”
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at a couple of additional examples.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The label “socialism” has long been avoided by many, but recent news stories have made it a more mainstream ideology.

Presumably, the pronoun “it”<em> </em>is supposed to refer to socialism, the newly mainstream ideology.

> The label “socialism” has long been avoided by many, but recent news stories have made <strong>socialism</strong> a more mainstream ideology.

Simply replacing the pronoun with the noun “socialism” makes clear what the ideology is.

Here is another:

> <u>Incorrect</u>:<em> </em>The moniker “Never Trumpers” was popularized during the 2016 United States presidential election, though they have dwindled in number since that time.<em> </em>

The pronoun “they” presumably should refer to the Never Trumpers, but since the sentence mentions the moniker and not the Never Trumpers themselves, the use of “they” is not logical.

> The moniker “Never Trumpers” was popularized during the 2016 United States presidential election, though <strong>the Never Trumpers</strong> have dwindled in number since that time.

The sentence now makes sense.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When a word such as “term,” “label,” or “name” introduces a noun in quotes, be on the lookout for a pronoun-antecedent error in the sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at a pronoun-antecedent error that may be less obvious.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: People often say the brand name Coke when they order it in a restaurant, but what they actually are asking for is any cola.

What exactly are the people ordering? Certainly not “the brand name Coke.” They are ordering a soda, not a brand name.

> People often say the brand name Coke when they order <strong>cola</strong> in a restaurant, but what they actually are asking for is any cola.

##  Sentence Correction Example 13  

When it began training in 1980, <u>SEAL Team Six was so named for the purpose of misleading</u> Soviet intelligence officials into thinking that six SEAL teams existed when, in fact, there were only two.

1. [x] (A) SEAL Team Six was so named for the purpose of misleading
2. [ ] (B) the name “SEAL Team Six” was used to mislead
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) the name “SEAL Team Six” used for the purpose of misleading
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) the purpose of using the name “SEAL Team Six” was to mislead
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) SEAL Team Six were named that way to mislead
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



In evaluating the answer choices, we must be sure to consider the full sentence created, not just the underlined portion. Notice that, in this case, the underlined portion of the sentence begins immediately after the introductory phrase “When it began its training in 1980.” So, the noun that immediately follows that phrase (and thus begins the underlined portion) must be something that could logically be the “it” that “began its training in 1980.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A)</b> When it began training in 1980, <b>SEAL Team Six was so named for the purpose of misleading
</b> Soviet intelligence officials into thinking that six SEAL teams existed when, in fact, there were only two.



Although the phrasing of the sentence created with this choice may seem somewhat unnatural at first, this choice is in fact the only one with which a grammatically correct, complete, and logical sentence is created.


The singular pronoun “it” in “When <em>it</em> began training in 1980” logically refers to the singular “SEAL Team Six,” and the singular subject “SEAL Team Six” is properly paired with the singular verb “was,” which is the main verb of the sentence.


> <b>(B)</b> When it began training in 1980, <b>the name “SEAL Team Six” was used to mislead
</b> Soviet intelligence officials into thinking that six SEAL teams existed when, in fact, there were only two.



While it makes sense to say that “the name ‘SEAL Team Six’ was used to mislead Soviet intelligence officials …,” we must remember to read the answer choice in the context of the full sentence. When we do so, we see that the sentence created with this choice is illogical.


Notice that “the name ‘SEAL Team Six’” is the only singular noun or noun phrase in the sentence. Thus, “it” in “When it began training in 1980,” can refer only to “the name ‘SEAL Team Six.’” Since the idea that the name “began training” is nonsensical, “the name ‘SEAL Team Six’” is an illogical antecedent of “it.”


> <b>(C)</b> When it began training in 1980, <b>the name “SEAL Team Six” used for the purpose of misleading
</b> Soviet intelligence officials into thinking that six SEAL teams existed when, in fact, there were only two.



In this version, “it” in “When it began training in 1980,” seems to refer to “the name ‘SEAL Team Six.’” However, since the idea that the name “began training” is nonsensical, “the name ‘SEAL Team Six’” is an illogical antecedent of “it.”


Additionally, notice that the version created with this choice is not a complete sentence. “Used” is not a finite verb. After all, “used” doesn’t name an action done by “the name ‘Seal Team Six.’” Rather, “used” is a participle that describes “the name ‘Seal Team Six.’” So, this version has no main verb and, thus, no independent clause.


> <b>(D)</b> When it began training in 1980, <b>the purpose of using the name “SEAL Team Six” was to mislead
</b> Soviet intelligence officials into thinking that six SEAL teams existed when, in fact, there were only two.



While it makes sense to say that “the purpose of using the name ‘SEAL Team Six’ was to mislead Soviet intelligence officials …,” we must remember to read the answer choice in the context of the full sentence. In context, this choice produces a nonsensical sentence.


The statement “When it began training in 1980, the purpose …” conveys that the purpose “began training,” a nonsensical meaning.


> <b>(E)</b> When it began training in 1980, <b>SEAL Team Six were named that way to mislead
</b> Soviet intelligence officials into thinking that six SEAL teams existed when, in fact, there were only two.



Notice that, in “When it began training in 1980, ‘SEAL Team Six’,” the singular pronoun “it” logically refers to the singular “SEAL Team Six.” However, it does not make sense to then also pair the singular “SEAL Team Six” with the plural verb “were,” as this choice does. After all, if “SEAL Team Six” is singular and is correctly referred to by the singular “it,” then “SEAL Team Six” cannot in the same sentence also be plural and agree with the plural “were.”


Additionally, the wording “‘SEAL Team Six were named that way” does not convey an entirely clear meaning. Named what “way”? There is no particular “way” described in the sentence.


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>Be sure that the noun to which a pronoun refers makes sense as the person or thing performing the action associated with that pronoun. 


<strong>Key Concept:</strong> Every sentence must have at least one subject and at least one finite verb.


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>Confirm that singular subjects are paired with singular verbs and that plural subjects are paired with plural verbs.


<strong>Key Concept:</strong> If a choice contains no grammar or meaning errors, even if it does not “sound” how we might expect it to, then that choice will be correct.


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>Don't select answer choices that produce sentence versions that convey illogical meanings.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

##  Sentence Correction Example 14  

<u>While Aaron had aspired to play the game of football professionally, he quit it upon discovering how much practice was required for learning how to kick it</u> well enough.

1. [ ] (A) While Aaron had aspired to play the game of football professionally, he quit it upon discovering how much practice was required for learning how to kick it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) Aaron, aspiring to play the game of football professionally, quitting when he discovered how much practice was required for learning how to kick
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) Though Aaron had aspired to play the game of football professionally, but he quit upon discovering how much practice was required for learning how to kick the ball
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) Although Aaron had aspired to play the game of football professionally, he quit that when he discovered how much practice was required for learning how to kick it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) Aaron, aspiring to play the game of football professionally, quit upon his discovering how much practice was required for learning how to kick

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



When the pronouns “it” and “that” are used in the second clauses of some versions of the sentence, the pronouns do not have logical antecedents. So, while a reader could figure out the intended meanings of those versions, those versions do not express a meaning effectively. The correct version, which does not include either “it” or “that,” effectively conveys a logical meaning.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>While Aaron had aspired to play the game of football professionally, he quit it upon discovering how much practice was required for learning how to kick it
</b> well enough.



In this version, the pronoun “it” is used twice.


The first “it” seems to refer to “the game of football.” While this reference is perhaps not ideal, since the sentence does not make clear that Aaron ever played the game of football, if we go with the idea that the first “it” refers to “the game of football,” the use of the first “it” basically makes sense.


However, since the football itself was never mentioned and the game of football was, the “it” at the end of the sentence also seems to refer to “the game of football.” As a result, the sentence seems to convey the nonsensical meaning that practice was required for learning to kick the game of football.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>Aaron, aspiring to play the game of football professionally, quitting when he discovered how much practice was required for learning how to kick
</b> well enough.



This version is composed of the noun “Aaron” and two phrases, “aspiring to play …” and “quitting when he discovered …” Therefore, this version is not a complete sentence.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> <b>Though Aaron had aspired to play the game of football professionally, but he quit upon discovering how much practice was required for learning how to kick the ball
</b> well enough.



This version uses the “the ball” rather than “it” to clearly convey that it was learning to kick the ball that would take practice, and this version has no pronoun-referent issues. However, because the sentence opens with the conjunction “though,” the conjunction “but” is redundant, and the structure of this version is therefore illogical.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>Although Aaron had aspired to play the game of football professionally, he quit that when he discovered how much practice was required for learning how to kick it
</b> well enough.



The pronoun “that” has no clear antecedent, as it is not clear what Aaron quit.


Also, since the football itself was not mentioned, “it” seems to refer to “the game of football,” and, as a result, the sentence seems to convey that practice was required for learning to kick the game of football.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E)</b> <b>Aaron, aspiring to play the game of football professionally, quit upon his discovering how much practice was required for learning how to kick
</b> well enough.



While this version does not make entirely clear what Aaron quit or what he had to learn to kick, because this version does not use the pronoun “it” or “that,” this version is not characterized by the types of pronoun-referent issues seen in the other versions and conveys a meaning that basically makes sense.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Sentence Correction Example 15  

The name “pie” is used for various types of pastries, <u>and it is served sometimes as a main course dish and sometimes as a dessert</u>.

1. [ ] (A) and it is served sometimes as a main course dish and sometimes as a dessert
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) sometimes they are served as main course dishes and sometimes as desserts
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) sometimes served as main course dishes and sometimes as desserts
4. [ ] (D) and is sometimes served as a main course dish or dessert
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) and they sometimes serve it as a main course dish and sometimes as a dessert
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



A name cannot logically be “served” as a dish, since a name is a word. So, even though the singular pronoun “it” agrees with the singular “name,” the original version conveys an illogical meaning, that the name “pie” is served. The correct version uses a structure such that the meaning conveyed is that pastries, rather than the name, are served.


> <b>(A)</b> The name “pie” is used for various types of pastries, <b>and it is served sometimes as a main course dish and sometimes as a dessert
</b>.



Since the singular pronoun “it” can only refer to a singular noun, “it” seems to refer to ‘the name “pie,”’ and thus this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that the name “pie” is served as a main course dish or dessert.


> <b>(B)</b> The name “pie” is used for various types of pastries, <b>sometimes they are served as main course dishes and sometimes as desserts
</b>.



While this version does a fairly good job of expressing a logical meaning, since there is only a comma and no conjunction or semicolon connecting the two clauses that make up this version, the structure of this version is not that of a proper sentence but, rather, a comma splice.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> The name “pie” is used for various types of pastries, <b>sometimes served as main course dishes and sometimes as desserts
</b>.



In this version, the phrase “sometimes served as main course dishes and sometimes as desserts” correctly modifies “pastries,” and the meaning conveyed is logical, as pastries, unlike the name “pie,” would be served as main course dishes or desserts.


> <b>(D)</b> The name “pie” is used for various types of pastries, <b>and is sometimes served as a main course dish or dessert
</b>.



While this version does not refer to ‘the name “pie”’ by using a pronoun, the meaning that this version conveys is nonsensical, since the only possible subject of “is … served” is ‘the name “pie,”’ and so, this version conveys that the name “pie” is served as a main course dish or dessert.


> <b>(E)</b> The name “pie” is used for various types of pastries, <b>and they sometimes serve it as a main course dish and sometimes as a dessert
</b>.



The pronoun “they” has no logical antecedent and does not refer to anyone in particular. Therefore, the use of “they” in this version is not ideal and probably would not be preferred by the GMAT.


Also, since the singular pronoun “it” can only refer to a singular noun, “it” seems to refer to ‘the name “pie,”’ and, thus, this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that “they” serve the name “pie” as a main course dish or dessert.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

##  Sentence Correction Example 16  

While the term “bourse” may refer generally to any non-U.S.

1. [ ] (A) it also is the Paris stock exchange specifically
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) but also it is specifically the Paris stock exchange
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) that could be the Paris stock exchange specifically
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) it may refer specifically to the Paris stock exchange
5. [ ] (E) the term also may be the Paris stock exchange specifically
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



> <b>(A)</b> While the term “bourse” may refer generally to any non-U.S. stock exchange, <b>it also is the Paris stock exchange specifically
</b>.



Since “it” refers to “<em>the term</em> ‘bourse’” rather than “bourse” itself, this version contains an illogical antecedent.


It does not make sense to say “<em>it</em> also is the Paris stock exchange specifically,” because the meaning conveyed is, “the term ‘bourse’ also is the Paris stock exchange specifically.” Of course, saying that a term “is” a stock exchange makes no sense.


> <b>(B)</b> While the term “bourse” may refer generally to any non-U.S. stock exchange, <b>but also it is specifically the Paris stock exchange
</b>.



Since “it” refers to “<em>the term</em> ‘bourse’” rather than “bourse” itself, this version contains an illogical antecedent.


It does not make sense to say, “but also<em> it</em> is specifically the Paris stock exchange,” because the meaning conveyed is, “but also<em> </em>the term ‘bourse’ is specifically the Paris stock exchange.” Of course, saying that a term “is” a stock exchange makes no sense.


Additionally, starting the second clause with the conjunction “but” is redundant because the first clause starts with the conjunction “while.” In other words, in this version, “but” and “while” perform the same function of connecting the two clauses in a way that expresses the contrast between the two ways the term “bourse” is used.


> <b>(C)</b> While the term “bourse” may refer generally to any non-U.S. stock exchange, <b>that could be the Paris stock exchange specifically
</b>.



 Although it isn’t entirely clear to what “that” refers, even if we assume that “that” refers to the noun “the term ‘bourse’,” the sentence still conveys an illogical meaning because “the term” cannot logically “be” the Paris stock exchange.


Since the sentence meaning is both unclear and illogical, this choice is not correct.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D)</b> While the term “bourse” may refer generally to any non-U.S. stock exchange, <b>it may refer specifically to the Paris stock exchange
</b>.



This choice is the only one that contains a pronoun with a logical antecedent. As in the previous choices, “it” refers to “the term ‘bourse’.” However, in this case, “the term” itself is not illogically called a stock exchange. Rather, this choice states that “it [the term] may <em>refer</em> specifically <em>to</em> the Paris stock exchange,” a meaning that makes sense.


Since this is the only choice that does not contain an illogical antecedent or convey an illogical meaning, this is the correct choice.


> <b>(E)</b> While the term “bourse” may refer generally to any non-U.S. stock exchange, <b>the term also may be the Paris stock exchange specifically
</b>.



 Don’t be fooled by the repetition of the noun “the term.” Even without the pronoun/antecedent error we see in other versions of the sentence, this version still conveys an illogical meaning because “the term” cannot logically “be” the Paris stock exchange.


So, although this version does not contain a pronoun with an illogical antecedent, it does not correct the mistake of illogically calling “the term” itself a stock exchange.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

Let’s now discuss a key GMAT Sentence Correction convention: in GMAT Sentence Correction, generally, a pronoun will not refer to a clause in a correct answer.
