## Lesson Content
Although, in everyday speech, people often use pronouns to refer to clauses, on the GMAT, constructions that involve pronouns referring to clauses are not correct.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

On the GMAT, versions in which pronouns refer to entire clauses are not correct.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: My dog remained lost for days, <strong>which</strong> caused me to lose sleep until it was found.

As currently structured, the relative pronoun “which” appears to refer to the entire clause “My dog was lost for days.” We don’t use “which” in this manner.

The GMAT would therefore prefer one of the following:

> My dog remained lost for days, <strong>causing</strong> me to lose sleep until it was found.

> I lost sleep until my <strong>dog, which</strong> remained lost for days, was found.

In the first preferred version, we replaced the relative clause beginning with the pronoun “which” with a phrase beginning with the present participle “causing.” This phrase appropriately modifies the entire clause.

In the second corrected version, we reordered the sentence so that the pronoun “which” would properly refer to the noun that precedes it, rather than to an entire clause.

Here’s another example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The job was less difficult than Shane had expected it to be, but <strong>that</strong> didn’t make up for the lack of pay.

The pronoun “that” appears to refer to the entire clause “The job was less difficult than Shane had expected it to be.” We don’t use the pronoun “that” in this manner.

> The job was less difficult than Shane had expected it to be, but <strong>the lack of difficulty</strong> didn’t make up for the lack of pay.

> The <strong>job’s being less difficult</strong> than Shane had expected it to be didn’t make up for the lack of pay.

The easiest correction is to simply replace the pronoun with a noun, or, in this case, a noun phrase, as is done to create the first correct version of the sentence.

OK, here’s another:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The road was severely damaged, and <strong>it </strong>made driving on it almost impossible.

The first “it” in the second part of the sentence could refer to “the road,” but that reference is not logical, and thus, “it” seems to refer to the sentence’s entire first clause, “The road was severely damaged.” Given the ambiguity of the meaning conveyed and the fact that the pronoun seems to refer to a clause, the GMAT would prefer a version like one of the following:

> The road was severely damaged, <strong>so</strong> driving on it was almost impossible.

> Driving on the road was almost impossible <strong>because</strong> it was severely damaged.

In both of the above sentences, we eliminated the pronoun that referred to a clause.

*<u>Caveat Emptor</u>: There are a few official GMAT questions that do in fact use pronouns to refer to entire clauses.

So, what would happen in the very unlikely event that, in taking the GMAT, you ruled out four answer choices because they are clearly wrong, and you’re now left with one answer that has no other errors of grammar or logic yet uses a pronoun that refers to an entire clause? Well, you would choose that answer as correct and move on, never for a moment losing any sleep over it.

##  Sentence Correction Example 17  

The social-network game <em>GreenSpace</em> allows users to send their avatars into outer space to clear away pieces of trash that humans have littered onto other planets, <u>which promotes</u> environmentalism.

1. [ ] (A) which promotes
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) and that promotes
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) promoting
4. [ ] (D) in which it promotes
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) and this is promoting
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A)</b> The social-network game <em>GreenSpace</em> allows users to send their avatars into outer space to clear away pieces of trash that humans have littered onto other planets, <b>which promotes
</b> environmentalism.



Remember, in GMAT Sentence Correction, a pronoun generally will not refer to an entire clause in a correct answer. In this case, the relative pronoun “which” seems to be attempting to refer to the entire independent clause that precedes it.


Notice that “which” cannot possibly modify the noun that directly precedes it, “planets,” because the plural “planets” does not agree with the singular “promotes” and furthermore because planets cannot possibly promote environmentalism.


Since “which” is attempting to modify an entire clause instead of one specific noun, let’s look for a better answer choice.


> <b>(B)</b> The social-network game <em>GreenSpace</em> allows users to send their avatars into outer space to clear away pieces of trash that humans have littered onto other planets, <b>and that promotes
</b> environmentalism.



Remember, in GMAT Sentence Correction, a pronoun generally will not refer to an entire clause in a correct answer. Although the meaning of the relative pronoun “that” in this context is not completely clear, “that” seems to be attempting to refer to the entire independent clause that precedes it.


Since “that” is attempting to modify an entire clause, let’s look for a better answer choice.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> The social-network game <em>GreenSpace</em> allows users to send their avatars into outer space to clear away pieces of trash that humans have littered onto other planets, <b>promoting
</b> environmentalism.



 Unlike the other versions, this version does not include a pronoun whose antecedent is ambiguous or a pronoun that attempts to refer to an entire clause. Instead, this version properly modifies the entire preceding clause with the phrase “promoting environmentalism.”


The meaning conveyed is clear and logical, and the sentence is grammatically correct.


> <b>(D)</b> The social-network game <em>GreenSpace</em> allows users to send their avatars into outer space to clear away pieces of trash that humans have littered onto other planets, <b>in which it promotes
</b> environmentalism.



The phrase “in which it promotes environmentalism” illogically modifies the noun “planets,” conveying a nonsensical meaning. “In which” is too far removed from “the social-network game <em>GreenSpace</em>” to properly modify that noun phrase.


Additionally, it is unclear to what the pronoun “it” refers. Logically, the game is what “promotes environmentalism,” but if the intended meaning is “in which the social-network game <em>GreenSpace</em> promotes environmentalism,” we are still left asking to what “in which” refers. In other words, in <em>what</em> does the game promote environmentalism?


Since this version does not convey a clear and logical meaning, this answer choice is not correct.


> <b>(E)</b> The social-network game <em>GreenSpace</em> allows users to send their avatars into outer space to clear away pieces of trash that humans have littered onto other planets, <b>and this is promoting
</b> environmentalism.



Remember, in GMAT Sentence Correction, a pronoun generally will not refer to an entire clause in a correct answer. Although the meaning of the pronoun “this” in this context is not completely clear, “this” seems to be attempting to refer to the entire independent clause that precedes it.


Since “this” is attempting to modify an entire clause, we should search for a better answer choice.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

##  Sentence Correction Example 18  

In 2015, the Alabama state legislature worked to eliminate many laws considered outdated and obsolete, <u>because of which Alabama’s law against bear wrestling was repealed, which caused</u> some to wonder whether bear wrestling was once again legal in Alabama.

1. [ ] (A) because of which Alabama’s law against bear wrestling was repealed, which caused
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) which resulted in the repeal of Alabama’s law against bear wrestling, and it caused
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) and it resulted in Alabama’s law against bear wrestling that was repealed, this, in turn, causing
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) and, because of this, Alabama’s law against bear wrestling was repealed and caused
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) and, as a result, Alabama’s law against bear wrestling was repealed, an outcome causing

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



This sentence is meant to convey that Alabama’s law against bear wrestling was repealed as a result of the state legislature’s working to eliminate laws considered outdated and obsolete, and that this repeal caused some people to wonder whether bear wrestling was once again legal in Alabama.


However, in incorrect versions of the sentence, illogical use of pronouns results in the versions’ being awkward and ineffective or conveying nonsensical meanings.


> <b>(A)</b> In 2015, the Alabama state legislature worked to eliminate many laws considered outdated and obsolete, <b>because of which Alabama’s law against bear wrestling was repealed, which caused
</b> some to wonder whether bear wrestling was once again legal in Alabama.



In this version, the first pronoun “which” could conceivably, but illogically, refer to “laws,” in which case the sentence would convey the illogical meaning that, because of laws, the law against bear was repealed.


However, what seems more likely is that “which” refers to the entire first clause of the sentence. Read either way, the sentence is awkward, does not clearly convey a meaning, and employs a pronoun to refer to a clause, a structure not preferred by the GMAT.


Also, “which” appears a second time, once again seeming to refer to a clause.


> <b>(B)</b> In 2015, the Alabama state legislature worked to eliminate many laws considered outdated and obsolete, <b>which resulted in the repeal of Alabama’s law against bear wrestling, and it caused
</b> some to wonder whether bear wrestling was once again legal in Alabama.



In this version, the pronoun “which” seems to refer to the entire previous clause, a structure not preferred by the GMAT. Meanwhile, “which” could instead refer to “laws.” So, the GMAT probably would not prefer this use of “which.”


Also, what the pronoun “it” refers to is not clear, as “it” could conceivably refer to “the repeal,” “law,” the sentence’s first clause, or the sentence’s second clause.


> <b>(C)</b> In 2015, the Alabama state legislature worked to eliminate many laws considered outdated and obsolete, <b>and it resulted in Alabama’s law against bear wrestling that was repealed, this, in turn, causing
</b> some to wonder whether bear wrestling was once again legal in Alabama.



The pronoun “it” could conceivably refer to “legislature” or the first clause of the sentence. Read either way the sentence awkward and ineffective.


Further, the meaning conveyed is that “it,” whatever “it” is, somehow resulted in the law against bear wrestling, which meaning is nonsensical.


Finally, what “this” refers to is not clear. Conceivably, “this” refers to “law,” in which case, this version nonsensically conveys that the law against bear wrestling caused some to wonder whether bear wrestling was legal.


> <b>(D)</b> In 2015, the Alabama state legislature worked to eliminate many laws considered outdated and obsolete, <b>and, because of this, Alabama’s law against bear wrestling was repealed and caused
</b> some to wonder whether bear wrestling was once again legal in Alabama.



The pronoun “this” seems to refer to the first clause of the sentence, a structure not preferred by the GMAT.


Also, this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that the law caused people to wonder whether bear wrestling was once again legal.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E)</b> In 2015, the Alabama state legislature worked to eliminate many laws considered outdated and obsolete, <b>and, as a result, Alabama’s law against bear wrestling was repealed, an outcome causing
</b> some to wonder whether bear wrestling was once again legal in Alabama.



This version closes with an abstract appositive, “an outcome causing ….” This uncommon structure is included in this version for the purpose of making this version sound a little off in order to obscure the fact that this choice is the correct answer.


All the same, this version has no pronoun use issues and effectively conveys a logical meaning.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Sentence Correction Example 19  

<u>Though at least one reviewer has characterized the Mexican food served at Johnny Tacos as inauthentic, this doesn’t deter</u> the many who patronize the cantina, excited to have an opportunity to experience Mexican food in Indonesia.

1. [ ] (A) Though at least one reviewer has characterized the Mexican food served at Johnny Tacos as inauthentic, this doesn’t deter
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) That at least one reviewer has characterized the Mexican food served at Johnny Tacos as inauthentic doesn’t deter
3. [ ] (C) At least one reviewer has characterized the Mexican food served at Johnny Tacos as inauthentic, the existence of which doesn’t deter
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) At least one reviewer has characterized as inauthentic the Mexican food served at Johnny Tacos, which hasn’t been a deterrent to
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) While at least one reviewer has characterized the Mexican food served at Johnny Tacos as inauthentic, seeing that has not deterred
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



In the original version, the pronoun “this” incorrectly refers to the clause that precedes it. Similar errors appear in all of the other versions except for one.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Though at least one reviewer has characterized the Mexican food served at Johnny Tacos as inauthentic, this doesn’t deter
</b> the many who patronize the cantina, excited to have an opportunity to experience Mexican food in Indonesia.



Since this version uses “this” to refer to an idea expressed in the preceding clause, the GMAT would probably prefer a different version with a different kind of structure.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B)</b> <b>That at least one reviewer has characterized the Mexican food served at Johnny Tacos as inauthentic doesn’t deter
</b> the many who patronize the cantina, excited to have an opportunity to experience Mexican food in Indonesia.



This version uses a noun clause as its subject, a structure that, while uncommon, effectively conveys the meaning to be conveyed, and so, the GMAT would probably prefer this very clear version over the any of the versions that use pronouns to refer to ideas or clauses.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>At least one reviewer has characterized the Mexican food served at Johnny Tacos as inauthentic, the existence of which doesn’t deter
</b> the many who patronize the cantina, excited to have an opportunity to experience Mexican food in Indonesia.



The pronoun “which” may seem to refer to a review, but since no review is mentioned in this version, this version mentions nothing such that the “existence” of that thing would deter people from patronizing the cantina. So, there is no clear antecedent of the pronoun “which.”


> <b>(D)</b> <b>At least one reviewer has characterized as inauthentic the Mexican food served at Johnny Tacos, which hasn’t been a deterrent to
</b> the many who patronize the cantina, excited to have an opportunity to experience Mexican food in Indonesia.



“Which” seems to refer to the entire preceding clause, because “the Mexican food served at Johnny Tacos” would not be something that “hasn’t been a deterrent to the many who patronize the cantina. So, this version is not ideally constructed and probably would not be preferred by the GMAT.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>While at least one reviewer has characterized the Mexican food served at Johnny Tacos as inauthentic, seeing that has not deterred
</b> the many who patronize the cantina, excited to have an opportunity to experience Mexican food in Indonesia.



Since the sentence mentions nothing that people would “see,” there is no clear referent for “that.” So, the GMAT would prefer a version that more effectively conveys a logical meaning, a version like the version created via the use of (B).


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
