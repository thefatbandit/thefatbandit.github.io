## Lesson Content
Sometimes we see a pronoun as part of a compound subject or compound object.

> <strong>Jack and I</strong> went snorkeling in Maui.

> Would you like to eat dinner with <strong>Sara and me</strong>?

> If<strong> neither he nor the director</strong> attends the event, we’ll use stock photographs of <strong>him and the other cast members</strong>.

Of course, in a Sentence Correction question, a pronoun in a compound subject or compound object could be of the wrong case.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Using an object pronoun when the pronoun is actually acting as the subject of the sentence or clause is an error in pronoun case.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: On the new chef’s first day, the restaurant owner and him spent hours talking about the menu.

Since “him” is part of the compound subject (doing the action) in the sentence, the pronoun should be the subject pronoun “he,” not the object pronoun “him”:

> On the new chef’s first day, the restaurant owner and <strong>he</strong> spent hours talking about the menu.

In such situations, determining the correct pronoun case can be confusing.

> Marty and (I/me) went to the beach.

To get the pronoun case correct, we drop the other part of the compound subject (the noun “Marty”).

> <strong>I</strong> went to the beach.

Thus, the sentence must be:

> Marty and <strong>I</strong> went to the beach.

OK, that sentence was easy.

> Sara gave the gifts to Bita and (I/me).

It’s tempting to think that the following is correct:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Sara gave the gifts to Bita and I.

However, if we apply our rule and drop the other part of the compound, “Bita,” we see that the result does not make sense:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Sara gave the gifts to I.

Thus, the correct sentence must be:

> Sara gave the gifts to Bita and <strong>me</strong>.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When we have a pronoun as part of a compound subject or a compound object, we can drop the part of the compound subject or compound object that is not the pronoun, and then determine how the sentence would need to read with just the pronoun present.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s consider another example:

> The investigators assured the CEO that the team being sent would include the director and (they/them).

To determine which of the two is the correct pronoun, let’s isolate (they/them) by removing “the director and”:

> The investigators assured the CEO that the team being sent would include <s>the director and</s> (they/them).

Now we can more easily tell that the action “include” is being done to the individuals referred to by the pronoun, and that we need the object pronoun “them.”

> The investigators assured the CEO that the team being sent would include the director and <strong>them</strong>.

English speakers commonly use incorrect pronouns in compound subjects or compound objects.

> The head of the legal department will brief <strong>my assistant and me</strong> tomorrow about any liability the company may face, but between <strong>you and me</strong>, I think we’re in the clear.

In the sentence above, you may be tempted to use “I” instead of “me,” as you’ve probably heard such constructions many times in spoken English.

Let’s look at another example:

> <strong>They and their detractors</strong> have been battling in the court of public opinion for weeks, though neither side’s story has gained much credibility.

In the sentence above, “they and their detractors” are the ones “battling,” and so the subject pronoun “they” is correct, however unnatural it may sound.

> <strong>They have been battling</strong> in the court of public opinion for weeks, though neither side’s story has gained much credibility.

Let’s look at one more example.

> Campaigning in this district means knocking on every door, shaking every hand, and listening to every concern, but <strong>neither</strong> <strong>we nor our supporters</strong> are willing to give up.

Looking at the sentence above, you may be tempted to use the pronoun “us” instead of “we.” Indeed, “us” is correct in the construction “neither <em>of</em> us” because, in that context, “us” is the object of the preposition “of.” However, in our current example, “neither we nor our supporters” forms a compound subject that performs the action of not being willing to give up.

> Campaigning in this district means knocking on every door, shaking every hand, and listening to every concern, but <strong>we </strong>are not willing to give up.

Although “neither we nor our supporters” may sound strange, by isolating the pronoun, we can clearly see that “we,” not “us,” is correct.

##  Sentence Correction Example 20  

Political pollsters lost credibility when they wrongly predicted the outcome of the presidential election, and <u>the debate between them and their critics about the merits of polling is yet unresolved</u>.

1. [x] (A) the debate between them and their critics about the merits of polling is yet unresolved
2. [ ] (B) them and their critics have yet to resolve the debate about the merits of polling
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) they still have not resolved the debate between they and their critics about polling merits
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) between they and their critics, the debate about polling merits has not been resolved yet
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) the debate about the merits of polling between them and their critics are still unresolved
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A)</b> Political pollsters lost credibility when they wrongly predicted the outcome of the presidential election, and <b>the debate between them and their critics about the merits of polling is yet unresolved
</b>.



This choice correctly uses the object pronoun “them” in the compound object “them and their critics,” which is the object of the preposition “between.”


The sentence does not contain any grammatical errors and conveys a clear and logical meaning.


> <b>(B)</b> Political pollsters lost credibility when they wrongly predicted the outcome of the presidential election, and <b>them and their critics have yet to resolve the debate about the merits of polling
</b>.



This choice mistakenly uses the object pronoun “them” where the subject pronoun “they” is needed.


“Them and their critics” are the people performing the action “have yet to resolve.” Since “them and their critics” are performing the action rather than having the action done to them, “<em>they</em> and their critics” would be correct.


We can more easily see this error in pronoun case by crossing off the second part of the compound subject:


“<em>them</em><s> and their critics</s> <em>have</em> yet to resolve the debate …”


“Them … have yet to resolve” is not grammatically correct. “<em>They</em> have yet to resolve” would be correct.


> <b>(C)</b> Political pollsters lost credibility when they wrongly predicted the outcome of the presidential election, and <b>they still have not resolved the debate between they and their critics about polling merits
</b>.



The subject pronoun “they” in “<em>they</em> and their critics” is incorrect because “they” is the object of the preposition “between.” Thus, we need the object pronoun “them,” as in “the debate between <em>them</em> and their critics.”


> <b>(D)</b> Political pollsters lost credibility when they wrongly predicted the outcome of the presidential election, and <b>between they and their critics, the debate about polling merits has not been resolved yet
</b>.



The subject pronoun “they” in “<em>they</em> and their critics” is incorrect because “they” is the object of the preposition “between.” We need the object pronoun “them,” as in “between <em>them</em> and their critics.”


> <b>(E)</b> Political pollsters lost credibility when they wrongly predicted the outcome of the presidential election, and <b>the debate about the merits of polling between them and their critics are still unresolved
</b>.



This choice has a subject-verb agreement error. Notice that the thing that is “still unresolved” is “the debate,” a singular noun. Thus, the plural verb “are” is incorrect. To better see this error, we can cross of the preposition phrase between the subject and verb:


“<em>the debate</em><s> about the merits of polling between them and their critics</s> <em>are</em> still unresolved”


“The debate … are” is not grammatically correct. “The debate … is” would be correct.


Notice also that, given the overall context of the sentence, the phrasing “merits of polling <em>between them and their critics</em>” conveys a somewhat illogical meaning. Logically, the polling is not what is between pollsters and critics; the <em>debate</em> is what is between the pollsters and critics. After all, the sentence discusses predicting the outcome of a presidential election. That prediction would not be based on polling that was just between pollsters and their critics. So, the placement of the phrase “between them and their critics” is off.


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>If a pronoun is doing the action, a subject pronoun must be used. If a pronoun is being acted upon or is the object of a preposition, an object pronoun must be used.


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>When checking for proper pronoun usage in a compound subject or a compound object, cross off the part of the compound that is not the pronoun and see how the sentence reads with just the pronoun present.


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>Confirm that singular subjects are paired with singular verbs and that plural subjects are paired with plural verbs. 


<strong>Key Concept:</strong> When checking for subject-verb agreement, cross off any prepositional phrases that appear between the subject and finite verb. election. That prediction would not be based on polling that was just between pollsters and their critics. So, the placement of the phrase “between them and their critics” is off. 


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

##  Sentence Correction Example 21  

After botanist Ariel Novoplansky exposed plants to simulated drought, he discovered <u>them and neighboring plants were</u> communicating with each other through their roots.

1. [ ] (A) them and neighboring plants were
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) them and neighboring plants as
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) them and neighboring plants as they were
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) they and neighboring plants to be
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) that neighboring plants and they were

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



Correctly answering this question requires determining what makes sense in terms of what the botanist would have discovered and also understanding the correct use of the subject pronoun “they” and the object pronoun “them.” While some of the incorrect choices may seem possibly correct, the correct answer is so clearly correct that we can confidently choose it over any other.


> <b>(A)</b> After botanist Ariel Novoplansky exposed plants to simulated drought, he discovered <b>them and neighboring plants were
</b> communicating with each other through their roots.



This version incorrectly uses the object pronoun “them” as the subject of “were.”


While it may seem that “them” is correct since “them” seems to be the object of “he discovered,” what he discovered was not the plants themselves. He discovered the plants were communicating.


> <b>(B)</b> After botanist Ariel Novoplansky exposed plants to simulated drought, he discovered <b>them and neighboring plants as
</b> communicating with each other through their roots.



This version sounds incorrect and is incorrect, since in English there is no idiom “discovered … as” as in “discovered the plants as communicating.”


> <b>(C)</b> After botanist Ariel Novoplansky exposed plants to simulated drought, he discovered <b>them and neighboring plants as they were
</b> communicating with each other through their roots.



This version could be tempting, as it is grammatically correct, since “them” correctly functions as the object of “discovered.” However, the meaning conveyed is nonsensical, as the botanist would not have discovered the plants after the botanist exposed the plants to simulated drought.


> <b>(D)</b> After botanist Ariel Novoplansky exposed plants to simulated drought, he discovered <b>they and neighboring plants to be
</b> communicating with each other through their roots.



This version could be tempting if one perceives the subject pronoun “they” to be correctly serving as the subject of “to be communicating.” Let’s test the version though, by eliminating the non-pronoun part of the compound structure and seeing how the sentence reads.


botanist Ariel Novoplansky … discovered they to be communicating


Probably, even to someone who doesn’t know exactly how the idiom “discovered … to be” works, that version would sound incorrect, and it is in fact idiomatically incorrect.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E)</b> After botanist Ariel Novoplansky exposed plants to simulated drought, he discovered <b>that neighboring plants and they were
</b> communicating with each other through their roots.



This version correctly uses the subject pronoun “they” as part of the compound subject of “were communicating” and conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
