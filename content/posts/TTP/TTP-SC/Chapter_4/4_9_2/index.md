## Lesson Content
In the list at the beginning of this section, we saw that “who” and “whoever” are subject pronouns and that “whom” and “whomever” are object pronouns.

While which of the pronouns are which case is not difficult to remember, which of them should be used in a particular situation is not always obvious, and of course, GMAT question writers can use “who”/“whoever” or “whom”/“whomever” in creating tricky Sentence Correction answer choices.

In a sentence like the following example, it’s fairly obvious that “who” should be used, because the person being referred to is clearly acting:

> That the department head would resign soon was obvious, but <strong>who</strong> would serve as the new head was not.

Using the subject pronoun “who” makes sense because the person referred to by “who” is someone who would take the action “serve.”

Similarly, that “whom” should be used in the following sentence is clear, because the person being referred to is clearly the receiver of an action:

> We have to know <strong>whom</strong> to call if the computer malfunctions.

Using the object pronoun “whom” makes sense, because the person referred to by “whom” is the receiver of the action; the person is the person to call.

The following strategy can help us to choose between “who” and “whom” or between “whoever” and “whomever.”

<strong>Strategy:</strong> One way to determine whether to use “who”/“whoever” or “whom”/“whomever” is to replace the pronoun with “he” or “him.” If “he” is correct, then “who” or “whoever” is the correct pronoun to use.

Let’s use this strategy to determine which pronoun is correct in the following sentence.

> Each of the experts (who/whom) had analyzed the results was given an opportunity to speak.

When we replace (who/whom) with “he,” we get “he had analyzed.” When we replace (who/whom) with “him,” we get “him had analyzed.” Obviously, “he” is the correct choice, and therefore, “who” is the correct pronoun to use in the sentence.

> Each of the experts <strong>who </strong>had analyzed the results was given an opportunity to speak.

OK, so far, we have seen “who” and “whom” used in situations such that whether a subject or object pronoun should be used is pretty clear.

> Nobody saw (who/whom) pulled the alarm.

Notice the issue in this case.

We have “Nobody saw (who/whom).” So, (who/whom) seems to be the object of “nobody saw.” Thus, we might expect the correct pronoun to be “whom,” as in “Nobody saw whom.”

At the same time, we have “(who/whom) pulled the alarm.” So, (who/whom) is the subject of “pulled the alarm.” Now, the correct pronoun appears to be “who,” as in “who pulled the alarm.”

So, should “whom” be used because the pronoun refers to the person nobody saw, or should “who” be used because the pronoun refers to the person who took the action “pulled the alarm”?

In cases like this one, the correct pronoun to use is subject pronoun “who.”

Even though, in a way, the person referred to is being acted upon, it is more important to make clear that the person took the action.

> Nobody saw <strong>who</strong> pulled the alarm.

If this rule seems difficult to keep straight, just remember that, whenever we have to choose between “who”/“whoever” and “whom”/“whomever,” if the pronoun serves as a subject, the correct pronoun to use is the subject pronoun “who”/“whoever.”

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Whenever we have to choose between “who”/“whoever” and “whom”/“whomever,” if the pronoun serves as a subject, the correct pronoun to use is the subject pronoun “who”/“whoever.”
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s consider another example:

> When they walked into the bank to rob it, they hadn’t considered (who/whom) might be inside.

Once again, “whom” could seem to be the correct pronoun to use, because the pronoun seems to be the object of “considered.”

Notice, however, that what is really being considered is the entire concept “who would be inside” and that the pronoun “(who/whom)” is a subject, taking the action “might be inside.” So, the correct pronoun to use in this case is “who.”

> When they walked into the bank to rob it, they hadn’t considered <strong>who </strong>might be inside.

##  Sentence Correction Example 22  

After the Swedish crown jewels were stolen, <u>they speculated that whomever had taken them had</u> done so on behalf of a specialist collector.

1. [ ] (A) they speculated that whomever had taken them had
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) there was speculation that whoever had taken them had
3. [ ] (C) the speculation was about whomever had taken them having
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) the speculation about them was whoever had taken them having
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) speculation about the jewels was that whoever had taken them, having
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

##### Question Level: easy



Answering this question correctly primarily requires noticing whether the subject pronoun “whoever” or the object pronoun “whomever” is being used correctly.


Once we have eliminated the choices that use the wrong pronouns, we can use structure and meaning issues to choose among the remaining choices.


> <b>(A)</b> After the Swedish crown jewels were stolen, <b>they speculated that whomever had taken them had
</b> done so on behalf of a specialist collector.



The pronoun “they” has no clear referent, as the only plural noun in the sentence, “jewels” would not speculate and no particular group of people who speculated is mentioned or otherwise defined.


Also, the object pronoun “whomever” is misused as the subject of the verb “had taken.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B)</b> After the Swedish crown jewels were stolen, <b>there was speculation that whoever had taken them had
</b> done so on behalf of a specialist collector.



This version correctly uses the subject pronoun “whoever” as the subject of the verb “had taken” and concisely conveys a logical meaning.


> <b>(C)</b> After the Swedish crown jewels were stolen, <b>the speculation was about whomever had taken them having
</b> done so on behalf of a specialist collector.



The wording “the speculation” tends to indicate that the sentence is about some particular speculation, while in reality what the sentence seems to be about is speculation in general about the motive for taking the jewels. So, that wording is not optimal.


Also, the object pronoun “whomever” is misused as the subject of the verb “had taken.”


Also, beyond including a pronoun error, the wording “the speculation was about whomever had taken them having done so on behalf of a specialist collector” is convoluted and does not convey a meaning that makes complete sense.


> <b>(D)</b> After the Swedish crown jewels were stolen, <b>the speculation about them was whoever had taken them having
</b> done so on behalf of a specialist collector.



While “whoever” is correctly used as the subject of “had taken,” this version does not effectively convey what the speculation was.


By simplifying the sentence by removing “about them,” we get “the speculation … was whoever had taken them having done so.” We can clearly see that there is an issue. The issue is that this version does not provide a clause to express what the speculation was, a clause such as “that whoever had taken them had done so ….” Rather, this version is structured so as to convey the nonsensical meaning that “the speculation” was “whoever.”


> <b>(E)</b> After the Swedish crown jewels were stolen, <b>speculation about the jewels was that whoever had taken them, having
</b> done so on behalf of a specialist collector.



This version could appear to be correct because “whoever” is correctly used as the subject of “had taken.”


However, this version conveys the ridiculous meaning that speculation about the jewels was that “whoever had taken them.”


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
