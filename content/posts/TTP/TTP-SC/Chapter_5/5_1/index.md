## Lesson Content
A modifier is a word, phrase, or clause that modifies, or changes, another word or expression.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A modifier is a word, phrase, or clause that modifies, or changes, another word or expression.
{{< /alert >}}

As you already know, in answering SC questions, the evaluation of a sentence’s meaning and logic is just as important as the evaluation of a sentence’s grammar and style.

The GMAT uses SC questions involving modifiers to test our skill in determining what makes sense and what doesn’t.

> Does the sentence make sense if read literally?

> Is the meaning conveyed by the sentence logical?

> Does the sentence say what the author intended it to say?

> Is the meaning of the sentence clear, or must we guess what the author intended?

In this chapter, we’ll comprehensively cover common types of modifiers, discuss how to recognize them, and discuss strategies for determining whether the ways in which modifiers are used in sentences make sense and are effective.

The major concepts we’ll cover include:

> • Adjectives and Adverbs

> • Misplaced Modifiers

> • Misplaced Introductory Modifiers

> • Dangling Modifiers

> • The Possessive Modification Trap

> • Squinting Modifiers

> • Prepositional Phrases

> • Modifiers of Time

> • Relative Clauses

> • Appositives

> • Participles and Participial Phrases

> • Absolute Phrases

Before we get into detail, let’s take a summary look at types of modifiers you’ll likely encounter in Sentence Correction questions.

<u>Adjectives</u>:

> The <strong>blue </strong>plane was sitting on the runway.

“Blue” modifies the noun “plane,” providing more detail about it.

> The <strong>hungry</strong> bear was delighted to find the camper’s food.

“Hungry” modifies the noun “bear,” providing more detail about it.

<u>Adverbs</u>:

> The little boy jumped up and down <strong>excitedly</strong>.

“Excitedly” modifies the verb “jumped,” describing how the boy jumped.

> You must <strong>carefully</strong> evaluate your options.

“Carefully” modifies the verb “evaluate,” telling us how we should evaluate.

<u>Prepositional Phrases</u>:

> The town <strong>of Manchester</strong> is beautiful.

“Of Manchester” modifies the noun “town,” telling us which town.

> He took the Ace <strong>of diamonds</strong> <strong>from the deck of cards</strong>.

“Of diamonds” modifies the noun “ace,” telling us which ace, and “from the deck of cards” modifies the verb “took,” telling us from where he took the ace of diamonds.

<u>Relative Clauses</u>:

> The boat <strong>that was used in the race</strong> is for sale.

“That was used in the race” modifies the noun “boat,” telling us which boat is for sale.

> The Shiba Inu, <strong>which is the oldest breed of dog</strong>, is so cool!

“Which is the oldest breed of dog” modifies the noun “Shiba Inu,” telling us more about it.

<u>Present Participles</u>:

> The <strong>crying</strong> baby needs a nap.

“Crying” modifies the noun “baby,” telling us about what the baby is doing.

> The <strong>giggling </strong>girl was happy to see her friend.

“Giggling” modifies the noun “girl,” telling us which girl.

<u>Past Participles</u>:

> The <strong>tired</strong> runner took a nap.

“Tired” modifies the noun “runner,” telling us which runner.

> <strong>Battered by the rain</strong>, the tent began to leak.

“Battered by rain” modifies the noun “tent,” telling us what happened to the tent.

<u>Appositives</u>:

> The fish, <strong>a piranha</strong>, swam near my feet.

“A piranha” modifies the noun “fish,” telling us what type of fish.

> Scott’s dog <strong>Blaze</strong> has red fur.

“Blaze” modifies the noun “Scott’s dog,” telling us more about Scott’s dog.

<u>Absolute Phrases</u>:

> <strong>Its sensitive antennae moving back and forth,</strong> the ant could smell the sweet chocolate chips.

“Its sensitive antennae moving back and forth” modifies “the ant” and also tells us more about the entire clause that follows the modifier.

> <strong>Her soufflé rising on the table and almost ready to be served,</strong> the chef polished the dessert plate.

“Her soufflé rising on the table and almost ready to be served” modifies “the chef” and tells us more about the entire clause that follows the modifier.

Don’t worry if any of these types of modifiers seem new to you; we’ll cover them all.

Let’s begin by discussing the two most basic types of modifiers, adjectives and adverbs.
