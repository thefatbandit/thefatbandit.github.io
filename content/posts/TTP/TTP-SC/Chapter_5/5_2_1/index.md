## Lesson Content
One way in which adjectives and adverbs can be used to create issues in Sentence Correction questions is via using an adverb when an adjective is required or vice versa.

How do we know whether we need an adjective or an adverb in a sentence? The context will always tell us whether an adjective or adverb is correct.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The meaning of a sentence will enable us to determine whether an adverb or an adjective should be used at a given point in a sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The professor was a supposedly expert in illuminated manuscripts.

We can tell that, in the above sentence, “supposedly” is misused, because “supposedly” is an adverb, but it is being used to modify a noun, “expert.” One way to fix the sentence is to replace the adverb “supposedly” with the adjective “supposed.”

> The professor was a <strong>supposed expert</strong> in illuminated manuscripts.

Here’s a less obviously incorrect example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Some people believe that true equality cannot be reached when people of different genders are educated and housed separate.

In this example, the adjective “separate” is misused to modify the verbs “educated” and “housed.” We need an adverb instead.

> Some people believe that true equality cannot be reached when people of different genders are <strong>educated </strong>and <strong>housed separately</strong>.

Noticing the misuse of the adverb and the misuse of the adjective in the last two examples was fairly straightforward, as the misused modifiers, “supposedly” and “separate” were directly adjacent to the words that they were modifying.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The reporters noticed that the prime minister’s attendance at the briefings had become less regularly.

Is the adverb “regularly” meant to modify the verb “become?” By considering the meaning of the sentence we can tell that “regularly” must be meant to modify something other than “become,” because saying that something “had become less regularly” doesn’t make sense.

By considering the sentence as a whole, we can tell that “regularly” must be meant to describe “attendance,” but since “attendance” is a noun, describing it by using the adverb “regularly” is incorrect.

> The reporters noticed that the prime minister’s <strong>attendance</strong> at the briefings had become less <strong>regular</strong>.

Of course, in a Sentence Correction question, such an issue might be solved in a less simple way, such as by changing the sentence so that the use of the adverb makes sense:

> The reporters noticed that the prime minister <strong>had been attending</strong> the briefings less <strong>regularly</strong>.

Since, in this version, the adverb “regularly” modifies the verb “had been attending,” the adverb is correctly used.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Be sure that a sentence does not use an adverb when an adjective is required or vice versa.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 1  

<u>The playing of some heavy metal guitarists is rather skillfully, but, ironically, so loudly</u> that a listener may have difficulty appreciating it.

1. [ ] (A) The playing of some heavy metal guitarists is rather skillfully, but, ironically, so loudly
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) Some guitarists, when playing heavy metal, do so rather skillfully but, ironically, so loudly
3. [ ] (C) When playing, heavy metal guitarists are, in some cases, rather skillful, but, ironically, it is so loudly
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) In the cases of some heavy metal guitarists, when they play it, it is rather skillful, but, ironically, so loud
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) Some guitarists who play heavy metal are rather skillful but, ironically, so loudly
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

##### Question Level: medium



The adverb “loudly” should not be used to modify nouns, such as “playing” or “guitarists.” So, to answer this question correctly, we have to notice whether “loudly” modifies a noun or modifies a verb when “loudly” appears in an answer choice.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>The playing of some heavy metal guitarists is rather skillfully, but, ironically, so loudly
</b> that a listener may have difficulty appreciating it.



This choice could be tempting because it is structured in an organized way that includes the use of parallel modifiers “skillfully” and “loudly.”


However, since “skillfully” and “loudly” are adverbs, using them to modify the gerund “playing,” which is essentially a noun, is incorrect.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B)</b> <b>Some guitarists, when playing heavy metal, do so rather skillfully but, ironically, so loudly
</b> that a listener may have difficulty appreciating it.



In this version, the adverbs “skillfully” and “loudly” are correctly used to modify the verb “do.”


> <b>(C)</b> <b>When playing, heavy metal guitarists are, in some cases, rather skillful, but, ironically, it is so loudly
</b> that a listener may have difficulty appreciating it.



Using the adjective “skillful” to modify “guitarists” makes sense.


However, the pronoun “it” has no clear referent.


Also, the adverb “loudly” is incorrectly used to modify the pronoun “it.”


> <b>(D)</b> <b>In the cases of some heavy metal guitarists, when they play it, it is rather skillful, but, ironically, so loud
</b> that a listener may have difficulty appreciating it.



In this version, “it” may refer to “heavy metal.” However, since “heavy metal” appears only as a modifier modifying “guitarists” and not as a noun, there is no referent for “it.”


Also, while using the adjectives “skillful” and “loud” to modify “it” could make sense, since we don’t know what “it” is, we don’t know what the sentence is describing as skillful and loud, and, certainly, the music, heavy metal, would not be skillful. So, this version does not make sense.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>Some guitarists who play heavy metal are rather skillful but, ironically, so loudly
</b> that a listener may have difficulty appreciating it.



In this version, the adjective “skillful” is correctly used to modify “guitarists.”


However, while using “loudly” to modify “play” would make sense, the sentence is structured in a way such that “loudly” does not modify “play” but, rather, incorrectly modifies “guitarists.”


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
