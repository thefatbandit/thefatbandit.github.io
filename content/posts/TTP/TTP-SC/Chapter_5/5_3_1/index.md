## Lesson Content
When an adjective is incorrectly separated from the noun it modifies, the meaning of the sentence can be distorted.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: On the tables at the event were <strong>nutritious </strong>trays of fruits and vegetables.

Hmm – those nutritious trays sure sound delicious, don’t they? Better have a good dentist on call, right? Of course, the adjective “nutritious” is misplaced.

> On the tables at the event were trays of <strong>nutritious fruits and vegetables.</strong>

Let’s look at another example sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The farmer showed up at my house with a <strong>fresh</strong> crate full of zucchini.

A “fresh crate,” huh? Had it grown on a crate vine? The adjective “fresh” is misplaced.

> The farmer showed up at my house with a crate full of <strong>fresh zucchini</strong>.

Let’s look at another example sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Because the fighter was trying to lose two more pounds before the big fight, his trainer purchased <strong>low-calorie</strong> bottles of sports drinks for him to drink.

Low-calorie bottles? Those sound very delicious, right? Of course, the adjective “low-calorie” is misplaced; the drinks are low-calorie; a bottle wouldn’t be.

> Because the fighter was trying to lose two more pounds before the big fight, his trainer purchased bottles of <strong>low-calorie sports drinks</strong> for him to drink.

To do well in Sentence Correction, we must pay close attention to the meaning and placement of every word in a sentence.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Read sentences carefully to ensure that adjectives modify the correct nouns. Logic and meaning will indicate where the adjective should be placed.

##### Question Level: easy



Using the adjective “gold” to modify “coins” makes sense, but it does not make sense to use “gold” to modify “collection.” So, most of the incorrect choices in this question can be identified via our noticing that they include either “gold collection of ancient coins” or “ancient gold collection of coins,” neither of which makes sense. To confirm that those choices are incorrect and to eliminate the last remaining incorrect choice, we can notice other errors in structure and meaning.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Workers, tearing down the Cressoni Theater, they discovered a gold collection of ancient coins that had engravings about emperors such as Valentinian III and Libio Severo and
</b> indicated that the coins were minted before 474 CE.



The adjective “gold” is illogically used to modify “collection,” rather than to modify “coins.”


Also, the pronoun “they” unnecessarily fills the role of subject, as “workers” has already filled that role.


Also, it is not clear whether the sentence is meant to convey that the workers indicated that the coins were minted before 474 CE or that the coins themselves indicated that the coins were minted before 474 CE, neither of which meanings is completely logical anyway.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B)</b> <b>When workers were tearing down the Cressoni Theater, they discovered a collection of ancient gold coins that had engravings about emperors such as Valentinian III and Libio Severo, the presence of which
</b> indicated that the coins were minted before 474 CE.



“Gold” logically modifies “coins,” and the sentence is well structured and, thus, effectively conveys that the workers found the coins and that the presence of the engravings indicated that the coins were minted before 474 CE.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>Workers tearing down the Cressoni Theater discovered a gold collection of ancient coins, which had engravings about emperors such as Valentinian III and Libio Severo, and they
</b> indicated that the coins were minted before 474 CE.



The adjective “gold” is illogically used to modify “collection,” rather than to modify “coins.”


Also, what “they” in “they indicated” refers to is not clear. It most logically would refer to the “engravings” but could, conceivably, refer to “workers” or even to “coins.”


> <b>(D)</b> <b>Workers, who, in tearing down the Cressoni Theater and discovering a collection of ancient gold coins that had engravings about emperors such as Valentinian III and Libio Severo,
</b> indicated that the coins were minted before 474 CE.



In this version, “gold” logically modifies “coins.”


However, this version is not a complete sentence. We can see more clearly that it is a fragment by removing the long phrase “in tearing down the Cressoni Theater … Libio Severo.” By doing so, we get the following, which is clearly just the noun “workers” followed by a relative clause that begins with “who.”


Workers, who … indicated that they were minted before 474 CE.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>When workers were tearing down the Cressoni Theater and discovering an ancient gold collection of coins that had engravings about emperors such as Valentinian III and Libio Severo, they
</b> indicated that the coins were minted before 474 CE.



In this version, “ancient gold” is illogically used to modify “collection” rather than “coins.”


Also, “when workers were tearing down the Cressoni Theater and discovering” conveys the somewhat muddled meaning that the workers were tearing down the theater and discovering coins at the same time, possibly continuously.


Also, since “they” in “they indicated” seems to refer to workers, the sentence conveys the not quite logical meaning that, when the workers were tearing down the theater and discovering the collection, the workers indicated that the coins were minted before 474 CE.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
