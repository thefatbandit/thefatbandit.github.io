## Lesson Content
When we evaluate a sentence, we must take care to ensure that any adverbs that the sentence contains are placed logically since, like the incorrect placement of an adjective, the incorrect placement of an adverb can significantly distort the meaning of a sentence.

Although we could use any adverbs as examples, for this discussion, let’s focus on some adverbs that are called limiting words, since these words frequently appear in misplaced modifier questions.

To see how placement of a limiting word can affect meaning, let’s consider some sentences that contain the commonly used adverb “only”:

> I ate <strong>only</strong> fruits and vegetables for two weeks.

> I ate fruits and vegetables for <strong>only</strong> two weeks.

> I <strong>only</strong> ate fruits and vegetables for two weeks.

Each of the sentences is grammatically correct.

In the first sentence, “only” is placed directly before “fruits and vegetables.” So, the sentence conveys that, for two weeks, I ate no foods other than fruits and vegetables.

In the second sentence, “only” is placed just before “two weeks.” So, the sentence conveys that the period of time during which I ate fruits and vegetables was only two weeks.

Since, in the third sentence, “only” is placed directly before “ate,” what the sentence conveys is not clear, and could be rather illogical.

Consider another example:

> Erica can <strong>only</strong> drink water.

> <strong>Only</strong> Erica can drink water.

> Erica can drink water <strong>only</strong>.

Each says something different.

The second says that Erica is the one person who can drink water; no other person can drink it.

The third says that Erica can drink water, but she cannot drink anything else.

As you can see, changes in the placement of the adverb “only” completely changed the meaning of the sentences, although no words were added or removed.

While one could make the case that all the sentences above about Erica may make sense, in a Sentence Correction question, only one choice is correct.

On the GMAT, we can often choose the choice that conveys a logical meaning by keeping in mind one simple idea: Generally, the best placement of a limiting adverb is immediately before the word or phrase that it is meant to modify.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Generally, the best placement of a limiting adverb is immediately before the word or phrase that it is meant to modify.
{{< /alert >}}

For example, let’s say we want to communicate that our golf team practices on Monday, but not on other days.

> (1) The team <strong>only</strong> practices on Mondays.

> (2) The team practices <strong>only</strong> on Mondays.

Because in the first sentence, “only” is placed directly before “practice,” the sentence communicates the idea that, although the team may meet (and possibly practice) on other days of the week, on Mondays the team does nothing but practice (instead of eating ice cream together and then practicing, or instead of sleeping or drinking water, for example).

If we keep in mind the idea that, generally, the best placement of a limiting adverb (in this case “only”), is directly before the word or phrase that it modifies, choosing the sentence that conveys the meaning that we wanted to convey is an easy task.

> The team practices <strong>only on Mondays</strong>.

Now look, you must interject some of your own critical analysis when applying this idea.

> The team practices on <strong>Mondays only.</strong>

Since the meaning is clear, the placement of “only” directly after “Monday” is also correct.

Of course, a Sentence Correction question will not start off by providing a meaning that the correct answer has to convey.

Let’s look at some more examples.

> I <strong>almost</strong> paid $1,500 for a round-trip ticket from Los Angeles to Istanbul.

> I paid <strong>almost</strong> $1,500 for a round-trip ticket from Los Angeles to Istanbul.

Do these sentences say the same thing? Of course not.

Consider the following two versions of a sentence.

> Unlike fish of many other types, white sharks <strong>only</strong> breed once every two years.

> Unlike fish of many other types, white sharks breed <strong>only</strong> once every two years.

The first sentence says something illogical; it says that the only thing white sharks do is breed once every two years.

Let’s look at another example.

> The missing documents were finally found by the computer expert who comes in weekly <strong>hidden in the external drive</strong>.

> The missing documents were, by the computer expert who comes in weekly, finally found <strong>hidden in the external drive</strong>.

The first sentence is comical, right? The expert comes in hidden in the external drive? Can you picture that scene? The second sentence, of course, expresses the meaning that the author intended; the missing documents were found hidden in the external drive.

When we answer a Sentence Correction question, we must carefully consider every word in the sentence.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When we answer a Sentence Correction question, we must carefully consider every word in the sentence. If we don’t read carefully and critically, we’ll miss small but important details, such as the effects of the placement of adjectives and adverbs.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 3  

Workers digging in New Zealand discovered an ancient <u>waka, which is a Maori word for “canoe,” that partially had been completed and then buried</u>.

1. [ ] (A) waka, which is a Maori word for “canoe,” that partially had been completed and then buried
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) waka that was the word that the Maori used for a canoe, which had partially been completed when they buried it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) waka being a Maori canoe, which partially was completed and then buried
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) waka, a Maori version of a canoe, that had been partially completed before being buried
5. [ ] (E) waka that was what the Maori called a canoe, which had been completed partially before its burial
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

##### Question Level: easy



The adverb “partially” appears in all of the versions of the sentence. However, in all versions but one, “partially” is not well placed. The point that the sentence is meant to make is that the waka was partially completed. In order for the sentence to convey this meaning, “partially” has to directly precede “completed.” When “partially” is placed directly before a word other than “completed,” such as “was” or “had,” the meaning conveyed becomes illogical or ambiguous.


> <b>(A)</b> Workers digging in New Zealand discovered an ancient <b>waka, which is a Maori word for “canoe,” that partially had been completed and then buried
</b>.



The sentence is about a waka, which is a type of canoe, not about a word. So, the use of the modifier “which is a Maori word for canoe,” which indicates that a waka is a word, does not make sense. A word would not be partially completed and then buried.


Also, the placement of “partially” directly before “had” does not make sense. The point to be made is not that the waka partially had but did not completely have something. The point is that the waka had been partially completed.


> <b>(B)</b> Workers digging in New Zealand discovered an ancient <b>waka that was the word that the Maori used for a canoe, which had partially been completed when they buried it
</b>.



The sentence is about a waka, which is a type of canoe, not about a word. So, the use of the modifier “that was the word that the Maori used for a canoe,” which indicates that a waka is a word, does not make sense. A word would not be partially completed and then buried.


Also, the placement of “partially” directly before “been” does not quite make sense. The point to be made is that the waka had been partially completed. So, optimally, “partially” would directly precede “completed” rather than precede “been.”


Also, the pronoun “they” has no logical referent, as “workers” is the only plural noun to which “they” could refer, but it does not make sense that the workers would have discovered a canoe that had been partially completed when the workers buried it.


> <b>(C)</b> Workers digging in New Zealand discovered an ancient <b>waka being a Maori canoe, which partially was completed and then buried
</b>.



The wording “waka being a Maori canoe” conveys the nonsensical meaning that the waka was actively choosing to be a Maori canoe.


Also, the adverb “partially” is misplaced. The point is not that the waka “partially was” completed and buried. The point to be made is that the waka was partially completed. So, “partially” should directly precede “completed” rather than precede “was.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D)</b> Workers digging in New Zealand discovered an ancient <b>waka, a Maori version of a canoe, that had been partially completed before being buried
</b>.



In this version, the modifier “a Maori version of a canoe” logically modifies “waka.”


Also, “partially” is placed just before “completed,” so the sentence conveys the logical meaning that the waka had been partially completed.


> <b>(E)</b> Workers digging in New Zealand discovered an ancient <b>waka that was what the Maori called a canoe, which had been completed partially before its burial
</b>.



This version is illogical because a waka is not “what the Maori called a canoe;” a waka is a Maori canoe.


Also, the placement of “partially” results in a sentence that does not convey a clear meaning. As written, the sentence could convey either that the waka was completed partially or that the waka was completed and that its completion was “partially before” its burial.


Also, the expression “its burial” tends to convey that something was buried in a ceremonial way rather than simply buried, a meaning that does not make sense in this context.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

Let’s continue by discussing an important rule that we can use to identify modifier and meaning issues: noun modifiers must be placed as closely as possible to what they modify.
