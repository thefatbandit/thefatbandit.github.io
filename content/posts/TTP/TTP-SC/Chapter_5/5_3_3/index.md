## Lesson Content
As you’ve begun to see, the positions of words, phrases, and clauses matter greatly to the meaning of a sentence, and small changes in the placement of words, phrases, and clauses can translate into dramatic changes in the meaning of a sentence.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A noun modifier must be placed as closely as possible to what it modifies.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following examples in which the modifiers are in bold and the nouns being modified are underlined.

> Correct:<em> </em>The <u>man</u> <strong>who was wearing the red scarf</strong> is upstairs.<em> </em>

> Incorrect: The <u>man</u> is upstairs <strong>who was wearing the red scarf</strong>.

The clause “who was wearing the red scarf” modifies the noun “man.” So, the clause should be placed as closely as possible to the noun “man.”

> Correct: The <u>house</u> <strong>that is adjacent to mine</strong> is quite large.

> Incorrect: The <u>house</u> is quite large <strong>that is adjacent to mine.</strong>

We see that "that is adjacent to mine” is meant to modify the noun “house.” So, the modifier must be placed as closely as possible to the noun “house.” It makes no sense to place the modifier next to the adjective “large.” The second sentence conveys that “large” is “adjacent to mine.” Of course, such a meaning is illogical.

> Correct: The <u>Boeing 747</u>, <strong>which is very fast</strong>, is an amazing plane and can fly for up to 20 hours.

> Incorrect: The <u>Boeing 747</u> is an amazing plane and can fly for up to 20 hours, <strong>which is very fast</strong>.

The modifier “which is very fast” is meant to modify the noun “Boeing 747,” so, we place this noun modifier right next to “Boeing 747,” the noun it modifies.

> Correct: <u>Barack Obama</u>, <strong>the 44th president of the United States</strong>, had been a community organizer.<em> </em>

> Incorrect: <u>Barack Obama</u> had been a community organizer, <strong>the 44th president of the United States</strong>.

The noun modifier “the 44th president of the United States” is meant to modify the noun “Barack Obama.” So, we put the modifier directly after this noun.

At this point, don’t worry if you were not able to recognize all of the structures in bold as modifiers.
