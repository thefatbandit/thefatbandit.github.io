## Lesson Content
Prepositional phrases of time include structures such as “by 1920,” “after the Cold War ended,” and “in 2018.” When we encounter such phrases, we must take extra care to ensure that their placement does not result in ambiguity, confusion, or an illogical meaning.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The board of directors said <strong>in 2020</strong> the price of the company’s product would need to double.

This sentence is confusing at best.

> The board of directors said the price of the company’s product would need <strong>to double in 2020. </strong>

The above sentence makes clear that the doubling must occur in 2020.

> <strong>In 2020, the board of directors said</strong> the price of the company’s product would need to double.

The above sentence makes clear that the board made that statement in 2020.

Let’s look at another example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The CEO reported <strong>in 2050</strong> her company would be selling self-flying cars.

What happened in 2050? Did the CEO report in 2050, or was her report that in 2050 her company would be selling self-flying cars? Consider the following more effective versions:

> The CEO reported that her company <strong>would be selling self-flying cars in 2050</strong>.

> <strong>In 2050, the CEO reported </strong>that her company would be selling self-flying cars.

Notice that both sentences make clear what happened when, although they convey different things.

##### Question Level: easy



The placement of the prepositional phrase of time “in 1947” has to result in a sentence that conveys a clear and logical meaning. In some versions “in 1947” is placed in ways such that the versions convey illogical or ambiguous meanings.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Secretary of State George Marshall proposed his four-year, multi-billion-dollar plan to rebuild Europe following the destruction of World War II in 1947, and his namesake Marshall Plan, which was meant
</b> to both aid economic redevelopment and stave off communism, was enacted the following year, marking what many historians consider the beginning of the Cold War.



First, we see that the prepositional phrase of time “in 1947” is placed a way such that the sentence’s meaning is confusing. Did Marshall propose his plan in 1947, or was the destruction in 1947? As the sentence is written, we can’t tell, right?


Also, “destruction of World War II” makes no sense, as it seems to convey that the war itself was destroyed. How can a World War be destroyed? A building or a road could be destroyed, but a World War could not.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>In 1947, Secretary of State George Marshall proposed his four-year, multi-billion-dollar plan to rebuild Europe following the destruction coming from World War II, and his namesake Marshall Plan, which was meaning
</b> to both aid economic redevelopment and stave off communism, was enacted the following year, marking what many historians consider the beginning of the Cold War.



 We see a much better placement of “in 1947.” Placed at the beginning of the sentence, the modifier makes clear that Marshall proposed his plan in 1947.


However, “destruction coming from World War II” is illogical. As written, it’s almost as if World War II has sent something in the mail.


Also, saying that the plan was “meaning to” is not quite logical. A plan might be meant to do something, but “meaning to” conveys that the plan itself was actively planning to do something.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>Secretary of State George Marshall proposed his four-year, multi-billion-dollar plan to rebuild Europe following its destruction of World War II, and, in 1947, his namesake Marshall Plan, which was meant
</b> to both aid economic redevelopment and stave off communism, was enacted the following year, marking what many historians consider the beginning of the Cold War.



 “Its destruction of World War II” makes no sense, as it seems to convey that the war itself was destroyed. How can a World War be destroyed? A building or a road could be destroyed, but a World War could not.


Also, if we look carefully at what follows the phrase “in 1947,” we see that “in 1947” is placed in a way such that the sentence conveys the illogical meaning that, in 1947, the plan was enacted the following year. The plan must have been enacted either in 1947 or the following year, not both.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>Proposed by secretary of State George Marshall was his four-year, multi-billion-dollar plan to rebuild Europe in 1947, following the destruction by World War II, his namesake Marshall Plan meaning
</b> to both aid economic redevelopment and stave off communism, was enacted the following year, marking what many historians consider the beginning of the Cold War.



Because of the placement of “in 1947,” this version conveys that the four-year plan was to rebuild Europe in 1947. Since 1947 is not four years, this meaning is not logical.


Also, “the destruction by World War II” does not quite make sense, as “the destruction” does not make clear that Europe was destroyed.


Also, saying that the plan was “meaning to” is not quite logical. A plan might be meant to do something, but “meaning to” conveys that the plan itself was actively planning to do something.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E)</b> <b>In 1947, Secretary of State George Marshall proposed his four-year, multi-billion-dollar plan to rebuild Europe following its destruction during World War II, and his namesake Marshall Plan, which was meant
</b> to both aid economic redevelopment and stave off communism, was enacted the following year, marking what many historians consider the beginning of the Cold War.



 In this version, the phrase “in 1947” is placed in a way such that the phrase clearly indicates that Marshall proposed the plan in 1947.


Also, “plan to rebuild Europe following its destruction during World War II” conveys a clear and logical meaning.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
