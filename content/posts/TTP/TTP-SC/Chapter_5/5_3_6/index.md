## Lesson Content
We often encounter sentences that open with introductory (opening) modifiers.

Consider the following example:

> <strong>Built to last</strong>, the lighthouse has weathered many storms.

We can see that the sentence opens with an introductory noun modifier, “built to last.” The noun that the introductory modifier is meant to modify, “the lighthouse,” appears right after the introductory modifier.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When an introductory modifier is a noun modifier, the noun that it modifies must appear right after the introductory modifier.
{{< /alert >}}

If the noun that the opening modifier is meant to modify doesn’t appear right after the introductory modifier, then the introductory modifier is misplaced.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

If the noun that the opening modifier is meant to modify doesn’t appear right after the introductory modifier, then the introductory modifier is misplaced.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following two sentences:

> <u>Incorrect</u>:<em> </em><strong>Tired and hungry, </strong><u>the aroma</u> from the campsite grill cheered the hikers as they climbed the last hill.

> <u>Correct</u>:<em> </em><strong>Tired and hungry,</strong> <u>the hikers</u> were cheered by the aroma from the campsite grill as they climbed the last hill<em>.</em>

The opening modifier, “tired and hungry,” is a noun modifier.

However, in the first sentence, the noun “the aroma” appears right after “tired and hungry,” and thus this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that the aroma was “tired and hungry.” In other words, “tired and hungry” is a misplaced introductory modifier.

In the second sentence, on the other hand, the noun that the modifier is supposed to describe, “the hikers,” does appear right after the modifier.

Of course, there are other ways to correctly phrase the sentence, and any of these variations could show up on the GMAT as correct answers.

> As they climbed the last hill, the tired and hungry hikers were cheered by the aroma from the campsite grill.

> The hikers, tired and hungry, were cheered by the aroma from the campsite grill as they climbed the last hill.

Both of these sentences are correct because the noun modifier “tired and hungry” is placed close to and clearly modifies a noun that it would logically modify, “hikers.”

Let’s consider another.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: <strong>An award-winning photograph</strong>, the flat look of Tugo Cheng’s colorful image of the Tianshan Mountains was achieved through taking the photograph when the sunlight was not strong.

This sentence contains a misplaced introductory modifier.

By moving the nouns around, we get the following version, which makes sense:

> <strong>An award-winning photograph, </strong><u>Tugo Cheng’s colorful image</u> of the Tianshan Mountains has a flat look achieved through taking the photograph when the sunlight was not strong.

Notice that, now, the opening modifier “an award-winning photograph,” logically modifies the noun “Tugo Cheng’s colorful image.”

A structure that often confuses test-takers is the use of an expletive “it” just after an introductory modifying phrase.

Consider the following sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: <strong>Running fast along the shore</strong>, it was clear that the Shiba Inu was having a great time.

At this point, you know that that sentence is incorrectly written.

We could rewrite the sentence as:

> Running fast along the shore, <strong>the Shiba Inu</strong> was clearly having a great time.

We see that the phrase “running fast along the shore” now properly modifies the noun “Shiba Inu.”

Here’s another example in which an introductory modifier illogically modifies an expletive “it:”

> <u>Incorrect</u>: <strong>Having beaten the returns of the S&amp;P 500 over the past ten years</strong>, it is likely that the Gemini Fund will soon close to new investors.

Look at the opening modifying phrase “Having beaten the returns of the S&P 500 over the past ten years.” What is that phrase intended to modify? Certainly not “it,” which does not have as its referent “the Gemini Fund.” A correct version is:

> Having beaten the returns of the S&P 500 over the past ten years, <strong>the Gemini Fund</strong> will likely soon close to new investors.

Notice that, now, the opening phrase properly modifies “the Gemini Fund.”

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When an introductory noun modifier is followed by a clause that has “it” as its subject, it may be that the modifier does not logically modify “it.”
{{< /alert >}}

Be vigilant for misplaced introductory modifiers!

##  Sentence Correction Example 7  

Now best known as the date of Julius Caesar’s assassination, <u>the Ides of March was, in ancient Rome, traditionally a day of feasting, revelry, and rituals honoring various deities</u>.

1. [x] (A) the Ides of March was,  in ancient Rome, traditionally a day of feasting, revelry, and rituals honoring various deities
2. [ ] (B) ancient Rome traditionally feasted, reveled, and honored various deities on the Ides of March
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) traditionally, ancient Rome had a day of feasting, revelry, and rituals in honor of their various deities on the Ides of March 
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) there was traditionally feasting, revelry, and rituals that honored various deities on the Ides of March in ancient Rome
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) the tradition on the Ides of March was a day of feasting, revelry, and rituals in which they honored various deities in ancient Rome 
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

##### Question Level: easy



In evaluating the answer choices in a Sentence Correction question, we must be sure to consider the full sentence created, not just the underlined portion. Notice that in this case, the nonunderlined portion of the sentence is the introductory modifier “Now best known as the date of Julius Caesar’s assassination,” which modifies whatever noun immediately follows it. So, the noun that immediately follows that phrase (and thus begins the underlined portion) must be something that could logically be “best known as the date of Julius Caesar’s assassination.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A)</b> Now best known as the date of Julius Caesar’s assassination, <b>the Ides of March was,  in ancient Rome, traditionally a day of feasting, revelry, and rituals honoring various deities
</b>.



In the sentence created with this choice, the introductory phrase “Now best known as the date of Julius Caesar’s assassination” logically modifies the noun phrase “the Ides of March.”


Thus, this version of the sentence effectively conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(B)</b> Now best known as the date of Julius Caesar’s assassination, <b>ancient Rome traditionally feasted, reveled, and honored various deities on the Ides of March
</b>.



It does not make sense to say that “ancient Rome” is “now best known as the date of Julius Caesar’s assassination,” as the sentence created with this choice does.


Remember, the introductory phrase in the sentence modifies the noun that immediately follows it. In this case that noun is “ancient Rome,” which is a place, not a “date.” 


> <b>(C)</b> Now best known as the date of Julius Caesar’s assassination, <b>traditionally, ancient Rome had a day of feasting, revelry, and rituals in honor of their various deities on the Ides of March 
</b>.



It does not make sense to say that “ancient Rome” is “now best known as the date of Julius Caesar’s assassination,” as the sentence created with this choice does.


Remember, the introductory phrase in the sentence modifies the noun that immediately follows it. In this case that noun is “ancient Rome,” which is a place, not a “date.” The placement of the adverb “traditionally” between the introductory phrase and the noun does not change the fact that the phrase still (illogically) modifies that noun.


Additionally, the pronoun “their” is missing an antecedent in the sentence. Notice that the logical antecedent, “Romans,” is not mentioned anywhere in the sentence. Only “ancient Rome,” a place (and a singular noun, which cannot be paired with the plural “their”), is mentioned.


> <b>(D)</b> Now best known as the date of Julius Caesar’s assassination, <b>there was traditionally feasting, revelry, and rituals that honored various deities on the Ides of March in ancient Rome
</b>.



What is “now best known as the date of Julius Caesar’s assassination”? The statement “there was traditionally feasting” is not a “date” and thus is not logically modified by the introductory phrase.


Logically, the “date” mentioned in the introductory phrase is “the Ides of March.” However, “the Ides of March” appears too far from the introductory phrase to be properly modified by it. “The Ides of March” should appear immediately after the introductory modifier in order for a logical meaning to be conveyed.    


> <b>(E)</b> Now best known as the date of Julius Caesar’s assassination, <b>the tradition on the Ides of March was a day of feasting, revelry, and rituals in which they honored various deities in ancient Rome 
</b>.



It does not make sense to say that “the tradition” is “now best known as the date of Julius Caesar’s assassination,” as the sentence created with this choice does. Remember, the introductory phrase in the sentence modifies the noun that immediately follows it. In this case that noun is “the tradition,” which is not a “date.” 


The logical “date” is “the Ides of March,” but that noun phrase is part of a prepositional phrase modifying “the tradition” and thus is not properly modified by the introductory phrase. “The Ides of March” should appear immediately after the introductory modifier in order for a logical meaning to be conveyed.   


Additionally, the pronoun “they” is missing an antecedent. Notice that the logical antecedent, “Romans,” is not mentioned anywhere in the sentence. Only “ancient Rome,” a place (and a singular noun, which cannot be paired with the plural “they”), is mentioned.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

##  Sentence Correction Example 8  

<u>Many in number, a key source of energy for generation of electrical power in Central America is the area’s volcanoes, which once merely posed as threats but now benefit</u> the people who live around them.

1. [ ] (A) Many in number, a key source of energy for generation of electrical power in Central America is the area’s volcanoes, which once merely posed as threats but now benefit
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) Key sources of energy for the generation of electrical power in Central America, the area’s many volcanoes no longer merely pose threats to but now benefit
3. [ ] (C) Being key sources of energy for electrical power generation in Central America, the mere threats posed by the area’s many volcanoes are now benefits to
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) As key sources of energy for electrical power generation in Central America, the area’s many volcanoes were merely posing as threats, but now they benefit
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) Key sources of energy for generation of electrical power in the area, Central America has many volcanoes, which once merely posed threateningly but now benefit
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

##### Question Level: hard



We can eliminate most of the answer choices in this question by noticing that they include opening modifiers that don’t logically modify the nouns that follow the modifiers.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Many in number, a key source of energy for generation of electrical power in Central America is the area’s volcanoes, which once merely posed as threats but now benefit
</b> the people who live around them.



This sentence contains a misplaced introductory modifier. The opening modifier, “many in number,” does not logically modify “a key source,” which is the noun that follows the modifier.


Also, “to pose as” means “to pretend to be.” So, in saying, “volcanoes, which once merely <em>posed as threats</em>,” the sentence conveys the nonsensical meaning that the volcanoes pretended to be threats.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B)</b> <b>Key sources of energy for the generation of electrical power in Central America, the area’s many volcanoes no longer merely pose threats to but now benefit
</b> the people who live around them.



The opening modifier, “key sources of energy for the generation of electrical power in Central America,” logically modifies “the area’s many volcanoes,” which is the noun that follows the modifier.


The wording of the end of the choice, “no longer merely pose threats to but now benefit,” is written to sound awkward and seem wrong. However, actually, it works well with the non-underlined portion, as we can see by combining the two to create “no longer merely pose threats to but now benefit the people,” which is a shorter way of saying, “no longer merely pose threats to the people but now benefit the people.”


> <b>(C)</b> <b>Being key sources of energy for electrical power generation in Central America, the mere threats posed by the area’s many volcanoes are now benefits to
</b> the people who live around them.



This sentence contains a misplaced introductory modifier. The opening modifier, “being key sources of energy …,” does not make sense as a modifier of the noun phrase that follows the modifier, “mere threats.”


Partly as a result of this modifier misplacement, this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that “mere threats” are “being sources of energy” and “are now benefits.”


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D)</b> <b>As key sources of energy for electrical power generation in Central America, the area’s many volcanoes were merely posing as threats, but now they benefit
</b> the people who live around them.



The opening modifier, “as key sources of energy for generation of electrical power in Central America,” works logically with the noun that follows the modifier, “the area’s many volcanoes.” However, the meaning conveyed by this version is nonsensical.


“To pose as” means “to pretend to be.” So, in saying, “As key sources of energy …, the area’s many volcanoes were merely posing as threats,” the sentence conveys the nonsensical meaning that, as key sources of energy, the volcanoes were pretending to be threats.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>Key sources of energy for generation of electrical power in the area, Central America has many volcanoes, which once merely posed threateningly but now benefit
</b> the people who live around them.



This sentence contains a misplaced introductory modifier. The opening modifier, “key sources of energy” is illogically modifying the noun “Central America.”


Also, “to pose threateningly” means “to choose to stand in a way that is threatening.” So, in saying, “volcanoes, which once merely posed threateningly,” the sentence conveys the nonsensical meaning that volcanoes chose to stand in ways that were threatening.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

Let’s now discuss dangling modifiers.
