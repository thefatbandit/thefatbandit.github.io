## Lesson Content
Dangling modifiers are similar to misplaced modifiers, except in that, in a case of a dangling modifier, the noun that the modifier is supposed to describe is completely absent from the sentence.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In a case of a dangling modifier, the noun that the modifier is supposed to describe is completely absent from the sentence. Such a sentence is always incorrect.
{{< /alert >}}

Dangling modifiers are common in Sentence Correction questions.

Take, for example, the following sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Having completed their final exams, parties all over campus began.

Since the noun “parties” follows the comma that separates the introductory phrase (which also happens to be a noun modifier), from the main clause, the sentence conveys that it was “parties” that had completed their final exams.

The only other noun in the clause that the modifier could modify is “campus,” another thing that would not complete an exam.

Although the sentence leads us to believe that students completed the final exams, the noun “students” is completely missing from the sentence.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Dangling modifiers result in sentences with ridiculous meanings. Such sentences can never be correct.
{{< /alert >}}

The easiest way to correct the dangling modifier is to simply place the noun that should be modified after the comma separating the introductory phrase from the main clause:

> Having completed their final exams, <strong>the students</strong> partied all over campus.

Note that the remainder of the sentence likely will need to be changed for the sentence to make sense.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A noun being modified in a sentence must not be absent from the sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

There are other ways in which a sentence containing a dangling modifier can be revised.

> Parties began all over campus as the students completed their final exams.

> All over campus, students joined parties after completing their final exams.

> After the students completed their final exams, parties all over campus began.

Each of these sentences expresses essentially the same meaning in a slightly different way.

Let’s look at another example.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Not realizing that they would have to hike for miles, their boots had been left at home.

In this clearly incorrect sentence, the dangling modifier indicates that the boots were unaware that they would have hike for miles.

To fix the sentence, we can use our strategy, placing the noun that should be modified after the comma separating the introductory phrase from the main clause.

> Not realizing that they would have to hike for miles, <strong>the anthropologists</strong> had left their boots at home.

Consider a final example sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Using the newest underwater sonography techniques, the missing pirate ship was finally found.

Notice that, although this sentence seems to be correct as written, there is a subtle yet important flaw: There is no noun present that could be reasonably portrayed as “using the newest underwater sonography techniques.” So, the logic of this sentence is off.

> Using the newest underwater sonography techniques, <strong>the archeologist</strong> finally found the missing pirate ship.

##  Sentence Correction Example 9  

Constructing physical locations, <u>an increasing amount of online shopping is moving offline</u>.

1. [ ] (A) an increasing amount of online shopping is moving offline
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) online shopping is increasingly moving offline
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) the number of online shops moving offline is increasing
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) an increasing amount of offline shopping that was online has moved  
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) shops that were online have increasingly moved offline

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A)</b> Constructing physical locations, <b>an increasing amount of online shopping is moving offline
</b>.



“Online shopping” is an inanimate thing that cannot possibly be “constructing physical locations.” So, it does not make sense to say “Constructing physical locations, an increasing amount of online shopping ….”


In fact, there is nothing mentioned in the sentence that could possibly be “constructing physical locations.” Thus, the opening modifier is a dangling modifier; there is no noun in the sentence that the modifier could logically modify.  


> <b>(B)</b> Constructing physical locations, <b>online shopping is increasingly moving offline
</b>.



“Online shopping” is an inanimate thing that cannot possibly be “constructing physical locations.” So, it does not make sense to say “Constructing physical locations, online shopping ….”


In fact, there is nothing mentioned in the sentence that could possibly be “constructing physical locations.” Thus, the opening modifier is a dangling modifier; there is no noun in the sentence that the modifier could logically modify.


> <b>(C)</b> Constructing physical locations, <b>the number of online shops moving offline is increasing
</b>.



A “number” cannot possibly be “constructing physical locations.” So, it does not make sense to say “Constructing physical locations, the number of online shops ….” The opening modifier is misplaced.


It would make sense to say that the shops themselves, not “the number,” are “constructing physical locations.”


> <b>(D)</b> Constructing physical locations, <b>an increasing amount of offline shopping that was online has moved  
</b>.



“Offline shopping” is an inanimate thing that cannot possibly be “constructing physical locations.” So, it does not make sense to say “Constructing physical locations, an increasing amount of offline shopping ….”


In fact, there is nothing mentioned in the sentence that could carry out that action. Thus, the opening modifier is a dangling modifier.


Notice also that the meaning conveyed by this choice is muddled and not quite logical. It does not make sense to say “offline shopping that was only online,” because shopping that was only online would not simultaneously be “offline shopping.” Logically, there can be “offline shopping” and there can be “shopping that was only online,” but if the shopping is one of those, it is by definition not the other.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E)</b> Constructing physical locations, <b>shops that were online have increasingly moved offline
</b>.



In this choice, the opening modifier “constructing physical locations” logically modifies the noun “shops.”


Furthermore, this choice produces a clear and logical timeline of events, making clear that the shops “were online only” previously, but they “have increasingly moved offline” since that time.


So, this version effectively conveys a meaning that makes sense.


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>Check to make sure that the noun a modifier is supposed to describe is present in the sentence.


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>If the noun that an introductory noun modifier is meant to modify doesn’t appear right after the modifier, then the modifier is misplaced.


<strong>Key Concept: </strong>Don't select answer choices that produce sentence versions that convey illogical meanings.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Sentence Correction Example 10  

<u>Having been alarmed by widespread declines in equities markets, gold’s price, which is often used</u> as a safe-haven investment during times of economic trouble, has risen dramatically in recent months.

1. [ ] (A) Having been alarmed by widespread declines in equities markets, gold’s price, which is often used
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) Because of alarm resulting from widespread declines in equities markets, the price of gold, which is often used
3. [ ] (C) Since being alarmed by widespread declines in equities markets, the price of gold, often used
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) As a result of alarm by widespread declines in equities markets, gold’s price, used often
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) Alarmed by widespread declines in equities markets, the price of gold, often used
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



Correctly answering this question requires noticing that the subject of each version is either “gold’s price” or “the price of gold” and that, therefore, any noun modifier that opens a version has to logically modify one of those subjects.


Also, the version created via the use of choice (E), though not logical, “sounds correct.” So, having read through choices (A), (B), (C), and (D), some of which “sound funny,” we could easily be tempted to incorrectly choose (E).


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Having been alarmed by widespread declines in equities markets, gold’s price, which is often used
</b> as a safe-haven investment during times of economic trouble, has risen dramatically in recent months.



The opening modifier “having been alarmed by” can logically modify only something that can have been alarmed. However, the noun “gold’s price,” which must be the target of the opening modifier, would not be alarmed, and there is in the sentence no suitable target for the modifier. Thus, the modifier is dangling.


Also, the use of the modifier “which is often used as a safe-haven investment …” to modify “gold’s price” results in the sentence’s conveying the illogical meaning that the price of gold, rather than gold itself, is used as a safe-haven investment.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B)</b> <b>Because of alarm resulting from widespread declines in equities markets, the price of gold, which is often used
</b> as a safe-haven investment during times of economic trouble, has risen dramatically in recent months.



Rather than open with a noun modifier before its main clause, this sentence opens with a prepositional phrase, “because of alarm …,” that explains why the price of gold has risen.


So, this version effectively conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>Since being alarmed by widespread declines in equities markets, the price of gold, often used
</b> as a safe-haven investment during times of economic trouble, has risen dramatically in recent months.



The opening modifier “Since being alarmed by …” can logically modify only something that can be alarmed. However, the noun “the price of gold,” which must be the target of the opening modifier, would not be alarmed, and there is in the sentence no suitable target for the modifier. Thus, the opening modifier is dangling.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>As a result of alarm by widespread declines in equities markets, gold’s price, used often
</b> as a safe-haven investment during times of economic trouble, has risen dramatically in recent months.



While the expression “alarmed by” is idiomatically correct, the wording “As a result of alarm by widespread declines” is illogical, as “by” must work with a verb in this type of context. For example, “as a result of alarm <em>caused </em>by widespread declines” would be correct.


Also, the use of the modifier “often used as a safe-haven investment …” to modify “gold’s price” results in the sentence’s conveying the illogical meaning that the price of gold, rather than gold itself, is used as a safe-haven investment.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>Alarmed by widespread declines in equities markets, the price of gold, often used
</b> as a safe-haven investment during times of economic trouble, has risen dramatically in recent months.



This choice could be tempting, as the version created via the use of this choice is concise and “sounds right.” However, the opening modifier “Alarmed by …” can logically modify only something that can be alarmed, and the noun “the price of gold,” which must be the target of the opening modifier, would not be alarmed, and there is in the sentence no suitable target for the modifier. Thus, the opening modifier is dangling.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

##  Sentence Correction Example 11  

Inspection of goods during production is often recommended, because, <u>when inspected</u> only after production, an entire order of finished goods can be found to be flawed.

1. [x] (A) when inspected
2. [ ] (B) when inspecting
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) when they inspect
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) inspecting them
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) by being inspected
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



To answer this question correctly, we have to notice that the subject of the final clause of the sentence is “an entire order of finished goods.” So, a noun modifier that precedes the final clause has to work effectively with that subject. Noun modifiers that cannot modify that subject may be left dangling.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A)</b> Inspection of goods during production is often recommended, because, <b>when inspected
</b> only after production, an entire order of finished goods can be found to be flawed.



The modifier created via the use of this choice, “when inspected only after production,” logically modifies the noun that follows the modifier, “an entire order of finished goods,” and the resulting sentence conveys a logical meaning.


> <b>(B)</b> Inspection of goods during production is often recommended, because, <b>when inspecting
</b> only after production, an entire order of finished goods can be found to be flawed.



The modifier created via the use of this choice is meant to modify whoever is inspecting goods, as it says “when inspecting only after production,” as if to indicate that the clause to follow will be about someone who is inspecting after production. However, no people are mentioned. So, the modifier created via the use of this choice is a dangling modifier.


> <b>(C)</b> Inspection of goods during production is often recommended, because, <b>when they inspect
</b> only after production, an entire order of finished goods can be found to be flawed.



The pronoun “they” has no logical referent, as the only plural noun in the sentence is “goods,” and goods would not inspect. So, the sentence mentions nobody whom the dependent clause “when they inspect only after production” would be about.


> <b>(D)</b> Inspection of goods during production is often recommended, because, <b>inspecting them
</b> only after production, an entire order of finished goods can be found to be flawed.



We can tell that the modifier created via the use of this choice is meant to modify someone who is inspecting after production; the modifier indicates that the clause that follows will be about someone “inspecting them after production.” However, no people are mentioned in the clause. So, the modifier is dangling.


> <b>(E)</b> Inspection of goods during production is often recommended, because, <b>by being inspected
</b> only after production, an entire order of finished goods can be found to be flawed.



The version created via the use of this choice conveys the nonsensical meaning that, “by being inspected after production,” the goods themselves somehow cause an entire order to be found to be flawed.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

Let’s now discuss a common trap that involves possessives and modification.
