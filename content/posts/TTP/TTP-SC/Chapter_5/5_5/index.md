## Lesson Content
Nouns in possessive form often don’t seem much different from ordinary nouns, but the two types of nouns do not work in the same way in sentences.

Consider the following sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Awarded the Nobel Prize for his work in chemistry, <strong>Linus Pauling’s research</strong> was wide-ranging and hard-hitting, and had a positive impact on many future generations of chemists.

The possessive “Linus Pauling’s” is not the same as the standalone noun “Linus Pauling.” Rather, “Linus Pauling’s” is a possessive noun, which, in this sentence, modifies “research.” “Linus Pauling’s research” cannot be “awarded the Nobel Prize for his work in chemistry.” It’s Linus Pauling himself who was awarded the Nobel Prize for his work.

Let’s look at another example sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Eating the delicious guacamole after having been on a restricted diet for two months, <strong>Robert’s face</strong> showed nothing but enjoyment.

We can see that the modifying phrase “Eating the delicious guacamole after having been on a restricted diet for two months” cannot logically modify “Robert’s face.” After all, “Robert’s face” is not capable of eating guacamole and was not on a restricted diet for two months.

Be careful when you encounter possessives after modifying phrases.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Be on the lookout for a modifying phrase followed by a possessive noun. In such a case, the modification and the resulting sentence may be illogical.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 12  

<u>Having only climbed halfway up the mountain, the heavy snow stalled the hikers’ progress, obscured the trail, and covered</u> landmarks normally used for navigating.

1. [ ] (A) Having only climbed halfway up the mountain, the heavy snow stalled the hikers’ progress, obscured the trail, and covered
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) Only having climbed halfway up the mountain, the hikers’ progress was stalled by the heavy snow that obscured the trail and covered
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) Having climbed only halfway up the mountain, the progress of the hikers was stalled by the heavy snow, which obscured the trail to cover
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) Only having climbed halfway up the mountain, the hikers found their progress stalled by the heavy snow that obscured the trail from covering
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) Having climbed only halfway up the mountain, the hikers found their progress stalled by the heavy snow, which obscured the trail and covered

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A)</b> <b>Having only climbed halfway up the mountain, the heavy snow stalled the hikers’ progress, obscured the trail, and covered
</b> landmarks normally used for navigating.



 The adverb “only” in “having only climbed” is misplaced. Because “only” is placed just before “climbed,” the sentence conveys the somewhat illogical meaning that the hikers had “only climbed” and had not done anything else. “Only” should instead be placed just before “halfway,” so that the sentence conveys the logical meaning that the hikers had climbed “only halfway.”


Also, since the opening modifier, “having only climbed …” modifies the noun that follows it, “the heavy snow,” this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that the heavy snow, rather than the hikers, was climbing up the mountain.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>Only having climbed halfway up the mountain, the hikers’ progress was stalled by the heavy snow that obscured the trail and covered
</b> landmarks normally used for navigating.



The adverb “only” is misplaced. “Only having climbed” conveys the somewhat illogical meaning that the hikers had only climbed and had not done anything else. What would be more logical is that the hikers found their progress stalled, “having climbed <em>only halfway</em>.”


Furthermore, the opening modifier “only having climbed halfway up the mountain” illogically modifies “the hikers’ progress.” The progress wasn’t climbing. The hikers were, but the noun “hikers” does not appear in the sentence. Only the possessive form “hikers’” is used, and hikers’ would not climb the mountain. “Hikers’” serves only to modify “progress.”


> <b>(C)</b> <b>Having climbed only halfway up the mountain, the progress of the hikers was stalled by the heavy snow, which obscured the trail to cover
</b> landmarks normally used for navigating.



In this version, the opening modifier “having climbed only halfway up the mountain” is placed so as to illogically modify “the progress of the hikers.” The progress of the hikers wasn’t climbing; the hikers were. So, the meaning conveyed by this version does not make sense.


Notice also that, “to” in “which obscured the trail <em>to</em> cover landmarks” seems to indicate purpose. As a result, “which obscured the trail to cover landmarks” conveys the illogical meaning that the snow obscured the trail for the purpose of covering landmarks, as if the snow chose to obscure the trail for that purpose.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>Only having climbed halfway up the mountain, the hikers found their progress stalled by the heavy snow that obscured the trail from covering
</b> landmarks normally used for navigating.



 The adverb “only” is misplaced. “Only having climbed” conveys the somewhat illogical meaning that the hikers had only climbed and had not done anything else. What would be more logical is that the hikers found their progress stalled, “having climbed <em>only halfway</em>.”


Also “heavy snow that obscured the trail <em>from</em> covering” does not make sense. The snow would not have obscured the trail <em>from</em> covering landmarks. The preposition “from” is not used in expressing how something was achieved. It would have obscured the trail <em>by</em> covering landmarks. “By” is used in expressing how something was achieved.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E)</b> <b>Having climbed only halfway up the mountain, the hikers found their progress stalled by the heavy snow, which obscured the trail and covered
</b> landmarks normally used for navigating.



The opening modifier, “having climbed only halfway up the mountain,” logically modifies the subject, “the hikers,” and the rest of the modifiers in this version are placed in ways such that the sentence effectively conveys a clear, logical meaning.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

##### Question Level: easy



Correctly answering this question takes noticing that the opening modifier “happy consuming many foods” would logically modify “Black Rat” but would not logically modify “Black Rat’s desire,” which is the subject of some of the choices. A test-taker who does not notice that “happy consuming many foods” does not logically modify the subjects in some choices could be tempted into choosing a choice that sounds well-constructed but does not convey a logical meaning.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Happy consuming many foods, including fruits, vegetables, and twigs, the Black Rat’s desire to be close to human populations and its ability to harbor a wide range of infectious bacteria in its blood that includes Yersinia pestis, which was the pathogen responsible for causing the Black Plague, make
</b> the Black Rat a highly effective disease vector.



The opening modifier, “happy consuming many foods,” does not logically modify the subject of the sentence, “the Black Rat’s desire … and its ability ….” While the Black Rat would be “happy consuming many foods,” “the Black Rat’s desire … and its ability” would not be happy or consume foods.


Also, the use of the modifier “that includes Yersinia pestis” after “blood” results in the sentence’s conveying the illogical meaning that some of the Black Rat’s blood includes Yersinia pestis. Blood would not “include” bacteria.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>Happy consuming many foods, such as fruits, vegetables, and twigs, the Black Rat’s desire to be close to human populations and its ability to harbor in its blood a wide-range of infectious bacteria, such as Yersinia pestis, the pathogen responsible for causing the Black Plague, makes
</b> the Black Rat a highly effective disease vector.



The opening modifier, “happy consuming many foods,” does not logically modify the subject of the sentence, “the Black Rat’s desire … and its ability ….” While the black rat would be “happy consuming many foods,” “the Black Rat’s desire … and its ability” would not be happy or consume foods.


Also, the singular main verb, “makes,” does not agree in number with the compound subject, “the Black Rat’s desire … and its ability …,” as we can more easily see by crossing off the modifiers that appear between the subject and the main verb:


the Black Rat’s desire to be close to human populations and its ability <s>to harbor in its blood a wide-range of infectious bacteria, such as Yersinia pestis, the pathogen responsible for causing the Black Plague,</s> makes


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> <b>Happy consuming many foods, such as fruits, vegetables, and twigs, the Black Rat has a desire to be close to human populations, which, along with its ability to harbor in its blood a wide-range of infectious bacteria, such as Yersinia pestis, the Black Plague pathogen, makes
</b> the Black Rat a highly effective disease vector.



In this version, the opening modifier, “happy consuming many foods,” logically modifies the subject of the sentence, “the Black Rat.”


Also, the singular verb “makes” in the relative clause “which, along with its ability …” agrees with the referent of the pronoun “which,” which referent is “desire to be close to human populations.” We might be tempted to believe that “which” refers to the plural “populations,” but, in that case, the sentence would not convey a meaning that makes sense, and the verb “makes” would not agree in number with the referent of “which.” So, we can tell that “which” must refer to the entire noun phrase “desire to be close to human populations.”


Notice that, as discussed in the chapter on subject-verb agreement, the additive phrase “along with its ability …” is not part of the subject of the relative clause in which it appears, and, therefore, its presence does not make the subject of that clause plural.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>The Black Rat’s desire to be close to human populations and happy consuming many foods, including fruits, vegetables, and twigs, and its ability to harbor in its blood a wide-range of infectious bacteria, such as Yersinia pestis, the pathogen responsible for causing the Black Plague, makes
</b> the Black Rat a highly effective disease vector.



In saying, “The Black Rat’s desire to be close to human populations and happy consuming many foods,” this version suggests the illogical idea that the Black Rat has a desire “to be happy … consuming many foods …,” as we can more easily see by crossing off some of the wording:


The Black Rat’s desire to be <s>close to human populations and</s> happy consuming many foods


Also, the singular main verb, “makes,” does not agree with the compound subject of the sentence, “the Black Rat’s desire … and … its ability …,” as we can more easily see by crossing off the modifiers that appear between the subject and the main verb:


The Black Rat’s desire <s>to be close to human populations and happy consuming many foods, including fruits, vegetables, and twigs,</s> and its ability <s>to harbor in its blood a wide-range of infectious bacteria, such as Yersinia pestis, the pathogen responsible for causing the Black Plague,</s> makes


> <b>(E)</b> <b>The Black Rat is happy consuming many foods, including fruits, vegetables, and twigs, and therefore desires to be in close proximity to humans and its ability to harbor in its blood a wide-range of infectious bacteria, such as Yersinia pestis, the pathogen responsible for causing the Black Plague, to make
</b> the Black Rat a highly effective disease vector.



If read quickly, this version might seem to be OK. However, by reading it carefully we can see that “The Black Rat is happy … and … <em>desires to be in close proximity to humans and its ability to harbor bacteria</em>” conveys the illogical meaning that the Black Rat “desires to be … in close proximity to its ability to harbor bacteria.” Of course, the Black Rat would not desire to be in proximity to an ability, since the idea of being in close proximity to an ability is nonsensical.


Also, let’s cross of some modifiers to understand what “to make the Black Rat a highly effective disease vector” does:


The Black Rat is happy consuming many foods<s>, including fruits, vegetables, and twigs,</s> and therefore desires to be in close proximity to humans and its ability to harbor in its blood a wide-range of infectious bacteria, <s>such as Yersinia pestis, the pathogen responsible for causing the Black Plague,</s> to make the Black Rat a highly effective disease vector.


We can see that this version conveys the following:


The Black Rat is happy consuming many foods … and therefore desires to be in close proximity to humans and its ability to harbor in its blood a wide-range of infectious bacteria, … to make the Black Rat a highly effective disease vector.


So, this version conveys that the Black Rat is happy consuming foods and desires to be in close proximity to humans in order to make the Black Rat a disease vector. Of course, that meaning is ridiculous.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

##  Sentence Correction Example 14  

Supposedly killed by a sheriff in 1881, <u>Billy the Kid may have remained alive, actually, his death faked so that he could live without fear of arrest</u>.

1. [x] (A) Billy the Kid may have remained alive, actually, his death faked so that he could live without fear of arrest
2. [ ] (B) it may be that Billy the Kid’s death was faked so that he could live without fear of arrest, and actually remained alive
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) Billy the Kid’s death may have been faked so that he could live without fear of arrest, and actually remained alive
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) it may be that, in order to live without fear of arrest, Billy the Kid’s death was faked, and actually remained alive
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) it may be that Billy the Kid actually remained alive, his death faked so that he could live without fear of arrest
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

One way in which we could end up incorrectly answering this question is by getting caught up in the flaws in choices (B), (C), and (D) and, thus, forgetting to keep the opening modifier in mind when considering (E) and, thus, choosing incorrect answer (E), which looks correct in isolation but does not work with the opening modifier.

The opening modifier, “Supposedly killed by a sheriff in 1881,” has to modify the noun or pronoun that follows it. So, since the pronoun “it” does not refer to anything that would have been killed, this version does not make sense.

The opening modifier, “Supposedly killed by a sheriff in 1881,” has to modify the noun that follows it. So, since “Billy the Kid’s death” would not have been killed, this version does not make sense.


Also, the structure of this version is flawed, and, as a result, this version conveys the illogical meaning that Billy the Kid’s death was faked so that he actually remained alive.

The opening modifier “Supposedly killed by a sheriff in 1881” has to modify the noun or pronoun that follows it. So, since the pronoun “it,” does not refer to anything that would have been killed, this version does not make sense.

Now, let’s discuss squinting modifiers.
