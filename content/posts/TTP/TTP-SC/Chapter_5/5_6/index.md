## Lesson Content
When a modifier is placed between two words or expressions, either of which it could modify, the conveyed meaning can be ambiguous.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: People who train <strong>consistently</strong> achieve score increases.

Notice that the adverb “consistently” is a squinting modifier because it is placed between two verbs, “train” and “achieve,” either of which it could be modifying.

For the sentence to have one clear meaning, the modifier must be placed differently, so that it becomes clear which word is being modified.

For example:

> People who <strong>consistently </strong>train achieve score increases.

or

> People who train achieve score increases <strong>consistently</strong>.

Notice that, in each sentence, “consistently” now clearly modifies one word.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

For a sentence to be effective, it must be clear what any modifiers in the sentence modify. Be on the lookout for squinting modifiers, which are modifiers between two words or phrases, either of which the modifier could modify.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at another example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The library books that Jack borrowed recently were taken from his locker by a bully.

Did Jack recently borrow the books or did the bully recently take them? Because of the placement of the adverb “recently” between the verbs “borrowed” and “were taken,” we can’t be sure.

> The library books that Jack <strong>recently </strong>borrowed were taken from his locker by a bully.

or

> The library books that Jack borrowed were <strong>recently</strong> taken from his locker by a bully.

Let’s look at one more example.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Listening to music often lifted Clarissa’s spirits.<em> </em>

In this case, the squinting modifier “often” appears between a noun, “music,” and a verb, “lifted,” rather than between two verbs.

> Clarissa lifted her spirits by listening to music <strong>often</strong>.

> <strong>Often</strong>, listening to music lifted Clarissa’s spirits.

##  Sentence Correction Example 15  

Aware <u>that cyclists who ride uphill quickly build strength and stamina, the team members choose</u> to train in the mountainous region in the western part of the country.

1. [ ] (A) that cyclists who ride uphill quickly build strength and stamina, the team members choose
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) that cyclists who ride uphill build strength and stamina quickly, the team members’ choice is
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) of cyclists who are riding up hills building strength and stamina quickly, the team members choose
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) that cyclists who ride uphill build strength and stamina quickly, the team members choose
5. [ ] (E) of cyclists riding up hills that quickly build strength and stamina, the choice of the team members is
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

##### Question Level: easy



In the original version, “quickly” is a squinting modifier; as placed it could modify either “ride uphill” or “build.” So, the meaning conveyed by the original version is ambiguous. In the correct version, “quickly” clearly modifies one verb.


> <b>(A)</b> Aware <b>that cyclists who ride uphill quickly build strength and stamina, the team members choose
</b> to train in the mountainous region in the western part of the country.



The adverb “quickly” could be meant to modify either “ride uphill” or “build.”


> <b>(B)</b> Aware <b>that cyclists who ride uphill build strength and stamina quickly, the team members’ choice is
</b> to train in the mountainous region in the western part of the country.



 “Quickly” is placed so as to clearly modify “build.”


However, the placement of “the team members’ choice” directly after the opening modifier, “aware that cyclists … build …,” results in a meaning issue, because “team members’” is not the subject of the sentence but, rather, a noun in the possessive form that modifies “choice.” As a result, the conveyed meaning is that the choice is “aware that cyclists … build ….” A choice would not be aware of something.


> <b>(C)</b> Aware <b>of cyclists who are riding up hills building strength and stamina quickly, the team members choose
</b> to train in the mountainous region in the western part of the country.



This version conveys the illogical meaning that the team members train in the mountainous region because they are aware of cyclists who are riding up hills.


Also, this version nonsensically conveys that the hills are “building strength and stamina.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D)</b> Aware <b>that cyclists who ride uphill build strength and stamina quickly, the team members choose
</b> to train in the mountainous region in the western part of the country.



 “Quickly” is placed so as to clearly modify “build,” and the rest of the sentence is well constructed and conveys a logical meaning.


> <b>(E)</b> Aware <b>of cyclists riding up hills that quickly build strength and stamina, the choice of the team members is
</b> to train in the mountainous region in the western part of the country.



This version conveys the illogical meaning that the team members train in the mountainous region because they are aware of cyclists riding up hills.


Also, the wording “hills that build strength and stamina” conveys the illogical meaning that the hills themselves build strength and stamina.


Also, the placement of the opening modifier “aware of …” before the subject, “the choice,” results in the sentence’s conveying the illogical meaning that the choice is aware.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
