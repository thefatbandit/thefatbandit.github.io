## Lesson Content
The relative pronouns “that” and “which” refer to nouns that represent things other than people.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The relative pronouns “that” and “which” refer to nouns that represent things other than people. In other words, never use “that” or “which” to refer to people.
{{< /alert >}}

Because both “that” and “which” refer to objects or ideas (but never to people), they are easily confused.

All the same, in Sentence Correction questions, the following common convention has been followed.

So, what are restrictive and nonrestrictive relative clauses?

A restrictive relative clause somehow restricts the meaning of the noun that the restrictive relative clause modifies.

A nonrestrictive relative clause, on the other hand, does not restrict the meaning of the noun that it modifies.

In Sentence Correction questions, if a relative clause is restrictive, the correct pronoun to use is “that.” If a clause is nonrestrictive, the correct pronoun to use is “which.”

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In Sentence Correction questions, the words “that” and “which” are not interchangeable. The relative pronoun “that” introduces restrictive relative clauses, whereas the relative pronoun “which” introduces nonrestrictive relative clauses.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s talk a little more about what it means to be restrictive or nonrestrictive.

> Juice <strong>that is frozen</strong> lasts for months.

In this sentence, the word “that” begins the relative clause “that is frozen,” which is restrictive.

For contrast, consider this sentence:

> Mount Everest, <strong>which is the tallest mountain on Earth</strong>, has lured adventure seekers since the early 1900s.

This sentence includes the nonrestrictive relative clause “which is the tallest mountain on Earth.” Why do we use a nonrestrictive clause that begins with the relative pronoun “which” in this sentence? Well, Mount Everest is the only Mount Everest.

There is a simple strategy that we can employ to determine whether a relative clause should be restrictive and begin with the restrictive relative pronoun “that” or be nonrestrictive and begin with the nonrestrictive relative pronoun “which:”

To determine whether the underlined clause is restrictive or nonrestrictive, eliminate it from the sentence.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

To determine whether an underlined clause is restrictive or nonrestrictive, eliminate it from the sentence. If it can be eliminated without the core meaning of the sentence changing, it is nonrestrictive and will use the pronoun “which.”
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following sentence:

> Science textbooks <u>(that/which) mention creationism</u> have been banned from public schools.

Let's cross out the relative clause – that is, the clause that begins with the relative pronoun – and read the sentence again.

> Science textbooks <s><u>(that/which) mention creationism</u></s> have been banned from public schools.

Now we ask: does the sentence mean the same thing without the relative clause? Clearly, the answer is no.

> Science textbooks <strong>that</strong> mention creationism have been banned from public schools.

Let's look at another example:

> John's best work pants <u>(that/which) got filthy while he was doing yard work</u> have to be washed before Monday.

Using the clause elimination strategy, we can reread the sentence without the relative clause:

> John's best work pants <s><u>(that/which) got filthy while he was doing yard work</u></s> have to be washed before Monday.

After reading the sentence without the relative clause, ask yourself whether the meaning of the sentence is still intact.

In other words, there is no need to categorize (restrict) John’s best work pants into a subset of pants to preserve the sentence's meaning.

> John's best work pants, <strong>which</strong> got filthy while he was doing yard work, have to be washed before Monday.

There’s something else to notice about the clause in the sentence above.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Nonrestrictive relative clauses must always be set off from the rest of sentences by commas.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example sentences, which contain nonrestrictive modifiers.

> The new phone model, <strong>which utilizes two cameras to take photos</strong>, is available in an array of colors.

> The Vegan Joint, <strong>which serves up healthy plant-based meals</strong>, has very tasty food.

> Last week, Tasha ate at Crossroads Kitchen, <strong>which has an amazing wine program</strong>.

Notice that the nonrestrictive relative clauses are always preceded by commas and followed by commas or periods.

Note, however, that, while nonrestrictive relative clauses are always set off by commas, it is not the case that the word “which” must always be preceded by a comma.

> Claudia does not know which apple [which one] she will eat.

Furthermore, when “which” is immediately preceded by a preposition, as in “in which,” “by which,” “through which,” “on which,” “from which,” and “to which,” no comma precedes “which.”

Consider the following example sentences:

> The car <strong>in which</strong> we were riding was black.

> The fog <strong>through which</strong> we traveled was thick.

> Maria does not know <strong>which </strong>dress she will wear to the gala.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When the word “which” is preceded by a preposition or is used to mean “which one,” no comma preceding “which” is necessary.
{{< /alert >}}

In contrast, restrictive relative clauses are not set off from the rest of sentences by commas, as we can see in the following example:

> The turtle <strong>that made the nest</strong> was huge.

However, there are situations in which a comma will correctly precede the word “that.”

Consider the following sentence:

> The items, a chair and a mirror, that were delivered yesterday are both in the front room.

Notice that there is a comma before “that” not because a comma is required prior to a restrictive relative clause, but because “a chair and a mirror” are modifying “the items,” telling us what items are being discussed.

> The car was moving so fast, streaking by, that we had difficulty seeing the driver.

Notice that the comma before “that” belongs to the modifier “streaking by.” In other words, the comma is a consequence of the sentence structure that precedes “that.” This sentence is correct as written.

> She said, in closing, that she considered the matter resolved.

Again, notice that the comma before “that” is there merely as a consequence of the sentence structure preceding the word “that.” This sentence is correctly written.

The message here is that, if we analyze a sentence non-holistically, incorrectly thinking that a comma should never precede “that” and focusing simply on whether a comma precedes “that,” we might make an incorrect decision.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Knowing the rules is an essential part of success in Sentence Correction. However, we must also analyze sentences holistically, carefully evaluating the use of each word, structure, and piece of punctuation as it fits into the entire sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 16  

Many first time visitors to New York <u>City, that is known for having its aggressive culture, are surprised and find</u> the locals to be friendly and helpful.

1. [ ] (A) City, that is known for having its aggressive culture, are surprised and find
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) City, which is known for having an aggressive culture, are surprised upon finding
3. [ ] (C) City, known for having an aggressive culture, are surprised, finding
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) City, which is known for their aggressive culture, are surprised by the finding that
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) City that is known for having an aggressive culture are surprised when they find
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



Finding the correct answer to this question takes noticing how restrictive and nonrestrictive relative clauses are used and noticing the effects that structure and word choice have on meaning.


> <b>(A)</b> Many first time visitors to New York <b>City, that is known for having its aggressive culture, are surprised and find
</b> the locals to be friendly and helpful.



The relative clause “that is known for having its aggressive culture” is set off from the rest of the sentence, and therefore it must be nonrestrictive. However, it begins with the relative pronoun “that,” which the GMAT uses to begin restrictive relative clauses.


Also, the use of the pronoun “its” results in the sentence’s conveying a somewhat illogical meaning. Clearly, the culture of New York City is the city’s culture. Saying that it is known for having its culture does not make sense in this context.


Also, the wording “surprised and find” illogically separates the visitors’ being surprised and their finding the locals to be friendly and helpful. The point is that they are surprised <em>because of</em> what they find, but this wording does not convey that there is a connection between the two aspects of their experiences.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B)</b> Many first time visitors to New York <b>City, which is known for having an aggressive culture, are surprised upon finding
</b> the locals to be friendly and helpful.



This version effectively uses a nonrestrictive relative clause “which is known for having an aggressive culture” to show the contrast between what the city is known for and the experience of first time visitors. The use of the pronoun “which” is in line with the GMAT’s stance on the correct pronoun to use to begin a nonrestrictive relative clause, and the wording “surprised upon finding” appropriately connects the two aspects of the experience, indicating that being surprised is a result of finding.


> <b>(C)</b> Many first time visitors to New York <b>City, known for having an aggressive culture, are surprised, finding
</b> the locals to be friendly and helpful.



Whether the phrase “known for having an aggressive culture” is meant to modify “visitors” or “New York City” is not clear.


Also, the wording “are surprised, finding” does not effectively connect being surprised and finding. As a result, the sentence conveys that the visitors are surprised, without indicating what surprises them, and then adds that they find the locals to be friendly and helpful without clearly connecting the act of finding to the rest of what the sentence describes.


> <b>(D)</b> Many first time visitors to New York <b>City, which is known for their aggressive culture, are surprised by the finding that
</b> the locals to be friendly and helpful.



The use of the pronoun “their” does not make sense. Since “which” could only refer to “New York City,” this version seems to convey the illogical meaning that New York City is known for the aggressive culture of first time visitors to the city. Alternatively, “their” could be perceived as referring to the residents of New York City. However, only the city, and not the residents, is mentioned. So, the idea that “their” refers to the residents is not supported by the wording of the sentence.


Also, the wording “surprised by the finding that” is not an ideal way to express that the visitors are surprised by what they find. “Surprised by the finding that” could convey that the visitors are surprised by someone else’s finding about New York City locals.


> <b>(E)</b> Many first time visitors to New York <b>City that is known for having an aggressive culture are surprised when they find
</b> the locals to be friendly and helpful.



Because there are no commas setting it off from the rest of the sentence, and because it begins with “that,” the relative clause “that is known for having an aggressive culture” is clearly restrictive. The use of a restrictive modifier in this context does not make sense, as there is only one New York City, and therefore restricting the noun “New York City” does not make sense.


Also, the wording “surprised when they find” is not ideal, as it does not clearly express that the surprise is the result of finding, but rather expresses only that the two experiences, being surprised and finding, occur simultaneously.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

##  Sentence Correction Example 17  

Spotting a double rainbow in the sky is widely regarded as a rare occurrence, <u>although weather conditions, which the twin reflections result from​,</u> are actually quite common.

1. [ ] (A) although weather conditions, which the twin reflections result from,
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) because the twin reflections, which are the result of weather conditions,
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) though the twin reflections are the result of weather conditions that
4. [ ] (D) although weather conditions, which result in the twin reflections,
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) since the weather conditions that result in the twin reflections
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



Notice that, in order to make sense, the sentence must make clear that the weather conditions mentioned are particular weather conditions: those that result in a double rainbow. In order for the sentence to specify which weather conditions it is about, the sentence has to use a restrictive, rather than a nonrestrictive, modifier.


> <b>(A)</b> Spotting a double rainbow in the sky is widely regarded as a rare occurrence, <b>although weather conditions, which the twin reflections result from,
</b> are actually quite common.



What weather conditions are actually quite common? Clearly it does not make sense to say that all weather conditions are actually quite common, since weather conditions are a constant feature of our atmosphere. As long as Earth exists, there will be “weather conditions.” So, saying, “weather conditions … are actually quite common,” is pointless.


Thus, it does not make sense to use the nonrestrictive clause “which the twin reflections result from” to modify “weather conditions.” The sentence needs to specify which weather conditions it is mentioning, making clear they are the specific weather conditions that result in a double rainbow, as in, “although weather conditions <em>that </em>the twin reflections result from are actually quite common.” So, a restrictive relative clause should be used instead.


> <b>(B)</b> Spotting a double rainbow in the sky is widely regarded as a rare occurrence, <b>because the twin reflections, which are the result of weather conditions,
</b> are actually quite common.



It’s true that twin reflections are the result of weather conditions, so the nonrestrictive clause “which are the result of weather conditions” is not incorrect.


However, does it make sense to say that spotting a double rainbow is considered rare <em>because</em> double rainbows are common? Of course not. So, what this version says does not make sense.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> Spotting a double rainbow in the sky is widely regarded as a rare occurrence, <b>though the twin reflections are the result of weather conditions that
</b> are actually quite common.



 This version uses a restrictive relative clause, which is not set off by commas and begins with “that,” to make clear that the weather conditions mentioned are specific weather conditions. The sentence meaning is clear and logical.


> <b>(D)</b> Spotting a double rainbow in the sky is widely regarded as a rare occurrence, <b>although weather conditions, which result in the twin reflections,
</b> are actually quite common.



What weather conditions are actually quite common? Clearly it does not make sense to say that all weather conditions are actually quite common, since weather conditions are a constant feature of our atmosphere. As long as Earth exists, there will be “weather conditions.” So, saying, “weather conditions … are actually quite common,” is pointless.


Thus, it does not make sense to use the nonrestrictive clause “which result in the twin reflections” to modify “weather conditions.” The sentence needs to specify which weather conditions it is mentioning, making clear that they are the specific weather conditions that result in a double rainbow, as in, “although weather conditions <em>that</em> result in the twin reflections are actually quite common.” So, a restrictive relative clause should be used instead.


> <b>(E)</b> Spotting a double rainbow in the sky is widely regarded as a rare occurrence, <b>since the weather conditions that result in the twin reflections
</b> are actually quite common.



This version correctly uses a restrictive relative clause to specify which weather conditions we’re concerned with – those that result in twin reflections – but the use of “since” does not make sense. Would spotting a double rainbow be considered rare <em>because</em> the conditions that result in it are common? Of course not. So, what this version says does not make sense.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)
