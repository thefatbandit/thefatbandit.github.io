## Lesson Content
Like faulty placement of adjectives and adverbs, misplacement of relative clauses can result in the creation of illogical sentences.

> <u>Incorrect</u>:<em> </em>The salesman sold the car to the woman that had 15,000 miles.

Notice that the restrictive relative clause “that had 15,000 miles” is incorrectly modifying “woman.” Of course, it’s not the woman who had 15,000 miles; it’s the car she purchased.

> The salesman sold to the woman <strong>the car that had 15,000 miles</strong>.

Now the relative clause correctly modifies the noun “car.”

Let’s look at another example.

> <u>Incorrect</u>:<em> </em>The teenage girl turned her nose up at the gift given to her by her parents for her birthday, which was very expensive.

We must determine what “was very expensive.” Although teenage girls can require expensive things, the girls themselves cannot be expensive, nor can parents, birthdays, or noses (this sentence is clearly not about plastic surgery).

> Although <strong>very expensive</strong>, the gift given to the teenage girl on her birthday by her parents provoked her to turn up her nose.

> The teenage girl turned up her nose at the <strong>very expensive</strong> gift given to her on her birthday by her parents.

> On her birthday, the teenage girl’s parents gave her a <strong>very expensive gift</strong>, which caused her to turn up her nose.

Each of these sentences is effective and avoids the confusion caused by the misplaced modifier in the original sentence.

So then, any time we encounter a relative clause in a Sentence Correction question, we must ensure that its placement makes sense.

Let’s look at another example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The plane is still sitting on the runway that was scheduled to depart two hours ago.

Notice that the relative clause “that was scheduled to depart two hours ago” is a misplaced modifier.

> The <strong>plane that was scheduled to depart two hours ago</strong> is still sitting on the runway.

Notice that now the relative clause properly modifies “plane.”

Here’s another:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Scientists have found that the Great Pyramid of Giza focuses electromagnetic energy, which is an ancient structure that remains largely intact today.

What is an ancient structure that remains largely intact today? Certainly not “electromagnetic energy.” So, by considering the meaning conveyed by the sentence, we can tell that the relative clause is misplaced; it should be modifying “the Great Pyramid of Giza.” A corrected version is:

> Scientists have found that the Great Pyramid of Giza<strong>, which is an ancient structure that remains largely intact today,</strong> focuses electromagnetic energy.

Now the relative clause is properly modifying the correct noun, “Great Pyramid of Giza.”

So, we can use a rule that will help us: with a few exceptions that we’ll discuss in the coming sections, relative clauses, including those that begin with “that” or “which,” must come immediately after the nouns that they modify.

💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

With a few exceptions that we’ll discuss in the coming sections, relative clauses, including those that begin with “that” or “which,” must come immediately after the nouns that they modify.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at a few more examples:

> Avocados <strong>that are ripe</strong> do not last long.

Notice that the relative clause “that are ripe” modifies the noun “avocados.” Thus, the proper placement of “that are ripe” is right after the noun it modifies, “avocados.”

> The Boeing 777, <strong>which is a very large commercial airliner</strong>, is an engineering marvel.

Notice that the relative clause “which is a very large commercial airliner” modifies the noun “Boeing 777.” Thus, the proper placement of “which is a very large commercial airliner” is right after the noun that it modifies, Boeing 777.

Relative clauses that begin with pronouns other than “that” or “which” follow a similar pattern.

> The woman on the plane is going to Boston who has a cat with her.

Notice that the relative clause “who has a cat with her” is misplaced and, thus, seems to be illogically modifying the noun “Boston.” Of course, it should be modifying the noun “woman.” Clearer versions are:

> The woman on the plane has a cat with her and is going to Boston.

> The woman who has a cat with her on the plane is going to Boston.

> On the plane to Boston is a woman who has a cat.

> The woman on the plane has a cat with her, and she is going to Boston.

##  Sentence Correction Example 18  

Berries and <u>plants that grow in the area surrounding the defunct Chernobyl nuclear facility contain radioactive substances in extraordinarily high concentrations and are</u> therefore unsafe to eat, but animals living there eat what grows there and often experience severe illnesses as a result.

1. [x] (A) plants that grow in the area surrounding the defunct Chernobyl nuclear facility contain radioactive substances in extraordinarily high concentrations and are
2. [ ] (B) plants growing in the area surrounding the defunct Chernobyl nuclear facility containing radioactive substances in extraordinarily high concentrations and
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) plants grow in the area that surrounds the defunct Chernobyl nuclear facility, contains radioactive substances in extraordinarily high concentrations, and is
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) plants, which grow in the area surrounding the defunct Chernobyl nuclear facility, contain radioactive substances in extraordinary high concentrations and are
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) plants grow in the area surrounding the defunct Chernobyl nuclear facility, which contains radioactive substances in extraordinary high concentrations and is
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Some answer choices in this question could appear to be correct if we read them without considering the non-underlined portions of the sentence. So, correctly answering this question takes both noticing the results of the ways in which modifiers are used and carefully considering the choices in combination with the non-underlined portions.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A)</b> Berries and <b>plants that grow in the area surrounding the defunct Chernobyl nuclear facility contain radioactive substances in extraordinarily high concentrations and are
</b> therefore unsafe to eat, but animals living there eat what grows there and often experience severe illnesses as a result.



Notice that, in this version, the restrictive modifier “that grow in the area …” indicates that the sentence is about a specific set of berries and plants, the berries and plants that grow in the area surrounding the defunct Chernobyl nuclear facility.


As a result, what this version conveys about those berries and plants and about the nuclear facility makes sense.


> <b>(B)</b> Berries and <b>plants growing in the area surrounding the defunct Chernobyl nuclear facility containing radioactive substances in extraordinarily high concentrations and
</b> therefore unsafe to eat, but animals living there eat what grows there and often experience severe illnesses as a result.



This choice could be tempting if read alone without the non-underlined portions, since it contains three participles that match each other: “growing,” “surrounding,” and “containing.”


However, closer examination reveals that the structure of this version is flawed.


Since the second part of the sentence is a clause connected to the first part by the conjunction “but,” the first part should also be a clause. However, the first part contains no main verb, as none of the verb forms in the first part, “growing,” “surrounding,” and “containing,” can, on their own, serve as main verbs, and, therefore, the first part of the sentence is not a clause.


So, this version is composed of a phrase illogically connected to a clause by the conjunction “but,” as we can more easily see by simplifying the sentence:


Berries and plants growing in the area … and therefore unsafe to eat, but animals living there eat what grows there …


> <b>(C)</b> Berries and <b>plants grow in the area that surrounds the defunct Chernobyl nuclear facility, contains radioactive substances in extraordinarily high concentrations, and is
</b> therefore unsafe to eat, but animals living there eat what grows there and often experience severe illnesses as a result.



In this version, the modifier “that surrounds … contains … and is therefore unsafe to eat” is placed directly after and, therefore, seems to modify “area.” So, this version conveys the illogical meaning that the area is unsafe to eat.


Alternatively, “contains … and is therefore unsafe to eat” could be taken as meaning that the berries and plants contain radioactive substances in high concentrations and are therefore unsafe to eat, but in that case, the singular verbs “contains” and “is” would not agree with the plural subject “berries and plants.”


> <b>(D)</b> Berries and <b>plants, which grow in the area surrounding the defunct Chernobyl nuclear facility, contain radioactive substances in extraordinary high concentrations and are
</b> therefore unsafe to eat, but animals living there eat what grows there and often experience severe illnesses as a result.



Because the relative clause that follows “berries and plants” in this version is a non-restrictive modifier, the version is not about a specific set of berries and plants but rather about berries and plants in general. So, this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that all berries and plants, which grow in the area surrounding the defunct nuclear facility (as if all berries and plants grow in that area and nowhere else), contain radioactive substances in high concentrations and are therefore unsafe to eat.


> <b>(E)</b> Berries and <b>plants grow in the area surrounding the defunct Chernobyl nuclear facility, which contains radioactive substances in extraordinary high concentrations and is
</b> therefore unsafe to eat, but animals living there eat what grows there and often experience severe illnesses as a result.



In this version, the modifier “which contains … and is therefore unsafe to eat” is placed directly after and, therefore, modifies “facility,” and thus this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that the nuclear facility is unsafe to eat.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

##  Sentence Correction Example 19  

The graduate students were eager to see the presentation by the archeologist, <u>an expert on pottery made by ancient Mayan people who arrived the day before</u>.

1. [ ] (A) an expert on pottery made by ancient Mayan people who arrived the day before
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) who was an expert on pottery made by ancient Mayan people and who arrived the day before
3. [ ] (C) an expert who arrived the day before on pottery made by ancient Mayan people
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) who was an expert on pottery made by ancient Mayan people, who arrived the day before
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) who arrived the day before being an expert on pottery made by ancient Mayan people
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



Depending on where the relative clause “who arrived the day before” is placed, the sentence may either make sense or convey a nonsensical meaning.


> <b>(A)</b> The graduate students were eager to see the presentation by the archeologist, <b>an expert on pottery made by ancient Mayan people who arrived the day before
</b>.



 The relative clause “who arrived the day before” is placed directly after and seems to modify “ancient Mayan people.” Thus, this version seems to convey the nonsensical meaning that ancient Mayan people arrived the day before.


Admittedly, we could decipher that, since ancient Mayan people would not have arrived the day before, it must have been the expert who arrived the day before. All the same, the GMAT would not prefer this version, considering it ineffective, because it so strongly seems to convey the nonsensical meaning that the ancient Mayan people arrived the day before and is, therefore, not well constructed.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B)</b> The graduate students were eager to see the presentation by the archeologist, <b>who was an expert on pottery made by ancient Mayan people and who arrived the day before
</b>.



Both “who was an expert on pottery …” and “who arrived the day before” are placed in ways such that they modify something that they would logically modify, “archeologist,” and such that the sentence conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(C)</b> The graduate students were eager to see the presentation by the archeologist, <b>an expert who arrived the day before on pottery made by ancient Mayan people
</b>.



“Who arrived the day before” is placed so as to clearly and logically modify “expert.” However, because “who arrived the day before” is placed just before “on pottery,” this version conveys that the expert had arrived on pottery.


> <b>(D)</b> The graduate students were eager to see the presentation by the archeologist, <b>who was an expert on pottery made by ancient Mayan people, who arrived the day before
</b>.



 The relative clause “who arrived the day before” is placed directly after and seems to modify “ancient Mayan people.” So, this version seems to convey the nonsensical meaning that ancient Mayan people arrived the day before.


> <b>(E)</b> The graduate students were eager to see the presentation by the archeologist, <b>who arrived the day before being an expert on pottery made by ancient Mayan people
</b>.



 “Who arrived the day before” is placed so as to clearly and logically modify “archeologist.” However, because “who arrived the day before” is placed just before “being an expert,” this version conveys an ambiguous meaning that seems to be that the archeologist arrived on the day before the archeologist was being an expert on pottery, a meaning that does not make sense.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

Let’s now discuss the situations in which relative clauses can modify their antecedents remotely.
