## Lesson Content
As we discussed in a previous section, the relative pronoun “that” can be correctly used to introduce a restrictive relative clause.

In formal writing, the relative pronoun “that” should refer only to objects or ideas, never people.

For example:

> The <strong>woman who</strong> lives next door to me is a pianist.

A woman is a “who.”

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The <strong>man that</strong> plays the violin also has a very good voice.

A man cannot be a “that.”

> The <strong>person with whom</strong> I was speaking is the <strong>writer who</strong> wrote and produced the movie that we watched last weekend.

“Who” and “whom” are used to refer to people.

> The idea <strong>that most excited him</strong> turned out to have the worst consequences

Since an “idea” is a thing, it is referred to by “that.”

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The person <strong>that was speaking</strong> is very smart.

A person cannot be a “that.”

> Archery, <strong>which is far more fun than I had expected</strong>, has failed to maintain its popularity.

“Which” refers to things.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The girl <strong>with which I was speaking</strong> was quite funny.

“Which” cannot describe people.

> The girl <strong>with whom I was speaking</strong> was quite funny.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The relative pronoun “that” should be used to refer only to objects or ideas, not people. Similarly, the relative pronoun “which” cannot be used to refer to people. The relative pronouns “who” and “whom” are used to refer to human antecedents.
{{< /alert >}}
