## Lesson Content
We should also be clear about the correct usage of “who” and “whom.”

As discussed in the Pronouns chapter, the pronouns “who” and “whom” should not be used interchangeably, even though both refer to people, not objects.

A subject pronoun performs an action, whereas an object pronoun is acted upon.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The subject pronoun “who” performs the action in a clause, whereas the object pronoun “whom” is acted upon.
{{< /alert >}}

For example:

> People <strong>who love other people</strong> are the happiest people in the world.

“Who” refers to people, and in this case, people are doing the action, loving, and are therefore the subject of the clause.

> Arnold’s sister, <strong>whom we all have met</strong>, graduated a year early.

Here, Arnold’s sister is not performing the action mentioned in the relative clause “whom we all have met.” She is the direct object of the verb “have met.”

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The relative pronouns “who” and “whom” refer to human antecedents. In a relative clause, “who” serves as a subject; “whom” is used as an object.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example:

> I didn’t see the person with (who/whom) you were talking.

The person taking the action described in the modifier “with (who/whom) you were talking” is “you.” That is, “you” did the talking.

> I didn’t see the person with <strong>whom</strong> you were talking.

Let’s look at another example:

> He found the attendant (who/whom) has my car keys.

In this case, “attendant” is the object of “found.” However, “who/whom” is not the object of “found.” If we isolate the relative clause that modifies “attendant,” we get “(who/whom) has my car keys.” It becomes clear that we need the subject pronoun “who,” because, within the relative clause, having the keys is the “action” and “who/whom” represents the subject taking that action.

> He found the attendant <strong>who</strong> has my car keys.

A strategy that can help with determining whether we need “who” or “whom” is to replace “who” or “whom” with another subject or object pronoun that is easier to use.

> <strong>he </strong>has my car keys

We wouldn’t say:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: <strong>him</strong> has my car keys

Since “he” is a subject pronoun, we need the subject pronoun “who.”

It’s helpful to be able to recognize some common subject pronouns and object pronouns.

> Subject Pronouns: I, you, he, she, it, we, they, who

> Object Pronouns: me, you, him, her, it, us, them, whom

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Commit the following two lists to memory.
{{< /alert >}}

Another thing to understand about these relative pronouns is that, when people make up unified groups such as armies, teams, companies, and committees, the relative pronoun “that” or “which” is generally used to refer to these groups.

For example, consider the noun “team.” Although a team of people is, obviously, composed of people, generally we would not say “a team who ….” Instead, we’d say “a team that ….”

The reason is that an entity made up of people such as a team, group, or class, is considered a thing when the context treats such an entity as a single unit.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Entities made up of people such as teams, groups, or classes, are considered things when the context treats such an entity as a single unit and, in such cases, are referred to via the use of the relative pronouns “that” or “which.”
{{< /alert >}}

##### Question Level: medium



Correctly answering this question requires both noticing whether relative pronouns are correctly used and carefully considering the meanings conveyed by the versions.


> <b>(A)</b> Althea <b>Gibson, who won the French Open in 1956, became the first African-American, winning
</b> a Grand Slam tennis title.



While this version correctly uses the subject pronoun “who” as the subject of the clause “who won the French Open in 1956,” it conveys an illogical meaning: that Althea Gibson became the first African-American, and that in the process of doing so, she won a Grand Slam tennis title.


> <b>(B)</b> Althea <b>Gibson that won the French Open in 1956 to become the first African-American winner of
</b> a Grand Slam tennis title.



In this version, “that” is incorrectly used to refer to a person, “Althea Gibson.”


Also, this version is composed of only a noun, “Althea Gibson,” and a long modifier. Thus, this version has no main verb.


> <b>(C)</b> Althea <b>Gibson, who won the French Open in 1956, becoming the first African-American to have won
</b> a Grand Slam tennis title.



While this version correctly uses the subject pronoun “who” as the subject of the clause “who won the French Open in 1956,” this version is composed of only a noun, “Althea Gibson,” and a long modifier “who won ….” Thus, this version has no main verb.


> <b>(D)</b> Althea <b>Gibson won the French Open in 1956, becoming the first African-American whom won
</b> a Grand Slam tennis title.



In this version, the object pronoun “whom” incorrectly serves as the subject of the clause, “whom won a Grand Slam tennis title.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E)</b> Althea <b>Gibson, winning the French Open in 1956, became the first African-American to win
</b> a Grand Slam tennis title.



This version clearly and correctly conveys that, in winning the French Open in 1956, Althea Gibson became the first African-American to win a Grand Slam tennis title.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
