## Lesson Content
An appositive is an element of a sentence that modifies or further describes another element of a sentence by renaming it.

On the GMAT, appositives generally appear in the forms of nouns, pronouns, noun phrases, or noun clauses that are used to modify other nouns, pronouns, noun phrases, or noun clauses.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Appositives generally appear on the GMAT in the form of nouns, pronouns, noun phrases, or noun clauses that modify other nouns, pronouns, noun phrases, or noun clauses.
{{< /alert >}}

Here are some examples.

> Nisha’s house<strong>, a bungalow, </strong>has solar panels on the roof.

The noun phrase “a bungalow” is an appositive modifying “house.”

> My sister <strong>Lucia</strong> is working to preserve the rainforest.

The noun “sister” is renamed by the proper noun “Lucia.” So, “Lucia” is an appositive that further identifies “sister.”

> <strong>An amazing example of Renaissance art,</strong> the statue dominated the exhibit.

The noun phrase “an amazing example of Renaissance art” is an appositive that modifies “statue.”

> The ship that Robert designed<strong>, the Shenandoah,</strong> was built in 1964.

“The Shenandoah” is an appositive that modifies the noun phrase “the ship that Robert designed.”

> King’s conclusion<strong>, that nonviolent methods would be effective,</strong> proved to be correct.

The noun clause “that nonviolent methods would be effective” is an appositive that modifies “King’s conclusion.”

> During the meeting, Laura<strong>, the most knowledgeable person in the room,</strong> presented several exceptional points in favor of ratifying the new construction proposal.

“The most knowledgeable person in the room” is an appositive phrase that further describes “Laura.” The appositive tells us that Laura was the most knowledgeable person in the room.

> Avocados<strong>, nutritious fruits packed with beneficial fats</strong>, are good complements for grapefruit or fresh peaches.

The noun phrase “nutritious fruits packed with beneficial fats” further describes the noun “avocados.” The appositive tells us that avocados are nutritious fruits packed with beneficial fats.

Sometimes students have difficulty determining whether a noun modifier meets the definition of an appositive.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

If, when we replace a noun in a sentence with its modifier, the sentence retains its core meaning, the noun modifier is an appositive.
{{< /alert >}}

Let's put this strategy to use in the following example:

> My niece Kristin is having a birthday party next Saturday.

First, we must identify the noun being modified in the sentence.

> Kristin is having a birthday party next Saturday.

Now, ask yourself whether the sentence retains its meaning.

Appositives are often more than one word.

> Next Saturday, Kristin is having a party<strong>, an event bound to be fit for a queen,</strong> in celebration of her birthday.

The technique for identifying the noun modifier as an appositive is the same, despite the modifier’s length.

> Next Saturday, Kristin is having an event bound to be fit for a queen in celebration of her birthday.

Therefore, the noun modifier is indeed an appositive.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Replace the noun being modified with its modifier to identify an appositive.
{{< /alert >}}
