## Lesson Content
Choosing the correct answer to a Sentence Correction question generally involves choosing the choice that most effectively conveys a meaning.

Consider the following pair of examples:

> In 1957, Chien-Shiung Wu was passed over by those in charge of awarding of the Nobel Prize in Physics, <strong>which many people found outrageous</strong>.

> In 1957, Chien-Shiung Wu was passed over by those in charge of awarding of the Nobel Prize in Physics, <strong>a decision that many people found outrageous</strong>.

Notice the difference between the effectiveness of the first version, which employs the relative clause “which many people found outrageous,” and the effectiveness of the second version, which employs the abstract appositive “a decision that many people found outrageous.”

In the first, what the relative clause “which many people found outrageous” is meant to modify is not entirely clear.

The second version, on the other hand, uses the abstract appositive “a decision that many people found outrageous” to rename and modify the entire idea presented in the preceding clause and, thus, to clearly convey that it was the decision to pass over Chien-Shiung Wu that many people found outrageous.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In Sentence Correction questions, abstract appositives may appear in sentence versions that are more effective than versions created via the use of other structures.
{{< /alert >}}

Of course, not every sentence that includes an abstract appositive is effective.

##  Sentence Correction Example 26  

The legislators voted to repeal the unpopular <u>law,</u> a decision cheered by many.

1. [x] (A) law,
2. [ ] (B) law that was
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) law and were
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) law, which was
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) law, being
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



All of the versions in this question are basically grammatically correct. So, correctly answering this question takes noticing which version employs modifiers in a way such that the version effectively conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A)</b> The legislators voted to repeal the unpopular <b>law,
</b> a decision cheered by many.



The abstract appositive “a decision cheered by many” renames and logically modifies what is presented in the preceding clause, the legislators’ voting to repeal the law. Since a law would not be a decision cheered by many, what the closing appositive modifies is clear.


> <b>(B)</b> The legislators voted to repeal the unpopular <b>law that was
</b> a decision cheered by many.



The relative clause “that was a decision cheered by many” seems to modify “unpopular law.” Thus, this version conveys the illogical meaning that the unpopular law itself was a decision cheered by many.


> <b>(C)</b> The legislators voted to repeal the unpopular <b>law and were
</b> a decision cheered by many.



By considering this choice along with the non-underlined portion of the sentence, we see that this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that the legislators were a decision cheered by many.


> <b>(D)</b> The legislators voted to repeal the unpopular <b>law, which was
</b> a decision cheered by many.



The pronoun “which” could refer to the entire preceding clause, but “which” could also refer to “law.” So, the meaning of this version is somewhat ambiguous. Also, in GMAT Sentence Correction, using a relative pronoun to refer to a clause is not preferred. So, the combination of ambiguity and a pronoun’s possibly referring to a clause makes this choice clearly inferior to choice (A), which uses an abstract appositive to concisely convey a clear meaning.


> <b>(E)</b> The legislators voted to repeal the unpopular <b>law, being
</b> a decision cheered by many.



The use of the closing participial phrase “being a decision cheered by many” results in the sentence’s conveying the nonsensical meaning that, as the legislators voted to repeal the law, the legislators were being a decision.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

##  Sentence Correction Example 27  

The results of experiments performed by Sir Frederick William Herschel indicated that there was a form of light not visible to <u>the human eye, which was a development</u> that led to the discovery of infrared.

1. [ ] (A) the human eye, which was a development
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) human eyes, which were developments
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) the human eye, and these were developments
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) human eyes, a development
5. [ ] (E) the human eye and that it was a development
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Most versions of the sentence attempt to use pronouns to refer to either parts of the main clause or the entire main clause, and none of these versions effectively convey logical meanings. Only the version that uses an abstract appositive to modify the entire main clause effectively conveys a logical meaning.


> <b>(A)</b> The results of experiments performed by Sir Frederick William Herschel indicated that there was a form of light not visible to <b>the human eye, which was a development
</b> that led to the discovery of infrared.



The relative pronoun “which” could refer to the entire preceding clause, a pronoun-referent relationship not preferred by the GMAT. Alternatively, “which” could refer to “a form of light” or to “the human eye.” So, the meaning of this version is somewhat ambiguous.


So, the combination of ambiguity and a pronoun’s possibly referring to a clause makes this choice clearly inferior to choice (D), which uses an abstract appositive to concisely convey a clear meaning.


> <b>(B)</b> The results of experiments performed by Sir Frederick William Herschel indicated that there was a form of light not visible to <b>human eyes, which were developments
</b> that led to the discovery of infrared.



 The modifier “which were developments …” seems to modify “human eyes.” Since it does not make sense that “human eyes” would be the developments leading to the discovery of infrared, this version does not make sense.


> <b>(C)</b> The results of experiments performed by Sir Frederick William Herschel indicated that there was a form of light not visible to <b>the human eye, and these were developments
</b> that led to the discovery of infrared.



Whether “these” refers to “results” or “experiments” is not clear.


Further, regardless of which of those nouns “these” refers to, the sentence does not make sense, as it does not make sense that the “results” or the “experiments” would be the developments leading to the discovery of infrared.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D)</b> The results of experiments performed by Sir Frederick William Herschel indicated that there was a form of light not visible to <b>human eyes, a development
</b> that led to the discovery of infrared.



In this version, the abstract appositive “a development leading to the discovery of infrared” modifies the entire preceding clause, and the resulting sentence clearly conveys that the results’ indicating that there was a form of light not visible to human eyes was a development leading to the discovery of infrared.


Notice, this correct answer uses “human eyes” rather than the more conventional “the human eye.” While using “the human eye” would probably be better than using “human eyes,” the use of “human eyes” is acceptable and serves the purpose of obscuring the fact that this choice is the correct answer, partly because there seems to be an agreement issue, since “human eyes” is plural, while “a development” is singular.


> <b>(E)</b> The results of experiments performed by Sir Frederick William Herschel indicated that there was a form of light not visible to <b>the human eye and that it was a development
</b> that led to the discovery of infrared.



The pronoun “it” could refer to “form of light” or to “the human eye,” but neither would be a development leading to the discovery of infrared.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
