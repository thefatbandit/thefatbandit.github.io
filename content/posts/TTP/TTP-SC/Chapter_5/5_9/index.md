## Lesson Content
Sentences containing participles or participial phrases appear quite often in Sentence Correction questions.

Let’s begin by discussing present participles and past participles.

<u>Present Participles and Past Participles</u>

A participle is a verb form that functions as a modifier.

> Present Participle Examples: “jumping,” “running,” “singing,” and “setting”

> Past Participle Examples: “ruined,” “laminated,” “spoken,” and “swollen”

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A participle is a verb form that functions as a modifier. Participles come in two varieties: present participles, which always end in -ing, and past participles, which usually end in –ed or -en.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s discuss participles functioning as adjectives.

<u>Participles Functioning as Adjectives</u>

One way a participle can be used in a sentence is as an adjective – simply modifying a noun.

In the following examples, present participles are modifying nouns:

> “jumping crickets,” “singing men,” “setting sun,” “running dog”

In the following examples, past participles are modifying nouns:

> “ruined castle,” “laminated copy,” “spoken word,” “swollen foot”

As in the above examples, generally, participles are associated with or modify nouns.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The noun associated with a participle can be either (1) the agent doing the action of the participle or (2) the receiver of the effect of the participle.
{{< /alert >}}

The agent of a participle is similar to the subject of a sentence.

A receiver is similar to an object in a sentence, in that the receiver is the thing being affected.

Each of the following examples includes two participles, one followed by an agent and the other by a receiver.

> Within minutes, the <strong>swimming sailors</strong> had righted the <strong>capsized boat</strong>.

The first participle, “swimming,” is followed by its agent, “sailors.” The second participle, “capsized,” is followed by its receiver, “boat.”

> The <strong>completed work of art</strong> was admired by the <strong>smiling patrons</strong>.

The first participle, “completed,” is followed by its receiver, “work of art.” The second participle, “smiling,” is followed by its agent, “patrons.”

Let’s now discuss the fact that a participle can never on its own be the main verb in a clause.
