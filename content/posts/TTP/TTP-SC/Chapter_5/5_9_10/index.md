## Lesson Content
Sentence Correction question creators can create issues in sentences by misplacing past participial phrases.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Awarded the Nobel Prize for his work in chemistry, research done by Linus Pauling was wide-ranging and hard-hitting.

Notice the opening past participial phrase, “awarded the Nobel Prize for his work in chemistry.” What follows this phrase must be the noun that it is meant to modify: Linus Pauling.

> Awarded the Nobel Prize for his work in chemistry, <strong>Linus Pauling</strong> conducted research that was wide-ranging and hard-hitting.

We can see that the opening phrase “awarded the Nobel Prize for his work in chemistry” is now properly modifying “Linus Pauling.” The noun that follows an introductory phrase that begins with a past participle must be the noun that that phrase intends to modify.

Let’s look at another example involving a past participle.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Acclaimed for his outlandish performance pieces and multimedia displays, the upcoming exhibition by this artist is sure to attract many visitors.

The upcoming exhibition is not “acclaimed for his outlandish performance pieces …” It is the artist who is acclaimed.

> Acclaimed for his outlandish performance pieces and multimedia displays, <strong>this artist</strong> is sure to attract many visitors to his upcoming exhibition.

Note that we had to rearrange the clause following the introductory phrase quite a bit to ensure that the correct noun was modified.

Here’s another example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: <strong>Known for its lovely architecture, </strong>Pedro de Heredia founded the city of Cartagena in 1533.

In this sentence, the past participial phrase “known for its lovely architecture” is placed close to and seems to modify “Pedro de Heredia.” Of course, this modification does not make sense.

> The city of Cartagena<strong>, known for its lovely architecture, </strong>was founded by Pedro de Heredia in 1533.

Let’s look at one more example.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Heralded as a pivotal breakthrough in cancer research, the lead scientist presented the group’s findings at an annual convention attended by top researchers from around the globe.

The findings, not the scientist, are being “heralded as a pivotal breakthrough.” A correct sentence might read:

> Heralded as a pivotal breakthrough in cancer research, <strong>the group’s findings</strong> were presented by the lead scientist at an annual convention attended by top researchers from around the globe.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Be on the lookout for an introductory phrase that begins with a past participle; we may have a misplaced modifier question on our hands. The noun that follows an introductory phrase that begins with a past participle must be the noun that that phrase is meant to modify.
{{< /alert >}}

Of course, these issues with past participle placement are not limited to the beginning of a sentence.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: A new wind farm is under construction in the valley<strong>, expected to provide enough power for a small city</strong>.

Since the past participial phrase “expected to provide …” directly follows “valley,” the phrase seems to modify “valley.” Of course, this modification does not make sense, as it’s not the valley that is expected to provide power; it’s the wind farm.

A new wind farm<strong>, expected to provide enough power for a small city, </strong>is under construction in the valley.

Now the past participial phrase “expected to provide …” is placed in a way such that the phrase modifies something that it would logically modify, “wind farm.”

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

One way in which Sentence Correction question writers can create issues involving past participial phrases is by misplacing the phrases, so that the phrases modify the wrong nouns.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 38  

<u>Lava balloons, bubbles surrounded by crusts of lava, their gas content allowing them to float on the surface of the ocean.</u>

1. [ ] (A) Lava balloons, bubbles surrounded by crusts of lava, their gas content allowing them to float on the surface of the ocean.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) Because of their gas content, lava balloons are bubbles allowed to float on the ocean’s surface surrounded by crusts of lava.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) The gas content of lava balloons, bubbles surrounded by crusts of lava, allows them to float on the surface of the ocean.
4. [ ] (D) When lava balloons are allowed to float on the ocean’s surface because of their gas content, it is because they are bubbles surrounded by crusts of lava.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) Surrounded by a crust of lava, the gas content of lava balloons is what allows these bubbles to float on the surface of the ocean.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



The choices in this question all convey different meanings, only one of which makes sense. Some versions are illogical partly because the past participial phrase “surrounded by a crust of lava” is placed in ways such that phrase modifies nouns that it does not logically modify.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Lava balloons, bubbles surrounded by crusts of lava, their gas content allowing them to float on the surface of the ocean.
</b>


Since the verb forms “surrounded” and “allowing” are participles rather than finite verbs, this version has no main verb and, thus, is not a complete sentence.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>Because of their gas content, lava balloons are bubbles allowed to float on the ocean’s surface surrounded by crusts of lava.
</b>


The placement of the past participial phrase “surrounded by crusts of lava” directly after “ocean’s surface” results in this sentence’s seeming to convey that the ocean’s surface is surrounded by crusts of lava.


Also, the overall meaning of this version is nonsensical, as the version seems to convey that lava balloons are bubbles that are allowed to float, as if they have been given permission, because of their gas content.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> <b>The gas content of lava balloons, bubbles surrounded by crusts of lava, allows them to float on the surface of the ocean.
</b>


This version uses the appositive “bubbles surrounded by crusts of lava” to effectively describe lava balloons and concisely conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>When lava balloons are allowed to float on the ocean’s surface because of their gas content, it is because they are bubbles surrounded by crusts of lava.
</b>


The overall meaning of this version is nonsensical, as it seems to suggest that lava balloons are only sometimes “allowed to float,” as if they are given permission because they have gas content, and that they are allowed to float because they are bubbles surrounded by crusts of lava. So, this version does not present a logical explanation of the floating of the bubbles.


Also, there is no clear referent for the pronoun “it,” as the pronoun “it” would not logically refer to an entire clause, and there is in the sentence no noun that “it” would logically represent.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>Surrounded by a crust of lava, the gas content of lava balloons is what allows these bubbles to float on the surface of the ocean.
</b>


Since “surrounded by a crust of lava” directly precedes “the gas content,” this version conveys the illogical meaning that the gas content, in other words the percentage of the composition of or the amount of gas contained in lava balloons, is surrounded by a crust of lava.


Further, this version conveys the illogical meaning that the gas content of all lava balloons is surrounded by a single crust of lava.


Also, the wording “the gas content of lava balloons is what allows these bubbles” does not clearly indicate that the lava balloons are the bubbles.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

##  Sentence Correction Example 39  

<u>Created by artist Jacques Majorelle situated on two and a half acres in the center of Marrakesh, the sights seen by visitors to the Majorelle Garden includes</u> many cacti and a cubist villa painted a stunning blue.

1. [ ] (A) Created by artist Jacques Majorelle situated on two and a half acres in the center of Marrakesh, the sights seen by visitors to the Majorelle Garden includes
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) When visitors to the Majorelle Garden see sights, created by artist Jacques Majorelle and situated on two and a half acres in the center of Marrakesh, it includes
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) Created by artist Jacques Majorelle, visitors to the Majorelle garden situated on two and a half acres in the center of Marrakesh see sights to include
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) Seen by visitors to the Majorelle garden, created by artist Jacques Majorelle and situated on two and a half acres in the center of Marrakesh, are sights including
5. [ ] (E) The sights seen by visitors to the Majorelle Garden that was created by artist Jacques Majorelle situated on two and a half acres in the center of Marrakesh including
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



To answer this question correctly, we have to notice the placement of past participial phrases in order to find a version that conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Created by artist Jacques Majorelle situated on two and a half acres in the center of Marrakesh, the sights seen by visitors to the Majorelle Garden includes
</b> many cacti and a cubist villa painted a stunning blue.



 Notice that the past participial phrase “situated on two and a half acres in the center of Marrakesh” is placed directly after “artist Jacques Majorelle” without a comma between the two and is followed by a comma. As a result, the phrase seems to illogically modify the artist, who would not be “situated on two and a half acres.”


Also, it does not even quite make sense to say that “sights” are “situated on two and a half acres.” So, that phrase is clearly misplaced.


Meanwhile, since the subject of the main clause is the plural “sights,” not the singular “the Majorelle Garden,” the clause’s verb, the singular “includes,” does not agree with the subject.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>When visitors to the Majorelle Garden see sights, created by artist Jacques Majorelle and situated on two and a half acres in the center of Marrakesh, it includes
</b> many cacti and a cubist villa painted a stunning blue.



 The first issue we come to as we read this version is that “sights” is modified by the phrase “created by artist Jacques Majorelle and situated on two and a half acres in the center of Marrakesh.” While it would make sense to describe a garden as created by artist Jacques Majorelle and situated on two and a half acres, it does not really make sense to describe sights in that way. This issue is not a clear, strong decision point, but it does make this version less than ideal.


The issue that makes this choice clearly incorrect is that the use of “it” is problematic.


Since sights would include many cacti and a cubist villa, the singular “it”’ seems to incorrectly refer to the plural “sights.”


On the other hand, we could decide that “it” must refer to the singular “the Majorelle Garden.” However, in that case, we have a meaning issue, because in that case, the sentence conveys the nonsensical meaning that the garden includes cacti and a villa only “when visitors to the Majorelle Garden see sights,” as if the cacti and villa cease to exist when the visitors don’t see sights.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>Created by artist Jacques Majorelle, visitors to the Majorelle garden situated on two and a half acres in the center of Marrakesh see sights to include
</b> many cacti and a cubist villa painted a stunning blue.



 Since what follows the opening past participial phrase “created by artist Jacques Majorelle” is “visitors,” the opening phrase is misplaced, since visitors to the garden would not have been created by Jacques Majorelle.


Also, “sights <em>to include</em> many cacti and a cubist villa” expresses that the sights will include those things in the future but do not currently include them. As a result, “visitors … see sights to include many cacti and a cubist villa” conveys the illogical meaning that the visitors see sights that will include many cacti and a villa but do not include them when the visitors see them.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D)</b> <b>Seen by visitors to the Majorelle garden, created by artist Jacques Majorelle and situated on two and a half acres in the center of Marrakesh, are sights including
</b> many cacti and a cubist villa painted a stunning blue.



 This version is a little tricky, because it is an inverted sentence in which the main verb, “are … seen,” precedes the subject, “sights.”


All the same, this version is well constructed, with the past participial phrase “created by artist Jacques Majorelle and situated on two and a half acres …” correctly placed to logically modify “the Majorelle Garden.”


This version is grammatically correct and effectively conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>The sights seen by visitors to the Majorelle Garden that was created by artist Jacques Majorelle situated on two and a half acres in the center of Marrakesh including
</b> many cacti and a cubist villa painted a stunning blue.



 The use of the restrictive relative clause “that was created by artist Jacques Majorelle …” to modify “the Majorelle Garden” is questionable because, presumably, there is only one “Majorelle Garden.” So, using a restrictive modifier to define which Majorelle Garden the sentence is about doesn’t seem to make sense. This issue is not a strong decision point, because it’s possible that there is more than one Majorelle Garden, but it does make this version suspect, especially in the context of the other versions, which tend to indicate that there is only one Majorelle Garden.


Next, we see that “situated on two and a half acres …” directly follows “Jacques Majorelle” and seems to illogically modify “Jacques Majorelle.” While it’s arguable that “situated on two and a half acres …” is meant to modify the entire preceding phrase, “the Majorelle Garden that was created by artist Jacques Majorelle,” the placement of “situated on two and a half acres …” is certainly not ideal.


Finally, what takes this choice out of contention for sure is that, since “including” is not a finite verb,” this version includes no main verb and, thus, is not a complete sentence.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

Let’s now discuss another way in which Sentence Correction question writers can create issues involving past participial phrases.
