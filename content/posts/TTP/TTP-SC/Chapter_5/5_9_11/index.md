## Lesson Content
A second way in which Sentence Correction question writers can create issues involving past participial phrases is to use a restrictive or nonrestrictive past participial phrase where using a past participial phrase of the other type, nonrestrictive or restrictive, would make more sense.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Only a tiny percentage of students pursue degrees in physics<strong> considered one of the most challenging college subjects</strong>.

Since there is no comma between “physics” and the past participial phrase “considered one of the …,” the use of the phrase restricts “physics” to one particular type of physics, the physics considered one of the most challenging college subjects.

> Only a tiny percentage of students pursue degrees in physics<strong>, considered one of the most challenging college subjects</strong>.

Let’s consider another example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The original Eiffel Tower <strong>built in 1887</strong> was constructed along with other unique structures for an exposition.

In this example, “built in 1887” restrictively modifies “the original Eiffel Tower,” and thus specifies that the sentence is about the tower built in 1887.

> The original Eiffel Tower<strong>,</strong> <strong>built in 1887,</strong> was constructed along with other unique structures for an exposition.

Let’s consider one more:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Most animals<strong>, found in the deepest regions of the ocean,</strong> have slow metabolisms that allow them to survive on small quantities of food.

In this case, the phrase “found in the deepest regions of the ocean” is set off from the rest of the sentence by commas and, therefore, is nonrestrictive.

> Most animals<strong> found in the deepest regions of the ocean</strong> have slow metabolisms that allow them to survive on small quantities of food.

Now, the sentence makes sense because is about only one category of animals, those found in the deepest regions of the ocean.

Of course, the GMAT generally would not merely add or remove commas to differentiate correct answers from incorrect choices.

##  Sentence Correction Example 40  

Publicized scientific <u>studies, retracted because of methodological errors or conflicts of interest, can continue to strongly influence public perception of an issue even after they disavow their research</u>.

1. [ ] (A) studies, retracted because of methodological errors or conflicts of interest, can continue to strongly influence public perception of an issue even after they disavow their research
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) studies retracted because of methodological errors or conflicts of interest can continue to strongly influence public perception of an issue even after the research is disavowed
3. [ ] (C) studies retracted from methodological errors or conflicts of interest can continue to strongly influence public perception of an issue even after they have disavowed the research
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) studies, retracted as a result of methodological errors or conflicts of interest, can continue to strongly influence public perception of an issue even after researchers disavow it 
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) studies that are retracted, resulting from methodological errors or conflicts of interest, can continue to strongly influence public perception of an issue even after being disavowed by researchers
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A)</b> Publicized scientific <b>studies, retracted because of methodological errors or conflicts of interest, can continue to strongly influence public perception of an issue even after they disavow their research
</b>.



The nonrestrictive past participial phrase in this choice, “retracted because of methodological errors or conflicts of interest,” conveys the illogical meaning that <em>all</em> “publicized scientific studies” are “retracted because ….”


Of course, common sense tells us that many (if not most) publicized scientific studies are never retracted, neither for the reasons mentioned in the sentence nor for any other reason.


In this context, it would make sense to use a restrictive past participial phrase to modify the noun phrase “publicized scientific studies,” rather than use a nonrestrictive past participial phrase. In other words, the past participial phrase should not be set off by commas. A restrictive past participial phrase would logically specify that a certain category of “publicized scientific studies” is being discussed, those publicized studies that are retracted (as opposed to publicized studies in general).


Notice also that the plural pronouns “they” and “their” are missing an antecedent in the sentence. These pronouns can’t logically refer to “studies,” “errors,” or “conflicts” since none of those things would “disavow” (deny support for) their research. Presumably, these pronouns are supposed to refer to the researchers who worked on the studies, as those would be the people who “disavow their research” in this context. However, “researchers” or any similarly logical antecedent does not actually appear anywhere in the sentence. 


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B)</b> Publicized scientific <b>studies retracted because of methodological errors or conflicts of interest can continue to strongly influence public perception of an issue even after the research is disavowed
</b>.



This choice logically uses a restrictive past participial phrase to specify that a certain category of “publicized scientific studies” is being discussed, those publicized studies that are “retracted because of methodological errors or conflicts of interest” (as opposed to publicized studies in general).


Thus, this version conveys a clear and logical meaning. Furthermore, this version contains no grammatical errors.


> <b>(C)</b> Publicized scientific <b>studies retracted from methodological errors or conflicts of interest can continue to strongly influence public perception of an issue even after they have disavowed the research
</b>.



The plural pronoun “they” is missing an antecedent in the sentence. “They” can’t logically refer to “studies,” “errors,” or “conflicts” since none of those things would “disavow” (deny support for) their research. Presumably, “they” is supposed to refer to the researchers who worked on the studies, as those would be the people who “have disavowed the research” (by retracting their studies). However, “researchers” or any similarly logical antecedent does not actually appear anywhere in the sentence. 


Additionally, it does not make sense to say “publicized scientific studies retracted <em>from</em> methodological errors or conflicts of interest,” as if the errors and conflicts had possession of the studies. Such a meaning is nonsensical.


> <b>(D)</b> Publicized scientific <b>studies, retracted as a result of methodological errors or conflicts of interest, can continue to strongly influence public perception of an issue even after researchers disavow it 
</b>.



The nonrestrictive past participial phrase in this choice, “retracted as a result of methodological errors or conflicts of interest,” conveys the illogical meaning that <em>all</em> “publicized scientific studies” are “retracted as a result of ….”


Of course, common sense tells us that many (if not most) publicized scientific studies are never retracted, neither for the reasons mentioned in the sentence nor for any other reason.


In this context, it would make sense to use a restrictive past participial phrase to modify the noun phrase “publicized scientific studies,” rather than use a nonrestrictive past participial phrase. In other words, the past participial phrase should not be set off by commas. A restrictive past participial phrase would logically specify that a certain category of “publicized scientific studies” is being discussed, those publicized studies that are retracted (as opposed to publicized studies in general).


Also, it’s unclear to what the singular pronoun “it” in “after researchers disavow <em>it</em>” is supposed to refer. Do researchers disavow “an issue”? “Public perception”? Given the overall context that the studies are retracted, it would seem most logical that the researchers disavow the studies. But of course, the singular pronoun “it” cannot refer to the plural noun “studies.” So, there is no clear and logical antecedent of “it” in the sentence.


> <b>(E)</b> Publicized scientific <b>studies that are retracted, resulting from methodological errors or conflicts of interest, can continue to strongly influence public perception of an issue even after being disavowed by researchers
</b>.



In this version, the present participial phrase “resulting from methodological errors or conflicts of interest” modifies “publicized scientific studies that are retracted.” As a result, this version communicates the illogical meaning that “publicized scientific studies” themselves are “resulting from methodological errors or conflicts of interest.”


Logically, retractions are what are “resulting from methodological errors or conflicts.” However, the noun “retractions” does not appear in this version, which instead uses the verb “retracted.”


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

##  Sentence Correction Example 41  

Because the atmospheric pressure on Mars is relatively low, liquid <u>water, considered by many scientists a component essential to the development of life forms, both complex ones and simple ones, is</u> not found on or near the surface of Mars in substantial quantities.

1. [x] (A) water, considered by many scientists a component essential to the development of life forms, both complex ones and simple ones, is
2. [ ] (B) water is considered by many scientists a component essential for the development of both complex and simple life forms but
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) water, which many scientists consider an essential component to the development of life forms that are both complex and simple, is
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) water many scientists consider to be a component essential to the development of life forms, both complex and simple, is
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) water considered by many scientists a component essential for the development of life forms, which are both complex and simple, is
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



Since the topic of the sentence is liquid water in general, not a specific type or category of liquid water, it makes sense to modify “liquid water” via the use of a nonrestrictive modifier.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A)</b> Because the atmospheric pressure on Mars is relatively low, liquid <b>water, considered by many scientists a component essential to the development of life forms, both complex ones and simple ones, is
</b> not found on or near the surface of Mars in substantial quantities.



In this version “liquid water” is modified via the use of a nonrestrictive past participial phrase, “considered by many …,” which provides additional information about liquid water without illogically restricting the water mentioned to a certain type.


Also, while the wording “life forms, both complex ones and simple ones” may seem wordy, the use of that wording results in the sentence’s clearly conveying that liquid water is considered essential to the development of multiple types of life forms.


(Notice, the use of “to” in some choices and “for” in other choices constitutes an insignificant split. Either of the prepositions is acceptable in the context in which they are used in this sentence.)


> <b>(B)</b> Because the atmospheric pressure on Mars is relatively low, liquid <b>water is considered by many scientists a component essential for the development of both complex and simple life forms but
</b> not found on or near the surface of Mars in substantial quantities.



This version conveys the illogical meaning that water is considered a component essential for the development of life forms because the atmospheric pressure on Mars is relatively low.


Also, it’s not clear whether the sentence is meant to convey that liquid water is considered a component but is considered not found or to convey that liquid water is considered a component but is not found.


> <b>(C)</b> Because the atmospheric pressure on Mars is relatively low, liquid <b>water, which many scientists consider an essential component to the development of life forms that are both complex and simple, is
</b> not found on or near the surface of Mars in substantial quantities.



The use of a nonrestrictive modifier beginning with “which” to modify “liquid water” makes sense and makes this version “sound right.” However, the wording within the modifier is not optimal.


Because, in this version, “component” is between “essential” and “to the development,” this version does not effectively convey that water is a “component essential to the development of life forms.” Rather, this version conveys that water is an “essential component” with making clear what the component is essential to.


Instead, this version conveys that water is an essential component “to the development of life forms,” as if to say that, according to the development of life forms, water is an essential component, as if the development of life forms has an opinion on the matter.


Also, the use of the wording “life forms that are both complex and simple” results in the sentence’s seeming to convey that water is essential to the development of certain types of life forms, life forms that are simultaneously complex and simple.


> <b>(D)</b> Because the atmospheric pressure on Mars is relatively low, liquid <b>water many scientists consider to be a component essential to the development of life forms, both complex and simple, is
</b> not found on or near the surface of Mars in substantial quantities.



Since there are no commas around the modifier “many scientists consider …,” the modifier is restrictive and illogically restricts the liquid water discussed in the sentence to a certain type, liquid water that many scientists consider a component essential to the development of life forms.


Also, “consider to be” is unidiomatic and does not make sense: the idiomatic version uses “consider” without “to be,” and, statements without “to be,” such as “they consider liquid water an essential component” make more sense than statements with “to be,” such as “they consider water to be an essential component.”


Also, what the modifier “both complex and simple” is meant to convey is not clear. Is it meant to convey that life forms are both complex and simple simultaneously? Is it meant to convey that water is necessary for the development life forms of two types, complex ones and simple ones?


> <b>(E)</b> Because the atmospheric pressure on Mars is relatively low, liquid <b>water considered by many scientists a component essential for the development of life forms, which are both complex and simple, is
</b> not found on or near the surface of Mars in substantial quantities.



While this version may seem correct, since there are no commas around the modifier “considered by many scientists …,” the modifier illogically restricts the liquid water discussed to only the liquid water considered by many scientists essential for the development of life forms, as if there are multiple types of liquid water, and as if “water considered by many scientists essential for the development of life forms” is a specific type of water.


Also, the wording “life forms, which are both complex and simple” does not convey a clear meaning, and seems to convey, for no reason clearly connected to what the rest of the sentence says, that life forms are simultaneously complex and simple.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)
