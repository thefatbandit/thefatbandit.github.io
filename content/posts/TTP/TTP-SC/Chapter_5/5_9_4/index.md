## Lesson Content
A present participial phrase can be used to restrictively modify a specific noun.

For example:

> The cat <strong>licking its paws</strong> is considering taking a nap.

Notice that the present participial phrase “licking its paws” serves to restrictively modify the noun “cat,” making clear to us that the sentence is not speaking of all cats; it’s speaking of a specific cat – the one licking its paws.

When a present participial phrase is used to restrictively modify a specific noun, the present participial phrase is placed adjacent to – usually after – the noun that it modifies, without a comma between the phrase and the noun:

> The woman <strong>sprinting in the soft sand</strong> has strong legs.

Of course, this sentence is not about all women; it’s about the one woman who is sprinting in the soft sand.

Let’s consider another sentence:

> The fish <strong>swimming in the stream</strong> made the water ripple.

The present participial phrase “swimming in the stream” modifies the noun “fish.” We can tell that the phrase is meant to modify the noun that immediately precedes it by noticing that there is no comma between “swimming in the stream” and the noun that it modifies, “fish.” The noun “fish” is restricted by the modifier “swimming in the stream.” The modifier defines which fish the sentence is about.

Let’s examine one more:

> The performers were given detailed instructions by the woman <strong>directing the production</strong>.

The present participial phrase “directing the production” modifies the noun “woman.” We can tell that the phrase is meant to modify the noun that immediately precedes it by noticing that there is no comma between “directing the production” and the noun that precedes it, “woman.” The participial phrase “directing the production” indicates that the woman mentioned in the sentence is a specific woman, the woman directing the production.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

We can tell that a present participial phrase modifies the noun that immediately precedes it by noticing that there is no comma between the phrase and the immediately preceding noun.
{{< /alert >}}

Now, incorrect sentences often contain present participial phrases that are misplaced and, thus, convey illogical meanings.

> The doctor carefully examined the patient’s knee<strong> wearing a long white coat</strong>.

The fact that the present participial phrase “wearing a long white coat” is not separated by a comma from the noun that precedes it, “knee,” indicates that the phrase modifies “knee.” Thus, the sentence conveys the illogical meaning that the patient’s knee was wearing a long white coat.

> The doctor <strong>wearing a long white coat</strong> carefully examined the patient’s knee.

We see that the present participial phrase “wearing a long white coat” is now correctly modifying the noun “doctor.”

Let’s examine another sentence:

> The engineers found cracks in the airplane’s wings <strong>inspecting the fleet</strong>.

The fact that the present participial phrase “inspecting the fleet” is not separated by a comma from the noun that precedes it, “wings,” indicates that the phrase modifies “wings.” Thus, the sentence conveys the illogical meaning that the airplane’s wings were inspecting the fleet.

A version that conveys a logical meaning is:

> The engineers <strong>inspecting the fleet</strong> found cracks in the airplane’s wings.

We see that the present participial phrase “inspecting the fleet” is now correctly modifying the noun “engineers.”

##  Sentence Correction Example 28  

<u>Arriving at Ellis Island during the early twentieth century, the percentage of the immigrants denied entry into the United States was small.</u>

1. [ ] (A) Arriving at Ellis Island during the early twentieth century, the percentage of immigrants denied entry into the United States was small.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) During the early twentieth century, only a small percentage of the immigrants were denied entry into the United States arriving at Ellis Island.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) During the early twentieth century arriving at Ellis Island, only a small percentage of the immigrants were denied entry into the United States.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) Arriving at Ellis Island, the United States denied entry to only a small percentage of the immigrants during the early twentieth century.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) During the early twentieth century, only a small percentage of the immigrants arriving at Ellis Island were denied entry into the United States.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



The key to correctly answering this question is noticing the effects of the placement of the participial phrase “arriving at Ellis Island.” In all versions but one, the phrase is misplaced.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Arriving at Ellis Island during the early twentieth century, the percentage of immigrants denied entry into the United States was small.
</b>


In this version, “arriving at Ellis Island” may seem to logically modify “immigrants.” However, this version is not exactly about immigrants. It’s about a percentage, “the percentage of immigrants denied entry.” So, what the phrase “arriving at Ellis Island” is really modifying is “the percentage of immigrants denied entry.” So, this version conveys the illogical meaning that “the percentage” was arriving at Ellis Island.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>During the early twentieth century, only a small percentage of the immigrants were denied entry into the United States arriving at Ellis Island.
</b>


The phrase “arriving at Ellis Island” is placed directly after “the United States” and is not separated from “the United States” by a comma. So, we can tell that the phrase modifies “the United States.” Thus, this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that the United States was arriving at Ellis Island.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>During the early twentieth century arriving at Ellis Island, only a small percentage of the immigrants were denied entry into the United States.
</b>


The phrase “arriving at Ellis Island” is placed directly after “early twentieth century” and is not separated from “century” by a comma. So, we can tell that the phrase modifies “early twentieth century.” Thus, this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that the twentieth century was arriving at Ellis Island.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>Arriving at Ellis Island, the United States denied entry to only a small percentage of the immigrants during the early twentieth century.
</b>


In this version, the opening modifier “arriving at Ellis Island” is placed so as to modify “the United States.” Thus, this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that the United States was arriving at Ellis Island.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E)</b> <b>During the early twentieth century, only a small percentage of the immigrants arriving at Ellis Island were denied entry into the United States.
</b>


In this version, the phrase “arriving at Ellis Island” is placed directly after “immigrants” and is not separated from “immigrants” by a comma. Thus, the phrase must modify “immigrants,” and therefore this version clearly indicates that it is describing the experience of immigrants arriving at Ellis Island.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Sentence Correction Example 29  

Over time, treasure hunters <u>have discovered gold and other precious metals that is located deep below the ocean’s surface using underwater sonar techniques</u>.

1. [ ] (A) have discovered gold and other precious metals that is located deep below the ocean’s surface using underwater sonar techniques
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) have discovered located deep below the surface of the ocean gold and other precious metals using underwater sonar techniques
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) using underwater sonar techniques and discovering gold and other precious metals located deep below the ocean’s surface
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) using underwater sonar techniques have discovered in locations deep beneath the surface of the ocean gold and other precious metals
5. [ ] (E) using underwater sonar techniques to discover gold and other precious metal located deep below the ocean’s surface
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Correctly answering this question requires noticing how the placement of participial phrases affects the meanings conveyed by the versions of the sentence. Remember that, when there is no comma separating a noun from a present participial phrase that follows the noun, that participial phrase directly modifies that noun.


> <b>(A)</b> Over time, treasure hunters <b>have discovered gold and other precious metals that is located deep below the ocean’s surface using underwater sonar techniques
</b>.



 The singular verb “is” in the relative clause “that is located deep below the ocean’s surface” does not agree in number with “gold and other precious metals,” which is the plural noun that the relative clause modifies. To be correct, the relative clause would need the plural verb “are” as in “gold and other precious metals that <em>are</em> located.”


Furthermore, there is no comma separating “ocean’s surface” and “using underwater sonar techniques.” So, the present participial phrase “using underwater sonar techniques” modifies “ocean’s surface.” As a result, this version conveys the illogical meaning that the ocean’s surface was using underwater sonar techniques.


> <b>(B)</b> Over time, treasure hunters <b>have discovered located deep below the surface of the ocean gold and other precious metals using underwater sonar techniques
</b>.



 There is no comma separating “gold and other precious metals” and “using underwater sonar techniques.” So, the present participial phrase “using underwater sonar techniques” modifies “gold and other precious metals.” As a result, this version conveys the illogical meaning that the gold and other precious metals were using underwater sonar techniques.


> <b>(C)</b> Over time, treasure hunters <b>using underwater sonar techniques and discovering gold and other precious metals located deep below the ocean’s surface
</b>.



 This version does not contain a main verb and, thus, is not a sentence.


The participles “using” and “discovering” are part of a present participial phrase restrictively modifying the noun “treasure hunters.” In other words, “using underwater sonar techniques and discovering gold and other precious metals located deep below the ocean’s surface” is one long restrictive modifier telling us which treasure hunters this version is about. The main verb of a sentence will never be found within a phrase modifying a noun.


If you have trouble seeing that this version is not a sentence, add in an action:


Over time, treasure hunters using underwater sonar techniques and discovering gold and other precious metals located deep below the ocean’s surface <strong>ate</strong> pizza.


Notice that we now have a sentence, the subject of which is “treasure hunters” and the main verb of which is “ate.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D)</b> Over time, treasure hunters <b>using underwater sonar techniques have discovered in locations deep beneath the surface of the ocean gold and other precious metals
</b>.



 Because there is no comma separating “treasure hunters” and “using underwater sonar techniques,” the participial phrase “using underwater sonar techniques” correctly modifies “treasure hunters.”


The sentence is grammatically correct and effectively conveys a logical meaning.


> <b>(E)</b> Over time, treasure hunters <b>using underwater sonar techniques to discover gold and other precious metal located deep below the ocean’s surface
</b>.



 This version does not contain a main verb and, thus, is not a sentence.


The participle “using” is part of a present participial phrase restrictively modifying the noun “treasure hunters.” In other words, “using underwater sonar techniques to discover gold and other precious metal located deep below the ocean’s surface” is one long restrictive modifier telling us which treasure hunters this version is about. The main verb of a sentence will never be found within a phrase modifying a noun.


If you have trouble seeing that this version is not a sentence, add in an action:


Over time, treasure hunters using underwater sonar techniques to discover gold and other precious metal located deep below the ocean’s surface<strong> played </strong>ping pong.


Notice that we now have a sentence, the subject of which is “treasure hunters” and the main verb of which is “played.”


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Sentence Correction Example 30  

Rhinoceros horn <u>purveyors prey on ignorant and sometimes desperate consumers selling them</u> this virtually useless material without special powers for exorbitant prices.

1. [ ] (A) purveyors prey on ignorant and sometimes desperate consumers selling them
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) purveyors preying on ignorant and sometimes desperate consumers sell
3. [ ] (C) purveyors, preying on ignorant and sometimes desperate consumers to sell
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) purveyors prey on ignorant and sometimes desperate consumers selling
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) purveyors, preying on ignorant and sometimes desperate consumers selling
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



A present participial phrase not separated from the preceding noun by a comma modifies the preceding noun.


Choosing the correct answer to this question requires noticing the meanings that result from participial phrases’ modifying nouns that directly precede the phrases and determining which meaning makes sense.


> <b>(A)</b> Rhinoceros horn <b>purveyors prey on ignorant and sometimes desperate consumers selling them
</b> this virtually useless material without special powers for exorbitant prices.



Since “selling” follows “consumers” and is not separated from “consumers” by a comma, the present participial phrase “selling them this virtually useless material without special powers for exorbitant prices” modifies “consumers.”


Thus, this version conveys the illogical meaning that purveyors prey on consumers who are selling virtually useless material for exorbitant prices. Of course, purveyors, not consumers, would be selling the virtually useless material.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B)</b> Rhinoceros horn <b>purveyors preying on ignorant and sometimes desperate consumers sell
</b> this virtually useless material without special powers for exorbitant prices.



In this version, the participial phrase “preying on ignorant and sometimes desperate consumers” clearly modifies “purveyors.”


As a result, this version conveys the logical meaning that rhinoceros horn purveyors sell virtually useless material without special powers.


> <b>(C)</b> Rhinoceros horn <b>purveyors, preying on ignorant and sometimes desperate consumers to sell
</b> this virtually useless material without special powers for exorbitant prices.



This version includes only a noun phrase, “rhinoceros horn purveyors,” followed by a participial phrase, “preying ….”


Since the participle “preying” cannot on its own serve as a main verb, this version contains no main verb and is not a complete sentence.


> <b>(D)</b> Rhinoceros horn <b>purveyors prey on ignorant and sometimes desperate consumers selling
</b> this virtually useless material without special powers for exorbitant prices.



Since “selling” follows “consumers” and is not separated from “consumers” by a comma, the present participial phrase “selling this virtually useless material without special powers for exorbitant prices” modifies “consumers.”


Thus, this version conveys the illogical meaning that purveyors prey on consumers who are selling virtually useless material for exorbitant prices. Of course, purveyors, not consumers, would be selling the virtually useless material.


Notice, this version is almost identical to the version created via the use of (A). The difference between the two choices – “them” is included in (A) but not in (D) – constitutes an insignificant split meant to confuse people analyzing the question.


> <b>(E)</b> Rhinoceros horn <b>purveyors, preying on ignorant and sometimes desperate consumers selling
</b> this virtually useless material without special powers for exorbitant prices.



This version includes only a noun phrase, “rhinoceros horn purveyors,” followed by a participial phrase, “preying ….” Thus, since the participle “preying” cannot on its own serve as the main verb of a sentence, this version contains no main verb and is not a complete sentence.


Also, since “selling” follows “consumers” and is not separated from “consumers” by a comma, the present participial phrase “selling this virtually useless material without special powers for exorbitant prices” modifies “consumers.”


Thus, this version conveys the illogical meaning that the purveyors prey on consumers who are selling virtually useless material for exorbitant prices. Of course, purveyors, not consumers, would be selling the virtually useless material.


Notice, this choice makes choice (D), which is similar to this choice, seem correct by comparison. We have to be careful not to be fooled into choosing an illogical choice by the fact that a similar choice is more clearly incorrect.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

Now, let’s move on to the second category of present participial phrases.
