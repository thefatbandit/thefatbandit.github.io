## Lesson Content
A present participial phrase that appears at the beginning or in the middle of a clause and is set off from the rest of the clause by a comma or commas can be treated as a nonrestrictive noun modifier.

> <strong>Sailing with the wind,</strong> the clipper ship quickly reached its destination.

The present participial phrase “sailing with the wind” modifies the noun “clipper ship.” It also explains how the clipper ship reached its destination.

All the same, for the purpose of evaluating their placement, we can treat phrases like this one as noun modifiers.

The same phrase could be placed in the middle of the clause instead.

> The clipper ship<strong>, sailing with the wind, </strong>quickly reached its destination.

Notice that, in both of the above examples, the participial phrase “sailing with the wind” is set off from the rest of the sentence by a comma or commas.

> The clipper ship<strong><s>, sailing with the wind,</s> </strong>quickly reached its destination.

Let’s revisit the “fish swimming in the stream” example, adding commas around the participial phrase this time.

> The fish<strong>, swimming in the stream,</strong> made the water ripple.

Unlike in the earlier version of this example, in which there were no commas around the phrase “swimming in the stream,” in this version, the phrase is set off by commas.

Now, rather than tell us which fish the sentence is about, the phrase tells us what the fish were doing as they made the water ripple.

The same phrase would work also at the beginning of the sentence.

> <strong>Swimming in the stream</strong>, the fish made the water ripple.

Let’s consider another example of this type of use of a present participial phrase.

> <strong>Hissing loudly,</strong> two cats faced off in the darkness.

> Two cats<strong>, hissing loudly, </strong>faced off in the darkness.

We can tell that the participial phrase, “hissing loudly” is nonrestrictive, because commas separate it from the rest of the sentence.

💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

For the purpose of evaluating placement, a present participial phrase that appears at the beginning or in the middle of a clause and is set off from the rest of the clause by a comma or commas can be treated as a nonrestrictive noun modifier.

### Targeted Practice Solutions

<u>Misplaced Modifiers Involving Non-Restrictive Present Participial Phrases </u>

A present participial phrase that appears at the beginning or in the middle of a clause and is set off from the rest of the clause by a comma or commas will often be misplaced and, thus, modify the wrong noun.

Let’s look at an example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Crying loudly for the entire overnight flight, the other passengers were extremely annoyed by the infant, cute as she was in her pink pajamas.

Notice that the present participial phrase “crying loudly for the entire overnight flight” is misplaced and illogically modifies the noun “the other passengers.” As a result, this sentence conveys the illogical meaning that it was the “other passengers” who were crying loudly.

In order for the sentence to convey a logical meaning, “the infant” must appear at the beginning of the clause, right after the introductory phrase “crying loudly …,” as in:

> Crying loudly for the entire overnight flight, <strong>the infant</strong>, cute as she was in her pink pajamas, was extremely annoying to the other passengers.

Let’s consider another example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Wafting through the air, some bears were attracted by the aroma of the cooking food.

We can see that the present participial phrase “wafting through the air” illogically modifies the noun “some bears.” Of course, “some bears” were not wafting through the air.

Wafting through the air, <strong>the aroma of the cooking food </strong>attracted some bears.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Be on the lookout for an introductory phrase that begins with a present participle; we may have a misplaced modifier question on our hands. The noun that follows an introductory phrase that begins with a present participle must be the noun that that phrase is meant to modify.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 31  

<u>Practicing daily, the aspiring singer’s skills were able to be improved and, thus, won</u> a spot in the major singing competition.

1. [ ] (A) Practicing daily, the aspiring singer’s skills were able to be improved and, thus, won
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) Practicing daily, the aspiring singer, being able to improve his skills, thus winning
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) Through daily practice, the aspiring singer’s skills were able to be improved and, thus, won
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) The aspiring singer, practicing daily, was able to improve his skills and, thus, won
5. [ ] (E) The aspiring singer was able, by improving his skills practicing daily, to thus win
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Arriving at the correct answer to this question takes noticing whether present participial phrases are placed so as to modify nouns that they would logically modify.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Practicing daily, the aspiring singer’s skills were able to be improved and, thus, won
</b> a spot in the major singing competition.



Since the opening present participial phrase “practicing daily” is followed by “the aspiring singer’s skills,” “practicing daily” illogically modifies “skills.” Of course, the singer’s skills would not have been “practicing daily.”


Also, “were able to” means “had the knowledge, skill, or ability to achieve something.” So, in saying, “the aspiring singer’s skills <em>were able to</em> be improved,” this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that the singer’s skills had the knowledge, skill, or ability to achieve being improved. Of course, the singer’s skills would not have these attributes. The singer himself would.


Finally, notice that the sentence says, “the singer’s skills were able … and, thus, won a spot,” conveying the nonsensical meaning that the singer’s skills, rather than the singer himself, won a spot in the competition.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>Practicing daily, the aspiring singer, being able to improve his skills, thus winning
</b> a spot in the major singing competition.



Since the participles “practicing,” “being,” and “winning” cannot serve as main verbs of a sentence, this version has no main verb and is not a complete sentence.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>Through daily practice, the aspiring singer’s skills were able to be improved and, thus, won
</b> a spot in the major singing competition.



“Were able to” means “had the knowledge, skill, or ability to achieve something.” So, in saying, “the aspiring singer’s skills <em>were able to</em> be improved,” this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that the singer’s skills had the knowledge, skill, or ability to achieve being improved. Of course, the singer’s skills would not have these attributes. The singer himself would.


Also, notice that the sentence says, “the singer’s skills were able … and, thus, won a spot,” conveying the nonsensical meaning that the singer’s skills, rather than the singer himself, won a spot in the competition.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D)</b> <b>The aspiring singer, practicing daily, was able to improve his skills and, thus, won
</b> a spot in the major singing competition.



In this version, the present participial phrase “practicing daily” is placed just after “aspiring singer” and clearly modifies “aspiring singer.” Of course, this placement make sense since an aspiring singer would practice daily.


This version is grammatically correct and conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>The aspiring singer was able, by improving his skills practicing daily, to thus win
</b> a spot in the major singing competition.



In this version, the present participial phrase “practicing daily” is placed just after “skills” and is not separated from “skills” by a comma. Therefore, the phrase seems to modify “skills.” Of course, skills would not practice daily.


Also, “by” and “thus” have similar functions in a sentence. So, using “thus” with “by” is redundant and does not make sense. The sentence should use one or the other as in:


The aspiring singer was able, by improving his skills practicing daily, to <s>thus</s> win a spot in the major singing competition.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Sentence Correction Example 32  

<u>Vegan athletes excel in sports from bodybuilding to sprinting that avoid experiencing the health issues attributable to consuming meat and animal products.</u>

1. [ ] (A) Vegan athletes excel in sports from bodybuilding to sprinting that avoid experiencing the health issues attributable to consuming meat and animal products.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) In sports from bodybuilding to sprinting, vegan athletes excel while avoiding experiencing the health issues attributable to consuming meat and animal products.
3. [ ] (C) While avoiding experiencing the health issues attributable to consuming meat and animal products, a vegan diet allows athletes to excel in sports from bodybuilding to sprinting.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) Bodybuilding and sprinting, a vegan diet allows athletes to excel in sports while avoiding experiencing the health issues attributable to consuming meat and animal products.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) Avoiding experiencing the health issues attributable to consuming meat and animal products, the diets of vegan athletes allow them to excel in sports from bodybuilding to sprinting.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



There are many misplaced modifiers in the answer choices in this question. So, correctly answering it takes finding a choice in which modifiers are placed in ways such that the resulting sentence conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Vegan athletes excel in sports from bodybuilding to sprinting that avoid experiencing the health issues attributable to consuming meat and animal products.
</b>


 The placement of the modifier “that avoid experiencing …” causes it to illogically modify “sports from bodybuilding to sprinting.” The resulting sentence conveys the nonsensical meaning that sports from bodybuilding to sprinting avoid experiencing health issues.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B)</b> <b>In sports from bodybuilding to sprinting, vegan athletes excel while avoiding experiencing the health issues attributable to consuming meat and animal products.
</b>


 The opening prepositional phrase, “in sports from bodybuilding to sprinting,” is logically followed by “vegan athletes excel,” and the remainder of the sentence makes sense.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>While avoiding experiencing the health issues attributable to consuming meat and animal products, a vegan diet allows athletes to excel in sports from bodybuilding to sprinting.
</b>


The opening phrase, “While avoiding experiencing health issues …,”<em> </em>illogically modifies “a vegan diet allows.” A vegan diet would not avoid experiencing health issues.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>Bodybuilding and sprinting, a vegan diet allows athletes to excel in sports while avoiding experiencing the health issues attributable to consuming meat and animal products.
</b>


Since the opening participial modifier, “bodybuilding and sprinting,” must modify the subject of the clause that follows the modifier, “a vegan diet,” this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that a vegan diet is bodybuilding and sprinting.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>Avoiding experiencing the health issues attributable to consuming meat and animal products, the diets of vegan athletes allow them to excel in sports from bodybuilding to sprinting.
</b>


Since the opening participial modifier, “avoiding experiencing the health issues …,” must modify the subject of the clause that follows the modifier “the diets of vegan athletes,” this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that vegan diets are avoiding experiencing health issues.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

##  Sentence Correction Example 33  

<u>Having demonstrated that people can change gene expression by meditating, it is the head researcher’s opinion that the study will likely have a significant impact on the field of epigenetics.</u>

1. [ ] (A) Having demonstrated that people can change gene expression by meditating, it is the head researcher’s opinion that the study will likely have a significant impact on the field of epigenetics.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) The head researcher’s opinion is that, since the study has demonstrated that people can change gene expression by meditating, its having a significant impact on the field of epigenetics is likely.
3. [ ] (C) Having demonstrated that people can change gene expression by meditating, there is a likelihood that the study will have a significant impact on the field of epigenetics in the head researcher’s opinion.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) The head researcher’s opinion, having demonstrated that people can change gene expression by meditating, is that it is likely that the study will have a significant impact on the field of epigenetics.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) The study, having demonstrated that people can change gene expression by meditating, has a likelihood that will have a significant impact on the field of epigenetics in the opinion of the head researcher.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



Correctly answering this question requires paying careful attention to introductory participial phrases and what those phrases seem to be modifying in order to eliminate choices in which the phrases are so placed as to modify things that they do not logically modify. At the same time, since not all incorrect choices include misplaced participial phrases, we have to be careful not to become so focused on participial phrase placement that we miss other issues.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Having demonstrated that people can change gene expression by meditating, it is the head researcher’s opinion that the study will likely have a significant impact on the field of epigenetics.
</b>


The sentence begins with the present participial phrase “Having demonstrated that people can change gene expression by meditating.” The noun or pronoun that follows this opening modifier must be a noun that is capable of “having demonstrated something.” We see that the pronoun “it” directly follows the opening modifier, but the pronoun “it” is not a noun or pronoun that is capable of “having demonstrated something.” Therefore, we have a misplaced introductory modifier, and this sentence is incorrect; the participial phrase is misplaced.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B)</b> <b>The head researcher’s opinion is that, since the study has demonstrated that people can change gene expression by meditating, its having a significant impact on the field of epigenetics is likely.
</b>


This choice is a bit tricky because, unlike many of the other choices, it uses a dependent clause, since the study has demonstrated …,” rather than a participial phrase. So, if we are focused on modifier placement issues, we might mistakenly perceive this choice as another one similar to (A), in which an introductory modifier illogically modifies “it.” However, if we read the sentence carefully, we see that it is well constructed and conveys a meaning that makes sense.


Notice that the last clause in this version is written to “sound funny,” and therefore seem incorrect, as a result of the last clause’s having as its subject the gerund phrase “its having a significant impact on the field of epigenetics.”


> <b>(C)</b> <b>Having demonstrated that people can change gene expression by meditating, there is a likelihood that the study will have a significant impact on the field of epigenetics in the head researcher’s opinion.
</b>


The participial phrase “having demonstrated …” is meant to modify the clause that follows it. However, the clause uses a delayed subject, and so, what appears right after the participial is the expletive “there.” Since “there” certainly would not have demonstrated something, it does not make sense for the “having demonstrated …” to modify “there.” So, it’s clear that the opening participial phrase is misplaced.


Also, “having demonstrated …, there is a likelihood that the study will have a significant impact” is illogical in another way as well. The sentence does not say that it is likely that the study will have a significant impact. Rather it conveys that, since something demonstrated that people can affect gene expression by meditating, a likelihood that the study will have a significant impact exists. Expressing this idea is absurd since, under all conditions, a likelihood that the study will have a significant impact exists. That likelihood may be anything from 0 percent to 100 percent, but it will always exist. So, expressing that it will exist under certain conditions, “having demonstrated,” does not make sense.


Finally, because the prepositional phrase “in the head researcher’s opinion” appears directly after “the field of epigenetics,” the prepositional phrase seems to modify “the field of epigenetics.” As a result, this version suggests the nonsensical idea that the field of epigenetics is located in the head researcher’s opinion.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>The head researcher’s opinion, having demonstrated that people can change gene expression by meditating, is that it is likely that the study will have a significant impact on the field of epigenetics.
</b>


 Because “having demonstrated that people can change gene expression by meditating” directly follows “the head researcher’s opinion,” this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that the head researcher’s opinion has demonstrated that people can change gene expression by meditating. The head researcher’s opinion might be that <em>the study</em> has demonstrated that people can change gene expression by meditating, but the opinion itself would have not demonstrated that.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E)</b> <b>The study, having demonstrated that people can change gene expression by meditating, has a likelihood that will have a significant impact on the field of epigenetics in the opinion of the head researcher.
</b>


In this version, the participial phrase “having demonstrated …” is placed so as to logically modify “the study.” However, even though that modifier placement is logical, we have to be careful not to choose this version, because what this version expresses does not make sense.


This version does not say that the study is likely to have a significant impact. Instead, it conveys the nonsensical meaning that the study “has a likelihood that will have a significant impact.” Of course, “a likelihood” would not “have a significant impact on the field of epigenetics.”


Furthermore, because the prepositional phrase “in the opinion of the head researcher” appears directly after “the field of epigenetics,” the prepositional phrase seems to modify “the field of epigenetics.” As a result, this version suggests the nonsensical idea that the field of epigenetics is located in the opinion of the head researcher.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

Let’s move on to the third category of present participial phrases.
