## Lesson Content
When a present participial phrase comes at the end of a clause and is set off from that clause by a comma, the participial phrase does not have to modify the noun closest to it.

For example:

> The horses ran across the field, <strong>kicking up dirt with their hooves</strong>.

Notice that the present participial phrase “kicking up dirt with their hooves” is separated from the clause by a comma.

Rather the phrase modifies the entire clause that precedes it, and the subject of the clause, “horses,” is the agent of the participle, “kicking,” that begins the phrase.

So, the sentence conveys that the horses ran and that, as the horses ran, they were kicking up dirt.

Let’s consider another example:

> Bees fly from flower to flower, <strong>making a soft buzzing sound</strong>.

We can see that the present participial phrase “making a soft buzzing sound” is set off from the clause that precedes it by a comma.

Also, we can see that the subject of the clause, “bees,” is the agent of the participle “making.”

Thus, the sentence conveys that bees fly from flower to flower and that, as the bees fly from flower to flower, they make a soft buzzing sound.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A present participial phrase can be used to modify an entire preceding clause, in which case the phrase is set off from the preceding clause by a comma and normally has as its agent the subject of the clause that it modifies.
{{< /alert >}}

In the above examples, the participle in the closing participial phrase has as its agent the subject of the preceding clause, and normally, closing present participial phrases work in that way.

One exception occurs in sentences that use “including.” Often, when a closing participial phrase begins with “including,” “including” does not take the subject of the preceding clause as its agent.

> Ringo Starr sang some songs that are still widely played today, <strong>including “Yellow Submarine.”</strong>

The meaning expressed by this example is not that Ringo Starr included “Yellow Submarine” as he sang.

A second exception occurs when the closing participial phrase modifies an actor-action pair that appears between the subject and the closing participial phrase.

> The video was of a man picking apples, <strong>singing as he worked</strong>.

In this case, we see that an actor, “a man,” engaging in an action, “picking apples,” appears between the subject of the sentence and the closing participial phrase “singing as he worked.”

Of course, the man picking apples, not the video, would have been the one “singing as he worked.”

So, we can tell that the closing participial phrase “singing as he worked” modifies the actor-action pair “a man” and “picking apples,” and that “a man,” rather than “the video,” is the agent of “singing.”

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Normally, the agent of the participle in a closing participial phrase is the subject of the preceding clause. However, there are some exceptions.
{{< /alert >}}

OK, now that we have covered the exceptions, let’s continue discussing what closing present participial phrases that modify entire clauses normally do.

Closing present participial phrases often describe:

> • the results of the actions mentioned in the preceding clauses.

> • the causes of the events described in the preceding clauses.

> • events that are related to the events described in the preceding clause and were occurring while the events described in the preceding clause were occurring.

Here’s an example in which a closing present participial phrase describes the results of the actions mentioned in the preceding clause:

> The strong winds blew apart structures and pushed ocean water into the town, <strong>turning the buildings on Main Street into piles of debris</strong>.

In the above example, the participial phrase “turning the buildings on Main Street into piles of debris” doesn’t modify “town.” It modifies the entire clause that precedes it, describing the results of the actions described by the clause.

Here’s an example in which a closing present participial phrase describes the cause of the events described by the preceding clause.

> The dolphins suddenly changed course, <strong>having sensed our presence in the water</strong>.

In this example, the participial phrase “having sensed our presence in the water” has as its agent the subject of the clause, “dolphins,” and explains why the dolphins changed course.

In the next example, a closing present participial phrase describes events that are related to the events described in the preceding clause and were occurring while the events described in the preceding clause were occurring.

> The reporter ran across the field, <strong>carrying a camera in one hand and holding her phone to her ear with the other</strong>.

In this example, the present participial phrase “carrying a camera … and holding her phone to her ear …” has as its agent the subject of the clause, “the reporter,” and describes what the reporter was doing as she ran across the field.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Present participial phrases that modify entire clauses often describe the results of the actions mentioned in the preceding clauses, the causes of the events described in the preceding clauses, or events that were occurring while the events described in the preceding clause were occurring.
{{< /alert >}}

<u>A Present Participial Phrase Located at the End of a Clause Can be Misplaced. </u>

A small change to sentence structure, the removal of a comma, can render a present participial phrase at the end of a clause misplaced.

Consider the following sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The horses ran across the field <strong>kicking up dirt with their hooves</strong>.

Notice that without the comma between “field” and “kicking,” the present participial phrase is now misplaced; it’s modifying the noun “field,” conveying an illogical meaning.

> The horses ran across the field<strong>,</strong> <strong>kicking up dirt with their hooves</strong>.

Notice that, in the correct version, the present participial phrase is separated from the clause by a comma.

Let’s examine another sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The strong winds blew apart structures and pushed ocean water into the town <strong>turning the buildings on Main Street into piles of debris</strong>.

Notice that without the comma between “town” and “turning,” the present participial phrase is now misplaced; it’s modifying the noun “town,” conveying an illogical meaning.

As we saw above, the following is correct:

> The strong winds blew apart structures and pushed ocean water into the town<strong>,</strong> <strong>turning the buildings on Main Street into piles of debris</strong>.

Notice that, in the correct version, the present participial phrase is separated from the clause by a comma.

Let’s examine another sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The dolphins suddenly changed course <strong>having sensed our presence in the water</strong>.

Notice that without the comma between “course” and “having,” the present participial phrase is now misplaced; it’s modifying the noun “course,” conveying an illogical meaning.

As we saw above, the following is correct:

> The dolphins suddenly changed course<strong>, </strong><strong>having sensed our presence in the water</strong>.

Notice that, in the correct version, the present participial phrase is separated from the clause by a comma.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A small change to sentence structure, the removal of a comma, can render a present participial phrase at the end a clause misplaced. Be on the lookout for misplaced modifiers.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 34  

According to the research, a particular meditation technique termed “open <u>monitoring,” it strengthens a neural signal associated with recognition of errors potentially improving the ability of people</u> to detect and respond to mistakes.

1. [ ] (A) monitoring,” it strengthens a neural signal associated with recognition of errors potentially improving the ability of people
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) monitoring” strengthens a neural signal associated with error recognition, which is potentially improvement in people’s ability
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) monitoring” strengthens a neural signal associated with error recognition, this has the potential to improve the ability of people
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) monitoring,” strengthening a neural signal associated with recognition of errors, potentially improving people’s ability
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) monitoring” strengthens a neural signal associated with error recognition, potentially improving people’s ability

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A)</b> According to the research, a particular meditation technique termed “open <b>monitoring,” it strengthens a neural signal associated with recognition of errors potentially improving the ability of people
</b> to detect and respond to mistakes.



Because there is no comma between the noun “errors” and the present participial phrase “potentially improving the ability of people,” the phrase appears to modify “errors.” Thus, this version seems to convey the illogical meaning that “errors” are “potentially improving the ability of people to detect and respond to mistakes.”


Of course, it does not make sense that the “errors” are “potentially improving the ability of people” to detect mistakes. A logical meaning would be that “open monitoring” improves people’s ability (by strengthening the neural signal). Thus, there should be a comma separating the closing participial phrase from the preceding clause, so that the phrase modifies the subject of the clause, “open monitoring,” not “errors.”


Additionally, the pronoun “it” is an unnecessary repetition of the subject of the sentence. In this context, “it” refers to the noun phrase “a particular meditation technique termed ‘open monitoring.’” Thus, this choice creates a sentence that repeats the subject twice in a row, saying essentially, “‘open monitoring,’ ‘open monitoring’ strengthens.”


> <b>(B)</b> According to the research, a particular meditation technique termed “open <b>monitoring” strengthens a neural signal associated with error recognition, which is potentially improvement in people’s ability
</b> to detect and respond to mistakes.



 In this version, there is nothing to which the pronoun “which” can logically refer. After all, nothing that precedes “which” can logically be described as “improvement in people’s ability to detect and respond to mistakes.”


While “open monitoring” may <em>cause</em> improvement in people’s ability to detect and respond to mistakes by strengthening a neural signal, the fact that “open monitoring” strengthens a neural signal is not itself “improvement in people’s ability to detect and respond to mistakes.” Also, neither “a neural signal” nor “error recognition” can logically be described as “improvement in people’s ability.”


> <b>(C)</b> According to the research, a particular meditation technique termed “open <b>monitoring” strengthens a neural signal associated with error recognition, this has the potential to improve the ability of people
</b> to detect and respond to mistakes.



This choice incorrectly joins two independent clauses with a comma alone. 


“A particular meditation technique termed “open monitoring” strengthens a neural signal associated with error recognition” is an independent clause; it stands alone as a complete thought.


Likewise, “this has the potential to improve the ability of people to detect and respond to mistakes” is an independent clause.


We need either a semicolon or a comma and coordinating conjunction (such as “and”) to join two independent clauses.


Additionally, what the pronoun “this” refers to is not clear, but “this” seems to illogically refer to the preceding clause. As a result, the sentence seems to communicate the illogical meaning that the fact that “opening monitoring” strengthens a neural signal has the potential to improve the ability of people to detect and respond to mistakes. A fact would not improve people’s ability to do something.


> <b>(D)</b> According to the research, a particular meditation technique termed “open <b>monitoring,” strengthening a neural signal associated with recognition of errors, potentially improving people’s ability
</b> to detect and respond to mistakes.



 This choice creates an incomplete sentence. Notice that neither “strengthening” nor “improving” is a finite verb, and thus neither can act as the main verb of the sentence. 


Likewise, “to detect and respond” are infinitive verbs (the second “to” before “respond” is implied), and thus they cannot be main verbs.


Without a main verb in the sentence, what we have is nothing more than a very long description of the meditation technique, not a complete sentence. 


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E)</b> According to the research, a particular meditation technique termed “open <b>monitoring” strengthens a neural signal associated with error recognition, potentially improving people’s ability
</b> to detect and respond to mistakes.



In this choice, the present participial phrase “potentially improving people’s ability” is separated from the preceding clause by a comma. Thus, the participial phrase modifies “open monitoring,” which logically is the thing that “strengthens a neural signal” and is therefore “potentially improving people’s ability.”


The sentence created makes sense and contains no grammatical errors.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

The stylish car <u>does not have a windscreen, but a recessed instrument panel acts as an aerodynamic windbreaker for the driver,</u> sitting in the cockpit.

1. [ ] (A) does not have a windscreen, but a recessed instrument panel acts as an aerodynamic windbreaker for the driver,

3. [x] (C) has not a windscreen but a recessed instrument panel that acts as an aerodynamic windbreaker for the driver
4. [ ] (D) does not have a windscreen, but a recessed instrument panel acts as an aerodynamic windbreaker with the driver
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) does not have a windscreen, but a recessed instrument panel acts as if there is an aerodynamic windbreaker as the driver is
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>

##### Question Level: hard



In this question, the correct version is structured in an unfamiliar way, so the correct version sounds incorrect. So, correctly answering this question requires carefully considering how participial phrases are used in the choices and discerning the meanings conveyed by the various versions. Without careful consideration of meaning, one might be tempted to choose a choice the use of which results in a version that sounds basically correct but does not convey a truly logical meaning.


> <b>(A)</b> The stylish car <b>does not have a windscreen, but a recessed instrument panel acts as an aerodynamic windbreaker for the driver,
</b> sitting in the cockpit.



In this version, the comma before the closing participial phrase “sitting in the cockpit” indicates that the phrase modifies the entire preceding clause and that the phrase has the subject of the clause, “recessed instrument panel,” as its agent. Thus, this version conveys the illogical meaning that the instrument panel sits in the cockpit.


> <b>(B)</b> The stylish car <b>has not a windscreen but a recessed instrument panel acting as an aerodynamic windbreaker for the driver while
</b> sitting in the cockpit.



In this version, the phrase “while sitting in the cockpit” is not a noun modifier, and it does not modify “driver.” Rather, the phrase works adverbially to modify “acting,” indicating that the instrument panel is acting while sitting in the cockpit. Thus, this version suggests the illogical idea that the instrument panel is sitting in the cockpit.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> The stylish car <b>has not a windscreen but a recessed instrument panel that acts as an aerodynamic windbreaker for the driver
</b> sitting in the cockpit.



In this version, the fact that there is no comma between “driver” and “sitting in the cockpit” indicates that “sitting in the cockpit” modifies “driver.” So, while this version may sound a little strange, it clearly and concisely conveys a logical meaning.


Also, this version is better than any of the others because of the following.


Unlike the versions created via the use of (A), (D), and (E), this version clearly conveys that the car has the instrument panel. The versions created via the use of (A), (D), and (E) convey that “a recessed instrument panel acts” but do not clearly indicate that the instrument panel is part of the car.


Also, “that acts …” in this version conveys that the instrument panel acts as a windbreaker for the driver more effectively than does “acting as …” in (B). The phrase in (B) seems to convey that the instrument panel is always acting as a windbreaker, even when the car is not moving, while the phrase in (A) simply conveys that the instrument panel acts as a windbreaker, presumably when the car is moving.


> <b>(D)</b> The stylish car <b>does not have a windscreen, but a recessed instrument panel acts as an aerodynamic windbreaker with the driver
</b> sitting in the cockpit.



This choice could be tempting, as the version created via the use of this choice starts off well. However, the wording “with the driver sitting in the cockpit” conveys a meaning that does not make sense, that the instrument panel is somehow <em>with </em>the driver sitting in the cockpit.


> <b>(E)</b> The stylish car <b>does not have a windscreen, but a recessed instrument panel acts as if there is an aerodynamic windbreaker as the driver is
</b> sitting in the cockpit.



This choice could be tempting, as the version created via the use of this choice starts off well. However, the wording “<em>acts as if</em> there is an aerodynamic windbreaker” conveys the nonsensical meaning that the instrument panel is actively doing something along the lines of pretending that there is a windbreaker.


Also, the wording “as the driver is sitting in the cockpit” conveys an ambiguous meaning, which could be that the instrument panel acts as a windbreaker while the driver is in the act of seating himself or herself.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

Now, let’s take our discussion of present participial phrases to a higher level of sophistication by going beyond the effects of placement to the overall logic and coherence of sentences.
