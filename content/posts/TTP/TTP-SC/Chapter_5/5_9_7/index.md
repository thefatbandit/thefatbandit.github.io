## Lesson Content
Present participial phrases always indicate simultaneity.

Consider the following sentence:

> <strong>Using fresh ingredients</strong>, the chef prepared the meal.

Notice that these two things happen at the same time.

However, notice what happens in the following two sentences:

> <u>Incorrect</u>:<strong> Using fresh ingredients</strong>, the chef looked for a recipe for the meal.

> <u>Incorrect</u>:<strong> Using fresh ingredients</strong>, the chef served the meal on white china.

Neither of these sentences is logical because both of them use present participial phrases to describe events that would not possibly have occurred simultaneously with the events described by the main clauses.

In the case of the first sentence, what the participial phrase describes, using fresh ingredients, would have occurred <em>after</em> what the main clause describes, looking for a recipe.

In the case of the second sentence, what the participial phrase describes, using fresh ingredients, would have occurred <em>before</em> what the main clause describes, serving the meal.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Participial phrases always indicate simultaneity; the event described by a participle phrase must occur at the same time as the event described by the clause that the phrase modifies.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Working hard for hours, the crew took a break.

At first, this example might seem to make sense, as it makes sense that a crew would work hard and take a break.

However, since the present participial phrase “working hard for hours” indicates simultaneity, what the above sentence actually conveys is that the crew worked and took a break at the same time.

Of course, those two events could not possibly have occurred at the same time.

> After working hard for hours, the crew took a break.

> The crew took a break after working hard for hours.

In general, when a writer utilizes a present participial phrase in discussing events that do not occur at the same time, he or she creates an illogical sentence.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When a writer utilizes a present participial phrase in discussing events that do not occur at the same time, he or she creates an illogical sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

Here’s another example.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The lively raccoon washed the food, eating it quickly.

This sentence may seem fine at first.

Notice, however, that the participial phrase “eating it quickly” doesn’t work with the rest of the sentence.

Both of the following sentences are potential corrections:

> The lively raccoon washed the food and then ate it quickly.

> Having washed the food, the lively raccoon ate it quickly.

Now, sometimes when a sentence uses a present participial phrase to modify a clause, the events discussed are simultaneous, but using the participial phrase to modify the clause still results in an illogical sentence.

Consider the following example.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The famous actress took a break from filming, knocking a sacred rock from where it had been placed and almost killing a sound technician.

Notice that the action described by the main clause and the action described by the present participial phrase could occur at the same time (she took a break and knocked the rock from where it had been placed).

However, the sentence as a whole is illogical.

In order for the sentence to be logical, it would have to be structured in a different way such as:

> While the famous actress was taking a break from filming, she knocked a sacred rock from where it had been placed and almost killed a sound technician.

The above version conveys that the two events “taking a break” and “knocked a sacred rock” occurred at the same time without connecting them in an illogical way.

What we’re seeing is that it’s not enough for a present participial phrase to logically modify a noun that appears in a sentence.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

It’s not enough for a present participial phrase to logically modify a noun that appears in a sentence. In order for a sentence to make sense, any participial phrase that appears in that sentence has to work logically with the rest of the sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 36  

Former Cuban president Fidel <u>Castro masterminded the creation of the famous Cohiba cigar, suddenly quitting smoking for health reasons, at age 59</u>.

1. [ ] (A) Castro masterminded the creation of the famous Cohiba cigar, suddenly quitting smoking for health reasons, at age 59
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) Castro, masterminding the creation of the famous Cohiba cigar, and then suddenly, at age 59, quit smoking for health reasons
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) Castro, who had masterminded the creation of the famous Cohiba cigar, suddenly quit smoking, at age 59, for health reasons
4. [ ] (D) Castro, who, having masterminded the creation of the famous Cohiba cigar, suddenly quit smoking, at age 59, for health reasons
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) Castro suddenly quit smoking for health reasons, at age 59, masterminding the creation of the famous Cohiba cigar
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Each version of this sentence contains a present participial phrase, a relative clause, or both a present participial phrase and a relative clause.


Since the verbs contained in participial phrases and relative clauses cannot serve as the main verbs of sentences, two versions are missing main verbs.


Choosing the correct answer from the three remaining choices requires noticing that, while the three remaining versions are complete sentences, only one of them conveys a logical meaning.


> <b>(A)</b> Former Cuban president Fidel <b>Castro masterminded the creation of the famous Cohiba cigar, suddenly quitting smoking for health reasons, at age 59
</b>.



This version is a complete sentence. However, the meaning conveyed by this version does not make sense.


The placement of the present participial phrase “suddenly quitting smoking …” indicates that the phrase modifies the entire preceding clause. Thus, since the events “masterminded the creation of the famous Cohiba cigar” and “suddenly quitting smoking” likely did not happen simultaneously and are not logically connected, the sentence does not make sense.


> <b>(B)</b> Former Cuban president Fidel <b>Castro, masterminding the creation of the famous Cohiba cigar, and then suddenly, at age 59, quit smoking for health reasons
</b>.



In this version, the participial modifier “masterminding the creation” is illogically connected by “, and” with the finite verb “quit.” We can more easily see this issue if we simplify the sentence.


Fidel Castro, masterminding the … cigar …, and then … quit smoking.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> Former Cuban president Fidel <b>Castro, who had masterminded the creation of the famous Cohiba cigar, suddenly quit smoking, at age 59, for health reasons
</b>.



 In this version, the modifier, “who had masterminded the creation of the famous Cohiba cigar,” adds information to the sentence in a logical way.


So, the sentence effectively conveys that Castro suddenly quit smoking at age 59 and provides some additional information, that Castro had masterminded the creation of the famous Cohiba cigar.


> <b>(D)</b> Former Cuban president Fidel <b>Castro, who, having masterminded the creation of the famous Cohiba cigar, suddenly quit smoking, at age 59, for health reasons
</b>.



The two verb forms in this version, “having masterminded” and “quit,” are parts of modifiers. “Having masterminded” modifies “who,” and “quit” is part of the modifier “who quit ….” Thus, this version is composed of only the noun phrase, “former Cuban president Fidel Castro,” and a long modifier, “who, having masterminded …” So, this version has no main verb and, therefore, is not a complete sentence.


> <b>(E)</b> Former Cuban president Fidel <b>Castro suddenly quit smoking for health reasons, at age 59, masterminding the creation of the famous Cohiba cigar
</b>.



While this version is a complete sentence, the meaning conveyed by this version does not make sense.


The placement of the present participial phrase, “masterminding the creation of the famous Cohiba cigar,” indicates that the phrase modifies the entire preceding clause. Thus, since the events “suddenly quit smoking” and “masterminding the creation of the famous Cohiba cigar” likely did not happen simultaneously and are not logically connected, the sentence does not make sense.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

##  Sentence Correction Example 37  

A 7,000-watt solar energy system now costs around <u>$15,000, being a price that many can afford</u>.

1. [ ] (A) $15,000, being a price that many can afford
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) $15,000, a price affordable for many
3. [ ] (C) $15,000 and is a price affordable for many
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) $15,000, which is at a price that many can afford
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) $15,000, making it an affordable price for many
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



While the sentence seems to be meant to convey that $15,000 is a price that many can afford, only one version conveys that meaning. So, finding the correct answer to this question takes noticing how the use of modifiers, along with other wording choices, affects the meanings conveyed by the various versions.


> <b>(A)</b> A 7,000-watt solar energy system now costs around <b>$15,000, being a price that many can afford
</b>.



The present participial phrase “being a price …” appears after the clause and is separated from the preceding clause by a comma. So, we can tell that the phrase modifies the entire preceding clause rather than the noun that immediately precedes the phrase. Thus, this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that a 7000-watt solar energy system is being a price that many can afford.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B)</b> A 7,000-watt solar energy system now costs around <b>$15,000, a price affordable for many
</b>.



This version uses an appositive, “a price affordable for many,” to modify the noun that precedes it, “$15,000,” and thus conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(C)</b> A 7,000-watt solar energy system now costs around <b>$15,000 and is a price affordable for many
</b>.



By combining this choice with the non-underlined portion, we get a version that conveys the nonsensical meaning that a solar energy system costs $15,000 and that the solar energy system itself is a price affordable for many.


> <b>(D)</b> A 7,000-watt solar energy system now costs around <b>$15,000, which is at a price that many can afford
</b>.



This version conveys the nonsensical meaning that $15,000 <em>is at </em>a price, as opposed to a more logical meaning, such as that $15,000 <em>is </em>a price.


> <b>(E)</b> A 7,000-watt solar energy system now costs around <b>$15,000, making it an affordable price for many
</b>.



Since the present participial phrase “making it an affordable price for many” appears after the clause and is separated from the preceding clause by a comma, we can tell that the phrase modifies the entire preceding clause. Thus, this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that, by costing $15,000, a 7000-watt solar energy system makes $15,000 an affordable price for many.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

Let’s now discuss past participial phrases, starting with past participial phrases used to restrictively modify nouns.
