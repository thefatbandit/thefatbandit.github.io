## Lesson Content
Past participial phrases, phrases based on past participles (usually “-ed” or “-en” verbs), can be used to restrictively modify nouns.

In these situations, the past participial phrase is placed directly before or after the noun that it modifies and is not separated from the noun by commas.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Past participial phrases can be used to restrictively modify nouns. In these situations, the past participial phrase is placed directly before or after the nouns that it modifies and is not separated from the noun by commas.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example:

> Pumpkins <strong>harvested in the fall</strong> can last until the following summer if stored in certain conditions.

In the above example, the past participial phrase “harvested in the fall” restrictively modifies the noun “pumpkins.” We can tell that the modifier is restrictive by noticing that there are no commas setting “harvested in the fall” off from the rest of the sentence.

Because this modifier is restrictive, it tells us which specific pumpkins this sentence is about, <em>pumpkins harvested in the fall</em>.

Let’s consider another example:

> After the storm, the newspaper published photographs of buildings <strong>damaged by floodwaters</strong>.

In the above example, the past participial phrase “damaged by floodwaters” restrictively modifies “buildings” and, thus, indicates that the newspaper didn’t publish photos of just any buildings; it published photographs of buildings damaged by floodwaters.

Here’s another example:

> The company hired a firm <strong>known for devising outrageous publicity stunts</strong> in a bid to quickly become a household name.

In this example, the sentence employs the restrictive past participial phrase “known for devising …” to convey that the company hired a specific type of firm, one known for devising outrageous publicity stunts.

Let’s look at one more:

> The <strong>damaged beyond recognition</strong> automobile once belonged to a famous actor.

In this example, the sentence utilizes the restrictive past participial phrase “damaged beyond recognition” to indicate exactly which automobile is mentioned.
