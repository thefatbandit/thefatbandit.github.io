## Lesson Content
A past participial phrase can be used to nonrestrictively modify a noun.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A past participial phrase can be used to nonrestrictively modify a noun. In such a case, the phrase may appear directly before or directly after the noun that it modifies and is separated from the noun and other sentence elements by commas.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example:

> At the top of the hill stood a small house, <strong>painted white</strong>.

The past participial phrase “painted white” nonrestrictively modifies the noun “house.” We can tell that the modifier is nonrestrictive by noticing that there is a comma setting “painted white” off from the rest of the sentence.

Since a nonrestrictive past participial phrase can appear before or after the noun that it modifies, we could also say:

> <strong>Painted white</strong>, a small house stood at the top of the hill.

Here’s another example:

> <strong>Considered the best small team in the field</strong>, the husband-wife duo regularly produces groundbreaking work.

In this example, we can tell that the past participial phrase “considered the best …” is used nonrestrictively, because it is set off from the rest of the sentence by a comma.

Notice that we could also say:

> The husband-wife duo, <strong>considered the best small team in the field,</strong> regularly produces groundbreaking work.

Consider one more example:

> <strong>Covered in mud,</strong> the cyclists skillfully navigated the challenging terrain.

> The cyclists<strong>, covered in mud, </strong>skillfully navigated the challenging terrain.

Notice that both sentences are correct because a nonrestrictive past participial phrase can appear before or after the noun that it modifies.
