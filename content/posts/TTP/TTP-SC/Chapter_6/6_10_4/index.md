## Lesson Content
Just as the simple present, simple past, and simple future tenses have continuous tense counterparts, the perfect tenses – the present perfect, past perfect, and future perfect – have continuous counterparts.

To compare the present perfect tense and the present perfect continuous tense, consider the following examples:

> That eagle <strong>has flown</strong> overhead many times.

> That eagle <strong>has been flying</strong> overhead for the last twenty minutes.

In the first example, the present perfect is used to convey that, over a period of time extending from the past to the present, the eagle has flown overhead on multiple occasions.

Now let’s compare the past perfect with the past perfect continuous:

> John told us that some people <strong>had waited</strong> all day.

> By the time we got in line, some people <strong>had been waiting</strong> for hours.

In the first sentence, the past perfect is used to convey that one event, people had waited, occurred before another, John told us.

Finally, let’s compare the future perfect with the future perfect continuous:

> When the runners complete the race, they <strong>will have run </strong>over 40 kilometers.

> When the runners approach the finish line, they <strong>will have been running</strong> for hours.

In the first example, the future perfect “will have run” is used to convey that, at a point in the future, the runners will have completed a certain action, running over 40 kilometers.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

There are continuous versions of the present perfect, the past perfect, and future perfect tenses. They are the present perfect continuous, the past perfect continuous, and the future perfect continuous. Like other continuous tenses, these perfect continuous tenses are used to highlight the ongoing nature of events described.
{{< /alert >}}
