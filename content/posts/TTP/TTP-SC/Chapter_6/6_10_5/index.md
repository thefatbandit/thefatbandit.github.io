## Lesson Content
Correctly answering a Sentence Correction question can require determining whether using a non-continuous tense or using a continuous tense makes more sense.

When sentences involve reasons to highlight the ongoing nature of events, versions that use continuous tenses are the best.

When sentences do not involve reasons to highlight the ongoing nature of events, versions that use non-continuous tenses are the best.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When sentences involve reasons to highlight the ongoing nature of events, versions that use continuous tenses are the best. When sentences do not involve reasons to highlight the ongoing nature of events, versions that use non-continuous tenses are the best.
{{< /alert >}}

When stating simple facts, such as “dogs bark” or “John plays tennis” the simple present is the correct tense to use.

Consider these sentences:

> Jinus <strong>plays </strong>tennis.

> Jinus <strong>is playing</strong> tennis.

Both sentences make sense, but they convey two different meanings.

Let’s consider another:

> Scott <strong>is playing</strong> the piano.

If Scott is currently playing the piano, then the sentence is correct as written.

> Scott <strong>plays </strong>the piano.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Use the simple present rather than the present continuous in stating that events occur, if the point of the sentence is not to express that events are ongoing at the present time.
{{< /alert >}}

How can we use this knowledge to score points on test day? We can determine whether the use of a non-continuous tense or the use of a continuous tense makes more sense by considering context clues.

<strong><u>Key Strategy</u></strong><strong>:</strong> Use context clues to determine whether the use of a non-continuous tense or the use of a continuous tense makes more sense.

When sentences involve reasons to highlight the ongoing nature of events, versions that use continuous tenses are the best.

When sentences do not involve reasons to highlight the ongoing nature of events, versions that use non-continuous tenses are the best.

Consider the following pair of versions:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: When razorfish sense the presence of a predator, they <u>are hiding</u> in the sandy substrate of the seabed.

> <u>Correct</u>: When razorfish sense the presence of a predator, they <u>hide</u> in the sandy substrate of the seabed.

We can eliminate version one because we can tell that what the sentence is meant to describe is what razorfish do when they sense the presence of a predator: they hide.

Version two, of course, makes sense because the use of the simple present tense “they hide,” effectively conveys what the razorfish do.

Here’s another pair:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Because average global temperatures are currently at record levels, sea levels <u>rise </u>at the rate of 3.4 millimeters per year.

> <u>Correct</u>: Because average global temperatures are currently at record levels, sea levels <u>are rising</u> at the rate of 3.4 millimeters per year.

Context clues allow us to quickly eliminate sentence version one.

In version two, the use of the present continuous makes sense, because the present continuous expression “sea levels <strong>are rising</strong> at the rate of 3.4 millimeters per year” conveys that sea levels are rising at that rate at this moment but may not have risen at that rate in the past and may not rise at that rate in the future.

So, in this case, the version that uses the present continuous is more effective than the version that uses the simple present.

<u>Deciding Whether to Use the </u><u>Simple Past or the Past Continuous</u>

Just as context clues tell us whether using the simple present or using the present continuous makes more sense, we can tell from context clues whether using the simple past or the past continuous makes more sense.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Just as context clues tell us whether using the simple present or using the present continuous makes more sense, we can tell from context clues whether using the simple past or the past continuous makes more sense.
{{< /alert >}}

Unless there is a clear reason to highlight the continuous nature of a past action, we don’t use the past continuous tense.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Unless there is a clear reason to highlight the continuous nature of a past action, we don’t use the past continuous tense.
{{< /alert >}}

How do we use this knowledge to score points on test day? We eliminate any sentence version that uses the past continuous tense when it does not make sense to describe a past action as continuous.

<strong><u>Key Strategy</u></strong><strong>:</strong> Eliminate any sentence version that uses the past continuous tense when it does not make sense to describe a past action as continuous.

Consider the following example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: In 2018, Bryce Carlson <u>was rowing</u><em> </em>across the Atlantic Ocean in 38 days 6 hours and 49 minutes, setting a record that stands to this day.

> <u>Correct</u>: In 2018, Bryce Carlson <u>rowed</u> across the Atlantic Ocean in 38 days 6 hours and 49 minutes, setting a record that stands to this day.

We can eliminate version one because what makes sense is that Bryce Carlson completed the trip in 38 days 6 hours and 49 minutes.

Version two makes sense because the simple past “rowed” is what we would expect when a sentence describes a past action that was completed.

In contrast, when a sentence expresses a past action that was clearly ongoing, we can eliminate sentence versions that do not use the past progressive tense.

<strong><u>Key Strategy</u></strong><strong>:</strong> When a sentence expresses a past action that was clearly ongoing, we can eliminate sentence versions that do not use the past progressive tense.

Consider the following:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The filming was delayed because, upon arriving on location, the crew saw that some bears <u>played</u> in the pond and waited until the bears left.

> <u>Correct</u>: The filming was delayed because, upon arriving on location, the crew saw that some bears <u>were playing</u><em> </em>in the pond and waited until the bears left.

We can eliminate sentence version one because what makes sense is not that the crew saw that bears played in the pond, but were not playing in the pond when the crew arrived, but, rather, that the bears were playing (the action was ongoing), when the crew arrived and while the crew waited.

We see that the use of the past continuous “were playing” in version two makes sense.

<u>Deciding Whether to Use the </u><u>Simple Future or the Future Continuous</u>

Unless there is a clear reason to highlight the continuous nature of a future action, we don’t use the future continuous tense.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Unless there is a clear reason to highlight the continuous nature of a future action, we don’t use the future continuous tense.
{{< /alert >}}

How do we use this knowledge to score points on test day? We eliminate any sentence version that uses the future continuous tense when it does not make sense to describe a future action as continuous.

<strong><u>Key Strategy</u></strong><strong>:</strong> Eliminate any sentence version that uses the future continuous tense when it does not make sense to describe a future action as continuous.

OK, let’s consider a sentence about future events:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: If the traffic doesn’t clear soon, by the time the musicians arrive, the couple <u>will say</u> their vows.

> <u>Correct</u>: If the traffic doesn’t clear soon, by the time the musicians arrive, the couple <u>will be saying</u> their vows.

We can tell from what the sentence says about the events that this sentence is not meant to convey that the couple “will say” their vows.

Now, to wrap up our discussion, let’s choose between present perfect and present perfect continuous:

> Since moving to New York, the pop singer <em>(has attended/has been attending) </em>the opera a few times.

In this case, the action, the pop singer’s attending the opera, has not been continuous but, rather, has occurred on a few discrete occasions.

> Since moving to New York, the pop singer <strong>has attended</strong><em> </em>the opera a few times.

In all of the above examples, context clues clearly indicate whether using a non-continuous tense or using a continuous tense makes more sense.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When we answer Sentence Correction questions, we can use context clues to determine whether it makes more sense to use a non-continuous tense, it makes more sense to use a continuous tense, or either a non-continuous tense or a continuous tense works.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 15  

The oldest continental <u>rocks that are about 4 billion years old are found in cratons, which are stable parts of the continental lithosphere that have been surviving several successive supercontinent cycles, each of which has been lasting</u> 300 to 500 million years.

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A)</b> The oldest continental <b>rocks that are about 4 billion years old are found in cratons, which are stable parts of the continental lithosphere that have been surviving several successive supercontinent cycles, each of which has been lasting
</b> 300 to 500 million years.



The oldest continental rocks are already in a specific category, “the oldest.” So, further restricting the category by using the restrictive modifier “that are about 4 billion years old,” which also restricts the rocks by age, does not make sense. What would make sense is adding the information that the oldest rocks are about 4 billion years old via the use of a nonrestrictive modifier, such as “which are about 4 billion years old.”


Also, using the present perfect continuous “have been surviving,” which expresses that the cratons have been surviving successive supercontinent cycles on an ongoing basis up to the present time clearly does not make sense, because, if the cycles are successive, they are not all ongoing at the present time. Rather, they occurred in succession, with one ending when the next began. So, all but the last of the cycles must have ended by the present time, and therefore there is no way for the cratons to be surviving the cycles on an ongoing basis up to the present time.


Also, using the present perfect continuous “has been lasting,” which expresses that the cycles have been lasting on an ongoing basis up to the present time does not make sense. After all, if the cycles are successive, they are not all ongoing at the present time. Rather, they have occurred in succession, with one ending before the next occurred. So, all but the last of the cycles must have ended by the present time, and, therefore, there is no way for the cycles to be “lasting” on an ongoing basis up to the present time.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B)</b> The oldest continental <b>rocks, which are about 4 billion years old, are found in cratons, stable parts of the continental lithosphere that have survived several successive supercontinent cycles, each of which has lasted
</b> 300 to 500 million years.



Using the nonrestrictive modifier “which are about 4 billion years old” to add information about “the oldest continental rocks” makes sense.


Using the present perfect “have survived,” which indicates that the survival of the cratons began in the past and continues in the present makes sense.


The reason why the use of the present perfect “has lasted” makes sense may not be obvious, since, clearly, if the cycles have been successive, then earlier cycles have not lasted to the present time but, rather, ended at some point in the past before later cycles occurred. At the same time, the sentence discusses the cycles as a group, and the lasting of cycles in the group, one after the other, has been occurring up to the present time. So, the use of the present perfect “has lasted” makes sense.


> <b>(C)</b> The oldest continental <b>rocks, about 4 billion years old, are found in cratons, stable parts of the continental lithosphere surviving several successive supercontinent cycles each of which was lasting
</b> 300 to 500 million years.



Describing the cratons as “surviving several successive supercontinent cycles” does not make sense, because, if the cycles are successive, they are not all ongoing at the present time. Rather, they occurred in succession, with one ending when the next began. So, all but the last of the cycles must have ended by the present time, and, therefore, there is no way for the cratons to be currently “surviving” several successive cycles.


Also, the use of the past continuous “was lasting” does not make sense. A cycle’s lasting for 300 to 500 million years is an event that is completed once it occurs. Once the cycle is over, it makes sense to say that the cycle lasted for 300 to 500 million years. It does not make sense to say that the cycle “was lasting for 300 to 500 million years.” It lasted that long once, not over a period of time.


> <b>(D)</b> The oldest continental <b>rocks, being about 4 billion years old, are found in cratons, stable parts of the continental lithosphere, to survive several successive supercontinent cycles, each of which were lasting
</b> 300 to 500 million years.



The use of the word “being” in “being about 4 billion years” old is unnecessary and somewhat illogical. As a result of the unnecessary use of “being,” this version seems to convey the illogical meaning that the oldest continental rocks are found in cratons because of the oldest continental rocks’ being about 4 billion years old.


Also, as a result of the wording “to survive several successive supercontinent cycles,” the sentence seems to convey that there is a purpose to the rocks’ being found in cratons, as if, on purpose, the rocks are found in cratons in order to survive supercontinent cycles.


Also, since “each” is always singular, the plural “were” does not agree with “each.”


Also, the use of the past continuous “were lasting” does not make sense. A cycle’s lasting for 300 to 500 million years is an event that is completed once it occurs. Once the cycle is over, it makes sense to say that the cycle lasted for 300 to 500 million years. It does not make sense to say that the cycle “was lasting for 300 to 500 million years.” It lasted that long once, not over a period of time. So, it does not make sense to say that the cycles “were lasting” for 300 to 500 million years.


> <b>(E)</b> The oldest continental <b>rocks are about 4 billion years old and are found in cratons, stable parts of the continental lithosphere, surviving several successive supercontinent cycles, each of them having been lasting
</b> 300 to 500 million years.



Because the participial phrase “surviving several successive supercontinent cycles” is set off by commas, it should modify the clause that it follows and have a logical connection to what that clause says. However, there is not a strong logical connection between what the phrase says and what the clause that precedes the phrase says. As a result, the sentence conveys the illogical meaning that, via being about 4 billion years old and being found in cratons, the oldest rocks are somehow “surviving successive supercontinent cycles.”


Also, whether “each of them having been lasting …” is meant to modify the rocks or the cycles is not clear.


Also, using the continuous participle “having been lasting” does not make sense. A cycle’s lasting for 300 to 500 million years is an event that is completed once it occurs. Once the cycle is over, it makes sense to say that the cycle lasted for 300 to 500 million years. It does not make sense to say that the cycle had been lasting for 300 to 500 million years. It lasted that long once, not over a period of time. So, it does not make sense to describe the cycles as “having been lasting” for 300 to 500 million years.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A)</b> In the teeth of an Aztec man who died in one of the two waves of <b>disease that were occurring in 1545 and 1576
</b> was bacterial DNA that matched that of salmonella.



Because of the use of the past continuous “were occurring,” this version conveys the illogical meaning that two waves of disease, each of which occurred during different years, were occurring at the same time.


> <b>(B)</b> In the teeth of an Aztec man who died in one of the two waves of <b>disease that were first in 1545 and then in 1576
</b> was bacterial DNA that matched that of salmonella.



What this version is meant to convey is not clear. What does waves “were first in 1545 and then in 1576” even mean?


Also, it is not the nature of waves that the same wave would exist in one year and then come back in another year. A wave would occur once, and then the next occurrence of a wave, in a later year, for instance, would be a different wave, not the same wave. So, what this version conveys by saying, “waves … were first in 1545 and then in 1576” does not make sense.


Further, it does not really make sense to say that waves of disease “were” in a year. Waves of disease may <em>occur</em> during a year, but would not simply <em>be</em> in a year.


> <b>(C)</b> In the teeth of an Aztec man who died in one of the two waves of <b>disease - it occurred in 1545 and again in 1576 –
</b> was bacterial DNA that matched that of salmonella.



While a bit awkward, this version might have worked if the pronoun “it” had a logical referent. However, “it” has nothing to refer to. The sentence mentions two waves, and the singular “it” would not logically refer to the plural “two waves.”


> <b>(D)</b> In the teeth of an Aztec man who died in one of the two waves of <b>disease then occurring in 1545 and 1576
</b> was bacterial DNA that matched that of salmonella.



Because of the use of the present participle “occurring,” this version conveys the illogical meaning that two waves of disease, which occurred during two different years, were occurring at the same time.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E)</b> In the teeth of an Aztec man who died in one of the two waves of <b>disease, which occurred in 1545 and in 1576,
</b> was bacterial DNA that matched that of salmonella.



This version uses the simple past “occurred” to convey the logical meaning that two waves of disease occurred during two years.


Some people are reluctant to choose this choice because they see that the modifier “which occurred in 1545 and in 1576” is nonrestrictive and, therefore, merely mentions that the two waves of disease occurred in 1545 and in 1576 but does not specify that the two waves of disease were the ones that occurred in 1545 and in 1576. However, the truth is that the sentence works fine without restricting the waves of disease in that way. The sentence might seem better with a restrictive modifier, but it's not incorrect with a nonrestrictive modifier. It still conveys a meaning that makes sense.

> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(A)</b> During the 1800s, <b>passenger pigeons traveled in flocks numbering in the millions, but by around the first of September, 1914, with the death of the last of them, the passenger pigeon had become
</b> completely extinct.



This version uses the simple past “traveled” to clearly and concisely describe what passenger pigeons did in the 1800s.


Also, this version correctly uses the past perfect “had become” to indicate that, by a certain point in time, around the first of September, 1914, the passenger pigeons’ becoming extinct had been completed.


> <b>(B)</b> During the 1800s, <b>although passenger pigeons were traveling in flocks numbering in the millions, by around the first of September, 1914, when the last of them died, passenger pigeons were becoming
</b> completely extinct.



Logically, the opening modifier “during the 1800s” has to modify an event described by the sentence. Notice, everything that follows “during the 1800s” is either a modifying clause or a modifying phrase except for the independent clause “passenger pigeons were becoming completely extinct.” So, “during the 1800s” must modify “passenger pigeons were becoming completely extinct.” Meanwhile, that clause is also modified by “by around the first of September, 1914.” Because both “during the 1800s” and “by around the first of September, 1914” modify “passenger pigeons were becoming completely extinct, the sentence conveys the nonsensical meaning that passenger pigeons were becoming extinct by around the first of September, 1914, in the 1800s.


Also, the use of the past continuous “were traveling” results in the sentence’s expressing that the passenger pigeons were, on an ongoing basis, traveling in flocks numbering in the millions. As a result, the sentence conveys the illogical meaning that the passenger pigeons were, on an ongoing basis, traveling in flocks numbering in the millions when the last of the passenger pigeons died, as if they could have been traveling in flocks numbering in the millions when there was only one left and when that one died.


Also, the use of the past perfect continuous “were becoming” results in the sentence’s conveying the illogical meaning that, by around the first of September, 1914, when the last of the passenger pigeons died, the pigeons were becoming completely extinct on an ongoing basis. Of course, when the last of the passenger pigeons died, they would not have been becoming completely extinct on an ongoing basis, because, when the last one died, they would have become completely extinct, at which point the process of their becoming extinct would have ended, not continued on an ongoing basis.


> <b>(C)</b> During the 1800s, <b>passenger pigeons were traveling in flocks that were numbering in the millions, but by around the first of September, 1914, when the last of them was dying, the passenger pigeon had become
</b> completely extinct.



There is no reason to use the past continuous “were traveling.” While the use of the past continuous is not strictly incorrect, the use of the simple past “traveled” would make more sense, because the point to be made is not that the passenger pigeons were traveling on an ongoing basis but, rather, simply that, during the 1800s, they traveled in flocks numbering in the millions.


Also, the wording “that were numbering in the millions” is unnecessarily wordy. It is just as effective and more concise to say, “numbering in the millions.”


Also, the use of the past continuous “was dying” does not make sense, because the passenger pigeon would not have become completely extinct when the last of the passenger pigeons was in the process of dying. The passenger pigeon did not become completely extinct until the last one died.


> <b>(D)</b> During the 1800s, <b>when passenger pigeons traveled in flocks that numbered in the millions, by around the first of September, 1914, the last of them died, and the passenger pigeon was becoming
</b> completely extinct.



This version conveys the nonsensical meaning that, in the 1800s, by around the first of September, 1914, the last of the passenger pigeons died.


Also, this version conveys the illogical meaning that, when passenger pigeons traveled in flocks that numbered in the millions, the last of the passenger pigeons died.


Also, this version uses the past continuous “was becoming” to convey the illogical meaning that, when the last of the passenger pigeons died, the passenger pigeon was becoming completely extinct on an ongoing basis. Of course, when the last passenger pigeon died, the passenger pigeon would not have been going extinct on an ongoing basis, because the process of the passenger pigeon’s going extinct would have been completed.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E)</b> During the 1800s, <b>passenger pigeons traveled in flocks that were numbered by the millions, but then, around the first of September, 1914, after the last of them had died, passenger pigeons became
</b> completely extinct.



The wording “flocks that were numbered by the millions” conveys the nonsensical meaning that the flocks were numbered <em>by</em> some “millions,” as if there were “millions,” of what we don’t know, that or who numbered the flocks, whatever numbering the flocks would even be.


Also, the use of the connector “but then” doesn’t quite make sense. It does not make sense to connect “in the 1800s” to “around the first of September, 1914” by saying “but then,” because the 1800s were not directly followed by the first of September, 1914, as if the 1800s ended and “then” it was the first of September, 1914.


Also, the use of the past perfect “had died” in “after the last of them had died” is unnecessary and somewhat illogical, because “after” makes the time sequence clear. It would be sufficient and more logical to say, “after the last of them died.”


Also, it does not make sense to say, “after the last of them had died, passenger pigeons became extinct.” Passenger pigeons became extinct <em>the moment</em> the last of them died, not <em>after</em> the last of them died.
