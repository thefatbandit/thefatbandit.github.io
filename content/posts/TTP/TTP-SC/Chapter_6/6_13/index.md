## Lesson Content
The subjunctive mood is used for statements that lack certainty, such as those that describe hypothetical or conditional situations, dreams, wishes, desires, or doubts.

In a sentence that describes a hypothetical situation, the form of “to be” that should be used is the verb “were,” never the verb “was.” At first, this usage will probably sound incorrect to you.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Sentences that describe hypothetical situations must use the subjunctive mood. In describing these hypothetical situations with the verb “to be,” we use the verb “were,” never the verb “was.”
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example:

> <u>Correct</u>: If I <strong>were</strong> a millionaire, I would buy my dream house.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: If I <strong>was</strong> a millionaire, I would buy my dream house.

This sentence is meant to convey that the speaker is not actually a millionaire.

Consider the following example:

> <u>Correct</u>: If I <strong>were </strong>a king, I would be rich.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: If I <strong>was </strong>a king, I would be rich.

Notice that this sentence is meant to convey a hypothetical situation: I am not actually a king, but if I were I’d be rich.

> <u>Correct</u>: <strong>Were</strong> I still living in South Africa, I would still be working to protect endangered wildlife.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: <strong>Was </strong>I still living in South Africa, I would still be working to protect endangered wildlife.

Notice that this sentence is meant to convey a hypothetical situation: If I were still living in South Africa, I would be doing something… (working to protect endangered wildlife).

How can we use this knowledge to score points on test day? One powerful strategy we can use is to quickly eliminate any sentence version that expresses a hypothetical situation yet doesn’t use the subjunctive mood.

Consider the following:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: If he <strong>was </strong>an experienced pilot, he would be aware that landing in such conditions is challenging.

> <u>Correct</u>: If he <strong>were </strong>an experienced pilot, he would be aware that landing in such conditions is challenging.

Sentence version one is incorrect because it describes a hypothetical situation yet does not use the subjunctive mood.

How can we see that the sentence is describing something theoretical? We can notice that “he” is not actually a pilot.

Let’s look at another example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: I wish that I <strong>was</strong> done with my GMAT preparation.

> <u>Correct</u>: I wish that I <strong>were</strong> done with my GMAT preparation.

Sentence version one is incorrect because it expresses a hypothetical situation yet does not use the subjunctive mood.

How can we see that the sentence is describing something theoretical? We can notice that “I” am not actually done with my GMAT preparation.

<u>Additional Context Clues to Help spot Sentences Requiring the Subjunctive Mood</u>

In identifying sentences that require the subjunctive mood, it’s helpful to know that these hypothetical clauses typically begin with “if,” “as if,” “were,” or “as though.”

Consider these example sentences using the subjunctive mood:

> <u>Correct</u>: You act as if getting laid off <strong>were</strong> the end of the world.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: You act as if getting laid off <strong>was</strong> the end of the world.

> <u>Correct</u>: She looked at me as though I <strong>were</strong> a monster.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: She looked at me as though I <strong>was</strong> a monster.

In all of the above examples, the subjunctive is necessary, and the use of the indicative is incorrect, because the sentences are about hypothetical, rather than actual, situations.

{{< /alert >}}

<u>One Variation on the Subjunctive Mood </u>

So far, the examples that we have discussed use the subjunctive form of “to be,” which is “were.”

Another form of the subjunctive that can appear in a Sentence Correction question is formed via the combination of “were” and an infinitive.

Consider the following examples:

> If Sheri <strong>were to arrive</strong> early, the surprise would be ruined.

> If the owner of the building <strong>were to decide</strong> to fix it up, the entire neighborhood would benefit.

> Bankruptcy could be avoided if the city <strong>were to manage</strong> its finances better.

> If I <strong>were to purchase</strong> a plane ticket to Hawaii, I would surely go diving with Tiger Sharks.

> Happiness could be found if the couple <strong>were to communicate</strong> better.

As it was in the previous examples, in these examples the subjunctive is used in clauses that describe situations that are hypothetical.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: If Sheri <strong>was to arrive</strong> early, the surprise would be ruined.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: If the owner of the building <strong>was to decide</strong> to fix it up, the entire neighborhood would benefit.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Bankruptcy could be avoided if the city <strong>was to manage</strong> its finances better.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: If I <strong>was to purchase</strong> a plane ticket to Hawaii, I would surely go diving with Tiger Sharks.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Happiness could be found if the couple <strong>was to communicate</strong> better.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Another form of the subjunctive that can appear in a Sentence Correction question is formed via the combination of “were” and an infinitive.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s now discuss situations in which the subjunctive mood is not used.
