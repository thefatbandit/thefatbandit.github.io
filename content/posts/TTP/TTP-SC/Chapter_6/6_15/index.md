## Lesson Content
Along with hypothetical situations, certain types of “command situations” require the use of the subjunctive mood, which, in these situations, is referred to as the “command subjunctive.”

In these situations, we’ll see a request, suggestion, demand, or command word followed by the word “that.”

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Request, suggestion, demand, or command words followed by the word “that” require the subjunctive mood, which, in these situations, is referred to as the command subjunctive.
{{< /alert >}}

This idea is illustrated in the sentences below:

> <u>Correct</u>: I <strong>demand that</strong> you <strong>be</strong> respectful at dinner tonight.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: I <strong>demand that</strong> you <strong>are</strong> respectful at dinner tonight.

Normally we would say “you are,” not “you be,” since we use the indicative mood in most cases.

Let’s look at another example sentence.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: I <strong>demand that </strong>you<strong> are</strong> at the gym by noon.

> <u>Correct</u>: I <strong>demand that</strong> you <strong>be</strong> at the gym by noon.

The good news is that we can use the command subjunctive with any verb, not just the verb “to be.” Consider the following sentence:

I <strong>insist that</strong> he <strong>respect </strong>me at dinner tonight.

Again, we have a command verb followed by the word “that,” which is our signal to use the command subjunctive – that is, the base form of the verb.

> <u>Correct</u>: I <strong>insist that</strong> he <strong>respect </strong>me at dinner tonight.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: I <strong>insist that</strong> he <strong>respects </strong>me at dinner tonight.

Let’s consider another:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: I <strong>insist that</strong> she <strong>eats</strong> her lunch before the meeting.

> <u>Correct</u>: I <strong>insist that</strong> she <strong>eat</strong> her lunch before the meeting.

Again, notice that we use the command subjunctive.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The subjunctive form of the verb “to be” used in command sentences is always “be.” Other verbs used in command sentences will be expressed in their unconjugated form.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at some other common situations:

> <u>Correct</u>: The <strong>coach requests that</strong> uniforms <strong>be</strong> returned on time.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The <strong>coach requests that</strong> uniforms <strong>are</strong> returned on time.

> <u>Correct</u>: The <strong>regulations require that</strong> everyone <strong>submit</strong> a customs form.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The <strong>regulations require that</strong> everyone <strong>submits</strong> a customs form.

> <u>Correct</u>: <strong>I suggest that</strong> his candidacy <strong>be reevaluated</strong>.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: <strong>I suggest that</strong> his candidacy <strong>is reevaluated</strong>.

It’s helpful to memorize this list of verbs that are often followed by the command subjunctive.

Now, to be clear, not every sentence that involves a request, suggestion, demand, or command must use the command subjunctive.

For instance, the following sentences are all correct:

> The spokesperson <strong>asked that everyone wait</strong> for the formal announcement.

> The spokesperson <strong>asked everyone to wait</strong> for the formal announcement

> After the debacle, the prime minister <strong>ordered that the secretary conduct</strong> a full review.

> After the debacle, the prime minister <strong>ordered the secretary to conduct</strong> a full review.

> Often, a lender <strong>will require that a borrower purchase</strong> insurance.

> Often, a lender <strong>will require a borrower to purchase</strong> insurance.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Not every sentence that involves a request, suggestion, demand, or command must use the command subjunctive.
{{< /alert >}}

So, what we’re discussing in this lesson is simply that the command subjunctive is required in situations in which a request, suggestion, demand, or command word is followed by “that.”

How can we use this knowledge to score points on test day? We can eliminate any sentence version that contains a “that” preceded by request, suggestion, demand, or command word but does not use the subjunctive mood.

<strong><u>Key Strategy</u></strong><strong>: </strong>Eliminate any sentence version that contains a “that” preceded by request, suggestion, demand, or command word but does not use the subjunctive mood, which, in these situations, is referred to as the command subjunctive.

Let’s practice eliminating such sentences.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The general requires <strong>you be</strong> at your command post from midnight to 8 a.m.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The general requires <strong>that you should be</strong> at your command post from midnight to 8 a.m.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The general requires <strong>that you will be</strong> at your command post from midnight to 8 a.m.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The general requires <strong>that you are to be</strong> at your command post from midnight to 8 a.m.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The general requires <strong>that you are </strong>at your command post from midnight to 8 a.m.

> <u>Correct</u>: The general <strong>requires that</strong> <strong>you be</strong> at your command post from midnight to 8 a.m.

Notice that all of the sentence versions except the final version are incorrect because they don’t use the command subjunctive.

Advocates of longer terms for members of the U.S.

1. [ ] (A) that, rather than run for reelection every two years, each representative will run every four years
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) that each representative run for reelection every four years instead of every two
3. [ ] (C) that, instead of every two years, each representative should run for reelection every four years
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) that, rather than running for reelection every two years, each representative is running every four
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) that each representative runs for reelection every four years instead of two years
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A)</b> Advocates of longer terms for members of the U.S. House of Representatives propose <b>that, rather than run for reelection every two years, each representative will run every four years
</b>, so that more time can be devoted to legislating than to campaigning.



 The phrase “propose that” should not be followed by the indicative “will run.” Advocates would not propose what will happen.


“Propose that” should be followed by the command subjunctive “run.”


Thus, “advocates propose that … each representative <em>will run</em> every four years” is incorrect.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B)</b> Advocates of longer terms for members of the U.S. House of Representatives propose <b>that each representative run for reelection every four years instead of every two
</b>, so that more time can be devoted to legislating than to campaigning.



 This version is the only one that correctly follows the phrase “propose that” with the command subjunctive “run.”


The sentence structure is logical, and the sentence effectively conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(C)</b> Advocates of longer terms for members of the U.S. House of Representatives propose <b>that, instead of every two years, each representative should run for reelection every four years
</b>, so that more time can be devoted to legislating than to campaigning.



The phrase “propose that” should not be followed by the indicative “should run.” Advocates would not propose what should happen. The idea that something “should” happen is an opinion, and advocates would not propose an opinion.


“Propose that” should be followed by the command subjunctive “run.”


> <b>(D)</b> Advocates of longer terms for members of the U.S. House of Representatives propose <b>that, rather than running for reelection every two years, each representative is running every four
</b>, so that more time can be devoted to legislating than to campaigning.



The phrase “propose that” should not be followed by the indicative “is running.” The present continuous “is running” indicates that the event “each representative is running” is currently ongoing, and advocates would not propose what is currently ongoing.


“Propose that” should be followed by the command subjunctive “run.”


> <b>(E)</b> Advocates of longer terms for members of the U.S. House of Representatives propose <b>that each representative runs for reelection every four years instead of two years
</b>, so that more time can be devoted to legislating than to campaigning.



The phrase “propose that” should not be followed by the indicative “runs.” The present indicative “runs” indicates that the event “each representative runs” occurs routinely, and advocates would not propose what already occurs routinely.


“Propose that” should be followed by the command subjunctive “run.”


Notice that, even though “representative” is a singular noun, because the command subjunctive is needed, “run” not “runs” is correct.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

##  Sentence Correction Example 21  

One long term solution to the nation’s <u>problems, which have been discussed but have not been widely implemented, is mandating secondary education to include</u> instruction focused on critical thinking.

1. [ ] (A) problems, which have been discussed but have not been widely implemented, is mandating secondary education to include
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) problems that has been discussed but not widely implemented is mandating that secondary education include
3. [ ] (C) problems, having been discussed but not widely implemented, is mandating that secondary education includes
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) problems being discussed but not widely implemented is mandating secondary education, so that it includes
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) problems that have been discussed but not widely implemented, is to mandate that secondary education should include
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



The expression “mandating that” should be followed by the command subjunctive.


> <b>(A)</b> One long term solution to the nation’s <b>problems, which have been discussed but have not been widely implemented, is mandating secondary education to include
</b> instruction focused on critical thinking.



Since the plural “have” agrees with the plural “problems,” the relative clause “which have been discussed but have not been widely implemented” seems to modify “problems.” However, while problems might be discussed, problems would not be implemented. So, if that relative clause is understood to modify “problems,” then the sentence does not make sense. Of course, a “solution” would be “discussed but … not widely implemented.” However, if the relative clause is understood to modify “solution,” the sentence is grammatically flawed, because the plural “have” does not agree with the singular “solution.”


Also, the expression “mandating secondary education to include” does not make sense. “Mandating” is not synonymous with “commanding” or “ordering.” One does not mandate someone or something to do something the way one would command someone to do something, such as include instruction focused on critical thinking. One mandates something. For instance, one would mandate “that secondary education include instruction focused on critical thinking.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B)</b> One long term solution to the nation’s <b>problems that has been discussed but not widely implemented is mandating that secondary education include
</b> instruction focused on critical thinking.



The relative clause “that has been discussed but not widely implemented” logically modifies “one solution to the nation’s problems.”


Also, “mandating that” is correctly followed by the command subjunctive expression “secondary education include.”


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> One long term solution to the nation’s <b>problems, having been discussed but not widely implemented, is mandating that secondary education includes
</b> instruction focused on critical thinking.



The use of the present participial phrase “having been discussed but not widely implemented” results in the sentence’s expressing the illogical meaning that, in the process of having been discussed, a “solution to the nation’s problems” is what it is. For clarity, here’s an analogous sentence:


Jose, having been discussed, is my brother.


Clearly, Jose’s having been discussed has no connection to his being someone’s brother.


Similarly, the solution to the nation’s problems’ having been discussed has no connection to the solution’s being mandating that secondary education include instruction focused on critical thinking. So, adding the information “having been discussed but not widely implemented” via the use of a present participial phrase, which seems to connect that information to the main topic of the sentence, does not make sense.


Also, this version incorrectly follows “mandating that” with the indicative voice expression “secondary education includes.”


> <b>(D)</b> One long term solution to the nation’s <b>problems being discussed but not widely implemented is mandating secondary education, so that it includes
</b> instruction focused on critical thinking.



The use of the restrictive participial phrase “being discussed but not widely implemented” to modify “one solution to the nation’s problems” makes sense.


However, “mandating secondary education, so that it includes” does not make sense. Mandating secondary education would not somehow result in secondary education’s including something.


> <b>(E)</b> One long term solution to the nation’s <b>problems that have been discussed but not widely implemented, is to mandate that secondary education should include
</b> instruction focused on critical thinking.



Since the plural “have” agrees with the plural “problems,” the relative clause “that have been discussed but not been widely implemented” seems to modify “problems.” However, while problems might be discussed, problems would not be implemented. So, if that relative clause is understood to modify “problems,” then the sentence does not make sense. Of course, a “solution” would be “discussed but … not widely implemented.” However, if the relative clause is understood to modify “solution,” the sentence is grammatically flawed, because the plural “have” does not agree with the singular “solution.”


Also, this version incorrectly follows “mandating that” with “secondary education should include.” One would not mandate that something should happen. The fact that something should happen is conceptually somewhat separate from the concept of mandating it. One would simply mandate that something happens.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

##  Sentence Correction Example 22  

Since, in recent years, local rivers’ overflowing their banks during spring and summer rainstorms <u>has become more frequent, the city is urging all who own homes in the flood zone to take</u> the flood damage mitigation measures listed on the city’s website.

1. [x] (A) has become more frequent, the city is urging all who own homes in the flood zone to take
2. [ ] (B) have become more frequent, the city urges that all who own homes in the flood zone take
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) having become more frequent, the city is urging all who own homes in the flood zone, so that they take
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) is becoming more frequent, the city has urged all who own homes in the flood zone that they should take
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) are becoming more frequent, the city has been urging all who own homes in the flood zone taking
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



Both of the following are correct.


The city urges that all who own homes in the flood zone take measures.


The city urges all who own homes in the flood zone to take measures.


So, the fact that one choice in this question is structured like one of the above examples and another choice is structured like the other of the above examples does not constitute a decision point that one can use to choose one of those choices over the other. Choosing one of those choices over the other requires finding another decision point.


Also, correctly answering this question takes avoiding becoming overly focused on whether “urged” or “urged that” is followed by something that works with it logically, because there are other issues that have to be considered as well, such as the subject-verb agreement issue in the version created via the use of choice (B).


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A)</b> Since, in recent years, local rivers’ overflowing their banks during spring and summer rainstorms <b>has become more frequent, the city is urging all who own homes in the flood zone to take
</b> the flood damage mitigation measures listed on the city’s website.



The singular “has become” agrees with the singular subject “overflowing.” (The inclusion in the sentence before and after “overflowing” of multiple plural nouns – “years,” “rivers,” “banks,” and “rainstorms” – was done for the purpose of confusing test-takers into misperceiving that the subject of the sentence is plural, rather than singular.)


Also, “the city is urging all … to take …” is correctly structured and expresses a logical meaning.


> <b>(B)</b> Since, in recent years, local rivers’ overflowing their banks during spring and summer rainstorms <b>have become more frequent, the city urges that all who own homes in the flood zone take
</b> the flood damage mitigation measures listed on the city’s website.



“The city urges that all … take …” is correctly structured and expresses a logical meaning.


However, the plural “have become” does not agree with the singular subject “overflowing.” (The inclusion in the sentence before and after “overflowing” of multiple plural nouns – “years,” “rivers,” “banks,” and “rainstorms” – was done for the purpose of confusing test-takers into misperceiving that the subject of the sentence is plural, rather than singular.)


> <b>(C)</b> Since, in recent years, local rivers’ overflowing their banks during spring and summer rainstorms <b>having become more frequent, the city is urging all who own homes in the flood zone, so that they take
</b> the flood damage mitigation measures listed on the city’s website.



In this sentence, “since” seems to be used as a conjunction similar to “because.” So, “since” should connect two clauses. In this version, what follows “since” is “overflowing … having become more frequent.” That structure is not a clause but, rather, an absolute phrase. So, in this version, the conjunction “since” incorrectly connects an absolute phrase – a modifier – with a clause.


An alternative possibility is that, in this version, “since” is used as preposition indicating a starting point in time, in which case the meaning meant to be conveyed would be the following. Since the point in time when overflowing became more frequent, the city has urged. However, the wording of the sentence is not structured to correctly express that meaning. For one thing, in that case, “the city is urging” should be the present perfect continuous “the city has been urging,” as in “Since x point in time, the city has been urging ….”


Also, “the city is urging all who own homes in the flood zone, so that they take” is poorly structured and, therefore, does not effectively convey a clear meaning. It does not make clear what the city is urging all to do. Rather it simply says that the city is urging all, and then suggests that the purpose of this nonspecific urging is to get “all” to take certain measures. So, the sentence fails to logically connect the ideas of urging and getting people to take measures. Contrast the wording of this version with “urging all … to take” in the version created via the use of (A) and “urges that all … take” in the version created via the use of (B).


> <b>(D)</b> Since, in recent years, local rivers’ overflowing their banks during spring and summer rainstorms <b>is becoming more frequent, the city has urged all who own homes in the flood zone that they should take
</b> the flood damage mitigation measures listed on the city’s website.



Since, “in recent years” is largely in the past, the use of the present tense “is becoming” is suboptimal. It makes more sense to use, as does (A), the present perfect “has become,” which matches what the sentence suggests, which is that the overflowing’s becoming more frequent began in the past and continues in the present.


Also, this version incorrectly follows “has urged ... that” with “they should take.” An action would be urged. “They should take” does not describe an action. It expresses a judgment. “Has urged ... that” should be followed by the command subjunctive expression “they take.”


> <b>(E)</b> Since, in recent years, local rivers’ overflowing their banks during spring and summer rainstorms <b>are becoming more frequent, the city has been urging all who own homes in the flood zone taking
</b> the flood damage mitigation measures listed on the city’s website.



The plural “are becoming” does not agree with the singular subject “overflowing.” (The inclusion in the sentence before and after “overflowing” of multiple plural nouns – “years,” “rivers,” “banks,” and “rainstorms” – was done for the purpose of confusing test-takers into misperceiving that the subject of the sentence is plural, rather than singular.)


Also, since, “in recent years” is largely in the past, the use of the present tense “are becoming” is suboptimal. It makes more sense to use, as does (A), the present perfect “has become,” which matches what the sentence suggests, which is that the overflowing’s becoming more frequent began in the past and continues in the present.


Also, “the city is urging all who own homes in the flood zone taking” is poorly structured and, therefore, does not effectively convey a clear meaning. It does not make clear what the city is urging all to do. Rather it says, basically, that the city is urging all taking measures. Thus, the sentence fails to logically connect the ideas of urging and getting people to take measures. Contrast the wording of this version with “urging all … to take” in the version created via the use of (A) and “urges that all … take” in the version created via the use of (B).


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

Let’s now discuss conditional sentences and the conditional mood.
