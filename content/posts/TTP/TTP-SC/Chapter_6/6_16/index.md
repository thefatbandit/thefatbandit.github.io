## Lesson Content
Like the subjunctive mood, the conditional mood is used in sentences about conditions and results that are purely hypothetical.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The conditional mood is used in sentences about conditions and results that are purely hypothetical.
{{< /alert >}}

The conditional mood can be formed by combining “would” or “could” with a finite verb.

Consider the following examples, each of which describes a purely hypothetical situation:

> If I knew how to speak French, I <strong>would move</strong> to Paris.

> If they could see us now, they <strong>would be laughing</strong>.

> If the fruit had been outside, it <strong>could have been eaten by rabbits</strong>.

> Had the method been published, people <strong>could have been using</strong> it.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The conditional mood is formed by combining “would” or “could” with a finite verb.
{{< /alert >}}

As we well know, when describing an action or event that is possible, we often use the verbs “will” or “can.” Notice that this usage is in contrast to the use of “would” or “could,” which are used in hypothetical situations.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

We often use the verbs “will” or “can” when describing an action or event that is actually possible, but we use “would” or “could” when describing actions or events that are purely hypothetical.
{{< /alert >}}

So how do we use this knowledge to score points on test day.

<strong><u>Key Strategy</u></strong><strong>:</strong> We eliminate any sentence versions that use “will” or “can” in describing actions or events that are purely hypothetical.

Consider the following sentence versions:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: If it were possible to fly by flapping one’s arms, I <strong>will get</strong> home faster.

> <u>Correct</u>: If it were possible to fly by flapping one’s arms, I <strong>would get</strong> home faster.

We can quickly eliminate sentence version one because a context clue (if it were possible …) alerts us that this sentence is describing a purely hypothetical situation.

We see that in sentence version two, the clause “I would get home faster” correctly uses the conditional “would get” to express a proposition that would be true “if it were possible to fly by flapping one’s arms.”

Consider the following sentence versions:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: If Ashanti had arrived early, the surprise <strong>will have been ruined</strong>.

> <u>Correct</u>: If Ashanti had arrived early, the surprise <strong>would have been ruined</strong>.

We can quickly eliminate sentence version one because a context clue (if Ashanti had arrived early) alerts us that this sentence is describing a purely hypothetical situation.

In the second sentence version, the clause “the surprise would have been ruined” correctly uses the conditional “would have been ruined” to express a proposition that would be true under the condition “Ashanti had arrived early.”
