## Lesson Content
Remember, the conditional mood is used in sentences describing purely hypothetical things.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The conditional mood is used in sentences describing purely hypothetical things. It is not used in situations that could actually be true.
{{< /alert >}}

For review, consider the following sentence versions:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: If the movie were as good as the trailer, it will be very popular.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: If the movie were as good as the trailer, it should be very popular.

> <u>Correct</u>: If the movie were as good as the trailer, it would be very popular.

In all three versions, the dependent clause that states the condition, “if the movie were as good as the trailer,” is in the subjunctive mood.

So, sentence versions one and two are incorrect because both of them use the indicative mood.

However, and this is important, not all conditional sentences must use the subjunctive mood.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Not all conditional sentences must use the subjunctive mood. Many sentences are conditional yet at the same time do not describe something hypothetical.
{{< /alert >}}

For example, consider the following correct conditional sentences that use the indicative mood:

> If Rene prefers to draw all day, then she <strong>should find</strong> a way to sell her art.

> If the engineers solve this problem, the robot <strong>will be </strong>the best in its class.

> If the CEO learns to meditate, everyone’s stress level <strong>may decrease</strong> significantly.

Notice that, in each case, the statement made in the main clause does not express something hypothetical.

Here’s another set:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: If she wears normal clothes and sunglasses, she would go unrecognized.

> <u>Correct</u>: If she wears normal clothes and sunglasses, she will go unrecognized.

> <u>Correct</u>: If she wears normal clothes and sunglasses, she may go unrecognized.

Notice that each sentence version is conditional yet at the same time does not describe something hypothetical.

Sentence version one, which uses the conditional “would go,” is incorrect, because we don’t use the conditional mood for sentences that discuss things that are possible.

On the other hand, sentence version two, which uses the simple future “she will,” is correct.

So, in order to arrive at the correct answers to Sentence Correction questions involving conditional statements, we have to notice whether the statements are about actual or hypothetical situations and whether the sentences use the conditional or indicative mood appropriately.

##  Sentence Correction Example 23  

If Sandeep <u>will be chosen as next president of West Highlands Animal Shelter, a no-kill shelter that is committed to rehabilitating abused animals, he will</u> surely bring much needed fresh energy to the position.

1. [ ] (A) will be chosen as next president of West Highlands Animal Shelter, a no-kill shelter that is committed to rehabilitating abused animals, he will
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) is chosen the next president of West Highlands Animal Shelter that is a no-kill animal shelter committed to rehabilitating abused animals, he would
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) were chosen to be the next president of West Highlands Animal Shelter, which is a no-kill shelter committed to rehabilitating animals of abuse, he will
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) was chosen as the next president of West Highlands Animal Shelter, being a no-kill animal shelter committed to the rehabilitation of abused animals, he would
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) were to be chosen as the next president of West Highlands Animal Shelter, a no-kill animal shelter committed to the rehabilitation of abused animals, he would

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Correctly answering this question takes careful analysis of the relationships between the “if” clauses and the main clauses in the various versions and identifying the choice in which the “if” clause and the main clause work together logically.


Also, some versions have modifier issues.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A)</b> If Sandeep <b>will be chosen as next president of West Highlands Animal Shelter, a no-kill shelter that is committed to rehabilitating abused animals, he will
</b> surely bring much needed fresh energy to the position.



This version is about what will happen under certain conditions. It makes sense to use the future tense “he will … bring” in describing what will happen.


However, it does not make sense that Sandeep will bring fresh energy under the condition that he will be chosen. What makes sense is that Sandeep will bring fresh energy under the condition that he is chosen. So, the use of the future tense “will be chosen” does not make sense.


> <b>(B)</b> If Sandeep <b>is chosen the next president of West Highlands Animal Shelter that is a no-kill animal shelter committed to rehabilitating abused animals, he would
</b> surely bring much needed fresh energy to the position.



This version illogically uses in the “if” clause the indicative expression “Sandeep is chosen,” which indicates that there is an existing possibility that Sandeep will be chosen as the next president of the shelter, with the conditional expression “he would … bring,” which indicates that the sentence is about a hypothetical situation rather than an existing possibility.


Also, since presumably there is only one West Highlands Animal Shelter, using the restrictive modifier “that is a no-kill animal shelter …,” which seems to restrict the definition of “West Highlands Animal Shelter” to a particular shelter, does not make sense. It’s already clear that West Highlands Animal Shelter is a particular shelter.


> <b>(C)</b> If Sandeep <b>were chosen to be the next president of West Highlands Animal Shelter, which is a no-kill shelter committed to rehabilitating animals of abuse, he will
</b> surely bring much needed fresh energy to the position.



This version illogically uses in the “if” clause the subjunctive expression “Sandeep were,” which indicates that Sandeep’s being chosen as the next president of the shelter is merely hypothetical, with the indicative expression “he will … bring,” which indicates that the sentence is about, not a hypothetical situation but the actual possibility that Sandeep, having been chosen as the next president of the shelter, will bring fresh energy.


Also, “rehabilitating animals of abuse” does not really make sense. One might rid animals of something or cure animals of something, but one would not rehabilitate animals of something, such as abuse.


> <b>(D)</b> If Sandeep <b>was chosen as the next president of West Highlands Animal Shelter, being a no-kill animal shelter committed to the rehabilitation of abused animals, he would
</b> surely bring much needed fresh energy to the position.



This version illogically uses in the “if” clause the indicative expression “Sandeep was chosen,” which indicates that it is possible that Sandeep was actually chosen as the next president of the shelter, with the conditional expression “he would … bring,” which indicates that the sentence is about a hypothetical situation rather than an actually possible situation.


Also, since the logical target of the participial modifier “being a no-kill animal shelter committed …” is either the subject of the clause that precedes the modifier or the subject of the clause that follows the modifier, which subjects are “Sandeep” and “he,” respectively, read literally, the sentence conveys that “Sandeep” is “being a no-kill animal shelter.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E)</b> If Sandeep <b>were to be chosen as the next president of West Highlands Animal Shelter, a no-kill animal shelter committed to the rehabilitation of abused animals, he would
</b> surely bring much needed fresh energy to the position.



This version logically uses in the “if” clause the subjunctive expression “Sandeep were,” which indicates that Sandeep’s being chosen as the next president of the shelter is hypothetical, with the conditional expression “he would … bring,” which indicates that the sentence is about a hypothetical situation.


Also, the appositive “a no-kill animal shelter committed …” logically modifies “West Highlands Animal Shelter.”


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Sentence Correction Example 24  

<u>If the company‘s car was damaged by the sandstorm, the company would</u> have had the car repaired by its insurance company, but because the car was damaged through operator negligence, the insurance company has denied payment for the repairs.

1. [ ] (A) If the company‘s car was damaged by the sandstorm, the company would
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) Were the company’s car damaged by the sandstorm, the company should
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) If the company’s car were damaged by the sandstorm, the company will
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) If the company’s car was damaged by the sandstorm, the company may
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) Had the company’s car been damaged by the sandstorm, the company could

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



The non-underlined portion of the sentence says that the car was damaged through operator negligence. So, the sentence indicates that the car was not damaged by the sandstorm. Thus, the car’s being damaged by the sandstorm is a hypothetical scenario, not an actual possibility. Since the condition that the car was damaged by the sandstorm and the consequences of the condition are hypothetical, the subjunctive mood must be used in the “if” clause and the conditional mood must be used in the independent clause about the hypothetical consequences of the car’s being damaged in the sandstorm.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>If the company‘s car was damaged by the sandstorm, the company would
</b> have had the car repaired by its insurance company, but because the car was damaged through operator negligence, the insurance company has denied payment for the repairs.



Since the non-underlined portion of the sentence indicates that the car was not damaged by the sandstorm, the use of the indicative “was damaged,” which indicates that the car may in fact have been damaged by the sandstorm does not make sense.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>Were the company’s car damaged by the sandstorm, the company should
</b> have had the car repaired by its insurance company, but because the car was damaged through operator negligence, the insurance company has denied payment for the repairs.



“Were the company’s car damaged” is a subjunctive expression functionally equivalent to “if the company’s car were damaged.” So, while this version does not use the word “if,” it does have a clause that uses the subjunctive and functions as an “if” clause would.


However, since the scenario does not hypothetically exist in the present but, rather, hypothetically existed in the past, the use of “were … damaged” does not make sense. “Had … been damaged” should have been used instead.


Also, “should” is used in the expression of a recommendation or an expectation, not to indicate that a situation is hypothetical. So, “should” should not be used in this context. “Would” should be used instead.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>If the company’s car were damaged by the sandstorm, the company will
</b> have had the car repaired by its insurance company, but because the car was damaged through operator negligence, the insurance company has denied payment for the repairs.



Since the car’s having been damaged by the sandstorm is hypothetical, the use of the subjunctive makes sense. However, since the scenario does not hypothetically exist in the present but, rather, hypothetically existed in the past, the use of “were damaged” does not make sense. “Had been damaged” should have been used instead.


Also, since the condition that the car had been damaged by the sandstorm and the consequences of that condition are hypothetical, the conditional should be used in the main clause that describes the consequence of the condition. However, “will have had” is not in the conditional mood. The formation of the conditional uses “would,” not “will.”


> <b>(D)</b> <b>If the company’s car was damaged by the sandstorm, the company may
</b> have had the car repaired by its insurance company, but because the car was damaged through operator negligence, the insurance company has denied payment for the repairs.



Since the non-underlined portion of the sentence indicates that the car was not damaged by the sandstorm, the use of the indicative “was damaged,” which indicates that the car may in fact have been damaged by the sandstorm does not make sense.


Also, since the scenario in which the car was damaged by the sandstorm is purely hypothetical, it is not the case that the company may have had the car repaired by the insurance company, and so, the use of the indicative “may have had” does not make sense.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E)</b> <b>Had the company’s car been damaged by the sandstorm, the company could
</b> have had the car repaired by its insurance company, but because the car was damaged through operator negligence, the insurance company has denied payment for the repairs.



“Had the company’s car been damaged” is a subjunctive expression functionally equivalent to “if the company’s car had been damaged.” So, while this version does not use the word “if,” it does have a clause that uses the subjunctive and functions as an “if” clause would. That clause is correctly used in combination with the conditional expression “the company could have had” to express that, in a hypothetical situation in which the car was damaged by a sandstorm, the company could have had the car repaired by the insurance company.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Sentence Correction Example 25  

The actor refused to acknowledge the allegations made by the tabloid photographer, saying that <u>doing so will only lend them</u> credence and encourage copycats.

1. [ ] (A) doing so will only lend them
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) to do it would only lend it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) to do this, he would only lend them
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) to do so would only lend them
5. [ ] (E) in so doing, it would be lending them
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



The underlined portion of the sentence refers to an action that has not happened and is not expected to happen. Therefore, the actor’s taking that action is hypothetical, not an actual possibility. So, while there is no “if” clause in the sentence, the sentence is about what would occur in a hypothetical situation, one in which the actor acknowledges the allegations. So, it makes sense to use of the conditional “would … lend” in describing the hypothetical result of the actor’s acknowledging the allegations.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A)</b> The actor refused to acknowledge the allegations made by the tabloid photographer, saying that <b>doing so will only lend them
</b> credence and encourage copycats.



“doing so” refers to “acknowledge the allegations,” an action that the actor does not plan to take. So, the sentence is about what <em>would</em> happen in a hypothetical situation in which the actor is “doing so.” Therefore, the use of the indicative “will … lend,” which expresses what will actually, not hypothetically, happen if the actor acknowledges the allegations does not make sense.


> <b>(B)</b> The actor refused to acknowledge the allegations made by the tabloid photographer, saying that <b>to do it would only lend it
</b> credence and encourage copycats.



While this version correctly uses the conditional “would … lend” in describing a hypothetical situation, it has pronoun use issues since, in neither instance in which the pronoun “it” appears, does “it” have a clear referent.


While, conceivably, the first “it” could refer to “acknowledge the allegations,” the expression “to do so” is considered correct, while the expression “to do it,” in which “do it” refers to an action, is not preferred by the GMAT.


The second “it” does not have any possible logical referent. If the second “it” were also to refer to “acknowledge the allegations,” the sentence would convey the nonsensical meaning that to acknowledge the allegations would lend acknowledging the allegations credence, and there is in the sentence no singular noun to which “it” could refer. “It” would not refer to “tabloid photographer,” because “it” is not used in referring to people.


> <b>(C)</b> The actor refused to acknowledge the allegations made by the tabloid photographer, saying that <b>to do this, he would only lend them
</b> credence and encourage copycats.



While this version correctly uses the conditional “would … lend” in describing a hypothetical situation, it has some clear flaws.”


While conceivably the “do this” could refer to “acknowledge the allegations,” the expression “to do so” is considered correct, while the expression “to do this,” in which “do this” refers to an action, is not preferred by the GMAT.


Also, “them” must refer to “allegations,” and “do this” must refer, though possibly not correctly, to “acknowledge the allegations.” Therefore, “to do this, he would only lend them credence and encourage copycats” conveys the nonsensical meaning that, in order to acknowledge the allegations, the actor would only lend the allegations credence and encourage copycats.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D)</b> The actor refused to acknowledge the allegations made by the tabloid photographer, saying that <b>to do so would only lend them
</b> credence and encourage copycats.



“To do so” refers to “acknowledge the allegations,” an action that the actor does not plan to take. So, the sentence is about what <em>would</em> happen in a hypothetical situation in which the actor chooses “to do so.” Therefore, the use of the conditional “would … lend,” which expresses what would happen, but won’t actually happen, makes sense.


Also, “them” logically refers to “the allegations.”


> <b>(E)</b> The actor refused to acknowledge the allegations made by the tabloid photographer, saying that <b>in so doing, it would be lending them
</b> credence and encourage copycats.



While this version correctly uses the conditional “would be lending” in describing a hypothetical situation, it has a pronoun use issue since the pronoun “it” does not have a logical referent. Since the one “doing” something must be the actor, “in so doing” has to modify what the actor would achieve “in so doing.” Therefore, “would be lending” must be what the actor would achieve “in so doing.” However, “it,” the subject of “would be lending,” would not refer to “the actor.”


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

Let’s now discuss the topic of verb voice.
