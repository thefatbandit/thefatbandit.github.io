## Lesson Content
In addition to using variations in verb tense and verb mood to test our skill in assessing the effectiveness of sentences, SC questions use verb voice.

There are two verb voices, active voice and passive voice.

Active voice is the more commonly used verb voice.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When the subject of a clause performs the action, the corresponding verb is in the active voice.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the sentence below:

>         S       V            DO

> <em>The <u>dog</u> <u>caught</u> the <u>ball</u>. </em>

In this sentence, we observe the most common sentence structure in the English language: subject, verb, direct object.

In the passive voice, the subject of the clause has an action being done to it.

>          S          V                      O

> <em>The <u>ball</u> <u>was caught</u> by the <u>dog</u>.</em>

In this passive voice construction, what was initially the direct object, “the ball,” now acts as the subject, followed by a passive voice construction of the verb “to catch,” and the former subject, “dog,” becomes the object of a prepositional phrase.

Note that the passive voice form of the verb is always the past participle preceded by a form of the helping verb “to be.” So, in this case, the passive verb is “was caught.”

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When the subject of a sentence has an action being done to it, the corresponding verb is in the passive voice. The passive voice is composed of a form of “to be” followed by the past participle of the main verb.
{{< /alert >}}

Although both the active voice and passive voice versions of the example above are grammatically correct, the two sentences are different because they are focused on different things.

Let’s look at some other examples of passive voice vs.

> This type of disruptive behavior is taken very seriously by the school.

The sentence is in the passive voice.

> The school takes this type of disruptive behavior very seriously.

Now the subject is “the school,” the verb is “takes,” and the object is “this type of disruptive behavior.” Both sentences are grammatically correct, but you’ll notice that the second sentence—the one in the active voice—is a bit more concise.

Let’s look at one more example:

> The paper was written by a PhD candidate, but its controversial premise earned it a lot of attention in the press.

Let’s focus on the main clause; it’s in the passive voice.

> A PhD candidate wrote the paper, which had a controversial premise that earned it a lot of attention in the press.

Both sentences are grammatically correct.
