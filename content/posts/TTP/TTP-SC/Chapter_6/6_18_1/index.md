## Lesson Content
Most of the time, the active voice is preferred to the passive voice, because in most cases, sentences are more concise and convey their meanings more clearly when they are written in active voice than when they are written in passive voice.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Most of the time, the active voice is preferred to the passive voice.
{{< /alert >}}

For example, notice that the active voice version of the two sentences below does a much better job of conveying what occurred than the convoluted and awkward passive voice version does.

> <br/>
<u>Active Voice:</u> In 1974, daredevil Evel Knievel mounted a rocket bike and sought to jump across the Snake River Canyon.

> <u>Passive Voice</u>: In 1974, a rocket bike was mounted and to jump across the Snake River Canyon was sought by daredevil Evel Knievel.

In the following two sentences, the version written in the active voice is more clear and concise:

> <u>Passive Voice</u>: By November of this year, the project will have been completely finished by the software developers.

> <u>Active Voice</u>: By November of this year, the software developers will have completely finished the project.

How can we use this knowledge to score points on test day? Unless there is a clear reason (see the discussion below) to use the passive voice or unless each version written in the active voice contains clear mistakes, choose the active voice over the passive voice.

Let’s now talk about instances in which the passive voice is preferred.

The instances in which the passive voice is preferred over the active voice will tend be those in which the passive voice helps to draw attention to the more important noun in the sentence.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The instances in which the passive voice is preferred over the active voice will tend be those in which the passive voice helps to draw attention to the more important noun in the sentence.
{{< /alert >}}

<u>When a Sentence is About Findings or Scientific Research</u>

When a sentence is about findings or scientific research, it may make sense to put the person or people doing the research in the background and bring the findings to the forefront.

> The results of the groundbreaking research <strong>were released</strong> to the public this morning by university officials.

In the sentence above, the focus is appropriately placed on the “results of the groundbreaking research,” not on the unnamed officials who released them.

> University officials <strong>released</strong> the results of the groundbreaking research to the public this morning.

The focus of the sentence has shifted from one sentence to the next, but both sentences are grammatically sound and effectively convey logical meanings.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When we report on findings or scientific research, we typically want to put the person or people doing the research in the background and bring the findings to the forefront. The passive voice helps us to accomplish this goal.
{{< /alert >}}

<u>When the Goal of a Sentence is to Emphasize the Receiver of an Action</u>

Another instance in which it makes sense to use the passive voice is when the goal of a sentence is to emphasize the receiver of an action over the action itself.

Consider the following example:

> The entire village <strong>was wiped out</strong> by the tsunami.

One could say, “The tsunami wiped out the entire village,” but because we have a natural event “doing” the action, we are just as likely to see the passive voice used in order to place the emphasis on what was affected by the event rather than on the event.

> My car <strong>was totaled</strong> in the accident, so now I need to buy a new one.

The important point is that the person no longer has a car and has to purchase a new one, so it makes sense to keep the emphasis on “my car” rather than on what totaled the car.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

We often place the emphasis on the thing receiving the action, rather than on the doer, when the action is the result of an event or force of nature.
{{< /alert >}}

<u>When the Doer of an Action is Unidentified in a Sentence</u>

Sometimes the doer of an action is unidentified in a sentence, either because the doer is not important or not known.

> Traditionally, stories <strong>are divided</strong> into three parts: beginning, middle, and end.

> Steering wheel locks <strong>are used</strong> to prevent car theft.

In reading the first example, we perhaps can assume that the doer of the action is “people in general,” but it is not necessary to spell that fact out for the sentence meaning to be clear.

> With the rise of McCarthyism during the 1950s, many people <strong>were blacklisted </strong>despite scant evidence that they belonged to the Communist Party.

We may know, based on our knowledge of history, who did the blacklisting, but the doer is not relevant to the meaning of the sentence.

> In the 19th century, before its adverse effects <strong>were known</strong>, cocaine <strong>was used</strong> for everything from pain relief and appetite suppression to local anesthesia and anemia treatment.

The main point of the sentence above is to identify the many uses of cocaine during the 19th century, not to identify who was using the drug.

Another case in which we often use passive voice is when stating a general truth.

> Rules <strong>are made</strong> to be broken.

> Trust <strong>is earned</strong>, not bought.

In these instances, the doer is figurative and implied.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Sometimes the doer of an action is unidentified in a sentence, because the doer is either not important or not known. In such situations, the passive voice is often used.
{{< /alert >}}

<u>Sentences about the Ingredients or Contents of Something</u>

Finally, the passive voice is often used in sentences about the ingredients or contents of something, which use phrases such as “made of,” “made up of,” “composed of,” and “filled with,” preceded by a form of the verb “to be.” We use the passive voice in these cases to keep the emphasis on the whole rather than its parts.

> Our solar system <strong>is made up of</strong> eight planets rotating around a single sun.

> Cakes <strong>are made of</strong> flour, sugar, butter, and baking soda.

> The Greater Antilles <strong>is composed of</strong> Cuba, Haiti, the Dominican Republic, Puerto Rico, Jamaica, and the Cayman Islands.

> The house <strong>was filled with</strong> love.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The passive voice is often used in sentences about the ingredients or contents of something, which use phrases such as “made of,” “made up of,” “composed of,” and “filled with,” preceded by a form of the verb “to be.” We use the passive voice in these cases to keep the emphasis on the whole rather than on its parts.

##  Sentence Correction Example 26  

In the Australian town of Coober Pedy, <u>residents are allowed by subterranean homes and businesses to escape summer temperatures, where 110 degrees Fahrenheit is often reached</u>.

1. [ ] (A) residents are allowed by subterranean homes and businesses to escape summer temperatures, where 110 degrees Fahrenheit is often reached
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) subterranean homes and businesses allow residents in escaping the 110 degrees Fahrenheit often reached by summer temperatures
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) summer temperatures that often reach 110 degrees Fahrenheit are escaped by residents, as allowed by subterranean homes and businesses
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) subterranean homes and businesses allow residents to escape summer temperatures that often reach 110 degrees Fahrenheit
5. [ ] (E) escaping summer temperatures by residents, which often reach 110 degrees Fahrenheit, is allowed by subterranean homes and businesses
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A)</b> In the Australian town of Coober Pedy, <b>residents are allowed by subterranean homes and businesses to escape summer temperatures, where 110 degrees Fahrenheit is often reached
</b>.



We have to remember that, unless there is a clear reason to use the passive voice in a GMAT SC sentence, the active voice is preferred. For instance, if the use of the passive voice in a particular context helps to make the sentence meaning clear, then we would have good reason to use the passive voice. 


In this case, however, the use of the passive “residents <em>are allowed by</em> subterranean


homes and businesses to escape …” actually makes the sentence meaning somewhat unclear and even comical. The passive voice in this context seems to indicate that subterranean homes and businesses are actively giving residents permission to escape summer temperatures, a nonsensical meaning. 


This use of the passive voice is a big red flag that we should search for a better answer choice.


Additionally, it does not make sense to use “where” to refer to “summer temperatures,” since temperatures are not a place. The relative pronoun “which” would make sense in this context.


> <b>(B)</b> In the Australian town of Coober Pedy, <b>subterranean homes and businesses allow residents in escaping the 110 degrees Fahrenheit often reached by summer temperatures
</b>.



The statement that “subterranean homes and businesses <em>allow residents in escaping</em> the 110 degrees Fahrenheit” conveys the nonsensical meaning that homes and businesses themselves are escaping the temperatures and that in doing so, those homes and businesses “allow residents.”  


Logically, the residents, not the homes and businesses, are doing the escaping, and thus it would make sense to say that “subterranean homes and businesses <em>allow residents to escape.</em>”


Furthermore, it is rather strange to say that the thing being escaped is the “110 degrees Fahrenheit,” rather than the “summer temperatures.” One would seek to escape the temperatures, not the degrees. Rephrasing the sentence to say “summer temperatures that often reach 110 degrees Fahrenheit” or something similar would result in a more logical meaning conveyed.


> <b>(C)</b> In the Australian town of Coober Pedy, <b>summer temperatures that often reach 110 degrees Fahrenheit are escaped by residents, as allowed by subterranean homes and businesses
</b>.



Although it is not grammatically incorrect or illogical to use the passive voice in “summer temperatures that often reach 110 degrees Fahrenheit <em>are escaped by </em>residents,” unless we have a particular reason to do so (for instance, if no other answer choice results in a grammatically correct sentence), the active voice is preferred. 


Additionally, this choice actually does contain an error in meaning: saying that the temperatures “are escaped by residents, <em>as allowed by</em> subterranean homes and businesses” seems to indicate that the homes and businesses are actively deciding to allow residents to escape or granting the residents permission to escape. Clearly, such a meaning is nonsensical.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D)</b> In the Australian town of Coober Pedy, <b>subterranean homes and businesses allow residents to escape summer temperatures that often reach 110 degrees Fahrenheit
</b>.



 This choice produces a sentence with a clear and logical meaning. Notice that this choice uses the active “allow” and “reach,” making crystal clear which nouns are performing which actions in the sentence, without attributing nonsensical actions to inanimate things, as we see in some other choices. 


The sentence contains no grammatical errors and effectively conveys a meaning that makes sense. 


> <b>(E)</b> In the Australian town of Coober Pedy, <b>escaping summer temperatures by residents, which often reach 110 degrees Fahrenheit, is allowed by subterranean homes and businesses
</b>.



This choice uses prepositions and pronouns in ways that create unclear and illogical meanings. First, “summer temperatures <em>by</em> residents” seems to indicate that the residents create the temperatures, a nonsensical meaning.


Moreover, since the modifier “which often reach 110 degrees Fahrenheit” is placed after “by residents,” rather than after “temperatures,” the sentence seems to convey the illogical meaning that the resident, rather than the temperatures “reach 110 degrees Fahrenheit.”


Even if we cross off the relative clause beginning with “which,” the phrasing “escaping summer temperatures by residents … is allowed by subterranean homes and businesses” does not make entirely clear who exactly is doing the “escaping.”


Thus, we should look for a version that conveys a clearer, more logical meaning.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Sentence Correction Example 27  

The process of refilling the automobile’s tank with hydrogen takes about five minutes, and once the process is complete, <u>hydrogen will have stored a quantity of energy sufficient for powering the automobile for about 500 kilometers of travel in the automobile’s tank</u>.

1. [ ] (A) hydrogen will have stored a quantity of energy sufficient for powering the automobile for about 500 kilometers of travel in the automobile’s tank
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) a quantity of energy sufficient for powering the automobile for about 500 kilometers of travel is stored in the automobile’s tank of hydrogen
3. [ ] (C) the hydrogen in the tank keeps in storage a quantity of energy sufficient for powering the automobile for about 500 kilometers of travel
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) hydrogen has stored a quantity of energy sufficient for powering the automobile for about 500 kilometers of travel in the automobile’s tank
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) a quantity of energy sufficient for powering the automobile for about 500 kilometers of travel will have been stored in the automobile’s tank by hydrogen
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



None of the versions of the sentence that use active voice main verbs convey a meaning as logical as that conveyed by the version that uses a passive voice main verb.


> <b>(A)</b> The process of refilling the automobile’s tank with hydrogen takes about five minutes, and once the process is complete, <b>hydrogen will have stored a quantity of energy sufficient for powering the automobile for about 500 kilometers of travel in the automobile’s tank
</b>.



Because it uses the active voice “hydrogen will have stored,” this version suggests that hydrogen will have actively stored energy in the automobile’s tank, as if hydrogen pulled the car up to the fueling station and filled it with fuel.


Also, the placement of the prepositional phrase “in the automobile’s tank” directly after “travel” results in the sentence’s seeming to convey that the 500 kilometers of travel would occur in the automobile’s tank.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B)</b> The process of refilling the automobile’s tank with hydrogen takes about five minutes, and once the process is complete, <b>a quantity of energy sufficient for powering the automobile for about 500 kilometers of travel is stored in the automobile’s tank of hydrogen
</b>.



This version uses the passive voice “is stored” to convey a logical meaning, that, once the fueling process is complete, a certain amount of energy “is stored” in the automobile’s tank. Notice that using the active voice, which would express who or what has stored the energy, is completely unnecessary, because who or what has stored the energy is not germane to what the sentence is expressing. The point is simply that the energy is stored. So, the passive voice works well, and of all the versions of the sentence, this one makes the most sense.


> <b>(C)</b> The process of refilling the automobile’s tank with hydrogen takes about five minutes, and once the process is complete, <b>the hydrogen in the tank keeps in storage a quantity of energy sufficient for powering the automobile for about 500 kilometers of travel
</b>.



The use of the active voice wording “hydrogen … keeps in storage” does not make sense in this context. Energy is stored in hydrogen or stored in the tank, but hydrogen does not keep energy “in storage,” as if hydrogen has a warehouse or a closet in which hydrogen keeps energy in storage while the car is operating.


> <b>(D)</b> The process of refilling the automobile’s tank with hydrogen takes about five minutes, and once the process is complete, <b>hydrogen has stored a quantity of energy sufficient for powering the automobile for about 500 kilometers of travel in the automobile’s tank
</b>.



Because it uses the active voice “hydrogen has stored,” this version suggests that hydrogen has actively stored energy in the automobile’s tank, as if hydrogen pulled the car up to the fueling station and filled it with fuel.


Also, the placement of the prepositional phrase “in the automobile’s tank” directly after “travel” results in the sentence’s seeming to convey that the 500 kilometers of travel would occur in the automobile’s tank.


> <b>(E)</b> The process of refilling the automobile’s tank with hydrogen takes about five minutes, and once the process is complete, <b>a quantity of energy sufficient for powering the automobile for about 500 kilometers of travel will have been stored in the automobile’s tank by hydrogen
</b>.



While energy can be stored in hydrogen, this version seems to suggest that filling the car with fuel and storing energy in the tank is done “by hydrogen,” is if the point to be made is not that energy is stored <em>in </em>hydrogen <em>in</em> the tank but rather that somehow hydrogen that is not necessarily in the automobile’s tank actively stores energy, not within itself, but in the automobile’s tank.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
