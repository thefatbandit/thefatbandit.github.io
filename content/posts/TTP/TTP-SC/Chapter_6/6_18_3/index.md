## Lesson Content
One theme that we’ve discussed often is how we’ll score the most points on SC questions when we seek out the most significant mistakes we can find and then eliminate the sentence versions containing them.

<strong><u>Strategic Reminder</u></strong><strong>:</strong> We’ll score the most points on SC questions when we seek out the most significant mistakes we can find and then eliminate the sentence versions containing them.

This theme is often underscored in questions involving verb voice because there may be times when we’re unsure whether the use of one of the two verb voices alone is sufficient to make a SC answer choice clearly incorrect.

For example, in the following sentence, the use of the active voice, although not ideal, is not sufficient to make the version wrong.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: <strong>While full of amazing images, </strong><strong>publishing houses rejected</strong> J.

Yes, the above sentence would work better if it were to use the passive voice rather than the active voice, because if the passive voice were used, the emphasis would be on J.

Too often people get caught up with the verb voice issue in a sentence like that, and in doing so, they miss the easy out.

Let’s consider another example sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: <strong>Enrollment </strong>in institutions of higher education <strong>is being sought</strong> by an increasing number of people with aspirations for better lives, <strong>realizing </strong>that knowledge leads to prosperity.

While the above sentence might work better if the active voice rather than the passive voice were used, the use of the passive voice is not clearly incorrect.

Since the closing participial phrase would logically have the subject of the preceding clause, “enrollment,” as its agent, the sentence conveys the nonsensical meaning that “enrollment” is “realizing that knowledge leads to prosperity.” So, this version is clearly incorrect.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Since use of one of the verb voices may not be sufficient to make an answer choice clearly incorrect, in answering these questions we can make the work lighter by seeking out other issues that make a choice clearly incorrect.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 29  

In 1988, <u>operatic singer Carlos Feller had made his debut at the Metropolitan Opera as Don Alfonso, who was regarded as his signature role, and returned</u> there in 1990 as Dr.

1. [ ] (A) operatic singer Carlos Feller had made his debut at the Metropolitan Opera as Don Alfonso, who was regarded as his signature role, and returned
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) operatic singer Carlos Feller made his debut at the Metropolitan Opera as who was regarded as his signature role, Don Alfonso, and later he returned
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) the debut at the Metropolitan Opera of operatic singer Carlos Feller was made as Don Alfonso, regarded as his signature role, and returned
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) operatic singer Carlos Feller made his debut at the Metropolitan Opera, in what was regarded as his signature role, Don Alfonso, and he returned
5. [ ] (E) the debut at the Metropolitan Opera of operatic singer Carlos Feller had been made as Don Alfonso, which was regarded as his signature role, later returning
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



In this question, the active voice version of the main clause “Carlos Feller made his debut” is clearly superior to the passive voice version “the debut … of Carlos Feller was made” because the passive voice version puts the focus of the sentence on the debut rather than on the sentence’s main topic, Carlos Feller, the passive voice version is awkward, and the passive voice wording does not clearly convey that Carlos Feller was in the role of Don Alfonso.


Having noticed that the active voice versions are better, we can use other issues to choose between the active voice versions and find the one correct answer.


> <b>(A)</b> In 1988, <b>operatic singer Carlos Feller had made his debut at the Metropolitan Opera as Don Alfonso, who was regarded as his signature role, and returned
</b> there in 1990 as Dr. Bartolo in Rossini's <em>Il Barbiere di Siviglia</em>.



While two events are mentioned, Carlos Feller’s making his debut and his returning, the use of the past perfect “had made” is unnecessary for making the time sequence clear and results in the sentence’s conveying a somewhat illogical meaning, as the dates of the events are mentioned, so the time sequence is clear without the use of the past perfect. Read literally, the main clause seems to convey that, in 1988, Carlos Feller had already made his debut, a meaning that does not seem to be what the author means to convey.


Also, since a role is a thing, not a person, it does not really make sense to use “who” to refer to the role “Don Alfonso.”


Also, since the sentence opens with “in 1988,” the sentence is essentially “in 1988 … Carlos Feller had made … and returned … in 1990,” conveying that, in 1988, Carlos Feller returned in 1990. This issue could easily be missed by a test-taker who does not consider the non-underlined portion along with the answer choice.


> <b>(B)</b> In 1988, <b>operatic singer Carlos Feller made his debut at the Metropolitan Opera as who was regarded as his signature role, Don Alfonso, and later he returned
</b> there in 1990 as Dr. Bartolo in Rossini's <em>Il Barbiere di Siviglia</em>.



Since a role is a thing, not a person, it does not really make sense to use “who” to refer to the role “Don Alfonso” or to say that Don Alfonso was “who was regarded as his signature role.”


Also, “later” in “later he returned there in 1990” is redundant, and the use of “later” is illogical, as, the use of the dates 1990 and 1988 is sufficient for conveying that one event happened after the other.


> <b>(C)</b> In 1988, <b>the debut at the Metropolitan Opera of operatic singer Carlos Feller was made as Don Alfonso, regarded as his signature role, and returned
</b> there in 1990 as Dr. Bartolo in Rossini's <em>Il Barbiere di Siviglia</em>.



The passive voice wording “the debut … was made” is suboptimal, since it places the focus of the sentence on the debut rather than on Carlos Feller, while Carlos Feller seems to be meant to be the main topic of the sentence, given what the sentence says about the debut, the role and his returning in 1990.


Also, the wording “the debut … was made as Don Alfonso” does not clearly convey that Carlos Feller was in the role of Don Alfonso, instead conveying the nonsensical meaning that the debut itself somehow “was made as Don Alfonso.”


Furthermore, “the debut … was made … and returned” conveys the nonsensical meaning that the debut returned in 1990.


Finally, since the sentence opens with “in 1988,” the sentence is essentially “in 1988, the debut ... was made … and returned … in 1990,” conveying that, in 1988, the debut returned in 1990. This issue could easily be missed by a test-taker who does not consider the non-underlined portion along with the answer choice.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D)</b> In 1988, <b>operatic singer Carlos Feller made his debut at the Metropolitan Opera, in what was regarded as his signature role, Don Alfonso, and he returned
</b> there in 1990 as Dr. Bartolo in Rossini's <em>Il Barbiere di Siviglia</em>.



The active voice wording “Carlos Feller made his debut … and returned” effectively conveys a meaning that makes sense.


Also, using the pronoun “what,” rather than “who,” to refer to “his signature role, Don Alfonso” makes sense, as a role is a thing, not a person.


> <b>(E)</b> In 1988, <b>the debut at the Metropolitan Opera of operatic singer Carlos Feller had been made as Don Alfonso, which was regarded as his signature role, later returning
</b> there in 1990 as Dr. Bartolo in Rossini's <em>Il Barbiere di Siviglia</em>.



The passive voice wording “the debut … had been made” is suboptimal, as it places the focus of the sentence on the debut rather than on Carlos Feller, while Carlos Feller seems to be meant to be the main topic of the sentence, given what the sentence says about the debut, the role and his returning in 1990.


Also, the wording “the debut … had been made as Don Alfonso” does not clearly convey that Carlos Feller was in the role of Don Alfonso, instead conveying the nonsensical meaning that the debut itself somehow “had been made as Don Alfonso.”


Furthermore, while two events are mentioned, the making of Carlos Feller’s debut and his returning, the use of the past perfect “had been made” is unnecessary for making the time sequence clear and results in the sentence’s conveying a somewhat illogical meaning, since the dates of the events are mentioned, and so, the time sequence is clear without the use of the past perfect. Read literally, the main clause seems to convey that, in 1988, Carlos Feller’s debut had already been made, a meaning that does not seem to be what the author means to convey.


Finally, the sentence is essentially, “in 1988 … the debut … had been made … later returning … in 1990,” and thus this version conveys the illogical meaning that the debut returned in 1990.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
