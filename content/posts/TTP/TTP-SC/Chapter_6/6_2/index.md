## Lesson Content
Despite its name, the present tense is not most commonly used in describing events that are presently occurring.

Consider the examples below:

> The red heart <strong>is</strong> a universal symbol of love.

> Sara <strong>loves</strong> going to the beach.

> The dogs <strong>enjoy </strong>running in the woods.

> Pigs <strong>don’t like </strong>too much direct sunlight on their sensitive skin.

> Brazilians <strong>celebrate </strong>Carnaval every year.

> Rome <strong>is </strong>the capital city of Italy.

> Eating pizza <strong>is</strong> not healthy.

> Flossing your teeth <strong>is</strong> a fun practice.

Notice that, in these example sentences, the present tense verbs are used for conveying facts that are true across times.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

We typically use the present tense to describe things that happen routinely or to discuss facts and universal truths that do not change over time.
{{< /alert >}}

Also, we often see present tense verbs used to describe policies, laws, rules, and regulations that were enacted at some point in the past but still have their effects in the present.

For example:

> Originally written in 1791, the 1st Amendment to the United States Constitution, which <strong>makes</strong> it illegal for Congress to pass any law that <strong>infringes</strong> upon citizen’s rights to freedom of speech, is often a topic of debate these days.

Notice that the verbs “makes,” and “infringes” are in the present tense because, although the 1st amendment was written in 1791, its effects are still in place today.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Present tense verbs are often used to describe policies, laws, rules, and regulations that were enacted at some point in the past but still have their effects in the present.
{{< /alert >}}
