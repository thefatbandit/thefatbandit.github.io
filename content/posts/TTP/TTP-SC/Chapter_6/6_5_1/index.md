## Lesson Content
When faced with an SC question involving verb tense, one powerful strategy that we can utilize is to use verb context clues in the sentence versions to eliminate those with verb tenses that simply can’t work together.

For example, consider the following three sentences.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Each time I <u>traveled</u> to Colombia, I <strong>visit</strong> Medellín and Cartagena.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Each time <u>will travel</u> to Colombia, I <strong>visit</strong> Medellín and Cartagena.

> <u>Correct</u>: Each time I <u>travel</u> to Colombia, I <strong>visit</strong> Medellín and Cartagena.

The verb clue here is the present tense verb “visit.” Remember that present tense verbs can describe not only actions that are occurring in the current moment, but also actions that occur regularly or are not subject to the passage of time.

The first sentence version does not make sense because the past tense verb “traveled” describes an action that has ended in the past.

The second sentence version does not make sense either because the future tense verb “will travel” describes an action that has not yet happened.

The third sentence version makes sense because the verbs “travel” and “visit” are both in the simple present and describe actions that happen with regularity.

Let’s look at another version of the sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Each time I <u>will travel</u> to Colombia, I <strong>visited</strong> Medellín and Cartagena.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Each time I <u>travel</u> to Colombia, I <strong>visited</strong> Medellín and Cartagena.

> <u>Correct</u>: Each time I <u>traveled</u> to Colombia, I <strong>visited</strong> Medellín and Cartagena.

The verb clue now is the past tense “visited,” and, thus, we can tell that the sentence is about actions that, although habitual, began and ended in the past.

The first sentence version does not make sense because the future tense verb “will travel” describes an action that has not yet happened.

The second sentence version does not make sense either because the present tense verb “travel” conveys that I regularly go to Colombia.

The third sentence version makes sense because it uses the past tense “traveled” and the past tense “visited.” Both of the actions ended in the past, so there is no confliction between the verb tenses.

Let’s examine one more example sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: I <u>traveled</u> to Colombia every summer, and I <strong>will </strong>visit Medellín and Cartagena during those trips.

> <u>Correct</u>: I <u>will travel</u> to Colombia every summer, and I <strong>will </strong>visit Medellín and Cartagena during those trips.

The future tense “will visit” is our clue, and it indicates that the sentence is about an action that will occur in the future.

The first sentence version does not make sense because the past tense verb “traveled” describes an action that occurred in the past.

The second sentence version makes sense because both “will travel” and “will visit” are in the future tense.

So, we can see that using verb context clues in sentences is a very smart way to eliminate sentence versions that can’t possibly be correct.
