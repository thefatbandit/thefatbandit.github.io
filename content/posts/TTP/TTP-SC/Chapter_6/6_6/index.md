## Lesson Content
When an event began in the past and continues into the present, we use the present perfect tense.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When an action began in the past and continues into the present, we use the present perfect tense
{{< /alert >}}

The present perfect tense is formed by combining “has” or “have” with the past participle of the main verb.

> I <strong>have lived</strong> here for five years.

The above sentence conveys that the event “lived” began in the past but is still happening in the present moment.

> Since 1904, the Explorers’ Club <strong>has served</strong> as a meeting point and unifying force for explorers and scientists worldwide.

The use of the present perfect “has served” indicates that the action, which began in 1904, is still occurring today.

> She <strong>has won</strong> the gold medal in the school spelling bee twice.

Notice that the present perfect “has won” is used in describing the accomplishment of winning the school spelling bee.

> He <strong>has been</strong> to Istanbul six times.

In the sentence above, the present perfect tense is used to describe past actions that have implications in the present moment.

> She <strong>has wrecked</strong> the car again.

In this final example, the present perfect tense is used to emphasize that, although the wreck happened in the past, it has implications in the present moment.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Present perfect tense = “have” or “has” + past participle.
{{< /alert >}}

Just as we saw with the simple past, present, and future tenses, commonly used context clues in a sentence will tell us if we must use (or can’t use) the present perfect tense.
