## Lesson Content
On the GMAT, when “since” is used to convey that a subject has engaged in an action from the past to the present, the present perfect tense must be used.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When “since” is used to convey that a subject has engaged in an action from the past to the present, the present perfect tense must be used.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the examples below.

> She <strong>has worked</strong> with a vocal coach ever <strong>since</strong> her early years as a singer.

The subject is no longer in her early years as a singer.

> <strong>Since</strong> the day she met Bernard, she <strong>has been</strong> happier than ever.

The sentence above describes the subject’s current mood, which began on a day in the past.

So, how do we put this knowledge into action on test day? We eliminate any sentence version that uses “since” to convey that a subject has engaged in an action from the past to the present YET does not use the present perfect tense.

<strong><u>Key Strategy</u></strong><strong>:</strong> Eliminate any sentence version that uses “since” to convey that a subject has engaged in an action from the past to the present YET does not use the present perfect tense.

Let’s continue practicing using context clues to eliminate sentences that can’t be correct:

> <u>Incorrect</u>:<strong> Since</strong> going to that seminar last week, he <u>was</u> much more motivated to improve his health.

> <u>Incorrect</u>:<strong> Since</strong> going to that seminar last week, he <u>will be</u> much more motivated to improve his health.

> <u>Correct</u>:<strong> Since</strong> going to that seminar last week, he <u>has been</u> much more motivated to improve his health.

In sentence version one, the past tense “was” does not make sense because “was” describes an action that ended in the past, but the sentence is attempting to describe something that continues in the present.

Similarly, the second sentence version is broken because the future tense “will be” describes an action that has not yet started, but the sentence is attempting to describe something that continues in the present.

The third sentence version works well because “since” indicates that the subject began to be motivated in the past and continues to be motivated in the present.

Let’s continue to practice eliminating sentence versions with verb tenses that can’t be correct:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The food served at the restaurant <u>became</u> much more interesting <strong>since</strong> the head chef returned from her research trip to Asia.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The food served at the restaurant <u>will become</u> much more interesting <strong>since</strong> the head chef returned from her research trip to Asia.

> <u>Correct</u>: The food served at the restaurant <u>has become</u> much more interesting <strong>since</strong> the head chef returned from her research trip to Asia.

Sentence version one uses “since” to attempt to tell us that the food began to become more interesting at the point in time when the chef returned and that this development continues into the present, but it illogically also uses the past tense “became,” which conveys the food’s getting more interesting has already ended.

Sentence version two is also illogical because the use of the future tense “will become” describes something that has not yet happened.

Sentence version three works well because “has become” works logically with “since” to convey that the food began to become more interesting at the point in time when the chef returned and that this development continues into the present.

##  Sentence Correction Example 3  

<u>Since he was nine years old, Hank is dreaming</u> of meeting his favorite basketball player, so now that he is finally going to, it is no surprise that he is nervous.

1. [ ] (A) Since he was nine years old, Hank is dreaming
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) Since nine years old, Hank dreamed
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) Since he was nine years old, Hank had dreamed
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) Since nine years old, Hank is dreaming
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) Since he was nine years old, Hank has dreamed

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Remember, when “since” is used to convey that a subject has engaged in an action “from the past point in time when” and continues to engage in that action in the present, it makes sense to use the present perfect tense.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Since he was nine years old, Hank is dreaming
</b> of meeting his favorite basketball player, so now that he is finally going to, it is no surprise that he is nervous.



This version illogically uses the present continuous “is dreaming” in describing an action that Hank has engaged in “since he was nine years old.”


Notice that the keyword “since” and the past tense verb “was” tell us that the action “dreaming” started in the past. On the other hand, the present continuous “is dreaming” tells us that the action is currently ongoing; it does not make any connection to the past starting point when “he was nine years old.”


> <b>(B)</b> <b>Since nine years old, Hank dreamed
</b> of meeting his favorite basketball player, so now that he is finally going to, it is no surprise that he is nervous.



“Since” means “from the past point in time when,” but “nine years old” is not a past point in time. “Nine years old” is a modifier used to modify a noun in an expression such as “he was nine years old.” So, “since nine years old” does not make sense.


Additionally, the simple past tense “dreamed” does not capture the logical meaning that Hank’s dreaming began at a past point and continues to the present, which meaning would be in line with “now that he is finally going to, it is no surprise that he is nervous.” “Dreamed” communicates that Hank dreamed of meeting his favorite basketball player in the past but no longer does.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>Since he was nine years old, Hank had dreamed
</b> of meeting his favorite basketball player, so now that he is finally going to, it is no surprise that he is nervous.



The use of the past perfect tense “had dreamed” does not make sense in this context.


The past perfect “had dreamed” indicates that, at some point in the past, Hank dreamed of meeting his favorite basketball player and that he no longer dreams of meeting his favorite basketball player.


Of course, if he no longer dreams of meeting his favorite basketball player, then the sentence is illogical. After all, the sentence says that “it is no surprise that he is nervous,” but the fact that Hank had dreamed of meeting his favorite basketball player but no longer does would not be a reason for him to be nervous “now that he is finally going to” meet him.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>Since nine years old, Hank is dreaming
</b> of meeting his favorite basketball player, so now that he is finally going to, it is no surprise that he is nervous.



“Since” means “from the past point in time when,” but “nine years old” is not a past point in time. “Nine years old” is a modifier used to modify a noun in an expression such as “he was nine years old.” So, “since nine years old” does not make sense.


Additionally, this version illogically uses the present continuous “is dreaming” in describing an action that Hank has engaged in “since nine years old.”


Notice that “since nine years old” seems meant to indicate that the action “dreaming” started in the past. However, present continuous tense tells us that the action is currently ongoing; it does not make any connection to any past starting point.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(E)</b> <b>Since he was nine years old, Hank has dreamed
</b> of meeting his favorite basketball player, so now that he is finally going to, it is no surprise that he is nervous.



“Since he was nine years old” makes sense. “Since” means “from the past point in time when,” and when “he was nine years old” is a point in time. So, “since” and “he was nine years old” work logically together.


Also, this version correctly uses the present perfect tense “has dreamed” to indicate that an action began in the past, “since he was nine years old,” and continues in the present, which meaning is in line with “now that he is finally going to, it is no surprise that he is nervous.”


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Sentence Correction Example 4  

Since the early weeks of 1970, conspiracy theorists and alien <u>enthusiasts are debating the authenticity of audio recordings released by NASA, which supposedly show that</u> astronauts on the far side of the moon, the side of the moon beyond radio contact with Earth, in May 1969, listening to unexplained music, potentially of extra-terrestrial origin.

1. [ ] (A) enthusiasts are debating the authenticity of audio recordings released by NASA, which supposedly show that
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) enthusiasts have debated the authenticity of audio recordings released by NASA, which supposedly show
3. [ ] (C) enthusiasts, debating the authenticity of audio recordings released by NASA, supposedly have shown
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) enthusiasts debated the authenticity of audio recordings released by NASA and supposedly showed
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) enthusiasts have debated the authenticity of audio recordings released by NASA, supposedly showing
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



> <b>(A)</b> Since the early weeks of 1970, conspiracy theorists and alien <b>enthusiasts are debating the authenticity of audio recordings released by NASA, which supposedly show that
</b> astronauts on the far side of the moon, the side of the moon beyond radio contact with Earth, in May 1969, listening to unexplained music, potentially of extra-terrestrial origin.



The use of the present continuous “are debating” does not make sense in this context because, while the action “debating” may be ongoing in the present, the sentence indicates that the debating began at a point in time in the past, “the early weeks of 1970.” The present continuous, which indicates that an event is ongoing in the present, does not match a timeframe that began in the past.


Also, the presence of “that” renders the end of the sentence illogical. “Recordings …, which … show that” should be followed by a clause describing what the recordings show, but, in this case, only a noun, “astronauts,” and modifiers that modify “astronauts” follow “show that.” So, basically the sentence says “recordings show that astronauts” without a verb that expresses what the astronauts did. “Listening,” a participle, cannot on its own serve as a main verb and simply modifies “astronauts.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B)</b> Since the early weeks of 1970, conspiracy theorists and alien <b>enthusiasts have debated the authenticity of audio recordings released by NASA, which supposedly show
</b> astronauts on the far side of the moon, the side of the moon beyond radio contact with Earth, in May 1969, listening to unexplained music, potentially of extra-terrestrial origin.



Because it makes sense that the conspiracy theorists and alien enthusiasts would have debated the authenticity of the recordings from the early weeks of 1970 to the present, the use of the present perfect “have debated” makes sense.


Also, since it makes sense that the recordings would still exist in the present and still show astronauts on the far side of the moon listening to music, the use of the present tense “show” makes sense.


Finally, “which supposedly show …” correctly modifies the phrase “audio recordings released by NASA.”


> <b>(C)</b> Since the early weeks of 1970, conspiracy theorists and alien <b>enthusiasts, debating the authenticity of audio recordings released by NASA, supposedly have shown
</b> astronauts on the far side of the moon, the side of the moon beyond radio contact with Earth, in May 1969, listening to unexplained music, potentially of extra-terrestrial origin.



While this version is grammatically correct. However, it expresses a meaning that does not make sense, as we can see by combining this answer choice with the non-underlined portion of the sentence:


Since the early weeks of 1970, conspiracy theorists and <strong>alien enthusiasts, debating the authenticity of audio recordings released by NASA, supposedly have shown</strong> astronauts on the far side of the moon … in May 1969


Notice that this version conveys the meaning that, in the process of debating the authenticity of audio recordings, conspiracy theorists and alien enthusiasts have shown astronauts on the far side of the moon in May 1969. People would not show astronauts by debating, and would not, by debating since the early weeks of 1970, show astronauts on the far side of the moon in May 1969.


> <b>(D)</b> Since the early weeks of 1970, conspiracy theorists and alien <b>enthusiasts debated the authenticity of audio recordings released by NASA and supposedly showed
</b> astronauts on the far side of the moon, the side of the moon beyond radio contact with Earth, in May 1969, listening to unexplained music, potentially of extra-terrestrial origin.



The use of the simple past “debated” with “since the early weeks of 1970” is not correct. “Since the early weeks of 1970” sets the beginning of a timeframe that began in 1970 and extents to the present. On the other hand, “debated” conveys that the debating went on only in the past, not up to the present time. So, the simple past tense “debated” does not work logically with “since the early weeks of 1970.”


Also, the sentence conveys the illogical meaning that, since the early weeks of 1970, the conspiracy theorists and alien enthusiasts showed astronauts on the dark side of the moon in 1969.


> <b>(E)</b> Since the early weeks of 1970, conspiracy theorists and alien <b>enthusiasts have debated the authenticity of audio recordings released by NASA, supposedly showing
</b> astronauts on the far side of the moon, the side of the moon beyond radio contact with Earth, in May 1969, listening to unexplained music, potentially of extra-terrestrial origin.



The participial phrase “supposedly showing …” at the end of the sentence set off from the rest of the sentence by a comma would most logically take the subject of the sentence, “conspiracy theorists and alien enthusiasts,” as its agent. Therefore, the sentence expresses the nonsensical meaning that, by debating the authenticity of audio recordings since the early weeks of 1970, conspiracy theorists and alien enthusiasts have shown astronauts on the far side of the moon in 1969.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

##  Sentence Correction Example 5  

According to scientists at NASA’s Goddard Institute for Space Studies, Earth’s average global temperature, <u>as a critical measure of Earth’s ability to sustain the existence of its many species, has increased by roughly 1.4 degrees Fahrenheit since about 1900; nearly two-thirds of that increase have occurred</u> since 1975.

1. [ ] (A) as a critical measure of Earth’s ability to sustain the existence of its many species, has increased by roughly 1.4 degrees Fahrenheit since about 1900; nearly two-thirds of that increase have occurred
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) being a critical measure of Earth’s ability to sustain the existence of its many species, increased by roughly 1.4 degrees Fahrenheit since about 1900, nearly two-thirds of which occurred
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) which is as critical as a measure of Earth’s ability to sustain the existence of its many species, increased by roughly 1.4 degrees Fahrenheit since about 1900; nearly two-thirds of the increase occurred
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) a critical measure of Earth’s ability to sustain the existence of its many species, has increased by roughly 1.4 degrees Fahrenheit since about 1900, nearly two-thirds of the increase having occurred
5. [ ] (E) which is critical when measuring Earth’s ability to sustain the existence of its many species, increased by roughly 1.4 degrees Fahrenheit since about 1900, with nearly two-thirds of that increase occurring
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



> <b>(A)</b> According to scientists at NASA’s Goddard Institute for Space Studies, Earth’s average global temperature, <b>as a critical measure of Earth’s ability to sustain the existence of its many species, has increased by roughly 1.4 degrees Fahrenheit since about 1900; nearly two-thirds of that increase have occurred
</b> since 1975.



While “two-thirds” may seem plural, since what “two-thirds” refers to is “two-thirds” of the increase, which is singular, “two-thirds” should be paired with a singular verb, not the plural “have.” When a fraction of something is a subject, whether that fraction should be paired with a singular verb or a plural verb depends on what the fraction is a fraction of. If it’s a fraction of something singular, then it needs a singular verb, which, in this case, would be “has.”


A more difficult error to see it that the use of the expression “as a critical measure …” does not make sense. By using that expression, the sentence suggests that the increasing of the average global temperature has been somehow tied to or conditioned upon the temperature’s being a critical measure. What makes sense is that the temperature has increased and that, as a separate matter, the temperature is a critical measure. Saying that the temperature has increased “as a critical measure” suggests that the temperature’s being a critical measure has a significance that it does not have.


> <b>(B)</b> According to scientists at NASA’s Goddard Institute for Space Studies, Earth’s average global temperature, <b>being a critical measure of Earth’s ability to sustain the existence of its many species, increased by roughly 1.4 degrees Fahrenheit since about 1900, nearly two-thirds of which occurred
</b> since 1975.



The pronoun, “which” has no logical referent, since the sentence does not mention the temperature increase in the form of a noun, and therefore there is nothing in the sentence to which “which” could refer that would have “occurred since 1975.”


The use of the simple past “increased” and “occurred” is not correct. “Since about 1900” and “since 1975” indicate that the events occurred over periods of time that began in the past and extend to the present, whereas “increased” and “occurred” convey that the events occurred only in the past, not up to the present. So, “increased” and “occurred” do not work logically with “since about 1900” and “since 1975.”


Finally, by using the participial phrase “being a critical measure …,” the sentence conveys the illogical meaning that, as a result of “being a critical measure,” the average global temperature increased.


> <b>(C)</b> According to scientists at NASA’s Goddard Institute for Space Studies, Earth’s average global temperature, <b>which is as critical as a measure of Earth’s ability to sustain the existence of its many species, increased by roughly 1.4 degrees Fahrenheit since about 1900; nearly two-thirds of the increase occurred
</b> since 1975.



By saying,“The average global temperature is as critical as a measure of Earth’s ability …,” this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that the average global temperate is “as critical as,” i.e., as important as, “a measure of Earth’s ability to sustain the existence of its many species.” Since the average global temperature is itself “a measure of Earth’s ability to sustain the existence of its many species,” this version conveys, essentially, that the average global temperature is as critical as itself.


Also, the use of the simple past “increased” and “occurred” is not correct. “Since about 1900” and “since 1975” indicate that the events occurred over periods of time that began in the past and extend to the present, whereas “increased” and “occurred” convey that the events occurred only in the past, not up to the present. So, “increased” and “occurred” do not work logically with “since about 1900” and “since 1975.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D)</b> According to scientists at NASA’s Goddard Institute for Space Studies, Earth’s average global temperature, <b>a critical measure of Earth’s ability to sustain the existence of its many species, has increased by roughly 1.4 degrees Fahrenheit since about 1900, nearly two-thirds of the increase having occurred
</b> since 1975.



This version uses the appositive “a critical measure of Earth’s ability …” to logically describe “the average global temperature” as “a critical measure ….”


Also, it uses the present perfect “has increased” with “since about 1900” and uses “having occurred” with “since 1975” to effectively indicate that the events described have been occurring over time since the points in time mentioned.


> <b>(E)</b> According to scientists at NASA’s Goddard Institute for Space Studies, Earth’s average global temperature, <b>which is critical when measuring Earth’s ability to sustain the existence of its many species, increased by roughly 1.4 degrees Fahrenheit since about 1900, with nearly two-thirds of that increase occurring
</b> since 1975.



This choice could be tempting because “which is critical …” sounds right. Notice, however, that the meaning conveyed is illogical. The average global temperature may be an important, or “critical,” measure. However, the average global temperature “is critical when measuring” conveys the meaning that, when the average global temperature is measuring, it is critical, suggesting that, when it is not measuring, it is not critical.


Also, the use of the simple past “increased” is not correct. “Since about 1900” indicates that the event occurred over a period of time that began in the past and extends to the present, whereas “increased” conveys that the event occurred only in the past, not up to the present. So, “increased” does not work logically with “since about 1900.”


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
