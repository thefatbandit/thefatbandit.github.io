## Lesson Content
The past perfect tense is used in describing a past action that occurred before another past action.

The past perfect tense = “had” + the past participle of a verb.

For example, “had been,” ”had eaten,” and “had performed” are all in the past perfect.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The past perfect tense is used with the action that occurred deeper in the past, and the simple past tense is used with the action that occurred in the more recent past.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following sentence:

> The tour guide directed the boat toward a location where whales <strong>had surfaced</strong>.

It’s important to see that both events described in the sentence occurred in the past, but one of the events occurred before the other.

Notice that the past perfect “had surfaced” indicates that the whales surfaced before the past action “the tour guide directed the boat.”

Let’s consider another example:

> She skipped the popcorn at the movies because she <strong>had eaten</strong> a big supper.

Here, the past perfect tense is used to let us know which of the two actions described in the sentence, her skipping and her eating, happened first.

Let’s look at another example:

> The mayor <strong>had done</strong> surprisingly little to address city residents’ call for the reclamation of brownfields and was not reelected for a second term.

Here, the use of the past perfect tense makes clear the sequence of events and their cause-and-effect nature.

So how do we put this knowledge in action on test day? When we encounter the past perfect tense, we check to be sure that it is used correctly and that it makes sense given the context clues provided in the sentence.

Consider the following sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The travel restrictions that <u>were</u> in place for nearly sixty years were finally repealed.

> <u>Correct</u>: The travel restrictions that <u>had been</u> in place for nearly sixty years were finally repealed.

Sentence version one doesn’t make sense.

Sentence version two works well because the past perfect “had been” indicates that the nearly sixty-year period preceded the relaxing of the restrictions, which also occurred in the past.

Consider the following sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Although the Green Party <u>sank</u> millions of dollars into its candidate, it lost the election, garnering less than two percent of the votes.

> <u>Correct</u>: Although the Green Party <u>had sunk</u> millions of dollars into its candidate, it lost the election, garnering less than two percent of the votes.

We can eliminate sentence version one because it conveys that the investment of millions of dollars (sank) and the election (lost) occurred at the same time.

Notice that, in using the past perfect “had sunk” and the simple past “lost,” sentence version two correctly establishes the fact that there are two past actions, one in the deeper past and one in the more recent past.
