## Lesson Content
SC questions may try to trick us into choosing wrong answers that reverse the correct placement of the past perfect tense.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

SC questions may try to trick us into choosing wrong answers that reverse the correct placement of the past perfect tense. These questions, which have two past actions mentioned, utilize the past perfect tense with the more recent past action.
{{< /alert >}}

For example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: She <strong>had skipped</strong> the popcorn at the movies because she ate a big supper.

Notice that the correct placement of the past perfect tense has been reversed, creating a sentence that is illogical.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The mayor did surprisingly little to address city residents’ call for the reclamation of brownfields and <strong>had not been reelected</strong> for a second term.

Here again, notice that the correct placement of the past perfect tense has been reversed, creating a sentence that is illogical.

We can use this knowledge to score points on test day by eliminating any sentence version that reverses the correct placement of the past perfect tense.

<strong><u>Key Strategy</u></strong><strong>:</strong> Eliminate any sentence version that reverses the correct placement of the past perfect tense.

Let’s give this strategy a try:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Although she <u>missed</u> some of the conferences, she <u>had been</u> able to negotiate an increase in compensation.

> <u>Correct</u>: Although she <u>had missed</u> some of the conferences, she <u>was</u> able to negotiate an increase in compensation.

We can easily eliminate sentence version one because she must have missed the conferences before negotiating an increase in compensation.

Sentence version two, however, correctly places the past perfect “had missed” with the action further back in time and the simple past “was” with the more recent past action.

##  Sentence Correction Example 6  

The little girl was ecstatic when her grandfather presented her with the completed <u>dollhouse, which he had spent months building, on her sixth birthday</u>, but she hardly plays with it anymore.

1. [x] (A) dollhouse, which he had spent months building, on her sixth birthday
2. [ ] (B) dollhouse that he was building for months on her sixth birthday
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) dollhouse on her sixth birthday, he built it for months
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) dollhouse, which he has spent months building, on her sixth birthday
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) dollhouse on her sixth birthday, which he has built for months
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

##### Question Level: easy



> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(A)</b> The little girl was ecstatic when her grandfather presented her with the completed <b>dollhouse, which he had spent months building, on her sixth birthday
</b>, but she hardly plays with it anymore.



The past tense verbs “was” and “presented” tell us that “was ecstatic” and “presented her with” are past events.


So, the use of the past perfect tense “had spent” makes sense in describing an event that occurred before those past events: her grandfather spent months building the dollhouse <em>before</em> he presented it to her and <em>before</em> she was ecstatic. The use of the past perfect “had spent” makes that time sequence clear.


Notice also that the relative clause “which he had spent months building” logically modifies the noun “dollhouse.”


> <b>(B)</b> The little girl was ecstatic when her grandfather presented her with the completed <b>dollhouse that he was building for months on her sixth birthday
</b>, but she hardly plays with it anymore.



It does not make sense to say that “her grandfather presented her with the completed dollhouse that he was building,” since “was building” indicates that he was still building the dollhouse at the time that he presented it to her, but the modifier “completed” tells us that he was not.


Also, the modifier following the noun “dollhouse,” “that he was building for months on her sixth birthday,” conveys the illogical meaning that the little girl’s grandfather was building a dollhouse for months on her sixth birthday. Of course, one cannot build something “for months” on a single day.


This structure is subtly different from the structure in (A). In (A), “which he had spent months building” is set off from the sentence by commas, and a comma separates “which he had spent months building” from “on her sixth birthday.” Thus, in (A), “which he had spent months building” is not connected to “on her sixth birthday” the way “that he was building for months” is connected to “on her sixth birthday” in (B).


> <b>(C)</b> The little girl was ecstatic when her grandfather presented her with the completed <b>dollhouse on her sixth birthday, he built it for months
</b>, but she hardly plays with it anymore.



This version connects two independent clauses using only a comma and, thus, is a run-on sentence. The independent clauses “The little girl was ecstatic when her grandfather presented her with the completed dollhouse on her sixth birthday” and “he built it for months …” should be joined by a semicolon or by a comma followed by a conjunction such as “and.”


Furthermore, the use of the simple past “built” doesn’t make sense. The girl’s grandfather’s building the dollhouse must have preceded his presenting it to her, but by using the simple past “built” and the simple past “presented,” the sentence conveys that these two events occurred at the same time.


> <b>(D)</b> The little girl was ecstatic when her grandfather presented her with the completed <b>dollhouse, which he has spent months building, on her sixth birthday
</b>, but she hardly plays with it anymore.



The use of the present perfect “has spent” does not make sense in this context.


The present perfect is used to communicate that an event began in the past and continues in the present. However, there are several clues in the sentence that tell us that the grandfather is no longer building the dollhouse. So, that event is not continuing in the present.


For one, the dollhouse is described as “completed” at the time that her grandfather “presented” (past tense) it to the little girl. Of course, the building of the dollhouse would not be ongoing in the present if the dollhouse was completed in the past.


Also, the past tense verbs “was” and “presented” tell us that the little girl’s sixth birthday was a past event, as does the fact that “she hardly plays with it (the dollhouse) anymore.” Thus, “has spent,” which indicates that the grandfather is still building the dollhouse, does not make sense in the context of what the rest of the sentence says.


> <b>(E)</b> The little girl was ecstatic when her grandfather presented her with the completed <b>dollhouse on her sixth birthday, which he has built for months
</b>, but she hardly plays with it anymore.



The use of the present perfect verb “has built” does not make sense. The present perfect is used to indicate that an event began in the past and continues in the present, but there are several clues in the sentence that tell us that the grandfather is no longer building the dollhouse. So, that event is not continuing in the present.


For one, the dollhouse is described as “completed” at the time that her grandfather “presented” (past tense) it to the little girl. Of course, the building of the dollhouse would not be ongoing in the present if the dollhouse was completed in the past.


Also, the past tense verbs “was” and “presented” tell us that the little girl’s sixth birthday was a past event, as does the fact that “she hardly plays with it (the dollhouse) anymore.” Thus, “has built,” which indicates that the grandfather is still building the dollhouse, does not make sense in the context of what the rest of the sentence says.


Furthermore, the placement of “which he has built …” directly after “sixth birthday” results in the sentence’s seeming to convey the nonsensical meaning that the little girl’s grandfather has built her sixth birthday for months. Notice that “on her sixth birthday” does not modify “dollhouse.” So, it does not make sense to say that “which he has built …” modifies the entirety of “dollhouse on her sixth birthday,” because “dollhouse” and “on her sixth birthday” are conceptually separate, not one noun phrase.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

Let’s now discuss another type of situation in which the past perfect must be used.
