## Lesson Content
A sentence must use the past perfect tense when the sentence is about an action or event completed<em> </em>by a specified date or time period in the past.

For example:

> By late evening on Election Day, it <strong>had become</strong> clear that the upstart candidate would soon be the president of the country.

Late evening on Election Day is a time in the past, and we have an event, becoming, that was completed before that time.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The past perfect tense must be used to indicate that an action or event was completed before a specified date or time period in the past.
{{< /alert >}}

How can we leverage this knowledge on test day? We can eliminate any sentence version that indicates that an action or event was completed before a specified date or time period in the past but does not correctly use the past perfect tense.

<strong><u>Key Strategy</u></strong><strong>:</strong> Eliminate any sentence version that indicates that an action or event was completed before a specified date or time period in the past but does not correctly use the past perfect tense.

Let’s give this a try:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: <strong>By 1910</strong>, everyone <u>learned</u> the jitterbug.

> <u>Correct</u>: <strong>By 1910</strong>, everyone <u>had learned</u> the jitterbug.

We can quickly eliminate sentence version one because the sentence is about an action or event completed<em> </em>by a specified date or time period in the past, but it incorrectly uses the simple past “learned” instead of the past perfect “had learned.”

Sentence version two correctly uses the past perfect tense in describing an action or event completed<em> </em>by a specified date or time period in the past.

Let’s try another:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: <strong>By the end of the month</strong>, all of the foreign troops <u>left</u> the war-torn nation.

> <u>Correct</u>: <strong>By the end of the month</strong>, all of the foreign troops <u>had left</u> the war-torn nation.

We can quickly eliminate sentence version one because the sentence is about an action or event completed<em> </em>by a specified date or time period in the past, but it incorrectly uses the simple past “left” instead of the past perfect “had left.”

Sentence version two correctly uses the past perfect tense in describing an action or event completed<em> </em>by a specified date or time period in the past.

<u>Be Careful</u>: Not every sentence about something occurring by a time in the past has to use the past perfect.

> By 1910, many people from all walks of life <strong>were using</strong> electric cars.

> By the time the music started, the sun <strong>was setting</strong>.

These sentences are not about actions completed by certain points in time.

The first sentence is about an action, the use of electric cars, that was ongoing, rather than completed, at a certain point in time.

The second sentence is about an event, the setting of the sun, that was ongoing, rather than completed, at a certain point in time.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Not every sentence about something occurring by a time in the past has to use the past perfect. If a sentence about something occurring by a time in the past is not about an action or event completed by a time in the past, it can use another tense.
{{< /alert >}}

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A)</b> By 1864, <b>President Lincoln made public his plan for reconstruction of the Southern States, promising that the United States - at that time known as the “Union” - would legally recognize the government of any state, which promised
</b> to emancipate its slaves, pledge support to the Union as the only government of the United States, and fully abide by the Constitution.



Since Lincoln’s making his plan public is an action that was completed by a point in time in the past, 1864, the use of the simple past “made” is not correct. The past perfect “had made” should be used.


Also, the use of the construction “any state, which” does not make sense. As a result of using that construction, the sentence conveys the illogical meaning that Lincoln promised that the Union would recognize the government of any state, and then adds the information that “any state” promised to emancipate its slaves, etc. Notice, “any state” does not refer to a particular state. So, it does not make sense to modify “any state” by using the nonrestrictive “which promised.” To more clearly see why this construction does not work, we can consider a simpler example:


<u>Incorrect</u>: Roberto said that he likes any cat, which is black.


Since there isn’t a particular cat that the sentence is about, it does not make sense to describe the cat.


Now, what would make sense is the following:


The union would recognize any state that promised …


In the above construction, the restrictive modifier “that promised” logically restricts “any state” to those that promised.


> <b>(B)</b> By 1864, <b>when President Lincoln had made public his plan for reconstruction of the Southern States and had promised that the United States - at that time known as the “Union” - would legally recognize the government of any state that had promised
</b> to emancipate its slaves, pledge support to the Union as the only government of the United States, and fully abide by the Constitution.



In this version, everything that follows “by 1864” is a long modifier modifying “1864.” So, this version has no main clause and is not a complete sentence.

</b> to emancipate its slaves, pledge support to the Union as the only government of the United States, and fully abide by the Constitution.



The past perfect “had made” is correctly used in expressing that Lincoln’s making the plan public occurred before a point in time in the past, 1864.


Also, the construction “any state that promised …” is logical because the use of the restrictive modifier “that promised …” to narrow down “any state” to “any state that promised …” makes sense.


> <b>(D)</b> By 1864, <b>President Lincoln made public his plan for reconstruction of the Southern States and promised that the United States - at that time known as the “Union” – will legally recognize the government of any state, which promised
</b> to emancipate its slaves, pledge support to the Union as the only government of the United States, and fully abide by the Constitution.



Since Lincoln’s making his plan public is an action that was completed by a point in time in the past, 1864, the use of the simple past “made” is not correct. The past perfect “had made” should be used.

Let’s now discuss why it is unnecessary to use the past perfect tense when the time sequence is clear without it.
