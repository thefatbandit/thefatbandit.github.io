## Lesson Content
At this point, you know that, when we have two past actions with one of the actions occurring deeper in the past, we can use the past perfect tense to make the time sequence clear.

However, if a sentence makes the sequence of events clear without the use of the past perfect tense, there is no need to use the past perfect.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

If a sentence makes clear the time sequence of past events without the use of the past perfect tense, the use of the past perfect tense is not necessary.
{{< /alert >}}

There are two common situations in which the use of the past perfect is not necessary.

> Kim <strong>inspected </strong>the airplane’s engine before she sat in the cockpit.

> Cody <strong>brushed </strong>his teeth before he went to the dentist.

The word “before” makes the time sequences clear in these sentences, so we would not be required to say:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Kim <strong>had inspected</strong> the airplane’s engine before she sat in the cockpit.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Cody <strong>had brushed</strong> his teeth before he went to the dentist.

The use of the past perfect tense in the above variations is unnecessary, and the resulting sentences do not quite make sense, as we can see by considering the following.

When we say, “Kim inspected the airplane’s engine before she sat in the cockpit,” the sequence of events is clear.

Let’s now consider a couple of examples that use the word after:

> Freddie purchased the sailboat after he <strong>learned </strong>to sail.

> The stock's value rose after the company's disgraced CEO <strong>resigned</strong>.

Although each of the above sentences discusses two past events, with one event occurring before the next, the past perfect tense is not necessary because the word “after” indicates which event happened first.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Freddie purchased the sailboat after he <strong>had learned</strong> to sail.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The stock's value rose after the company's disgraced CEO <strong>had resigned</strong>.

The timeline of events is made clear by the sentences’ using “after,” and so, the past perfect tense is not required.

Other context clues can also make sequences of events clear.

> Modest Mussorgsky <strong>was </strong>a prolific composer until his use of alcohol caught up with him.

Even though this sentence uses the simple past “was,” the context clue “until” indicates that “Modest Mussorgsky was” preceded “his use of alcohol caught up.” So, the use of the past perfect is not necessary.

> Evy McDonald <strong>developed </strong>a method for solving the problem in 1988, but, even in 2018, few people were aware of the method’s existence.

Even though this sentence uses the simple past “developed,” the dates mentioned, “1988” and “2018,” clearly indicate that “Evy McDonald developed” preceded “few people were.” So, the use of the past perfect is not necessary.

How can we use this knowledge to score points on test day? We can resist the urge to eliminate sentence versions with two past actions on ONLY the grounds that they don’t use the past perfect.

<strong><u>Key Strategy</u></strong><strong>:</strong> Resist the urge to eliminate sentence versions with two past actions on ONLY the grounds that they don’t use the past perfect.

Consider the following pair of sentences:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: For years, Antonio had learned by watching his brother at work, and then he built a career, which was successful in music.

> <u>Correct</u>: Before he built a successful career in music, Antonio, for years, learned by watching his brother at work.

Having noticed that the sentence is about two past events that occurred in sequence, a test-taker looking for the use of the past perfect tense might be tempted to choose version one and eliminate version two, because version one uses the past perfect tense and version two does not.

However, the use of the past perfect tense in version two is not necessary because the use of “before” in version two is sufficient for making clear the sequence of the events described.

Meanwhile version one is flawed, because “and then” does not do a good job of connecting the past “built” with the deeper past “had learned,” and because, in version one, “career, which was successful in music” conveys the illogical meaning that the career itself succeeded in music.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Being aware that the use of the past perfect tense is not always necessary for making clear the sequence of past events can help us to avoid being fooled into choosing an incorrect choice.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 9  

Immediately after <u>he had tasted the sauce, not containing</u> enough salt, the head chef fired his sous chef.

1. [ ] (A) he had tasted the sauce, not containing
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) he tasted the sauce, because of not containing
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) tasting the sauce, for not containing
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) he tasted the sauce, which did not contain
5. [ ] (E) he had tasted the sauce, and it did not contain
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A)</b> Immediately after <b>he had tasted the sauce, not containing
</b> enough salt, the head chef fired his sous chef.



The use of the past perfect tense “had tasted” is unnecessary because the phrase “immediately after” makes clear that the event “tasted” precedes the event “fired.” Thus, the simple past tense “tasted,” rather than the past perfect “had tasted,” is all that is required to convey a clear meaning in this context.


Furthermore, because the present participial phrase “not containing enough salt” is set off from the rest of the sentence by commas, we can tell that it does not modify the noun that directly precedes it, “sauce,” but rather modifies the subject of one or the other of the sentence’s two clauses. So, “not containing enough salt, modifies either “he” or “the head chef,” both of which name the same person. As a result, the sentence conveys the ridiculous meaning that the head chef did not contain enough salt and that, for some reason, his “not containing enough salt” prompted him to fire the sous chef.


> <b>(B)</b> Immediately after <b>he tasted the sauce, because of not containing
</b> enough salt, the head chef fired his sous chef.



“Because of not containing enough salt” does not make clear that it was the sauce that did not contain enough salt and instead seems to indicate that the head chef was “not containing enough salt,” and that his “not containing enough salt” somehow prompted him to fire the sous chef.


Notice, however, that the use of the simple past tense “tasted,” rather than the past perfect “had tasted,” is correct even though the event “tasted” precedes the event “fired,” because the sequence of events is made clear by the phrase “immediately after.”


> <b>(C)</b> Immediately after <b>tasting the sauce, for not containing
</b> enough salt, the head chef fired his sous chef.



The phrase“for not containing enough salt” does not make clear that it was the sauce that did not contain enough salt and instead seems to indicate that the reason the head chef fired the sous chef was that the sous chef did not contain enough salt. Of course, this meaning is nonsensical.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D)</b> Immediately after <b>he tasted the sauce, which did not contain
</b> enough salt, the head chef fired his sous chef.



This version effectively conveys a clear and logical meaning.


By using the simple past tense “tasted” along with the phrase “immediately after,” this version makes clear that the event “tasted” precedes the event “fired.”


Also, by using the relative pronoun “which” to clearly refer to “the sauce,” this version makes clear what “did not contain enough salt.”


> <b>(E)</b> Immediately after <b>he had tasted the sauce, and it did not contain
</b> enough salt, the head chef fired his sous chef.



 The use of the past perfect tense “had tasted” is unnecessary because the phrase “immediately after” makes clear that the event “tasted” precedes the event “fired.” Thus, the simple past tense “tasted,” rather than the past perfect “had tasted,” is all that is required to convey a clear meaning in this context.


Furthermore, “Immediately after he had tasted the sauce, <em>and </em>it did not contain” seems to indicate that the head chef’s tasting the sauce and the sauce’s not containing enough salt were two separate events, after which the head chef fired the sous chef. The logical meaning that the sauce did not contain enough salt <em>when</em> the head chef tasted it is not conveyed by connecting these two clauses with the conjunction “and.”


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Sentence Correction Example 10  

Before <u>having become a popular companion animal, collies, which are also known as “rough collies,” had served for centuries as a herding dog</u> in Scotland.

1. [ ] (A) having become a popular companion animal, collies, which are also known as “rough collies,” had served for centuries as a herding dog
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) becoming popular as being a companion animal, collies that are also known as “rough collies” had for centuries served as herding dogs
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) its becoming popular as a companion animal, the collie, also known as “the rough collie,” for centuries served as a herding dog
4. [ ] (D) they became popular as a companion animal, collies, also known as “rough collies,” have served for centuries as herding dogs
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) they had become popular as companion animals, the collie, which is also known as “the rough collie,” had served for centuries as a herding dog
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



The use of the past perfect is not necessary for indicating the sequence of past events because “before” indicates that one past event occurred before the other. Having established that the presence of “before” means that the use of the simple past “served” is correct, we won’t be tempted into choosing an incorrect choice by the fact that it uses the past perfect.


> <b>(A)</b> Before <b>having become a popular companion animal, collies, which are also known as “rough collies,” had served for centuries as a herding dog
</b> in Scotland.



Since the sequence of past events is made clear via the use of “before,” the use of the past perfect “had served” for describing the earlier event is unnecessary. So, we have to avoid being tempted by the use of the past perfect into choosing this choice, which is flawed for the following reasons.


The plural “collies” does not work logically with the singular “companion animal” in the opening modifier, which modifies “collies.” In order to work together logically, both nouns have to be either singular or plural.


Also, it does not make sense to say that the plural “collies” served as “a herding dog.”


> <b>(B)</b> Before <b>becoming popular as being a companion animal, collies that are also known as “rough collies” had for centuries served as herding dogs
</b> in Scotland.



Since the sequence of past events is made clear via the use of “before,” the use of the past perfect “had served” for describing the earlier event is unnecessary. So, we have to avoid being tempted by the use of the past perfect into choosing this choice, which is flawed for the following reasons.


It does not make sense to say that the collie became popular as “being a companion animal.” The collie simply became popular as “a companion animal.”


Also, the plural “collies” does not work logically with the singular “companion animal” in the opening modifier, which modifies “collies.” In order to work together logically, both nouns have to be either singular or plural.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> Before <b>its becoming popular as a companion animal, the collie, also known as “the rough collie,” for centuries served as a herding dog
</b> in Scotland.



Since the sequence of past events is made clear via the use of “before,” the use of the simple past “served” for describing the earlier event makes sense.


Also, the singular “its” agrees with the singular “collie,” and this version effectively expresses a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(D)</b> Before <b>they became popular as a companion animal, collies, also known as “rough collies,” have served for centuries as herding dogs
</b> in Scotland.



It does not make sense to say that the plural “they” became popular as the singular “a companion animal.”


Also, since the main clause is about an event that occurred before a point in time in the past, the point in time when collies became popular as companion animals, the use of the present perfect “have served,” which seems to connect the event the collie’s serving to the present, does not make sense.


> <b>(E)</b> Before <b>they had become popular as companion animals, the collie, which is also known as “the rough collie,” had served for centuries as a herding dog
</b> in Scotland.



The use of the past perfect “had become,” is perhaps not ideal, but is not strictly incorrect and does not constitute a strong reason to eliminate this choice. While the use of the simple past “became” would probably make more sense, tenses can be used in nuanced ways to express meanings that are slightly different from each other, and, in this case, by using the past perfect, a writer could express a meaning slightly different from that expressed when the simple past is used.


At the same time, since the sequence of past events is made clear via the use of “before,” the use of the past perfect “had served” for describing the earlier event is unnecessary and somewhat illogical. So, we have to avoid being tempted by the use of the past perfect “had served” into choosing this choice, which is clearly flawed for the following reason.


The singular “collie” does not work logically with the plural “they” or the plural “companion animals.”


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)
