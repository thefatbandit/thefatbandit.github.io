## Lesson Content
The future perfect tense indicates that an event that has not yet happened will be completed before another event that has not yet happened or before a specific time in the future.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The future perfect tense indicates that an event that has not yet happened will be completed before another event that has not yet happened or before a specific time in the future.
{{< /alert >}}

The future perfect tense is formed by combining “will,” the helping verb “have,” and the past participle of the main verb.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The future perfect tense = “will” + “have” + past participle.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example:

> I <strong>will have worked</strong> for 35 years by the time I retire.

In the above example, the use of the future perfect tense lets us know that the future action of working for 35 years will be completed before the retirement, which has not yet occurred.

> I <strong>will have traveled</strong> to 86 countries before this trip is over.

In the above example, the future perfect tense lets us know that the future action of traveling to 86 countries will occur before the trip is over.

The following example uses the simple future tense:

> By Monday, he <strong>will have</strong> a week to complete the assignment.

Here, when Monday comes, the person will have a week to complete the assignment.

What about the following, which uses the future perfect?

> By Monday, he <strong>will have had</strong> a week to finish the assignment.

In this sentence, we see that by a future Monday, the person “he” will already have had a week to complete an assignment.

##  Sentence Correction Example 11  

The company <u>will have cornered the market by the time the patents related to the product will expire</u>.

1. [ ] (A) will have cornered the market by the time the patents related to the product will expire
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) corners the market by the time the patents related to the product will expire
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) will have cornered the market by the time the patents related to the product expire
4. [ ] (D) cornering the market by the time the patents related to the product expires
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) will corner the market by the time the patents related to the product will expire
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

##### Question Level: medium



> <b>(A)</b> The company <b>will have cornered the market by the time the patents related to the product will expire
</b>.



The use of the simple future tense “will expire” in “The company will have cornered the market by the time the patents … <em>will expire</em>” does not make sense.


The use of the future perfect tense “will have cornered” with “by the time” is meant to communicate that “the company <em>will have cornered </em>the market” by a particular point in time.


Notice, however, that “the patents … will expire” does not indicate a point in time. After all, “the patents … will expire” is not connected to any particular point in time. Rather, “the patents … will expire” communicates that, at <em>some</em> time in the future, the patents will expire.


So, saying, “The company will have cornered the market by the time the patents … will expire” doesn’t make sense because “by the time the patents … will expire” does not actually point to any particular point in time.


> <b>(B)</b> The company <b>corners the market by the time the patents related to the product will expire
</b>.



 The use of the simple future tense “will expire” in “The company corners the market by the time the patents … <em>will expire</em>” does not make sense.


“The company corners the market by the time” is meant to communicate that the company corners the market by a particular point in time.


Notice, however, that “the patents … will expire” does not indicate a point in time. After all, “the patents … will expire” is not connected to any particular point in time. Rather, “the patents … will expire” communicates that, at <em>some</em> time in the future, the patents will expire.


So, saying, “The company corners the market by the time the patents … will expire” doesn’t make sense because “by the time the patents … will expire” does not actually point to any particular point in time.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> The company <b>will have cornered the market by the time the patents related to the product expire
</b>.



The future perfect tense “will have cornered” and the simple present tense “expire” logically pair with each other to convey that one future event will be completed (“the company <em>will have cornered</em> the market”) by the time another future event occurs (“patents related to the product <em>expire</em>”).


Although the sentence discusses two future events, the use of the present tense “patents … expire” makes sense as a marker of a point in time by which “the company will have cornered the market. In other words, the sentence effectively conveys that “by the time” marked by the event “the patents … expire,” the event “cornered the market” will have occurred.


For an explanation of why the future tense “will expire” would not work, see the explanation for choice (A), (B), or (E).


Furthermore, the future perfect tense “will have cornered” tells us that, at that future point when the patents expire, the event “cornered” will have already happened. The time sequence is clear and logical.


> <b>(D)</b> The company <b>cornering the market by the time the patents related to the product expires
</b>.



This version does not contain a main verb and, thus, is not a complete sentence.


Notice that “cornering,” a participle, cannot serve at the main verb of a sentence.


Furthermore, the verb “expires” is part of the prepositional phrase “by the time the patents related to the product expires.”


Thus, this version is composed of only “the company” and a long participial phrase, “cornering the market …,” which modifies “company.”


Additionally, the singular verb “expires” in “by the time the patents related to the product expires” does not agree with its plural subject “patents.” We have to be careful not be fooled by the presence of the singular noun “product” just before “expires” into thinking that the singular “expires” is correct. The subject is “patents,” and “related to the product” is merely a modifier that modifies “patents.”


> <b>(E)</b> The company <b>will corner the market by the time the patents related to the product will expire
</b>.



The use of the simple future tense “will expire” in “The company will corner the market by the time the patents … <em>will expire</em>” does not make sense.


The use of the future tense “will corner” with “by the time” is meant to communicate that “the company <em>will corner </em>the market” by a particular point in time.


Notice, however, that “the patents … will expire” does not indicate a point in time. After all, “the patents … will expire” is not connected to any particular point in time. Rather, “the patents … will expire” communicates that, at <em>some</em> time in the future, the patents will expire.


So, saying, “The company will corner the market by the time the patents … will expire” doesn’t make sense because “by the time the patents … will expire” does not actually point to any particular point in time.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

##  Sentence Correction Example 12  

By the time more doctors <u>will begin to prescribe to their patients a diet free from animal products, many people will have died as a result of complications related to the “Western Diet,” which is a diet</u> high in meat, eggs, and dairy, yet low in fruits and vegetables.

1. [ ] (A) will begin to prescribe to their patients a diet free from animal products, many people will have died as a result of complications related to the “Western Diet,” which is a diet
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) begin to prescribe a diet to their patients free of animal products, many people will die as a result of complications related to the “Western Diet,” which is a diet
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) begin prescribing a diet to their patients free from animal products, many people will have died as a result of complications from the “Western Diet,” a diet
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) prescribe to their patients a diet free from animal products, many people were dying as a result of complications related to the “Western Diet” that is a diet
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) begin to prescribe to their patients a diet free of animal products, many people will have died as a result of complications related to the “Western Diet,” a diet

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



> <b>(A)</b> By the time more doctors <b>will begin to prescribe to their patients a diet free from animal products, many people will have died as a result of complications related to the “Western Diet,” which is a diet
</b> high in meat, eggs, and dairy, yet low in fruits and vegetables.



The use of the simple future tense “will begin” in “By the time more doctors <em>will begin</em> …, many people will have died” does not make sense.


The use of “by the time” with the future perfect “will have died” is meant to communicate that “many people will have died” by a particular point in time.


Notice, however, that “more doctors will begin” does not indicate a point in time. After all, “more doctors will begin” is not connected to any particular point in time. Rather, “more doctors will begin” communicates that, at <em>some</em> time in the future, more doctors will begin.


So, saying, By the time more doctors will begin …, many people will have died” doesn’t make sense because “by the time more doctors will begin” does not actually point to any particular point in time.


Also, because this version uses the preposition “from” rather than the preposition “of,” this version suggests the nonsensical idea that a diet could be free from animal products, as if the diet has escaped from or been liberated from animal products.


> <b>(B)</b> By the time more doctors <b>begin to prescribe a diet to their patients free of animal products, many people will die as a result of complications related to the “Western Diet,” which is a diet
</b> high in meat, eggs, and dairy, yet low in fruits and vegetables.



Because the modifier “free of animal products” is placed directly after “patients,” this version suggests the nonsensical idea that doctors’ patients may be free of animal products.


Also, the use of the simple future tense “will die” does not make sense, because people’s dying is an event that the sentence indicates will have occurred by a certain point in time in the future, “the time doctors begin to prescribe ….” The sentence should use the future perfect “will have died” instead.


> <span class="incorrect-answer"><i class="fal fa-times-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(C)</b> By the time more doctors <b>begin prescribing a diet to their patients free from animal products, many people will have died as a result of complications from the “Western Diet,” a diet
</b> high in meat, eggs, and dairy, yet low in fruits and vegetables.



Because the modifier “free from animal products” is placed directly after “patients,” this version suggests the nonsensical idea that doctors’ patients may be free from animal products.


Also, because this version uses the preposition “from” rather than the preposition “of,” this version suggests the nonsensical idea that patients could be free from animal products, as if the patients mentioned have escaped from or been liberated from animal products.


> <b>(D)</b> By the time more doctors <b>prescribe to their patients a diet free from animal products, many people were dying as a result of complications related to the “Western Diet” that is a diet
</b> high in meat, eggs, and dairy, yet low in fruits and vegetables.



Because this version uses the preposition “from” rather than the preposition “of,” this version suggests the nonsensical idea that a diet could be free from animal products, as if the diet has escaped from or been liberated from animal products.


Also, the context clue “by the time doctors prescribe” indicates that the sentence is about an event that will have occurred by a point in time in the future. So, the use of the past continuous “were dying” does not make sense.


Also, since the Western Diet is a particular diet, using the restrictive relative clause “that is a diet …” to narrow the Western Diet down to “the Western Diet that is high in meat, eggs, and dairy, yet low in fruits and vegetables” does not make sense.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E)</b> By the time more doctors <b>begin to prescribe to their patients a diet free of animal products, many people will have died as a result of complications related to the “Western Diet,” a diet
</b> high in meat, eggs, and dairy, yet low in fruits and vegetables.



The context clue “by the time doctors begin to prescribe” indicates that the sentence is about an event that will have occurred by a point in time in the future. So, the use of the future perfect “will have died” makes sense.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
