## Lesson Content
The future in the past is used when sentences about past events refer to events that were in the future when the past events occurred.

Consider the following example:

> At the rally, the candidate said that he <strong>would take</strong> campaign donations only from individuals.

The sentence conveys that, at a time in the past, the candidate said that he was going to do something in the future, “take campaign donations only from individuals.”

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The future in the past is used when sentences about past events refer to events that were in the future when the past events occurred.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at some more examples:

> Even though the student was unfamiliar with the concepts, she knew that she <strong>would understand </strong>them eventually.

The sentence is about what the student knew about the future, that she would understand, at a point in time in the past.

> The director said that the cast <strong>would</strong> have a party after the final show.

This sentence is about plans for the future that the cast had in the past.

> During yesterday’s meeting, the division manager explained that <strong>she would soon be releasing</strong> the long-awaited new product.

This sentence is about plans for the future that the division manager had in the past.

To create the simple future tense, we use “will.”

To create the future in the past, we can use the past tense version of “will,” which is “would.”

Consider the following examples:

> <u>Future Tense</u>: They are convinced that they <strong>will find</strong> gold soon.

> <u>Future in the Past</u>: They were convinced that they <strong>would find</strong> gold soon.

> <u>Future Tense</u>: Eleni hopes that she <strong>will arrive</strong> in time to see the eclipse.

> <u>Future in the Past</u>: Eleni hoped that she <strong>would arrive</strong> in time to see the eclipse.

How do we use this knowledge to score points on test day? We resist the urge to automatically decide that a sentence using the future tense “will” is correct because the sentence, at some point, describes an event in the “future.” If the future event described is actually the future in the past, we must use “would.”

Let’s give this strategy a try.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Having seen his beautiful creations, it was predicted that the choreographer will become well known by the end of the decade.

> <u>Correct</u>: Having seen his beautiful creations, many predicted that the choreographer would become well known by the end of the decade.

Sentence version one doesn’t work because the opening modifier, “having seen his beautiful creations” doesn’t logically modify “it.” In other words, version one contains a misplaced introductory modifier.

The second version works well because the opening modifier “Having seen his beautiful creations,” correctly modifies “many.” Also, the sentence describes the future in the past and correctly uses “would” instead of “will.”

##  Sentence Correction Example 13  

Because he felt that the artist <u>will become widely known, the gallery owner purchased many of the artist’s works while selling them later,</u> after the prediction proved to be correct.

1. [ ] (A) will become widely known, the gallery owner purchased many of the artist’s works while selling them later,
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) would become widely known, the gallery owner purchased many of the artist’s works and, later, sold them,
3. [ ] (C) is going to become widely known, the gallery owner was purchasing many of the artist’s works, selling them later,
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) was going to become widely known, the gallery owner, purchasing many of the artist’s works, sold them later,
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) is becoming widely known, the gallery owner purchased and sold many of the artist’s works later,
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



In this question, the non-underlined portion provides two context clues that we can use to determine which tenses the correct answer should use: “he felt” and “after the prediction proved.”


> <b>(A)</b> Because he felt that the artist <b>will become widely known, the gallery owner purchased many of the artist’s works while selling them later,
</b> after the prediction proved to be correct.



The use of the future tense “will become” doesn’t make sense with the past tense “he felt that.” The use of the future in the past “would become” makes more sense.


To confirm that this choice is incorrect, we can notice that “purchased many of the artist’s works while selling them later” conveys the nonsensical meaning that the gallery owner purchased the works simultaneously with “selling them later.” How could the owner perform a past action “purchased” and a future action “selling them later” at the same time?


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(B)</b> Because he felt that the artist <b>would become widely known, the gallery owner purchased many of the artist’s works and, later, sold them,
</b> after the prediction proved to be correct.



It makes sense to use the future in the past “would become widely known” with the past tense “he felt that.”


Also, the timeline presented by the remainder of the sentence “later, sold them, after the prediction proved to be correct” makes sense.


Notice that the heavy use of commas around “later” and “sold them” is employed to make this choice “sound funny” and, thus, obscure the fact that it is the correct answer.


> <b>(C)</b> Because he felt that the artist <b>is going to become widely known, the gallery owner was purchasing many of the artist’s works, selling them later,
</b> after the prediction proved to be correct.



The use of the future tense “is going to become” doesn’t make sense with the past tense “he felt that.” The use of the future in the past “would become” makes more sense.


To confirm that this choice is incorrect, we can notice that “was purchasing many of the artist’s works, selling them later” does not clearly present a logical timeline and seems to convey the nonsensical meaning that the gallery owner purchased the works simultaneously with “selling them later.”


> <b>(D)</b> Because he felt that the artist <b>was going to become widely known, the gallery owner, purchasing many of the artist’s works, sold them later,
</b> after the prediction proved to be correct.



It makes sense to use the future in the past “was going to become widely known” with the past tense “he felt that.”


However, the main clause, “the gallery owner, purchasing many of the artist’s works, sold them later,” conveys the illogical meaning that, when the gallery owner sold the works “later,” he was purchasing the works that he sold.


> <b>(E)</b> Because he felt that the artist <b>is becoming widely known, the gallery owner purchased and sold many of the artist’s works later,
</b> after the prediction proved to be correct.



It does not make sense to use the present tense “is becoming” in describing what the gallery owner felt in the past. Also, the present tense “is becoming” would not be used to express a prediction – it would make sense to use the future tense or the future in the past.


Also, what this version conveys does not really make sense, as it seems to indicate that the gallery owner predicted that the artist would become widely known, and that as a result of making that prediction, the gallery owner “later,” after the artist became widely known, both purchased and sold many of the artist’s works. Purchasing and selling the artist’s works after the artist became widely known is not a logical consequence of predicting that the artist would become widely known.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

##  Sentence Correction Example 14  

<u>While the organizers of the Race Across America were hoping for a safer version of the 3000 mile bike race that would supplant the original version, having proved it to be unpopular, the new version was phased out</u> after being held for only two years.

1. [ ] (A) While the organizers of the Race Across America were hoping for a safer version of the 3000 mile bike race that would supplant the original version, having proved it to be unpopular, the new version was phased out
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) The organizers of the Race Across America, hoping that a safer version of the 3000 mile bike race would supplant the original version, the new version proved to be unpopular, and they phased it out
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) Although the organizers of the Race Across America were hoping that a safer version of the 3000 mile bike race will supplant the original version, the new version proved to be unpopular, so they phased it out
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) The organizers of the Race Across America were hoping that a safer version of the 3000 mile bike race would supplant the original version, but proving to be unpopular, the new version was phased out
5. [ ] (E) The organizers of the Race Across America had hoped that a safer version of the 3000 mile bike race was going to supplant the original version, but because it proved to be unpopular, they phased out the new version
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



In this question, the sentence mentions multiple events, and the correct answer must present these events in a time sequence that makes sense.


“After being held for only two years” in the non-underlined portion is a context clue that provides some indication of what tenses should be used in the correct answer.


Also, we have to make sure that the part of the answer choice that precedes “after being held” would be logically modified by “after being held.” If we forget to make sure that the choice we choose works logically with “after being held,” we may choose a trap choice.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>While the organizers of the Race Across America were hoping for a safer version of the 3000 mile bike race that would supplant the original version, having proved it to be unpopular, the new version was phased out
</b> after being held for only two years.



This choice conflicts with itself, in that it first says that the organizers were “hoping for a safer version,” indicating that such a version did not exist, but then says that “the new version proved it to be unpopular,” a statement that indicates that, actually, the new version did exist.


Also, the modifier “having proved it to be unpopular” does not convey a clear meaning, since it’s not clear who or what is the agent of “having proved.” Did the organizers prove something to be unpopular? Did the new version of the race prove itself to be unpopular? Also, “it” does not have a clear referent.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>The organizers of the Race Across America, hoping that a safer version of the 3000 mile bike race would supplant the original version, the new version proved to be unpopular, and they phased it out
</b> after being held for only two years.



While, at first, this version may seem to flow nicely, by reading it carefully, we can see that it is illogical.


It starts off seeming to have as its subject “the organizers of the Race Across America,” which is followed by a modifier “hoping that a safer version ….” However, without including a verb to go with the seeming subject “the organizers …,” the sentence jumps to another subject, “the new version.” We can see the issue more clearly by removing the modifier “hoping that a safer version,” since we get the following, which is clearly nonsensical:


“The organizers of the Race Across America … the new version proved to be unpopular ….”


Also, the end of the answer choice does not work logically with the non-underlined portion. When we combine the answer choice with the non-underlined portion, we get the following clause:


“they phased it out, after being held for only two years.”


That part conveys the nonsensical meaning that the organizers phased out the new version after the organizers were held for two years.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>Although the organizers of the Race Across America were hoping that a safer version of the 3000 mile bike race will supplant the original version, the new version proved to be unpopular, so they phased it out
</b> after being held for only two years.



The use of the simple future tense “will supplant” in this version does not make sense, for two reasons. One is that the expression “were hoping” implies that the organizers’ hoping ended in the past. So, what they hoped for, the supplanting of the original race by the new version, should be expressed in the past tense, as “would supplant.” Also indicating that the new version’s supplanting the original version was in the past is past tense “the new version proved to be unpopular, so, they phased it out.” Since, when this statement was made, the possibility that the new version would supplant the old version was in the past, the use of the simple future “will supplant” does not make sense. The future in the past “would supplant” is required.


Also, the end of the answer choice does not work logically with the non-underlined portion. When we combine the answer choice with the non-underlined portion, we get the following clause:


“they phased it out, after being held for only two years.”


That part conveys the nonsensical meaning that the organizers phased out the new version after the organizers were held for two years.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">YOUR CHOICE</span></span><b>(D)</b> <b>The organizers of the Race Across America were hoping that a safer version of the 3000 mile bike race would supplant the original version, but proving to be unpopular, the new version was phased out
</b> after being held for only two years.



The use of the future in the past “would supplant” in this version makes sense, as the sentence indicates that the organizers were, in the past, hoping for the supplanting of the original version by the safer version.


Notice that the use in this version of the past continuous “were hoping,” rather than the past perfect “had hoped,” which use is correct but may seem incorrect, is employed to obscure the fact that the choice is correct.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>The organizers of the Race Across America had hoped that a safer version of the 3000 mile bike race was going to supplant the original version, but because it proved to be unpopular, they phased out the new version
</b> after being held for only two years.



The use of the future in the past “was going to supplant” is not entirely illogical. However, since “was going to” is usually used when events are planned or likely to occur, it would make more sense to use the future in the past “would supplant,” since the supplanting was hoped for rather than planned and was not clearly likely to occur.


Also, which noun “it” refers to is not entirely clear, although the most logical referent for “it” is “the new version.”


While those issues may make this choice not as good as the correct answer, neither of them is a strong reason to eliminate this choice. However, we can eliminate this choice with confidence because the end of the answer choice does not work logically with the non-underlined portion. When we combine the answer choice with the non-underlined portion, we get the following clause:


“they phased out the new version after being held for only two years.”


That clause conveys the nonsensical meaning that the organizers phased out the new version after the organizers were held for two years.


Notice, the use of the past perfect “had hoped” in this choice is employed to make this choice seem more correct than the correct answer, which uses the past continuous “were hoping” instead. Also, the use of the blatantly correct dependent clause structure “because it proved to be unpopular” is another move employed to make this incorrect choice “sound right.”


A key aspect of getting this question correct is to see that both this choice and choice (D) seem correct and then check to see how each one works with the non-underlined portion.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
