## Lesson Content
Parallelism is all around us, and it lends clarity and forcefulness to writing.

> “Early to bed, early to rise, makes a man <strong>healthy</strong>, <strong>wealthy</strong>, and <strong>wise</strong>.”

In the above example, the three adjectives “healthy,” “wealthy,” and “wise” are parallel.

> “Friends, Romans, countrymen, lend me your ears; I come <strong>to bury</strong> Caesar, not <strong>to praise</strong> him.”

In this second example, a parallel structure is made up of two verbs in infinitive form (“to + verb”).

> “Today's students can put <strong>dope in their veins</strong> or <strong>hope in their brains</strong>.”

This third example contains two noun phrases that are parallel: “dope in their veins” and “hope in their brains.”

As you move through this chapter, you’ll find that there is a natural harmony or cadence to parallel structure.

In this chapter, we’ll carefully discuss parallelism and various types of flaws in parallel structure that you could encounter in Sentence Correction questions.
