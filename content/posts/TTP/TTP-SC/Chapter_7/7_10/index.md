## Lesson Content
We have discussed the fact that parallelism is largely based on logic and the fact that structures don’t have to match exactly in order to be acceptably parallel.

At the same time, we have seen that some structures are more parallel than others.

> Rome is one of the few cities in the world <strong>where a great number of well-preserved ancient structures can be found </strong>and <strong>that has many examples of stunning Renaissance architecture</strong>.

> Rome is one of the few cities in the world <strong>where a great number of well-preserved ancient structures can be found</strong> and <strong>where there are many examples of stunning Renaissance architecture</strong>.

Notice that the first version uses a relative clause beginning with “where” and a second relative clause beginning with “that has,” while the second version uses two relative clauses beginning with “where.” So, the second version is more parallel than the first.

All the same, the first version is not obviously incorrect.

So, what if these two versions were in a Sentence Correction question?

Here’s a strategy that we can employ in such a situation:

If you have narrowed your choices down to two that seem basically correct and have no significant difference other than degree of parallelism, go with the more parallel choice.

So, how do we use this knowledge to score points come test day? Easy! If we must choose between two choices, both of which seem basically correct, we choose the choice that creates the more parallel structure.

Here’s another pair of sentence versions:

> Allobates zaparo, a frog species <strong>found</strong> <strong>in the rainforest</strong> and <strong>that resembles</strong> <strong>a poison dart frog</strong>, is not actually poisonous.

> Allobates zaparo, a frog species <strong>found in the rainforest</strong> and <strong>resembling a poison dart frog</strong>, is not actually poisonous.

In the first version, “found in the rainforest” is paired in a list with “that resembles the poison dart frog.”

In the second version, “found in the rainforest” is paired in a list with “resembling a poison dart frog.”

Since “found in the rainforest,” “that resembles the poison dart frog,” and “resembling a poison dart frog” can all correctly modify “frog species” and there is no obvious error in either version, how could we choose between the two sentence versions?

We could use our strategy of choosing the more parallel version and choose the second version.

Let’s consider another pair:

> Having considered the rate at which the project was progressing, they decided <strong>to hire a second developer</strong>, who would work closely with the web design team, and <strong>to hire a third developer in two months</strong>.

> Having considered the rate at which the project was progressing, they decided <strong>to hire a second developer</strong>, who would work closely with the web design team, and <strong>that they would hire a third developer in two months</strong>.

Once again, we have two sentences that are basically the same, except in that the structure of the first version, which uses “to” twice, is more parallel than the structure of the second version, which uses “to” and then “that they would.” Since neither choice is obviously incorrect, faced with a choice between two versions like these in a Sentence Correction question, once again, we would use the strategy of simply choosing the more parallel version, which, in this case, is the first version.

Now, remember, we should use this strategy of choosing the choice that creates the more parallel version only in cases in which there is no other reason to choose either version.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The strategy of choosing the choice that creates the more parallel version of a sentence should be used only as a last resort when we don’t see any other decision point that we can use to choose between two choices in an SC question.

##  Sentence Correction Example 27  

Wanting to develop creatively, <u>Mikhail Baryshnikov’s defection to the United States from his native Russia was from hoping that he would experience artistic freedom and</u> be able to explore a variety of forms of dance.

1. [ ] (A) Mikhail Baryshnikov’s defection to the United States from his native Russia was from hoping that he would experience artistic freedom and
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) Mikhail Baryshnikov defected to the United States from his native Russia, hoping to experience artistic freedom and that he would
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) Mikhail Baryshnikov defecting from his native Russia to the United States and hoping to experience artistic freedom and
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) the defection of Mikhail Baryshnikov to the United States from his native Russia was from his hoping to experience artistic freedom and to
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) Mikhail Baryshnikov defected from his native Russia to the United States, hoping to experience artistic freedom and

##### Question Level: medium



Some of the sentence versions in this question have clear errors. At the same time, once we’ve eliminated the choices that create those versions, we’re left with two sentence versions that are not blatantly incorrect, the (B) version and the (E) version. So, to decide between choice (B) and choice (E), we have to scan the two sentence versions to find a significant difference that we can use as a decision point.


In doing so, we see that the only significant difference between the two versions is that the (E) version is a bit more parallel than the (B) version. Therefore, to get this question correct, we choose (E).


> <b>(A)</b> Wanting to develop creatively, <b>Mikhail Baryshnikov’s defection to the United States from his native Russia was from hoping that he would experience artistic freedom and
</b> be able to explore a variety of forms of dance.



 We can readily eliminate this version because the opening modifier, “wanting to develop creatively,” is misplaced so as to illogically modify “Mikhail Baryshnikov’s defection.” After all, “Mikhail Baryshnikov’s defection” would not have been “wanting to develop creatively.”


Notice also that it’s not logical to say that “Mikhail Baryshnikov’s defection … was from hoping.” After all, while he defected <em>because</em> he was hoping to do certain things, his defection did not come “from hoping.”


> <b>(B)</b> Wanting to develop creatively, <b>Mikhail Baryshnikov defected to the United States from his native Russia, hoping to experience artistic freedom and that he would
</b> be able to explore a variety of forms of dance.



 This version has no major issues. So, often, people answering this question are left with this choice and choice (E). At that point, arriving at the correct answer requires considering the differences between this version and the (E) version to find a reason to choose one choice over the other.


There are two differences between this version and the (E) version.


One is that, whereas this version says, “defected to the United States from his native Russia,” the (E) version says, “defected from his native Russia to the United States.” There’s no significant grammatical or logical difference between the two. So, that difference does not enable us to choose one choice over the other.


The other difference is that, whereas this version says, “hoping to experience artistic freedom and that he would be able to,” the (E) version says, “hoping to experience artistic freedom and be able to.” We see that what this version says is a little less parallel than what the (E) version says.


So, since there’s no other significant difference between what this version says and what the (E) version says, we can eliminate this choice and choose (E) because the (E) version is a bit more parallel.


> <b>(C)</b> Wanting to develop creatively, <b>Mikhail Baryshnikov defecting from his native Russia to the United States and hoping to experience artistic freedom and
</b> be able to explore a variety of forms of dance.



 Since neither “defecting” nor “hoping” can on its own serve as a main verb, this version has no main verb and thus is not a complete sentence.


> <b>(D)</b> Wanting to develop creatively, <b>the defection of Mikhail Baryshnikov to the United States from his native Russia was from his hoping to experience artistic freedom and to
</b> be able to explore a variety of forms of dance.



 We can readily eliminate this version because the opening modifier, “wanting to develop creatively,” is misplaced so as to illogically modify “the defection of Mikhail Baryshnikov.” After all, “the defection” would not have been “wanting to develop creatively.”


Notice also that it’s not logical to say that “Mikhail Baryshnikov’s defection … was from his hoping.” After all, while he defected <em>because</em> he was hoping to do certain things, his defection did not come “from his hoping.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E)</b> Wanting to develop creatively, <b>Mikhail Baryshnikov defected from his native Russia to the United States, hoping to experience artistic freedom and
</b> be able to explore a variety of forms of dance.



 This choice is grammatically correct and logically constructed and conveys a meaning that makes sense.


Also, as discussed in the explanation of choice (B), this choice is a bit more parallel than choice (B), which does not have any major issues.


So, this choice is the best answer to this question.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Sentence Correction Example 28  

A system of drainage canals is being installed in the city for the purpose of <u>mitigating flooding in the near term with prevention of it</u> in the more distant future.

1. [ ] (A) mitigating flooding in the near term with prevention of it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) mitigating flooding in the near term and preventing it
3. [ ] (C) flood mitigation in the near term and prevention of it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) flood mitigating in the near term and its prevention
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) mitigating flooding in the near term and the prevention of it
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

##### Question Level: medium



> <b>(A)</b> A system of drainage canals is being installed in the city for the purpose of <b>mitigating flooding in the near term with prevention of it
</b> in the more distant future.



 Notice that, in this version, “for the purpose of mitigating flooding in the near term with prevention of it in the more distant future” conveys a nonsensical meaning. After all, it seems to convey that the purpose is to mitigate flooding in the near term “with,” i.e., “by means of” prevention of it in the future. Of course, it’s not possible to mitigate flooding in the near term by preventing in the more distant future. So, we can eliminate this choice because this version has a meaning issue.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B)</b> A system of drainage canals is being installed in the city for the purpose of <b>mitigating flooding in the near term and preventing it
</b> in the more distant future.



 This choice is grammatically correct and logically constructed and conveys a meaning that makes sense.


Also, as discussed in the explanation of choice (E), this choice is a bit more parallel than choice (E), which does not have any major issues.


So, this choice is the best answer to this question.


> <b>(C)</b> A system of drainage canals is being installed in the city for the purpose of <b>flood mitigation in the near term and prevention of it
</b> in the more distant future.



 This choice may appear correct at first glance because “mitigation” matches “prevention.”


However, notice that the pronoun “it” has no noun to refer to. After all, in this version “flood” is used as an adjective to modify “mitigation,” not as a noun. Also, “it” cannot logically refer to “flood mitigation” since it would not make sense to say that the system of canals is being installed for “prevention of flood mitigation.”


> <b>(D)</b> A system of drainage canals is being installed in the city for the purpose of <b>flood mitigating in the near term and its prevention
</b> in the more distant future.



 The clearest reason to eliminate this choice is that “it” has no noun to refer to. After all, in this version “flood” is used as an adjective to modify “mitigating, not as a noun. Also, “it” cannot logically refer to “flood mitigating” since it would not make sense to say that the system of canals is being installed for “prevention of flood mitigating.”


Another sign that this choice is incorrect is that people don’t normally say, “flood mitigating.” They say, “flood mitigation.” Is that a clear reason to eliminate this choice? Perhaps not, but it’s a reason to suspect that this choice is not the correct answer.


> <b>(E)</b> A system of drainage canals is being installed in the city for the purpose of <b>mitigating flooding in the near term and the prevention of it
</b> in the more distant future.



 This version has no major issues. So, often, people answering this question are left with this choice and choice (B). At that point, arriving at the correct answer requires considering the difference between this version and the (B) version to find a reason to choose one choice over the other.


The only difference is that, whereas this version says, “mitigating flooding in the near term and the prevention of it,” the (B) version says, “mitigating flooding in the near term and preventing it.” We see that what this version says is a little less parallel than what the (B) version says.


So, since there’s no other significant difference between what this version says and what the (B) version says, we can eliminate this choice and choose (B) because the (B) version is a bit more parallel.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
