## Lesson Content
Gerunds, verb forms that end in –ing, such as “sailing” or “writing,” are essentially nouns, and infinitives, verbs preceded by “to,” such as “to sail” or “to write,” can also be used as nouns.

However, despite this similarity between gerunds and infinitives, the convention followed by the GMAT is that gerunds and infinitives are not parallel.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The GMAT prefers versions that do not use gerunds and infinitives together in lists.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: I like <strong>playing</strong> tennis, <strong>cycling</strong> in the mountains, and <strong>to relax</strong> at the end of the day.

Even though it may seem fine as it is, the sentence would not be preferred by the GMAT because it contains a list that includes two gerunds, “playing” and “cycling,” and an infinitive, “to relax.”

The GMAT would prefer one of the following.

A list composed of infinitives:

> I like <strong>to play</strong> tennis, <strong>to cycle</strong> in the mountains, and <strong>to relax</strong> at the end of the day.

A list composed of gerunds:

> I like <strong>playing</strong> tennis, <strong>cycling</strong> in the mountains, and <strong>relaxing</strong> at the end of the day.

##  Sentence Correction Example 29  

Before he began work on a painting, the modern artist Balthus liked <u>to meditate for long periods in front of the canvas and smoking</u> to increase his concentration.

1. [ ] (A) to meditate for long periods in front of the canvas and smoking
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) meditating for long periods in front of the canvas and smoking
3. [ ] (C) to meditate, long periods in front of the canvas, and with smoking
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) meditating for long periods in front of the canvas and to smoke
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) for long periods to meditate in front of the canvas and smoking
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A)</b> Before he began work on a painting, the modern artist Balthus liked <b>to meditate for long periods in front of the canvas and smoking
</b> to increase his concentration.



 A list of two elements in which one element begins with an infinitive verb and the other element begins with a gerund would not be preferred on the GMAT. This version contains a list of two elements introduced by “liked”:


<strong>liked</strong> to meditate for long periods in front of the canvas and <strong>liked</strong> smoking


Since “to meditate” is an infinitive and “smoking” is a gerund, this construction is not preferred. However, since this version of the sentence does not contain any conclusive grammar or meaning errors, let’s not rule it out altogether before examining the other answer choices. Remember, the best of five possible answer choices in a GMAT question may not always be written in the best way imaginable.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B)</b> Before he began work on a painting, the modern artist Balthus liked <b>meditating for long periods in front of the canvas and smoking
</b> to increase his concentration.



 This version contains a list of two elements both of which begin with gerunds, “meditating” and “smoking”:


<strong>Balthus liked</strong> meditating for long periods in front of the canvas and <strong>Balthus liked</strong> smoking


The sentence contains no grammar or meaning errors. With all that considered, this answer choice is preferable to choices (A) and (D), which we can now eliminate, since two gerunds are considered more parallel than a gerund and an infinitive verb on the GMAT.


> <b>(C)</b> Before he began work on a painting, the modern artist Balthus liked <b>to meditate, long periods in front of the canvas, and with smoking
</b> to increase his concentration.



This third element in the list in this version, “with smoking,” is not parallel to the infinitive verb “to meditate” or the noun phrase “long periods in front of the canvas.”


Furthermore, if we carry over the what introduces the list, “Balthus liked,” we can see that the third element does not convey a logical meaning:


<strong>Balthus liked</strong> to meditate, <strong>Balthus liked</strong> long periods in front of the canvas, and <strong>Balthus liked</strong> with smoking to increase his concentration.


“Balthus liked with smoking to increase his concentration” is a nonsensical statement.


The sentence is not parallel or logical.


> <b>(D)</b> Before he began work on a painting, the modern artist Balthus liked <b>meditating for long periods in front of the canvas and to smoke
</b> to increase his concentration.



A list of two elements in which one element begins with a gerund and the other element begins with an infinitive verb would not be preferred on the GMAT. This version contains a list of two elements introduced by “liked”:


<strong>liked</strong> meditating for long periods in front of the canvas and <strong>liked</strong> to smoke


Since “meditating” is a gerund and “to smoke” is an infinitive, this construction is not preferred. However, notice that, as in (A), there are no conclusive grammar or meaning errors in this version. In fact, the only difference between this version and the version created with (A) is that the infinitive verb and the gerund are reversed in order. However, both choices feature an infinitive verb and a gerund as the two elements in a list, providing another clue that neither choice (since both are essentially the same) will end up being the best choice.


> <b>(E)</b> Before he began work on a painting, the modern artist Balthus liked <b>for long periods to meditate in front of the canvas and smoking
</b> to increase his concentration.



A list of two elements in which one element begins with an infinitive verb (“to meditate”) and the other element begins with a gerund (“smoking”) would not be preferred on the GMAT. This version contains a list of two such elements, introduced by “Balthus liked”:


<strong>Balthus</strong> <strong>liked</strong> for long periods to meditate in front of the canvas and <strong>Balthus liked</strong> smoking


Notice also that “for long periods” is a squinting modifier; it could modify “liked” or “to meditate.” Was the activity of meditation something that Balthus liked for long periods of his life, or was the activity something that Balthus liked to do for long periods at a time? Given the current placement of modifier, we can’t be sure which meaning is intended.


Since the sentence contains a list that is not parallel, and the sentence conveys an ambiguous, meaning this choice is incorrect.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
