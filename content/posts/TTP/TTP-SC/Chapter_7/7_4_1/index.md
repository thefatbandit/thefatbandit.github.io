## Lesson Content
There are many methods that can be used to determine whether structures in a sentence are written with proper parallelism, but many of these are either just too grammatical or too complicated to work well on test day.

Instead, we’re going to use a method that is based on logic and is easy to use on test day.

To determine whether a structure built around a FANBOYS is parallel:

<u>Step 1</u>: Notice the FANBOYS conjunction.

As we know, FANBOYS conjunctions are always triggers to check for parallelism.

For example, if we encountered the following sentence, we would notice the FANBOYS conjunction it contains, which is “and”:

> Computers must be fast <strong>and</strong> to be reliable.

<u>Step 2</u>: Determine what elements the FANBOYS conjunction connects.

> Computers must be (fast) <strong>and</strong> (to be reliable.)

In this sentence, the FANBOYS conjunction connects the two things that computers must be.

<u>Step 3</u>: Locate the word(s) that introduce(s) the elements connected by the FANBOYS conjunction.

> <strong>Computers must be</strong> (fast) and (to be reliable.)

We see that what the “and” connects are two things that computers must be.

<u>Step 4</u>: Determine whether the elements connected by the FANBOYS conjunction work logically with what introduces those elements.

The way to determine whether each of the elements connected by the FANBOYS conjunction works logically with what introduces those elements is to create two separate test sentences using the introductory word(s) and the elements themselves.

> Computers must be<strong> fast</strong>.

> Computers must be <strong>to be reliable</strong>.

Notice that, although the first sentence makes sense, the second does not.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Computers must be fast <strong>and</strong> to be reliable.

Notice that we needed to know almost nothing about grammar to see that this sentence uses faulty parallelism.

We could correct the sentence by fixing the element after the FANBOYS conjunction:

> <u>Correct</u>: Computers must be <strong>fast</strong> and <strong>reliable</strong>.

Notice that, now, the sentence passes our parallelism test:

> Computers must be <strong>fast</strong>.

> Computers must be <strong>reliable</strong>.

Because both of our test sentences make logical sense, the parallelism across the FANBOYS conjunction is done correctly.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Each of the elements connected by a FANBOYS conjunction must work logically with what introduces those elements.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s try another sentence:

> To succeed at this company, team members must communicate and having open minds.

Reading the sentence, we notice the use of the FANBOYS conjunction “and,” which alerts us to check for parallelism.

Next, we determine what the FANBOYS conjunction connects.

Next, we determine what introduces the elements connected by the FANBOYS conjunction.

We know that each of the elements connected by the FANBOYS conjunction must work logically with what introduces them.

> Team members must <strong>communicate</strong>.

> Team members must <strong>having open minds</strong>.

Notice that, although the first sentence makes sense, the second does not.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: To succeed at this company, team members must <strong>communicate </strong>and <strong>having open minds</strong>.

We could correct the sentence by fixing the element after the FANBOYS conjunction:

> To succeed at this company, team members must <strong>communicate </strong>and <strong>have </strong><strong>open minds</strong>.

Now, the structure built around “and” is properly parallel.

So, we now know that the FANBOYS conjunctions are always triggers for us to check for parallelism and that, when one of the FANBOYS is used in a sentence, parallelism must be maintained.

Now, not every Sentence Correction question that includes one of the FANBOYS will involve flawed parallel structure.

So, how can we use this knowledge to score points on test day? Simple! We eliminate any sentence versions that don’t maintain proper parallelism across a FANBOYS conjunction.

##  Sentence Correction Example 2  

The new type of sailboat <u>is capable of harnessing even the slightest breeze or to travel</u> on foils to take advantage of heavy winds, so it is a versatile racing craft.

1. [ ] (A) is capable of harnessing even the slightest breeze or to travel
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) is able to harness even the slightest of breezes or travel
3. [ ] (C) has ability of harnessing even the slightest breezes or to travel
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) has the capability of harnessing even the slightest breeze or by traveling
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) can harness even the slightest of breezes or traveling
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



All versions of the sentence use the FANBOYS conjunction “or.” Since “or” must connect parallel elements, what precedes “or” and what follows “or” must be parallel.


To determine whether the elements connected by “or” are parallel, we can combine them with what introduces them and see whether the resulting constructions make sense.


> <b>(A)</b> The new type of sailboat <b>is capable of harnessing even the slightest breeze or to travel
</b> on foils to take advantage of heavy winds, so it is a versatile racing craft.



 In this version, we have the following:


The new type of sailboat is capable of <strong>harnessing even the slighted breeze</strong> or <strong>to travel …</strong>


Combining each of the elements connected by “or” with what introduces them, we get the following:


The new type of sailboat is capable of <strong>harnessing even the slighted breeze</strong> – makes sense


The new type of sailboat is capable of <strong>to travel …</strong> – does not make sense


Since the second construction does not make sense, the elements are not parallel.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B)</b> The new type of sailboat <b>is able to harness even the slightest of breezes or travel
</b> on foils to take advantage of heavy winds, so it is a versatile racing craft.



 In this version, we have the following:


The new type of sailboat is able to <strong>harness even the slightest of breezes</strong> or <strong>travel …</strong>


Combining each of the elements connected by “or” with what introduces them, we get the following:


The new type of sailboat is able to <strong>harness even the slighted of breezes</strong> – makes sense


The new type of sailboat is able <strong>to travel …</strong> – makes sense


Since both constructions make sense, the elements are parallel, and this version is correct overall.


> <b>(C)</b> The new type of sailboat <b>has ability of harnessing even the slightest breezes or to travel
</b> on foils to take advantage of heavy winds, so it is a versatile racing craft.



 In this version, we have the following:


The new type of sailboat has ability of <strong>harnessing even the slighted breeze</strong> or <strong>to travel …</strong>


In this case, the sentence simply doesn’t make sense, since “has ability of harnessing” is not logical or idiomatically correct. What would be correct is “has the ability to harness.”


Also, even “has ability … <strong>to travel</strong>” is a little off. It should be “has the ability … <strong>to travel</strong>.”


> <b>(D)</b> The new type of sailboat <b>has the capability of harnessing even the slightest breeze or by traveling
</b> on foils to take advantage of heavy winds, so it is a versatile racing craft.



 In this version, we have the following:


The new type of sailboat has the capability of <strong>harnessing even the slightest breeze</strong> or <strong>by traveling …</strong>


Combining each of the elements connected by “or” with what introduces them, we get:


The new type of sailboat has the capability of <strong>harnessing even the slighted breeze</strong> – makes sense


The new type of sailboat has the capability of <strong>by traveling …</strong> – does not make sense


Since the second construction does not make sense (and by the way, even the first one is not quite idiomatically correct), the elements are not parallel.


> <b>(E)</b> The new type of sailboat <b>can harness even the slightest of breezes or traveling
</b> on foils to take advantage of heavy winds, so it is a versatile racing craft.



 In this version, we have the following:


The new type of sailboat can <strong>harness even the slightest of breezes</strong> or <strong>traveling …</strong>


Combining each of the elements connected by “or” with what introduces them, we get:


The new type of sailboat can <strong>harness even the slighted of breezes</strong> – makes sense


The new type of sailboat can <strong>traveling …</strong> – does not make sense


Since the second construction does not make sense, the elements are not parallel.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
