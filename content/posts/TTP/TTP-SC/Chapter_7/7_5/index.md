## Lesson Content
Without realizing it, you’ve been not only learning about parallelism in structures built across FANBOYS but also learning about parallelism in lists, which frequently appear in SC questions.

More specifically, you’ve been learning about parallelism in lists of two items because the structures built across FANBOYS that we have been discussing are essentially two-item lists.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Structures built around FANBOYS conjunctions are two-element lists. These lists must maintain proper parallel structure.
{{< /alert >}}

For example:

> Maria went swimming <strong>and</strong> took a bath.

Notice that we have a two-element list because Maria did two things:

> (1) Maria went swimming.

> (2) Maria took a bath.

For example:

> The current in the river was strong <strong>yet </strong>manageable.

Notice that we have another two-element list because the current was two things:

> (1) The current in the river was strong.

> (2) The current in the river was manageable.

For example:

> People living at high altitudes must learn to tolerate thin air <strong>or</strong> suffer the consequences.

Notice that we have another two-element list because people living at high altitudes have two choices:

> (1) People living at high altitudes must learn to tolerate thin air.

> (2) People living at high altitudes must suffer the consequences.

At this point, we’re going to discuss parallelism in lists in general, considering in more detail how to determine whether a list is properly parallel.
