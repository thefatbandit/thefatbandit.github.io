## Lesson Content
“Carrying over” simply refers to the concept that the sentence structures that introduce a list are understood to be repeated throughout the list, although these elements are not explicitly rewritten.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

“Carrying over” refers to the concept that certain elements of a structure involving a list are understood to be repeated throughout the list, although these elements are not explicitly rewritten.
{{< /alert >}}

You saw this concept (although we didn’t use the term carrying over) a short while ago in the sections on parallelism across FANBOYS.

For example, in the sentence:

> Bananas are healthy and satisfying.

“Bananas are” introduces the two elements in the list to convey the meaning that:

> Bananas are healthy.

> Bananas are satisfying.

Notice that “bananas are” carries over to each element in the list without being explicitly written because “bananas are” introduces the list.

Here is an easy way to visualize what’s happening.

> <strong>Bananas are</strong> (healthy and satisfying).

Now, because “bananas are” carries over to each item in the list, we’ll distribute “bananas are” to each of the items in the list to see what the sentence communicates.

> Bananas are healthy.

> Bananas are satisfying.

Let’s apply this concept to a list of three elements.

> Members post their comments, complaints, and suggestions on the appropriate message boards.

The same as in a list of two items, in a three-item list, whatever is connected to or introduces the list is understood to carry over to all the elements in the list without being explicitly written.

Here’s an easy way to visualize what’s happening.

> <strong>Members post their</strong> (comments, complaints, and suggestions) on the appropriate message boards.

Now, we will distribute “members post their” to each of the items in the list to see what the sentence communicates:

> <strong>Members post their </strong>comments, <strong>members post their </strong>complaints,and<strong> members post their </strong>suggestions on the appropriate message boards.

Notice that the sentence communicates three things via the use of the list:

> Members post their comments.

> Members post their complaints.

> Members post their suggestions.

This exercise of using parentheses and then distributing (carrying over) is important because it’s an easy, effective way to determine whether items in a list make logical sense.

> Members post their comments.

> Members post their complaints.

> Members post their suggestions.

Now that we understand how carry over works in lists, let’s leverage this new knowledge and learn a simple strategy involving the idea of carry over that can be used to determine whether a list is parallel.
