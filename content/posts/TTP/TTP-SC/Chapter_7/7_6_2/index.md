## Lesson Content
To determine whether the items in a list are parallel, we’ll follow the following steps:

<u>Step1</u>: Notice the list in the sentence.

Lists are always parallelism triggers.

> Please leave a message including your name, your number, and explain why you called.

<u>Step 2</u>: Identify the elements of the list.

Our next move in analyzing a sentence for proper parallelism in a list is to identify the elements of the list.

> Please leave a message including <strong>your name</strong>, <strong>your number</strong>, and <strong>explain why you called</strong>.

We see that the list in the sentence consists of three things that the message is including: “your name,” “your number,” and “explain why you called.”

<u>Step 3</u>: Determine the word(s) that introduce(s) the list.

> <strong>Please leave a message including</strong> your name, your number, and explain why you called.

We have some flexibility here.

<u>Step 4</u>: Carry over the word(s) that introduce(s) the list to each element of the list and check to see whether the words that introduce the list work logically with each of the elements.

Let’s use some parentheses and distribute (carry over):

> <strong>Please leave a message including</strong> (your name, your number, and explain why you called).

> <strong>Please leave a message including</strong> your name, <strong>please leave a message including</strong> your number, and <strong>please leave a message including</strong> explain why you called.

The words that carry over to the items in the list must make logical sense with the items in the list.

> Please leave a message including your name.

> Please leave a message including your number.

> Please leave a message including explain why you called.

Notice that, although the first two test sentences make sense, the third is illogical.

<u>Incorrect</u>: Please leave a message including your name, your number, and explain why you called.

Notice that we did not need to rely on knowledge of grammar to see that the third element does not work logically with the rest of the sentence.

We could fix the sentence by changing the last item to make it a noun phrase like the other two items.

> Please leave a message including your name, your number, and your reason for calling.

Notice that now the list makes perfect sense:

> <strong>Please leave a message including</strong> (your name, your number, and your reason for calling).

> <strong>Please leave a message including</strong> your name, <strong>please leave a message including</strong> your number, and <strong>please leave a message including</strong> your reason for calling.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The words that carry over to the items in the list must make logical sense with the items in the list.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s consider another example sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Above all else, the Governor’s new platform emphasized collective fiscal responsibility, mutual respect among all citizens, and the metropolitan school district to become the best in the nation.

We see that the sentence contains a list.

> Above all else, the Governor’s new platform emphasized <strong>collective fiscal responsibility</strong>, <strong>mutual respect among all citizens</strong>, and <strong>the metropolitan school district to become the best in the nation</strong>.

Next we determine that “the Governor’s new platform emphasized” introduces the list.

> Above all else, <strong>the Governor’s new platform emphasized</strong> (collective fiscal responsibility, mutual respect among all citizens, and the metropolitan school district to become the best in the nation).

So, the sentence says:

> Above all else, <strong>the Governor’s new platform</strong> <strong>emphasized</strong> collective fiscal responsibility, <strong>the Governor’s new platform emphasized</strong> mutual respect among all citizens, and <strong>the Governor’s new platform emphasized</strong> the metropolitan school district to become the best in the nation.

Of course, the last element in the list does not make sense with what introduces the list.

What did the Governor’s new platform emphasize?

> The Governor’s new platform emphasized collective fiscal responsibility.

> The Governor’s new platform emphasized mutual respect among all citizens.

> The Governor’s new platform emphasized the metropolitan school district to become the best in the nation.

Notice that, although the first two elements of the list work with the wording that introduces the list, the third does not.

So, how could we make the sentence logical?

> Above all else, the Governor’s new platform emphasized collective fiscal responsibility, mutual respect among all citizens, and the need for the metropolitan school district to become the best in the nation.

Notice that now the list and the sentence are logically written.

> Above all else, the Governor’s new platform emphasized the need for the metropolitan school district to become the best in the nation.

<strong>Remember, when a list is introduced, each of the elements in the list must work logically with the part of the sentence that introduces the list.</strong>

How can we use this knowledge to score points on test day? We can eliminate any sentence versions containing lists that do not maintain proper parallelism.

    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

##### Question Level: easy



In this sentence, a three-item list is introduced by “World War II was caused by.” In the correct version of the sentence, all three items in the list work logically with “World War II was caused by.”


> <b>(A)</b> The historian argued that World War II was caused by Germany’s resentment of the onerous conditions imposed <b>with the Treaty of Versailles, Italy hoped to expand its colonies in Africa, and Japan’s desire to increase their
</b> access to natural resources such as oil.



The second item in the list does not work with “World War II was caused by,” as we can see by combining the two:


World War II was caused by Italy hoped to expand its colonies. – does not make sense


Also, it does not make sense to say that conditions were imposed “with” the treaty. They were imposed <em>by </em>the treaty.


Also, the plural pronoun “their” does not agree with its antecedent, the singular “Japan.”


> <b>(B)</b> The historian argued that World War II was caused by Germany’s resentment of the onerous conditions imposed <b>by the Treaty of Versailles, Italy’s hope to expand its colonies in Africa, and Japan desired to increase its
</b> access to natural resources such as oil.



The third item in the list does not work with “World War II was caused by,” as we can see by combining the two.


World War II was caused by Japan desired to increase its access to natural resources. – does not make sense


> <b>(C)</b> The historian argued that World War II was caused by Germany’s resentment of the onerous conditions imposed <b>on the Treaty of Versailles, Italy’s hope to expand its colonies in Africa, and Japan’s desire to increase their
</b> access to natural resources such as oil.



While the expression “imposed on” is idiomatically correct, using “imposed on” in this context does not make sense, as the conditions were not imposed on the treaty. They were imposed by the treaty.


Also, the plural pronoun “their” does not agree with its antecedent, the singular “Japan.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D)</b> The historian argued that World War II was caused by Germany’s resentment of the onerous conditions imposed <b>by the Treaty of Versailles, Italy’s hope to expand its colonies in Africa, and Japan’s desire to increase its
</b> access to natural resources such as oil.



In this version, all three items in the list work logically with “World War II was caused by.”


Also, it makes sense to say that conditions were imposed “by” the treaty of Versailles.


> <b>(E)</b> The historian argued that World War II was caused by Germany’s resentment of the onerous conditions imposed <b>from the Treaty of Versailles, Italy’s hope to expand its colonies in Africa, and Japan’s desire for increasing its
</b> access to natural resources such as oil.



 The list in this version is parallel, but the meaning conveyed by this version doesn’t make sense.


It doesn’t make sense to say, “conditions imposed <em>from</em> the treaty.” The conditions were imposed “by” the treaty.


Also, it doesn’t make sense to say, “Japan’s desire for increasing its access to natural resources.” That phrase communicates that Japan desired the act of increasing its access rather than desired the actual increased access. The point to be made is that Japan desired increased access or that Japan desired to increase its access.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

While sharks are types of fish, sharks differ from most other fish in that sharks, unlike other fish, have skeletons composed of cartilage rather than bone, <u>have been covered in sharp, toothlike scales called denticles rather than smooth scales, and have not a single covered gill slit, having up to seven exposed gill slits on each side</u>.

    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) they are covered in sharp, tooth-like scales called denticles rather than smooth scales, and have not a single, covered gill slit but up to seven exposed gill slits on each side

    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)

##### Question Level: medium



The sentence contains a list introduced by “sharks, unlike other fish.” In the correct version, all of the elements of the list work logically with “sharks, unlike other fish.”


> <b>(A)</b> While sharks are types of fish, sharks differ from most other fish in that sharks, unlike other fish, have skeletons composed of cartilage rather than bone, <b>have been covered in sharp, tooth like scales called denticles rather than smooth scales, and have not a single covered gill slit, having up to seven exposed gill slits on each side
</b>.



All elements of the list in this version work logically with what introduces the list, “sharks, unlike other fish.” However, the meaning conveyed by this version does not make sense.


It does not make sense to say that “sharks have been covered in denticles,” as that wording suggests that someone other than the sharks covered the sharks in denticles.


Also, the meaning conveyed by “have not a single covered gill slit, having up to seven exposed gill slits on each side” is unclear, and it seems to be the somewhat illogical meaning that, as a result of sharks’ having up to seven exposed gill slits, sharks don’t have a single, i.e., don’t even have one, covered gill slit.


> <b>(B)</b> While sharks are types of fish, sharks differ from most other fish in that sharks, unlike other fish, have skeletons composed of cartilage rather than bone, <b>covered in sharp, tooth-like scales called denticles rather than smooth scales, and having up to seven gill slits on each side, rather than a single, covered gill slit
</b>.



In this version, everything that follows “skeletons composed of cartilage rather than bone” appears to be a modifier modifying “skeletons composed of cartilage rather than bone.” Thus, since it does not make sense to describe a skeleton as covered in denticles and having gill slits, this version does not make sense.


> <b>(C)</b> While sharks are types of fish, sharks differ from most other fish in that sharks, unlike other fish, have skeletons composed of cartilage rather than bone, <b>they are covered in sharp, tooth-like scales called denticles rather than smooth scales, and have not a single, covered gill slit but up to seven exposed gill slits on each side
</b>.



In this version, the second element of the list does not work logically with “sharks, unlike other fish,” since it unnecessarily repeats the subject via the use of the pronoun “they,” as we can see by combining “sharks, unlike other fish” with the second element:


sharks, unlike other fish, they are covered in sharp, tooth-like scales called denticles rather than smooth scales


> <b>(D)</b> While sharks are types of fish, sharks differ from most other fish in that sharks, unlike other fish, have skeletons composed of cartilage rather than bone, <b>are covered in sharp, tooth-like scales called denticles rather than smooth scales, and by having up to seven exposed gill slits on each side, rather than a single covered gill slit
</b>.



In this version, the third element of the list does not work logically with “sharks, unlike other fish,” as we can see by considering the following:


… <strong>sharks, unlike other fish,</strong> (have skeletons composed of cartilage rather than bone, are covered in sharp, tooth-like scales called denticles rather than smooth scales, and by having up to seven exposed gill slits on each side, rather than a single covered gill slit).


… <strong>sharks, unlike other fish,</strong> have skeletons composed of cartilage rather than bone, <strong>sharks, unlike other fish,</strong> are covered in sharp, tooth-like scales called denticles rather than smooth scales, and <strong>sharks, unlike other fish,</strong> by having up to seven exposed gill slits on each side, rather than a single covered gill slit


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E)</b> While sharks are types of fish, sharks differ from most other fish in that sharks, unlike other fish, have skeletons composed of cartilage rather than bone, <b>are covered in sharp, tooth-like scales called denticles rather than smooth scales, and have, instead of a single, covered gill slit, up to seven exposed gill slits on each side
</b>.



All of the elements of the list in this version work logically with what introduces the list, “sharks, unlike other fish,” as we can see by considering the following:


… <strong>sharks, unlike other fish,</strong> (have skeletons composed of cartilage rather than bone, are covered in sharp, tooth-like scales called denticles rather than smooth scales, and have, instead of a single, covered gill slit, up to seven exposed gill slits on each side).


… <strong>sharks, unlike other fish,</strong> have skeletons composed of cartilage rather than bone, <strong>sharks, unlike other fish,</strong> are covered in sharp, tooth-like scales called denticles rather than smooth scales, and <strong>sharks, unlike other fish,</strong> have, instead of a single, covered gill slit, up to seven exposed gill slits on each side.


The fact that the first two elements of the list in this version use “rather than,” while the third element uses “instead of” does not render the elements non-parallel.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
