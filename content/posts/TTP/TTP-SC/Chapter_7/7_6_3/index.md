## Lesson Content
From our discussion of carrying over, we know that we need not explicitly write a word such as “to” before every element in a list in a sentence like the following:

> The members of the committee decided <strong>to </strong>reduce marketing expenditures, <strong>to </strong>increase headcount significantly, and <strong>to </strong>change the schedule.

In other words, one “to” in front of the first element of the list is perfect, as you’ve seen in sentences like the following:

> The members of the committee decided <strong>to</strong> reduce marketing expenditures, increase headcount significantly, and change the schedule.

In other words, the “to” can introduce the list, and in such a case, the “to” is understood to carry over to every element in the list.

So, why are we bringing this concept up again? Well, test-takers are sometimes fooled into accepting as correct sentences like the following:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The members of the committee decided <strong>to</strong> reduce marketing expenditures, increase headcount significantly, and <strong>to </strong>change the schedule.

Notice that the “to”s are haphazardly placed in the list.

A repeating element, such as “to,” that is essential to what introduces a list must either appear once, before the first item in the list, OR appear in every one of the items in the list.

In the examples above, “to” is essential to what introduces the list, because “to” is necessary for connecting “decided” to the verbs that make up the list.

Now, the good news is that this rule applies to any repeating element that’s essential to what introduces a list, whether it be a preposition, such as “to” or “by,” or another part of speech.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Repeating elements such as “to,” “from,” and “that” cannot be haphazardly placed in a list if they are essential to what introduces the list.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following examples:

> <u>Correct</u>: The three-week expedition to Bolivia consisted mostly <strong>of </strong>taking photos of the country’s archaeology found in remote areas, enjoying the natural beauty of its mountains, lakes, and deserts, and spending time in the metropolitan city of La Paz.

Notice that, in the above example, the preposition “of” leads the three-element list and is understood to apply to each of the list elements.

> <u>Correct</u>: The three-week expedition to Bolivia consisted mostly <strong>of </strong>taking photos of the country’s archaeology found in remote areas, <strong>of </strong>enjoying the natural beauty of its mountains, lakes, and deserts, and <strong>of </strong>spending time in the metropolitan city of La Paz.

Notice that “of” is now present in each of the items in the three-element list.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The three-week expedition to Bolivia consisted mostly taking photos of the country’s archaeology found in remote areas, <strong>of </strong>enjoying the natural beauty of its distant mountains, lakes, and desserts, and <strong>of </strong>spending time in the metropolitan city of La Paz.

“Of” can lead the list or be present in each element in the list.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The three-week expedition to Bolivia consisted mostly taking photos of the country’s archaeology found in remote areas, <strong>of </strong>enjoying the natural beauty of its distant mountains, lakes, and desserts, and spending time in the metropolitan city of La Paz.

“Of” can lead the list or be present in each element in the list.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The three-week expedition to Bolivia consisted mostly taking photos of the country’s archaeology found in remote areas, enjoying the natural beauty of its distant mountains, lakes, and desserts, and <strong>of </strong>spending time in the metropolitan city of La Paz.

“Of” can lead the list or be present in each element in the list.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The three-week expedition to Bolivia consisted mostly <strong>of </strong>taking photos of the country’s archaeology found in remote areas, enjoying the natural beauty of its mountains, lakes, and desserts, and <strong>of </strong>spending time in the metropolitan city of La Paz.

“Of” can lead the list or be present in each element in the list.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A repeating element that is essential to what introduces a list must either appear once, before the first item in the list, OR appear in every one of the items in the list. Such a repeating element cannot appear haphazardly in the list.
{{< /alert >}}

Of course, not all repeating elements are essential to what introduces the lists in which they appear.

> The groom was congratulated by <strong>his </strong>friends, <strong>his </strong>parents, and guests he was meeting for the first time.

In the above example, the fact that “his” appears in the first and second list elements but not in the third is not a flaw.

On the other hand, “by” is essential to what introduces the list, because “by” is what connects “congratulated” to the nouns that make up the list.

> The groom was congratulated by his friends, by his parents, and by guests he was meeting for the first time.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

A repeating element that is NOT essential to what introduces a list does not have to carry over to or appear before every item in the list.
{{< /alert >}}

How can we use this knowledge to score points on test day? We can eliminate any sentence version in which words that are essential to what introduces a list are haphazardly placed in front of some but not all elements of a list.

##  Sentence Correction Example 5  

New teachers should expect that students will challenge their authority, that materials will be outdated or unavailable, <u>and the administration will provide scant support</u>.

1. [ ] (A) and the administration will provide scant support
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) while receiving scant support from the administration
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) and that the administration’s support will be scant
4. [ ] (D) as the administration will provide scant support
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) since they will receive scant support from the administration
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

##### Question Level: easy



Since “that” is necessary for connecting “new teachers should expect” to the statements that express what new teachers should expect, “that” cannot be placed at the beginning of some but not all of the list elements that express what new teachers should expect.


> <b>(A)</b> New teachers should expect that students will challenge their authority, that materials will be outdated or unavailable, <b>and the administration will provide scant support
</b>.



The list in this version is not logical. “That” is necessary for connecting “new teachers should expect” to the statements that express what new teachers should expect, but “that” is placed at the beginning of two elements that express what new teachers should expect but not at the beginning of the third element.


> <b>(B)</b> New teachers should expect that students will challenge their authority, that materials will be outdated or unavailable, <b>while receiving scant support from the administration
</b>.



The meaning conveyed by this version is ambiguous because this version contains a misplaced modifier. Because “while receiving scant support …”<em> </em>follows the clauses about students and materials, the sentence could be interpreted as conveying that students and materials will receive scant support.


Also, since in this version, “while receiving scant support …” is a modifier, rather than a list item, the list is composed of two items, “that students will …” and “that materials will ….” Therefore, those two noun clauses should be connected by “and,” but no “and” connects them.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(C)</b> New teachers should expect that students will challenge their authority, that materials will be outdated or unavailable, <b>and that the administration’s support will be scant
</b>.



This version is clear and concise, and correctly includes “that” at the beginning of all three list items that express what new teachers should expect.


> <b>(D)</b> New teachers should expect that students will challenge their authority, that materials will be outdated or unavailable, <b>as the administration will provide scant support
</b>.



This version would work if there were an “and” between the two clauses about students and materials. However, since there is not an “and” between those two clauses, “as the administration …” seems to be a third item in the list that is not parallel to the other two items and does not work with what introduces the list, as we can see by combining the two:


new teachers should expect as the administration will provide scant support


> <b>(E)</b> New teachers should expect that students will challenge their authority, that materials will be outdated or unavailable, <b>since they will receive scant support from the administration
</b>.



In this version, “since they will receive …” is a modifying clause, rather than a list item. So, the list is composed of two items, “that students will …” and “that materials will ….” Therefore, those two items should be connected by “and,” but no “and” connects them.


Also, what “they” in “since they will receive …” refers to is not clear.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)
