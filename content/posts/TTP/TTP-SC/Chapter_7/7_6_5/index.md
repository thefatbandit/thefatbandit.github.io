## Lesson Content
Sometimes, in order for a sentence including a list to clearly convey a logical meaning, it’s necessary to repeat a preposition, such as “for,” “of,” “to,” “in,” “by,” or “because of” by placing the preposition before every item in the list.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Sometimes, in order for a sentence including a list to clearly convey a logical meaning, it’s necessary to repeat a preposition, such as “for,” “of,” “to,” “in,” “by,” or “because of” by placing the preposition before every item in the list.
{{< /alert >}}

For example, consider the following two sentences:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The physicist was aware <strong>of </strong>Stephen Hawking’s work on black holes and Einstein’s theories.

> <u>Correct</u>: The physicist was aware <strong>of </strong>Stephen Hawking’s work on black holes and <strong>of </strong>Einstein’s theories.

Notice, the first sentence does not repeat “of,” while the second does repeat “of.” So, what difference does repeating “of” make?

Let’s consider the first sentence.

> - The physicist was aware of Stephen Hawking’s work, work that was on black holes and Einstein’s theories.

> - The physicist was aware of Stephen Hawking’s work on black holes, and the physicist was aware of Einstein’s theories.

So, because it uses “of” just once, the first sentence does not clearly convey one meaning.

Now, let’s consider the second sentence.

> - The physicist was aware of Stephen Hawking’s work on black holes, and the physicist was aware of Einstein’s theories.

OK, now, what if someone wanted to convey that bags are needed for various types of items.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: For the expedition, we need bags <strong>for </strong>our supplies, the cameras, and the laptops.

Because that sentence uses “for” just once, what the sentence is meant to convey is not clear.

> - For the expedition, we need bags for our supplies, we need the cameras, and we need the laptops.

> - For the expedition, we need bags for our supplies, we need bags for the cameras, and we need bags for the laptops.

Now, here’s a version of the sentence in which “for” is used three times, rather than just once.

For the expedition we need bags <strong>for </strong>our supplies, <strong>for </strong>the cameras, and <strong>for </strong>the laptops.

Now the sentence conveys one meaning.

> - For the expedition we need bags for our supplies, we need bags for the cameras, and we need bags for the laptops.

Let’s look at another sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The manager wanted to know whether the additional employee training had been followed by material changes <strong>in</strong> product quality and frequency of significant defects.

As written, the sentence could be about product quality of significant defects and frequency of significant defects.

To make the sentence such that it more clearly conveys that meaning, we could add another “in,” as in:

> The manager wanted to know whether the additional employee training had been followed by material changes <strong>in</strong> product quality and <strong>in </strong>frequency of significant defects.

How can we use this knowledge to score points on test day? We can eliminate any sentence version with an unclear meaning resulting from its failure to effectively use prepositions to make a list clear.

##  Sentence Correction Example 6  

What the study found is that, often, a decrease in a company’s sales occurs because <u>incentives do not reward employees’ providing high quality service or</u> management's becoming out of touch with the customer.

1. [ ] (A) incentives do not reward employees’ providing high quality service or
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) of incentives that do not reward employees’ providing high quality service or
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) of incentives, which do not reward employees’ providing high quality service, or because of
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) of incentives, which do not reward employees’ providing high quality service or
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) of incentives that do not reward employees’ providing high quality service or because of

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Most versions of the sentence in this question contain a list that involves “employees’ providing high quality service” and “management’s becoming out of touch with the customer.”


At the same time, each version of the list is constructed a little differently, and in only one version of the list is “because of” repeated to create a list that clearly communicates a logical meaning.


> <b>(A)</b> What the study found is that, often, a decrease in a company’s sales occurs because <b>incentives do not reward employees’ providing high quality service or
</b> management's becoming out of touch with the customer.



 By carrying over what introduces the list in this version, we can see that this version conveys an illogical meaning.


a decrease in a company’s sales occurs because <strong>incentives do not reward</strong> (employees’ providing high quality service or management’s becoming out of touch with the customer)


a decrease in a company’s sales occurs because <strong>incentives do not reward</strong> employees’ providing high quality service or <strong>incentives do not reward</strong> management’s becoming out of touch with the customer


We can see that this version conveys that “a decrease in a company’s sales occurs because … <strong>incentives do not reward</strong> management’s becoming out of touch with the customer.” Of course, that meaning does not make sense since a company would not have a decrease in sales because “incentives do not reward management’s becoming out of touch with the customer.”


> <b>(B)</b> What the study found is that, often, a decrease in a company’s sales occurs because <b>of incentives that do not reward employees’ providing high quality service or
</b> management's becoming out of touch with the customer.



 Since “because of” is used only once in this version, this version seems to convey an illogical meaning, as we can see by carrying over what seems to introduce the list in this version.


a decrease in a company’s sales occurs because of <strong>incentives that do not reward</strong> (employees’ providing high quality service or management’s becoming out of touch with the customer)


a decrease in a company’s sales occurs because of <strong>incentives that do not reward</strong> employees’ providing high quality service or <strong>incentives that do not reward</strong> management’s becoming out of touch with the customer


We can see that this version seems to convey that “a decrease in a company’s sales occurs because of … <strong>incentives that do not reward</strong> management’s becoming out of touch with the customer.” Of course, that meaning does not make sense since a company would not have a decrease in sales “because of … incentives that do not reward management’s becoming out of touch with the customer.”


Now we could argue that there is a logical way to read this version, which is that a decrease in a company’s sales occurs “because of” incentives and “because of" management’s becoming out of touch with the customer. However, this version does seem to convey the illogical meaning discussed above. So, the GMAT would prefer a version that does not read as if it conveys an illogical meaning and that clearly conveys a meaning that makes sense. Thus, the GMAT would prefer choice (E) over this choice.


> <b>(C)</b> What the study found is that, often, a decrease in a company’s sales occurs because <b>of incentives, which do not reward employees’ providing high quality service, or because of
</b> management's becoming out of touch with the customer.



 Notice that, because “which do not reward employees’ providing high quality service” is a nonrestrictive modifier, this version does not communicate that the incentives mentioned are a specific type of incentives. So, it conveys the somewhat illogical meaning that a decrease in a company’s sales occurs because of incentives in general.


Then, “incentives, which do not reward employees’ providing high quality service,” suggests the illogical meaning that incentives in general “do not reward employees’ providing high quality service.”


> <b>(D)</b> What the study found is that, often, a decrease in a company’s sales occurs because <b>of incentives, which do not reward employees’ providing high quality service or
</b> management's becoming out of touch with the customer.



 Notice that, because “which do not reward employees’ providing high quality service …” is a nonrestrictive modifier, this version does not communicate that the incentives mentioned are a specific type of incentives. So, it conveys the somewhat illogical meaning that a decrease in a company’s sales occurs because of incentives in general.


Then, “incentives, which do not reward employees’ providing high quality service …,” suggests the illogical meaning that incentives in general “do not reward employees’ providing high quality service.”


Finally, “a decrease in a company’s sales occurs because of incentives, which do not reward … management’s becoming out of touch with the customer,” suggests the illogical idea that a decrease in a company’s sales occurs because of incentives not rewarding management’s becoming out of touch.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E)</b> What the study found is that, often, a decrease in a company’s sales occurs because <b>of incentives that do not reward employees’ providing high quality service or because of
</b> management's becoming out of touch with the customer.



 Since “because of” is repeated in this version, this version clearly conveys the logical meaning that a decrease in a company’s sales occurs “because of incentives that do not reward employees’ providing high quality service” or “because of management’s becoming out of touch with the customer.”


Since this version more clearly conveys a meaning that makes sense than the (B) version, the GMAT would prefer this choice over choice (B).


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Sentence Correction Example 7  

The use of social media sites, many of which were at first used primarily by teenagers and college students, <u>have become popular with adults using the sites for updating friends and celebrities using the sites for promotion and for</u> communicating with fans.

1. [ ] (A) have become popular with adults using the sites for updating friends and celebrities using the sites for promotion and for
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) has become popular with adults using the sites for updating friends, and with celebrities, who use the sites for promotion and for
3. [ ] (C) became popular with adults, using the sites for updating friends, and with celebrities, using the sites for promoting and
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) is becoming popular with adults, from their use of the sites for updating friends, and with celebrities, from their use of the sites for promoting and
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) has become popular with adults using the sites for updating friends and celebrities, using the sites for promoting and for
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



There are two two-item lists in the sentence. In the best version, the first list is “with adults … and with celebrities,” and the second is “for promotion and for communicating.” Both lists best express meanings that make sense when the prepositions, “with,” in the first list, and “for,” in the second list, are repeated.


> <b>(A)</b> The use of social media sites, many of which were at first used primarily by teenagers and college students, <b>have become popular with adults using the sites for updating friends and celebrities using the sites for promotion and for
</b> communicating with fans.



The plural “have” does not agree with the subject “the use.”


Also, since “with” is not repeated before “celebrities,” whether this version is meant to convey that adults update friends and celebrities or that social media sites are popular with adults and with celebrities is not clear.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B)</b> The use of social media sites, many of which were at first used primarily by teenagers and college students, <b>has become popular with adults using the sites for updating friends, and with celebrities, who use the sites for promotion and for
</b> communicating with fans.



In this version, the repetition of the preposition “with” makes clear that the use of social media sites has become popular with two groups, adults and celebrities.


Also, the repetition of the preposition “for” makes clear that celebrities uses social media for two purposes, promotion and communicating.


> <b>(C)</b> The use of social media sites, many of which were at first used primarily by teenagers and college students, <b>became popular with adults, using the sites for updating friends, and with celebrities, using the sites for promoting and
</b> communicating with fans.



Because the two participial phrases “using the sites for updating friends” and “using the sites for promoting …” are separated from what precedes them by commas, they modify the entire preceding clause, which is “The use of social media sites … became popular with adults.” So, the agent of “using” in “using the sites for updating friends” and “using the sites for promoting …” is the subject of the preceding clause, which is “the use of social media sites.” Thus, this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that “the use of social media” has been “using the sites for updating friends” and “using the sites for promoting ….”


Also, because “for promoting and communicating with fans” uses “for” only once, it does not convey a clear meaning, and could be read as conveying that celebrities use social media for promoting fans.


Notice, “promoting” seems more parallel to “communicating” than “promotion” is. So, the structure of this version may seem more parallel than the structure of the correct version. We have to be careful not to be tempted into choosing an incorrect choice simply because the structure of the version created by that choice is slightly more parallel than that of the correct version.


> <b>(D)</b> The use of social media sites, many of which were at first used primarily by teenagers and college students, <b>is becoming popular with adults, from their use of the sites for updating friends, and with celebrities, from their use of the sites for promoting and
</b> communicating with fans.



The use of “from” in this version is not logical. It does not make sense to say that the use of social media sites is popular “from” the use of the sites by people.


Also, because “for promoting and communicating with fans” uses “for” only once, it does not convey a clear meaning, and could be read as conveying that celebrities use social media for promoting fans.


> <b>(E)</b> The use of social media sites, many of which were at first used primarily by teenagers and college students, <b>has become popular with adults using the sites for updating friends and celebrities, using the sites for promoting and for
</b> communicating with fans.



Since “with” is not repeated before “celebrities,” whether this version is meant to convey that adults update friends and celebrities or that social media sites are popular with adults and with celebrities is not clear.


Also, while “promoting” seems more parallel to “communicating” than “promotion” is, “promoting” is not as good a word choice as “promotion” is. While the difference is subtle, “promotion” is a better choice for describing the activity of promotion in a self-contained way. “Promoting” works better if followed by a direct object, as in “promoting themselves.”


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

Let’s now discuss the fact that, sometimes, “that” must be repeated to make a list clear.
