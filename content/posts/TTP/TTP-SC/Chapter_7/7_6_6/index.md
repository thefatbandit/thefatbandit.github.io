## Lesson Content
Similar to what we saw with repeated prepositions, sometimes, in order for a sentence including a list to clearly convey a logical meaning, it’s necessary to repeat a “that” by placing it before every item in the list.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Sometimes, in order for a sentence including a list to clearly convey a logical meaning, it’s necessary to repeat a “that” by placing it before every item in the list.
{{< /alert >}}

To see why it’s sometimes necessary to repeat “that,” consider the following two sentences:

> Breck said <strong>that</strong> John will be the new class president and <strong>that</strong> Peter will be the new secretary.

> Breck said <strong>that</strong> John will be the new class president, and Peter will be the new secretary.

Do the above sentences say the same thing? No – they do not.

The first sentence conveys that Breck said two things.

He said that John will be the new class president.

The second sentence conveys that Breck said one thing, that John will be the new class president, and then the second sentence mentions something else, that Peter will be the new secretary.

Notice also that there is no comma before “and” in the first version of the sentence.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When two noun clauses are connected by “and,” it is not necessary, though it is OK, for there to be a comma before “and.”
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at another example:

> Jim said <strong>that</strong> he is going to arrive soon, and he is going to find a way to resolve the issue.

> Jim said <strong>that</strong> he is going to arrive soon and <strong>that</strong> he is going to find a way to resolve the issue.

Notice that the second sentence makes clear that Jim said both things:

> - he is going to arrive soon.

> - he is going to find a way to resolve the issue.

However, the first sentence, as written, conveys that Jim said only one thing:

> - he is going to arrive soon.

Then, yes, it’s mentioned that he is going to find a way to resolve the issue, but, since there is no “that” in front of that statement, that statement was not said by Jim.

What we are going to see now is that there are instances in which not repeating “that” will result in illogical sentences.

Consider the following example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Caterina claims that workers in other countries handle tasks that were once handled by local workers, and robots do too.

If we look carefully at the sentence, we see that it could be interpreted as saying two things:

> - Caterina claims that workers in other countries handle tasks that were once handled by local workers.

> - Robots also make that claim.

Of course, the second part doesn’t make sense.

> Caterina claims <strong>that</strong> workers in other countries handle tasks that were once handled by local workers and <strong>that</strong> robots do too.

Notice that the addition of the second “that” completely changes the sentence, making it such that it clearly conveys one logical meaning.

> - that workers in other countries handle tasks that were once handled by local workers

> - that robots also handle tasks that were once handled by local workers

Of course, now both statements make sense.

How can we use this knowledge to score points on test day? We can eliminate any sentence with an unclear or illogical meaning resulting from its failure to effectively use the word “that” to make a list clear.

##  Sentence Correction Example 8  

The environmental scientist noted that certain varieties of fungi break down plastics in a matter of <u>weeks, which could help with making</u> a significant reduction in plastic waste a reality.

2. [ ] (B) weeks and they could help to make
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

##### Question Level: medium



> <b>(A)</b> The environmental scientist noted that certain varieties of fungi break down plastics in a matter of <b>weeks, which could help with making
</b> a significant reduction in plastic waste a reality.



 In this version, the modifier “which could help with making …” is misplaced. The only logical target for the modifier is “varieties of fungi,” but the modifier is too far from “varieties of fungi” to modify that phrase. So, it instead seems to modify either “weeks” or the entire clause “certain varieties of fungi break down plastics in a matter of weeks,” neither of which could logically “help with making a significant reduction in plastic waste a reality.”


> <b>(B)</b> The environmental scientist noted that certain varieties of fungi break down plastics in a matter of <b>weeks and they could help to make
</b> a significant reduction in plastic waste a reality.



 The first sign that this choice is incorrect is that there is no comma before “and,” which connects two independent clauses, “the environmental scientist noted …,” and “they could help to make ….” When “and” connects two independent clauses, “and” must be preceded by a comma.


If there were a “that” before “they can help to make …,” then “and” would correctly connect two noun clauses “that certain varieties of fungi break down …” and “that they could help to make …” without a comma. However, since there is no “that” before “they could help to make …,” that rule does not apply in this case.


Also, since there is no “that” before “they could help to make,” this version does not make complete sense. What would make the most sense is that the environmental scientist noted two things, “that certain varieties of fungi break down plastics” and “that they could help to make a significant reduction.” However, since there is no “that” before “they could help to make,” this version does not express that the environmental scientist noted those two things. Rather, it expresses that the environmental scientist noted “that certain varieties of fungi break down plastics” and then states as a separate fact, “they could help to make a significant reduction.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(C)</b> The environmental scientist noted that certain varieties of fungi break down plastics in a matter of <b>weeks and that they could help make
</b> a significant reduction in plastic waste a reality.



By repeating “that” before the second element in the list of two elements, “<strong>that</strong> certain varieties of fungi break down plastics in a matter of weeks and <strong>that</strong> they could help make a significant reduction,” this version effectively expresses that the environmental scientist noted two things, “that certain varieties of fungi break down plastics” and “that they could help make a significant reduction.”


The list is parallel, the sentence is grammatically correct, and the sentence meaning is clear and logical.


> <b>(D)</b> The environmental scientist noted that certain varieties of fungi break down plastics in a matter of <b>weeks, and that it could help and make
</b> a significant reduction in plastic waste a reality.



 The first sign that this version is incorrect is that “it” has no logical referent. “Fungi” is plural. So, “it” can’t logically refer to “fungi.”


Meanwhile, even if we didn’t know that “fungi” is plural, we could eliminate this choice because this version conveys an illogical meaning since “they could help and make” illogically separates the act “help” from the act “make.” The logical point to be made is that the varieties of fungi could “help make a … reduction,” not that they could do two separate things, “help” and “make a … reduction.”


> <b>(E)</b> The environmental scientist noted that certain varieties of fungi break down plastics in a matter of <b>weeks, these fungi could help make
</b> a significant reduction in plastic waste a reality.



This version joins two independent clauses with a comma alone and therefore cannot be correct.


Both “The group of environmental scientists is researching …” and “these fungi could make a significant reduction …” could stand alone as complete sentences. Thus, they must be joined either by a semicolon or by a comma and a coordinating conjunction, such as “and.”


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)
