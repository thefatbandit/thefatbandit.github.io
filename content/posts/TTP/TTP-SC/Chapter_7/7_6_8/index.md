## Lesson Content
We now know how to recognize a nested list, and we understand the importance of keeping the outer list and the nested list straight.

Our next important concept to understand is what carries over in the nested list and in the outer list.

Please leave your name and your number and explain why you called.

We know that the outer list is the following.

> Please:

<strong>leave</strong> (your name and your number)

<strong>explain</strong> why you called

We know that the nested list is:

> leave:

your name

your number

It’s important to see that “leave” carries over to the nested list.

> Please <strong>leave</strong> (your name and your number) and explain why you called.

As we distribute the “leave” through the parentheses, we get:

> Please <strong>leave</strong> your name and<strong> leave </strong>your number and explain why you called.

The reason that “leave” carries over is that “leave” introduces the nested list.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Whatever introduces a nested list is understood to carry over to each of the elements in the nested list without being explicitly written.
{{< /alert >}}

Therefore, each of the elements in the nested list must work logically with what introduces the list.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In a situation involving a nested list, the essential elements that introduce a nested list are understood to carry over to each of the items in the nested list. Therefore, the elements of the nested list must work logically with whatever introduces them.
{{< /alert >}}

In:

> Please leave your name and your number and explain why you called.

Notice that “leave” does not carry over to “explain why you called” because “explain why you called” is not part of the nested list, so we would not expect “leave” to carry over to it.

Let’s look at another previous example:

> The managing partner instructed the traders to sell the financial stocks, purchase precious metal stocks and technology stocks, and determine the yield to maturity of ten-year government bonds.

We already know that the outer list is:

> The managing partner instructed the traders to:

<strong>sell</strong> the financial stocks

<strong>purchase </strong>(precious metal stocks and technology stocks)

<strong>determine</strong> the yield to maturity of ten-year government bonds

We already know that the nested list is:

> purchase:

precious metals

technology stocks

Let’s begin with the nested list.

> <strong>purchase</strong> precious metal stocks and <strong>purchase</strong> technology stocks

Notice that “purchase” carries over to each part of the nested list.

Now, let’s zoom out to the outer list.

> <strong>The managing partner instructed the traders to</strong> sell the financial stocks, <strong>the managing partner instructed the traders to</strong> purchase precious metal stocks and technology stocks, and <strong>the managing partner instructed the traders to</strong> determine the yield to maturity of ten-year government bonds.

We see that “the managing partner instructed the traders to” carries over to each of the three elements of the outer list.

It’s important to reiterate that, when we encounter a nested list, we must be careful to keep straight the carry over in the nested list and the carry over in the outer list.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When we encounter a nested list, we must be careful to keep straight the carry over in the nested list and the carry over in the outer list.
{{< /alert >}}

We must also ensure that the items in the nested list make sense with the structure that introduces them.

Let’s now make the sentence incorrect.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The managing partner instructed the traders to sell the financial stocks, purchase precious metal stocks and technology stocks, and to determine the yield to maturity of ten-year government bonds.

Let’s see:

> <strong>The managing partner instructed the traders to</strong> (sell the financial stocks, purchase (precious metal stocks and technology stocks), and to determine the yield to maturity of ten-year government bonds).

> <strong>The managing partner instructed the traders</strong> <strong>to </strong>sell the financial stocks, <strong>the managing partner instructed the traders to</strong> <strong>purchase</strong> precious metal stocks and <strong>purchase</strong> technology stocks, and <strong>the managing partner instructed the traders</strong> <strong>to</strong> to determine the yield to maturity of ten-year government bonds.

Notice that the third item in the outer list does not make sense:

> the managing partner instructed the traders <strong>to to</strong> determine the yield to maturity of ten-year government bonds

Thus, the sentence is incorrect.

Let’s look at another example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: At the last lesson, our piano teacher recommended that we spend more time practicing classical pieces, improve our understanding of meter, measures, and tempo, and devoting more weekly hours to practice.

Let’s break the sentence down:

Let’s begin with the nested list.

> <strong>improve our understanding of</strong> (meter, measures, and tempo)

> <strong>improve our understanding of </strong>meter, <strong>improve our understanding of </strong>measures, and <strong>improve our understanding of </strong>tempo

We see that the nested list makes sense.

We see that “our piano teacher recommended that” introduces the outer list:

> At the last lesson, <strong>our piano teacher recommended that we</strong> (spend more time practicing classical pieces, improve our understanding of (meter, measures, and tempo), and devoting more weekly hours to practice).

> At the last lesson, <strong>our piano teacher recommended that</strong> <strong>we</strong> spend more time practicing classical pieces,<strong> our piano teacher recommended that we</strong> improve our understanding of (meter, measures, and tempo), and <strong>our piano teacher recommended that we</strong> devoting more weekly hours to practice.

We can see that the last item in the outer list does not make sense.

So, how do we use this knowledge to score points on test day? When we encounter a sentence with a nested list, we eliminate any version in which the items in the nested list don’t make sense with the structure that introduces them or in which the items in the outer list don’t make sense with the structure that introduces them.
