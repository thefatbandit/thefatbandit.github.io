## Lesson Content
As we know, it’s common to encounter SC questions in which the original sentence contains a broken list (a list constructed without proper parallelism).

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

It’s common to encounter SC questions in which the original sentence contains a broken list (a list constructed without proper parallelism). In these situations, it’s common to find that the correct answer fixes the broken list by changing it into a nested list.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Grizzly bears are known to eat seeds, berries, roots, and catch fish.

Notice that the list is not logically created:

> <strong>Grizzly bears are known to eat</strong> (seeds, berries, roots, and catch fish).

> <strong>Grizzly bears are known to eat</strong> seeds, <strong>Grizzly bears are known to eat</strong> berries, <strong>Grizzly bears are known to eat</strong> roots, and <strong>Grizzly bears are known to eat</strong> catch fish.

We see that the fourth element in the list does not make sense with “grizzly bears are known to eat.”

From our study in this chapter, we already know that one way that correct answers will fix sentences like that is by adjusting the last element in the list so that it works logically with what introduces the list.

For example, we might see the following as a correct option that fixes the broken list.

> Grizzly bears are known to eat seeds, berries, roots, and fish.

However, the correct answer may also fix the broken list by changing it into a nested list.

We might encounter something like the following:

> Grizzly bears are known <strong>to eat seeds, berries, and roots</strong>, and catch fish.

Notice what has happened.

> Grizzly bears are known <strong>to eat</strong> (seeds, berries, and roots), and <strong>catch</strong> (fish).

The outer list is:

> Grizzly bears are known to:

<strong>eat </strong>(seeds, berries, and roots)

<strong>catch </strong>fish

The nested list is:

> eat:

seeds

berries

roots

So, by separating the fourth item (the item that was not parallel) from the list and creating a nested list, this sentence has fixed the parallelism issue by eliminating the need for the fourth element to be parallel with the other three elements in the list.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

If an element is no longer in a list, it need not be parallel with the rest of the list.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s break the sentence down:

> <strong>Grizzly bears are known to</strong> (eat seeds, berries, and roots and catch fish).

Notice that the “and” before “catch” connects the two components of the outer list.

Let’s distribute “Grizzly bears are known to” through the parentheses:

> <strong>Grizzly bears are known to</strong> eat seeds, berries, and roots and <strong>grizzly bears are known to</strong> catch fish.

We can see the structure of the sentence is now logical.

Let’s revisit an example that we have seen before:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Please leave a message including your name, your number, and explain why you called.

We know that this list is broken; it’s not parallel.

> Please leave a message including your name, your number, and your reason for calling.

Notice that this fix has retained the original structure of the list.

We now know that there is another way a correct answer might fix the sentence; it might detach the last element from the list.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When the original sentence contains a broken three-part list, a correct answer may fix the list by detaching the last element from the list and creating instead a two-item list, with a nested list in the first item in the list.
{{< /alert >}}

> <u>Three-Item Broken List</u>: Please leave a message including your name, your number, and explain why you called.

> <u>Fixed Two-Item List with the First Item A Nested List</u>: Please leave a message including your name and your number and explain why you called.

> <strong>Please </strong>(leave a message including your name and your number and explain why you called.

> <strong>Please </strong>leave a message including your name and your number and <strong>please </strong>explain why you called.

Can you see what we did? By adding the “and” we’ve taken the third element out of the list, and have created a nested list.

> Please:

leave a message including your name and your number

explain why you called

Notice that, in the first element of the list, we also have a nested list.

> leave a message including:

your name

your number

So, by separating the third item (the item that was not parallel to the others) from the list and creating a nested list, we’ve fixed the parallelism issue by eliminating the need for the third element to be parallel to the other two elements in the list.

After all, if an element is no longer in a list, it need not be parallel to the other items in the list.

By changing the sentence so that “explain why you called” is no longer in the same list with “your name” and “your number,” we have once again created a list in which all the elements, namely “your name” and “your number,” are grammatically similar.

Let’s look at another example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Kelly is my best friend because she is smart, loyal, and keeps my secrets.

We know that this list is broken.

> <strong>Kelly is my best friend because </strong>she is smart, <strong>Kelly is my best friend because she </strong>is loyal, and <strong>Kelly is my best friend because she is </strong>keeps my secrets.

We already know that one way that correct answers will fix sentences like that is by adjusting the last element in the list so that it works logically with what introduces the list.

> Kelly is my best friend because she is smart, loyal, and trustworthy.

We now know that there is another way a correct answer might fix the sentence; it might detach the last element from the list.

> Kelly is my best friend because she is smart and loyal and keeps my secrets.

Notice that we’ve fixed the broken list by removing the third element in the list from the list and creating a nested list.

So, how can we use all this knowledge to score points on test day? When the original sentence contains a broken three-part list, we can eliminate that answer and know that a correct answer may fix the list by detaching the last element from the list and creating instead a two-item list, with a nested list in the first item in the list.

##  Sentence Correction Example 9  

Groundhogs primarily feed on wild grasses, flowering plants, <u>berries, and</u> create burrows that have multiple chambers.

1. [ ] (A) berries, and
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) and on berries, they
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) berries,
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) and berries and
5. [ ] (E) berries, and they
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A)</b> Groundhogs primarily feed on wild grasses, flowering plants, <b>berries, and
</b> create burrows that have multiple chambers.



 The fourth item in the list, “create burrows,” does not work logically with what introduces the list, “feed on”:


<strong>feed on</strong> wild grasses, <strong>feed on</strong> flowering plants, <strong>feed on</strong> berries, and <strong>feed on</strong> create burrows


“Feed on create burrows” does not make sense.


> <b>(B)</b> Groundhogs primarily feed on wild grasses, flowering plants, <b>and on berries, they
</b> create burrows that have multiple chambers.



 This version contains a list of three items, the first and third of which are introduced by the preposition “on.” However, the second item is not introduced by “on.” Thus, the sentence conveys an illogical meaning:


<strong>on</strong> wild grasses, <strong>on</strong> flowering plants, and <strong>on</strong> on berries


Of course, “on on berries” makes no sense. In order to convey a logical meaning, either all three elements or only the first element in the list should be preceded by “on.”


Notice also that this version contains a comma splice; the independent clauses “Groundhogs primarily feed on wild grasses …” and “they create burrows” are joined with a comma alone. Two independent clauses must be joined by either a semicolon or a comma and coordinating conjunction.


> <b>(C)</b> Groundhogs primarily feed on wild grasses, flowering plants, <b>berries,
</b> create burrows that have multiple chambers.



 This version contains a comma splice; the independent clauses “Groundhogs primarily feed on wild grasses, flowering plants, berries,” and “create burrows that have multiple chambers” are joined with a comma alone. Two independent clauses must be joined by either a semicolon or a comma and coordinating conjunction.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D)</b> Groundhogs primarily feed on wild grasses, flowering plants, <b>and berries and
</b> create burrows that have multiple chambers.



 This version contains an outer list of two elements and a nested list of three elements, with each list joined by “and” before the final element.


<strong>Groundhogs primarily</strong> feed on wild grasses, flowering plants, and berries and <strong>groundhogs primarily</strong> create burrows that have multiple chambers.


Groundhogs primarily <strong>feed on</strong> wild grasses, <strong>feed on</strong> flowering plants, and <strong>feed on</strong> berries and create burrows that have multiple chambers.


Each list is parallel, and the sentence meaning is logical.


> <b>(E)</b> Groundhogs primarily feed on wild grasses, flowering plants, <b>berries, and they
</b> create burrows that have multiple chambers.



The fourth item in the list, “they create burrows,” does not work logically with what introduces the list, “feed on”:


<strong>feed on</strong> wild grasses, <strong>feed on</strong> flowering plants, <strong>feed on</strong> berries, and <strong>feed on</strong> they create burrows


“Feed on they create burrows” does not make sense.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Sentence Correction Example 10  

Workers who have control over their schedules report lower levels of <u>stress, of burnout, and</u> greater job satisfaction.

1. [ ] (A) stress, of burnout, and
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) stress, of burnout, or
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) stress or of burnout or of
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) stress or burnout and
5. [ ] (E) stress, of burnout, or of
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Since it would not make sense for workers to have lower levels of greater job satisfaction, logic dictates that what introduces the list at the end of the sentence must be “workers … report.”


Once we have determined that “workers … report” introduces the list, the list that makes sense with what introduces the list is an outer list, “lower levels … and greater job satisfaction,” which contains a nested list, “lower levels of stress or burnout.”


> <b>(A)</b> Workers who have control over their schedules report lower levels of <b>stress, of burnout, and
</b> greater job satisfaction.



In this version, the repetition of “of” at the beginnings of the first two items in the list signals that what introduces the list is “lower levels.” However, the last item in the list does not work logically with what introduces the list, as we can see by combining the two:


lower levels greater job satisfaction


> <b>(B)</b> Workers who have control over their schedules report lower levels of <b>stress, of burnout, or
</b> greater job satisfaction.



In this version, the repetition of “of” at the beginnings of the first two items in the list signals that what introduces the list is “lower levels.” However, the last item in the list does not work logically with what introduces the list, as we can see by combining the two:


lower levels greater job satisfaction


> <b>(C)</b> Workers who have control over their schedules report lower levels of <b>stress or of burnout or of
</b> greater job satisfaction.



Since this version includes “of” before every item in the list, it may seem to fix the list seen in the original version, which included “of” before only the first and second items in the list. However, this version still has the main issue that makes the original version incorrect: while the repetition of “of” signals that what introduces the list is “lower levels,” the last item in the list does not work logically with what introduces the list, as we can see by combining the two:


Workers who have control over their schedules report … lower levels of greater job satisfaction


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D)</b> Workers who have control over their schedules report lower levels of <b>stress or burnout and
</b> greater job satisfaction.



By using an outer list and a nested list, rather than, as the other versions do, using a three-item list, this version solves the problem present in all the other versions, and conveys a logical meaning.


The outer list is:


workers … report:


lower levels of stress or burnout


greater job satisfaction


The nested list is:


lower levels of:


stress


burnout


Notice, via the use of “or” followed by “and,” this correct answer is written to seem to be illogical and incorrect. In answering Sentence Correction questions, we have to be careful not to be fooled by odd sounding correct answers into choosing incorrect choices.


> <b>(E)</b> Workers who have control over their schedules report lower levels of <b>stress, of burnout, or of
</b> greater job satisfaction.



Because this version uses a third “of” but does not repeat “or,” as the (C) version does, this choice is a tempting trap, because this version seems to solve problems in the (A) version and problems in the (C). The truth is, however, that this version has the same main issue that the other incorrect versions have: while the repetition of “of” signals that what introduces the list is “lower levels,” the last item in the list does not work logically with what introduces the list, as we can see by combining the two:


Workers who have control over their schedules report … lower levels of greater job satisfaction


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Sentence Correction Example 11  

While industry insiders insist that fish farming reduces pressure on wild fish stocks, outside experts have suggested that actually the farms <u>do more harm than help, by catalyzing the spread of diseases, of parasites such as sea lice, and causing astronomical increases in</u> the levels of pollution in wild ecosystems.

1. [ ] (A) do more harm than help, by catalyzing the spread of diseases, of parasites such as sea lice, and causing astronomical increases in
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) do more harm than they help, by catalyzing the spread of diseases, the spread of parasites such as sea lice, and causing astronomical increases in
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) harm more than they help, by catalyzing the spread of diseases and that of parasites such as sea lice and causing astronomical increases in
4. [ ] (D) are more harmful than helpful, catalyze both the spread of diseases and parasites such as sea lice, and causing astronomical increases in
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) are harming more than helping, catalyze both the spread of diseases, parasites such as sea lice, and causing to astronomically increase
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



There are in the sentence two areas in which issues appear.


First, the comparison between the fish farms’ harming and the fish farms’ helping must both be logical and work with the rest of the sentence.


Then, the list of ways in which the farms are harmful must be parallel and make sense.


> <b>(A)</b> While industry insiders insist that fish farming reduces pressure on wild fish stocks, outside experts have suggested that actually the farms <b>do more harm than help, by catalyzing the spread of diseases, of parasites such as sea lice, and causing astronomical increases in
</b> the levels of pollution in wild ecosystems.



“Do more harm than help” does not work, since while “farms do harm” makes sense, “farms do … help” does not make sense in this context because “help” is not something that a farm would “do.”


Also, the list of ways in which the farms are harmful is not logical. The repetition of “of” at the beginnings of the first two items in the list signals that what introduces the list is “by catalyzing the spread,” but the third item in the list does not work logically with “by catalyzing the spread” as we can see by considering the following:


<strong>by catalyzing the spread</strong> of diseases – makes sense


<strong>by catalyzing the spread </strong>of parasites such as sea lice – makes sense


<strong>by catalyzing the spread</strong> causing astronomical increases in the levels of pollution in wild ecosystems – does not make sense


> <b>(B)</b> While industry insiders insist that fish farming reduces pressure on wild fish stocks, outside experts have suggested that actually the farms <b>do more harm than they help, by catalyzing the spread of diseases, the spread of parasites such as sea lice, and causing astronomical increases in
</b> the levels of pollution in wild ecosystems.



In this version “do more harm than they help” nonsensically conveys that the farms do more harm than the harm they help.


Also, the list of ways in which the farms are harmful is not logical. What seems to introduce the list is “by catalyzing,” but the third item in the list does not work logically with “by catalyzing,” as we can see by combining the two:


<strong>by catalyzing </strong>causing astronomical increases


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(C)</b> While industry insiders insist that fish farming reduces pressure on wild fish stocks, outside experts have suggested that actually the farms <b>harm more than they help, by catalyzing the spread of diseases and that of parasites such as sea lice and causing astronomical increases in
</b> the levels of pollution in wild ecosystems.



 “Harm more than they help” effectively expresses the comparison between the fish farms’ harming and the fish farms’ helping.


Also, this version logically expresses the ways in which the fish farms are harmful by using an outer list and a nested list.


The outer list is:


by:


catalyzing …


causing …


The nested list is:


catalyzing:


the spread of diseases


that of parasites such as sea lice


Notice, the use of “that,” which refers to “the spread,” in the second item in this nested list may make this choice seem to be incorrect. To correctly answer sentence correction questions, we have to carefully evaluate logic and avoid being thrown off by structures that “sound wrong” at first.


> <b>(D)</b> While industry insiders insist that fish farming reduces pressure on wild fish stocks, outside experts have suggested that actually the farms <b>are more harmful than helpful, catalyze both the spread of diseases and parasites such as sea lice, and causing astronomical increases in
</b> the levels of pollution in wild ecosystems.



In this version, the comparison between the fish farms’ harming and the fish farms’ helping, “more harmful than helpful,” is effective.


However, the list that appears in this version does not work logically with what introduces the list, “the farms,” as we can see by considering the following:


<strong>the farms</strong> (are more harmful than helpful, catalyze both the spread of diseases and parasites such as sea lice, and causing astronomical increases in)


<strong>the farms</strong> are more harmful than helpful, <strong>the farms</strong> catalyze both the spread of diseases and parasites such as sea lice, and <strong>the farms</strong> causing astronomical increases in


We can see that “the farms causing astronomical increases” does not make sense in this context.


Also, the nested list “catalyze both the spread of diseases and parasites such as sea lice” does not make sense, since “catalyze … parasites” does not make sense.


> <b>(E)</b> While industry insiders insist that fish farming reduces pressure on wild fish stocks, outside experts have suggested that actually the farms <b>are harming more than helping, catalyze both the spread of diseases, parasites such as sea lice, and causing to astronomically increase
</b> the levels of pollution in wild ecosystems.



The list that makes up the choice does not work logically with what introduces the list, “the farms,” as we can see by considering the following:


<strong>the farms</strong> (are harming more than helping, catalyze both the spread of diseases, parasites such as sea lice, and causing to astronomically increase)


<strong>the farms</strong> are harming more than helping, <strong>the farms</strong> catalyze both the spread of diseases, parasites such as sea lice, and <strong>the farms</strong> causing to astronomically increase


We can see that “the farms causing to astronomically increase” does not make sense in this context.


Also, “both” is meant to introduce a two-item list, but, in this case, there is no “and” between the two items that “both” seems to introduce, “the spread of diseases” and “parasites such as sea lice.” So, the list introduced by “both” is not logically constructed, and what items are meant to be in the list is not entirely clear.


Also, the items in the list introduced by “both” do not work logically with what introduces the list, “catalyze,” as we can see by considering the following:


catalyze … the spread of diseases – makes sense


catalyze … parasites such as sea lice – does not make sense


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

Let’s now discuss correlative conjunctions and parallelism.
