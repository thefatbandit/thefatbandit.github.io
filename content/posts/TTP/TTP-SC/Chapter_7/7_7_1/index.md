## Lesson Content
One of the most commonly used correlative conjunctions is “not only … but also.” Likely, you’re used to seeing this conjunction in sentences.

For example:

> Marty eats <strong>not only</strong> avocados <strong>but also</strong> bananas.

> Jeff <strong>not only</strong> visited Patagonia <strong>but also</strong> spent time on Easter Island.

> She wrote him <strong>not only</strong> a love letter <strong>but also</strong> a poem.

> <strong>Not only</strong> does Inder love studying for the GMAT, <strong>but also</strong> he loves writing his essays.

> Los Angeles is <strong>not only</strong> a city with beautiful weather <strong>but also</strong> a city with great food.

Let’s now move on to a more general discussion of how to use “not only … but also” and other correlative conjunctions by considering the elements that precede “not only … but also” structures.
