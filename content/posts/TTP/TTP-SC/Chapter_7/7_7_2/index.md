## Lesson Content
Whatever precedes the first part of a “not only … but also” structure is understood to carry over to the second part of the structure, and thus does not have to be repeated in order for a complete, logical thought to be expressed.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Whatever precedes the first part of a “not only … but also” correlative structure is understood to carry over to, without being written in, the second part of the correlative structure.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following sentence:

> Jim is <strong>not only</strong> crazy <strong>but also</strong> fun.

We see that “Jim is” introduces the “not only … but also” structure.

> Not only <strong>Jim is</strong> crazy but also<strong> Jim is</strong> fun.

One helpful way to visualize the “not only … but also” structure is to put parentheses before the “not only” and after the end of anything related to the “but also,” creating what we refer to as a “contained unit.”

> <strong>Jim is</strong> (not only crazy but also fun).

“Jim is” precedes “not only.” Thus, “Jim is” also carries over to the second part of the structure.

> To see this concept very clearly, we can distribute “Jim is” through the parentheses<strong>. </strong>

Thus, the sentence conveys the meaning:

> <u>Implied Unwritten Meaning</u>: Not only <strong>Jim is</strong> crazy but also<strong> Jim is</strong> fun.

Notice that, without the sentence’s explicitly repeating “Jim is,” since “Jim is” precedes “not only,” “Jim is” is understood to carry over to what appears after “but also.”

Now, what about the following sentence?

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Jim is not only crazy but also Jim is fun.

The above sentence may seem fine until we examine it closely.

> <strong>Jim is</strong> (not only crazy but also Jim is fun).

Now, distribute the “Jim is” through the parentheses:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Not only <strong>Jim is </strong>crazy but also<strong> Jim is </strong>Jim is fun.

Of course, such a sentence is madness, right?

Notice that it’s incorrect to rewrite “Jim is” after the “but also.” After all, as we’ve said, the “Jim is” is already understood to carry over to what appears after “but also,” so rewriting the “Jim is” after “but also” is redundant, and results in an illogical sentence.

In general, an illogical, redundant sentence will result from repeating the element preceding “not only” after “but also.”

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

An illogical, redundant sentence will result from repeating the element preceding “not only” after “but also.”
{{< /alert >}}

How can we use this knowledge to gain points on test day? Whenever we encounter “not only … but also” in sentence versions, we eliminate any of those versions that repeat the element preceding “not only” after “but also,” creating illogical, redundant sentences.

Before reading the explanation, try to see why the following sentence is incorrect.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Jim is not only crazy but also is fun.

Notice that this version incorrectly repeats the element preceding “not only” (the<br/>
is”) after “but also,” creating an illogical, redundant sentence. So, we would eliminate it immediately. If we have a hard time seeing this issue, we can use parentheses and determine what the above sentence actually says:

> <strong>Jim is</strong> (not only crazy but also is fun).

> Not only <strong>Jim is</strong> crazy but also <strong>Jim is </strong>is fun.

After carrying over “Jim is,” we see that the sentence does not make sense.

Remember, whatever precedes the “not only” is understood to carry over to the second part of the structure <u>without being rewritten</u>.

Let’s look at another example sentence.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Jim ran not only the Boston Marathon but also Jim ran the Ironman at Kona.

Again, this sentence may seem ok, until we examine it more closely:

> <strong>Jim ran</strong> (not only the Boston Marathon but also Jim ran the Ironman at Kona).

Notice that creating a contained unit with parentheses makes it easy for us to see that “Jim ran” is understood to carry over to both parts of the “not only … but also” structure.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Not only <strong>Jim ran</strong> the Boston Marathon but also <strong>Jim ran</strong> Jim ran the Ironman at Kona.

Remember, the elements preceding our contained unit don’t need to be repeated because they are understood to be repeated.

A corrected version of the sentence is:

> Jim ran <strong>not only</strong> the Boston Marathon <strong>but also</strong> the Ironman at Kona.

If we look at the correlative structure as a contained unit, we can easily see which elements apply to the entire unit:

> <strong>Jim ran</strong> (not only the Boston Marathon but also the Ironman at Kona).

If we distribute “Jim ran” through the structure, the sentence makes perfect sense:

> Not only<strong> Jim ran </strong>the Boston Marathon but also <strong>Jim ran </strong>the Ironman at Kona.”

##  Sentence Correction Example 12  

The treatment not only was more efficacious than researchers had expected it to <u>be, but also the results of it were</u> minimal side effects among the trial participants.

1. [ ] (A) be, but also the results of it were
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) be, but also it resulted in
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) be but also resulted in
4. [ ] (D) be but also its results were
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) be, but the treatment also resulted in
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A)</b> The treatment not only was more efficacious than researchers had expected it to <b>be, but also the results of it were
</b> minimal side effects among the trial participants.



Remember, what precedes a “not only … but also” construction carries over to the second part of the construction. In this case, “the treatment” carries over, conveying the following illogical meaning:


not only <strong>the treatment</strong> was more efficacious than researchers had expected it to be, but also <strong>the treatment</strong> the results of it were minimal side effects ...


“The treatment the results of it were” is a nonsensical statement. Therefore, the “not only … but also” structure in this version is not parallel.


> <b>(B)</b> The treatment not only was more efficacious than researchers had expected it to <b>be, but also it resulted in
</b> minimal side effects among the trial participants.



Remember, what precedes a “not only … but also” construction carries over to the second part of the construction. In this case, “the treatment” carries over, conveying the following illogical meaning:


not only <strong>the treatment</strong> was more efficacious than researchers had expected it to be, but also <strong>the treatment</strong> it resulted in minimal side effects ...


The pronoun “it” is redundant in “the treatment <em>it</em> resulted in minimal side effects,” and thus this version is incorrect.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(C)</b> The treatment not only was more efficacious than researchers had expected it to <b>be but also resulted in
</b> minimal side effects among the trial participants.



This version avoids the broken parallelism issues found in some of the other choices andconveys a logical meaningby using a parallel construction of verbs in the two halves of the “not only … but also” idiom (not only <em>was</em> … but also <em>resulted</em>). We can use carryover to confirm that the meaning is logical:


not only <strong>the treatment</strong> was more efficacious than researchers had expected it to be, but also <strong>the treatment</strong> resulted in minimal side effects ...


The sentence contains no grammatical errors and conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(D)</b> The treatment not only was more efficacious than researchers had expected it to <b>be but also its results were
</b> minimal side effects among the trial participants.



Remember, what precedes a “not only … but also” construction carries over to the second part of the construction. In this case, “the treatment” carries over, conveying the following illogical meaning:


not only <strong>the treatment</strong> was more efficacious than researchers had expected it to be, but also <strong>the treatment</strong> its results were ...


We can see that “the treatment its results were” does not make sense.


> <b>(E)</b> The treatment not only was more efficacious than researchers had expected it to <b>be, but the treatment also resulted in
</b> minimal side effects among the trial participants.



Since “the treatment” precedes the “not only … but also” structure, “the treatment” is understood to carry over to both the first and the second parts of the structure. So, it does not make sense to repeat “the treatment” between “but” and “also,” as we can see by carrying over “the treatment.”


not only <strong>the treatment</strong> was more efficacious than researchers had expected it to be, but the treatment also <strong>the treatment</strong> its results were ...


We can see that “but the treatment also the treatment” does not make sense.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

##  Sentence Correction Example 13  

Across the United States, major hospitals are not only serving a greater number of plant-based meal options at their cafeterias <u>but also are educating a vegan diet’s ability to reverse disease to patients</u>.<u> </u>

1. [ ] (A) but also are educating a vegan diet’s ability to reverse disease to patients
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) but also educate patients on the ability of a vegan diet to reverse disease
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) but also educating patients on the ability of a vegan diet that reverses disease
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) and also educate patients on a vegan diet’s ability to reverse disease
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) but also educating patients on the disease-reversing ability of a vegan diet

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Since “major hospitals are” precedes the “not only … but also” correlative structure, “major hospitals are” is understood to carry over to what follows “not only” and what follows “but also,” and those elements must work logically with “major hospitals are.”


> <b>(A)</b> Across the United States, major hospitals are not only serving a greater number of plant-based meal options at their cafeterias <b>but also are educating a vegan diet’s ability to reverse disease to patients
</b>


In this version, what follows “but also” does not work logically with what precedes the correlative structure, “major hospitals are,” as we can see by combining the two:


major hospitals are … are educating patients


Also, the wording “educating a vegan diet’s ability to reverse disease to patients” does not make clear that hospitals are educating patients and seems to convey the nonsensical meaning that hospitals are educating a vegan diet’s ability. If a word different from “educating,” such as “demonstrating,” were used instead of “educating,” the wording would make sense, but the way in which “educating” is used in this version is not logical. One educates someone. One does not educate something to someone.


> <b>(B)</b> Across the United States, major hospitals are not only serving a greater number of plant-based meal options at their cafeterias <b>but also educate patients on the ability of a vegan diet to reverse disease
</b>


In this version, what follows “but also” does not work logically with what precedes the correlative structure, “major hospitals are,” as we can see by combining the two:


major hospitals are … educate patients


> <b>(C)</b> Across the United States, major hospitals are not only serving a greater number of plant-based meal options at their cafeterias <b>but also educating patients on the ability of a vegan diet that reverses disease
</b>


In this version, like in the choice (E) version, what follows “not only” and what follows “but also” work logically with what precedes the correlative structure, “major hospitals are.”


However, this version has a meaning issue. The wording “on the ability of a vegan diet that reverses disease” does not connect “the ability” to “reverses disease,” with the result that what ability “the ability” refers to is not made clear.


> <b>(D)</b> Across the United States, major hospitals are not only serving a greater number of plant-based meal options at their cafeterias <b>and also educate patients on a vegan diet’s ability to reverse disease
</b>


This version illogically pairs “not only” with “and also.”


Also, what follows “and also” does not work logically with what precedes the correlative structure, “major hospitals are,” as we can see by combining the two:


major hospitals are … educate patients


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E)</b> Across the United States, major hospitals are not only serving a greater number of plant-based meal options at their cafeterias <b>but also educating patients on the disease-reversing ability of a vegan diet
</b>


In this version, what follows “not only” and what follows “but also” work logically with what precedes the correlative structure, “major hospitals are,” as we can see by considering the following:


<strong>major hospitals are </strong>(not only serving a greater number of plant-based meal options at their cafeterias but also educating patients)


not only <strong>major hospitals are </strong>serving a greater number of plant-based meal options at their cafeterias but also <strong>major hospitals are </strong>educating patients


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
