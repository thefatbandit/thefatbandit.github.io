## Lesson Content
In the last section, we saw that an essential element that precedes “not only” is understood to carry over, without being written, to what comes after “but also.”

In contrast, an element that comes directly after “not only” does not carry over to after “but also.” So, if any such element is necessary after “but also,” it must be repeated.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In a “not only … but also” structure, elements that follow “not only” are not understood to carry over to after “but also.” So, if any of those elements are necessary after “but also,” they must be repeated.
{{< /alert >}}

Take the following sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Jim <strong>not only</strong> is crazy <strong>but also</strong> fun.

If we don’t look carefully, the sentence may seem to make sense.

We can use parentheses to help us see this issue more clearly.

> <strong>Jim</strong> (not only is crazy but also fun).

What happens when we distribute “Jim” through the parentheses?:

> Not only <strong>Jim </strong>is crazy but also <strong>Jim</strong> fun.

Of course, this sentence does not make sense.

> Jim <strong>not only</strong> is crazy <strong>but also</strong> is fun.

Notice that, now, the sentence is logical.

> <strong>Jim</strong> (not only is crazy but also is fun).

> Not only <strong>Jim</strong> is crazy but also <strong>Jim</strong> is fun.

Let’s look at another example sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Kate <strong>not only </strong>loves to read books <strong>but also</strong> to ski.

If we don’t look closely, the above sentence might seem to make sense.

We can use parentheses to help us see this fact:

> <strong>Kate</strong> (not only loves to read books but also to ski).

After distributing “Kate,” we see that the sentence communicates:

> Not only <strong>Kate</strong> loves to read books but also <strong>Kate</strong> to ski.

The sentence clearly does not make sense.

> Kate <strong>not only </strong>loves to read books <strong>but also </strong>loves to ski.

So how can we use this knowledge to score points on test day? We can eliminate any sentences that are illogical because of a faulty use of the “not only … but also” construction.

Let’s look at another example sentence.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The senator drafted the proposal <strong>not only</strong> for the good of the people <strong>but also</strong> the wealth of the nation.

Since the preposition “for” appears after, rather than before, “not only,” “for” does not carry over to after “but also.”

Let’s use some parentheses and distribute to see what this sentence communicates:

> <strong>The senator drafted the proposal</strong> (not only for the good of the people but also the wealth of the nation).

> Not only <strong>the senator drafted the proposal </strong>for the good of the people but also <strong>the senator drafted the proposal</strong> the wealth of the nation.

> We see that the sentence is not logical.

A correct version is:

> The senator drafted the proposal <strong>not only </strong>for the good of the people <strong>but also </strong>for the wealth of the nation.

Let’s use some parentheses and distribute to double check:

> <strong>The senator drafted the proposal</strong> (not only for the good of the people but also for the wealth of the nation).

> Not only <strong>the senator drafted the proposal </strong>for the good of the people but also<strong> the senator drafted the proposal </strong>for the wealth of the nation

Of course, the following sentence is also correct:

> The senator drafted the proposal for <strong>not only</strong> the good of the people <strong>but also</strong> the wealth of the nation.

Now the preposition “for” appears before “not only” and thus applies to the entire correlative construction.

Let’s look at one more example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Theo went to the café not only to purchase green juice but also see his friend Jana.

Let’s use some parentheses and distribute to see what this sentence communicates:

> <u>Incorrect</u>:<strong> Theo went to the café</strong> (not only to purchase green juice but also see his friend Jana).

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Not only <strong>Theo went to the café </strong>to purchase green juice but also <strong>Theo went to the café </strong>see his friend Jana.

Of course, this sentence is not logical.

> Theo went to the café <strong>not only</strong> to purchase green juice <strong>but also</strong> to see his friend Jana.

Notice the two parallel infinitives “to purchase” and “to see.” We can distribute “Theo went to the café” just to be sure that the sentence is clear and logical:

> Not only <strong>Theo went to the café</strong> to purchase green juice but also <strong>Theo went to the café</strong> to see his friend Jana.

The sentence now makes sense.

The Piccirilli Brothers collaborated with Daniel Chester French on numerous <u>occasions; not only they carved</u> his sculpture of Abraham Lincoln, which sits in the Lincoln Memorial, but also nearly all of the marble statues he designed.

4. [ ] (D) occasions; not only did they carve
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A)</b> The Piccirilli Brothers collaborated with Daniel Chester French on numerous <b>occasions; not only they carved
</b> his sculpture of Abraham Lincoln, which sits in the Lincoln Memorial, but also nearly all of the marble statues he designed.



Whatever appears after the first part of a “not only … but also” construction does not carry over (without being explicitly written) to after the second part. In this case, “they carved,” which appears after “not only,” does not carry over to after “but also,” so we do not have a parallel construction.


Thus, the meaning conveyed is unclear. The implied meaning seems to be that “not only <em>they</em> carved his sculpture,” but also somebody else did. Of course, “nearly all of the marble statues,” which appears after “but also,” cannot possibly carve a sculpture, so that meaning is nonsensical.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B)</b> The Piccirilli Brothers collaborated with Daniel Chester French on numerous <b>occasions, carving not only
</b> his sculpture of Abraham Lincoln, which sits in the Lincoln Memorial, but also nearly all of the marble statues he designed.



 This version features parallel noun phrases in a “not only … but also” construction, conveying the clear and logical meaning that the Piccirilli Brothers were responsible for carving both “his sculpture of Abraham Lincoln” and “nearly all of the marble statues he designed.”


Notice that “carving” (which takes as its agent the subject of the preceding clause, “the Piccirilli Brothers”) appears directly <em>before</em> the start of the “not only … but also” construction and so <em>does</em> carry over, without being explicitly written, to the second part, as in the following:


<strong>carving</strong> (not only his sculpture of Abraham Lincoln … but also nearly all of the marble statues he designed)


not only <strong>carving</strong> his sculpture of Abraham Lincoln … but also <strong>carving</strong> nearly all of the marble statues he designed


> <b>(C)</b> The Piccirilli Brothers collaborated with Daniel Chester French on numerous <b>occasions, and not only carved
</b> his sculpture of Abraham Lincoln, which sits in the Lincoln Memorial, but also nearly all of the marble statues he designed.



Whatever appears after the first part of a “not only … but also” construction does not carry over (without being explicitly written) to after the second part. In this case, “carved,” which appears after “not only,” does not carry over to after “but also,” so we do not have a parallel construction.


Thus, the thought conveyed is incomplete. “Not only carved” seems to indicate that the Piccirilli Brothers performed some other action in addition to “carved,” but no additional action is mentioned after “but also.”


> <b>(D)</b> The Piccirilli Brothers collaborated with Daniel Chester French on numerous <b>occasions; not only did they carve
</b> his sculpture of Abraham Lincoln, which sits in the Lincoln Memorial, but also nearly all of the marble statues he designed.



Whatever appears after the first part of a “not only … but also” construction does not carry over (without being explicitly written) to after the second part. In this case, “did they carve,” which appears after “not only,” does not carry over to after “but also,” so we do not have a parallel construction.


Thus, the thought conveyed is incomplete. “Not only did they carve” seems to indicate that the Piccirilli Brothers performed some other action in addition to “carve,” but no additional action is mentioned after “but also.” 


> <b>(E)</b> The Piccirilli Brothers collaborated with Daniel Chester French on numerous <b>occasions, not only carving
</b> his sculpture of Abraham Lincoln, which sits in the Lincoln Memorial, but also nearly all of the marble statues he designed.



 Whatever appears after the first part of a “not only … but also” construction does not carry over (without being explicitly written) to after the second part. ##  Sentence Correction Example 15  

Muhammad Ali, who was born Cassius Clay, is considered <u>one of the greatest boxers and most well-known athletes in sports history, though his lasting fame can be attributed to not only his audacious style in the ring but also to his political activism, particularly by being</u> a conscientious objector to the Vietnam War.

2. [ ] (B) as one of the greatest boxers and most well-known athletes in sports history, although his lasting fame can be attributed not only to his audacious style in the ring but also his political activism, particularly as

##### Question Level: medium



> <b>(A)</b> Muhammad Ali, who was born Cassius Clay, is considered <b>one of the greatest boxers and most well-known athletes in sports history, though his lasting fame can be attributed to not only his audacious style in the ring but also to his political activism, particularly by being
</b> a conscientious objector to the Vietnam War.



Since “to” appears before “not only,” “to” is understood to be carried over to what follows “but also.” So, repeating “to” after “but also” does not make sense.


Also, “particularly by being” has no logical modification target and seems to modify the preceding clause, with “being” having as its agent “his lasting fame.” As a result, the sentence seems to convey the nonsensical meaning that Muhammad Ali’s fame was being a conscientious objector.


> <b>(B)</b> Muhammad Ali, who was born Cassius Clay, is considered <b>as one of the greatest boxers and most well-known athletes in sports history, although his lasting fame can be attributed not only to his audacious style in the ring but also his political activism, particularly as
</b> a conscientious objector to the Vietnam War.



The correlative structure in this version is flawed, because “to,” which appears after “not only” is not repeated after “but also.” As a result of that omission, what appears after “but also” does not work logically with what precedes the correlative structure, as we can see by combining the two:


… <strong>his lasting fame can be attributed</strong> his political activism …


Also, “considered as one of the greatest boxers” is unidiomatic and not quite logical. The GMAT would prefer “considered one of the greatest boxers.”


> <b>(C)</b> Muhammad Ali, who was born Cassius Clay, is considered <b>to be one of the greatest boxers and most well-known athletes in sports history, though his lasting fame can be attributed to not only his audacious style in the ring but his political activism as well, particularly by being
</b> a conscientious objector to the Vietnam War.



Since “not only … but … as well” is an acceptable structure, and since both what follows “not only” and what is between “but” and “as well” work logically with what precedes the correlative structure, the correlative structure in this version works.


However, “considered to be one of the greatest boxers” is unidiomatic and not quite logical. The GMAT would prefer “considered one of the greatest boxers.”


Also, “particularly by being” has no logical modification target and seems to modify the preceding clause, with “being” having as its agent “his lasting fame.” As a result, the sentence seems to convey the nonsensical meaning that Muhammad Ali’s fame was being a conscientious objector.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D)</b> Muhammad Ali, who was born Cassius Clay, is considered <b>one of the greatest boxers and most well-known athletes in sports history, though his lasting fame can be attributed to not only his audacious style in the ring but also his political activism, particularly as
</b> a conscientious objector to the Vietnam War.



Both what follows “not only” and what follows “but also” work logically with what precedes the correlative structure, “his lasting fame can be attributed to.”


Also, this version effectively conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(E)</b> Muhammad Ali, who was born Cassius Clay, is considered <b>to be one of the greatest boxers and most well-known athletes in sports history, although his lasting fame can be attributed not only to his audacious style in the ring but also to political activism, particularly
</b> a conscientious objector to the Vietnam War.



Since both what follows “not only” and what follows “but also” work logically with what precedes the correlative structure, the correlative structure in this version works.


However, “considered to be one of the greatest boxers” is unidiomatic and not quite logical. The GMAT would prefer “considered one of the greatest boxers.”


Also, since “political activism” is not preceded by “his,” the sentence does not make clear that the political activism mentioned is that of Muhammad Ali.


Also, “particularly a conscientious objector” has no logical target, and seems to modify “political activism,” with the result that the sentence seems to convey the nonsensical meaning that political activism is somehow a conscientious objector.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
