## Lesson Content
As you saw with the correlative conjunction “not only … but also,” whatever essential element precedes “not only” is understood to carry over to the “but also” part of the construction.

> not only … but … as well

> not … but

> both … and

> either … or

> neither … nor

For example, consider the following sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Each day, Sam is charged with <strong>either</strong> mopping the floors <strong>or</strong> he must take out the trash.

Just prior to “either,” we see the essential element “Sam is charged with.” Let’s use some parentheses and distribute this essential element.

> Each day, <strong>Sam is charged with</strong> (either mopping the floors or he must take out the trash).

The essential element is understood to carry over to both what follows “either” and what follows “or.”

So, the sentence communicates:

> Each day either <strong>Sam is charged with</strong> mopping the floors or <strong>Sam is charged with</strong> he must take out the trash.

Of course, this sentence does not make sense.

> Each day, Sam is charged with mopping the floors.

> Each day, Sam is charged with he must take out the trash.

The sentence could be correctly written as follows:

> Each day, Sam is charged with <strong>either </strong>mopping the floors <strong>or </strong>taking out the trash.

Now, the correlative expression has two matching elements, expressed by two gerunds, both of which work logically with “Sam is charged with.”

Let’s look at another example of a sentence containing a correlative expression:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The embassy in Los Angeles is neither efficient with its visa processing system nor is it organized with its customer communications.

Remember that the essential elements prior to the “neither” carry over to after the “nor.” Thus, in this sentence, “the embassy in Los Angeles is” is understood to carry over to directly after the “nor.”

So, what does this sentence say?

> <strong>The embassy in Los Angeles is</strong> (neither efficient with its visa processing system nor is it organized with its customer communications).

> Neither<strong> the embassy in Los Angeles is</strong> efficient with its visa processing system nor <strong>the embassy in Los Angeles is</strong> is it organized with its customer communications.

We can see that the part of the sentence that follows “nor” does not work logically with “the embassy in Los Angeles is.”

A correct version of the sentence is:

> The Embassy in Los Angeles is <strong>neither</strong> efficient with its visa processing system <strong>nor</strong> organized with its customer communications.

Notice that now, when we distribute “the embassy in Los Angeles is,” each part is logical:

> Neither<strong> the embassy in Los Angeles is</strong> efficient with its visa processing system nor <strong>the embassy in Los Angeles is</strong> organized with its customer communications.

Let’s look at another example sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The new park will be a place where residents can either walk their dogs or where they can play soccer.

Let’s break the sentence down, keeping in mind that whatever essential elements precede “either” are understood to come after “or.” In this case, the preceding elements are “the new park will be a place where residents can.”

> <strong>The new park will be a place where residents can </strong>(either walk their dogs or where they can play soccer).

> Either<strong> the new park will be a place where residents can </strong>walk their dogs or <strong>the new park will be a place where residents can </strong>where they can play soccer.

Of course, this sentence does not make sense.

> The new park will be a place where residents can (either) walk their dogs.

> The new park will be a place where residents can (or) where they can play soccer.

Notice that the second part of the sentence does not work logically with what precedes the correlative construction.

> The new park will be a place where residents can <strong>either</strong> walk their dogs <strong>or</strong> play soccer.

Now the sentence is clear and logical.

> The new park will be a place where residents can (either) walk their dogs.

> The new park will be a place where residents can (or) play soccer.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

What precedes the first part of a correlative conjunction carries over to the second part of the correlative construction.
{{< /alert >}}

Now, here’s a version of the sentence that we have been discussing that’s illogical, not because of what does carry over, but because of what does not carry over:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The new park will be a place where either residents can walk their dogs or play soccer.

The key to seeing that this sentence is incorrect is to notice what carries over and what does not carry over.

> <strong>The new park will be a place where</strong> (either residents can walk their dogs or play soccer).

> Either<strong> the new park will be a place where </strong>residents can walk their dogs or <strong>the new park will be a place where</strong> play soccer.

> The new park will be a place where (either) residents can walk their dogs.

> The new park will be a place where (or) play soccer.

The sentence does not work because, since “residents can” appears after, rather than before, “either,” “residents can” does not carry over to what appears after “or.”

We could correct the sentence by repeating “residents can” after “or,” as in:

> The new park will be a place where either <strong>residents can walk their dogs</strong> or <strong>residents can play soccer</strong>.

Remember that elements that follow the first part of a correlative conjunction do not carry over to what follows the second part of the correlative conjunction.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Elements that follow the first part of a correlative conjunction do not carry over to what follows the second part of the correlative conjunction.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at another example:

> It was not his money that impressed her but his generosity with everyone he encountered that did.

We see the correlative conjunction “not … but.” Just prior to the “not” we see “it was.” Thus, this essential element will carry over to the second part of the correlative construction.

> <strong>It was</strong> (not his money that impressed her but his generosity with everyone he encountered that did).

> (Not) <strong>it was </strong>his money that impressed her (but) <strong>it was</strong> his generosity with everyone he encountered that did.

Notice that, while “not” and but” don’t really work as placed in this breakdown version, both parts of the sentence make sense with the “it was” that precedes the correlative conjunction.

What about the following?

> It was not his money but that he was generous with everyone he encountered that impressed her.

We see the correlative conjunction “not ...

> <strong>It was</strong> (not his money but that he was generous with everyone he encountered) that impressed her.

> (Not) <strong>it was </strong>his money (but) <strong>it was</strong> that he was generous with everyone he encountered that impressed her.

Notice that, while “not” and but” don’t really work as placed in this breakdown version, both parts of the sentence work with the essential elements that precede the correlative conjunction.

Let’s look at another example:

> Peter cleaned his apartment both for his girlfriend’s comfort and for his own sanity.

We see that “Peter cleaned his apartment” precedes “both.” Therefore, “Peter cleaned his apartment” must carry over to the second part of the correlative construction.

> <strong>Peter cleaned his apartment</strong> (both for his girlfriend’s comfort and for his own sanity).

> Both <strong>Peter cleaned his apartment</strong> for his girlfriend’s comfort, and <strong>Peter cleaned his apartment </strong>for his own sanity.

If necessary, we can break the sentence into two sections to confirm that it makes sense.

> Peter cleaned his apartment (both) for his girlfriend’s comfort.

> Peter cleaned his apartment (and) for his own sanity.

Let’s consider another example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The old steel town both was eerily quiet and economically depressed.

We see that “The old steel town” precedes the correlative conjunction “both … and.” Thus, “The old steel town” is understood to carry over to both parts of the correlative construction.

> <strong>The old steel town </strong>(both was eerily quiet and economically depressed.)

The sentence communicates:

> Both <strong>the old steel town </strong>was eerily quiet, and <strong>the old steel town</strong> economically depressed.

We see that this sentence does not make sense.

> The old steel town was eerily quiet.

> The old steel town economically depressed.

We could fix the sentence by moving was to before, rather than after, “both,” as in:

> The old steel town was both eerily quiet and economically depressed.

Now “the old steel town was” precedes the correlative construction, and both “eerily quiet” and “economically depressed” work logically with “the old steel town was.”

Here’s an example that uses another correlative conjunction, “rather … than”:

> Marshall joked that he would rather get a root canal than eat his wife’s cooking, but his wife didn’t find the joke the least bit amusing.

We see that “he would” introduces the correlative conjunction “rather … than.” Thus, “he would” carries over to the second part of the correlative construction.

> Marshall joked that <strong>he would</strong> (rather get a root canal than eat his wife’s cooking).

We can break the part of the sentence that involves the correlative construction into two clauses:

> … he would (rather) get a root canal …

> … he would (than) eat his wife’s cooking …

We see that both of these clauses make perfect sense.

So, how do we use this knowledge to score points on test day? Easy! We eliminate any sentence versions that are illogical because they incorrectly utilize (or do not utilize correctly) essential elements that introduce correlative constructions.

Modern airships, such as blimps, are filled not, as many assume, with hydrogen, the inflammable gas associated with the <em>Hindenburg</em> <u>disaster, but filled</u> with helium, a gas almost as buoyant as hydrogen yet completely inert.

1. [ ] (A) disaster, but filled
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) disaster; they are filled instead
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) disaster; instead, filled
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) disaster, but
5. [ ] (E) disaster, but are filled instead
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



When “not x … but y” is used, the item that comes after “not” has to be parallel to the item that comes after “but,” and both items have to work logically with what precedes the correlative structure.


> <b>(A)</b> Modern airships, such as blimps, are filled not, as many assume, with hydrogen, the inflammable gas associated with the <em>Hindenburg</em> <b>disaster, but filled
</b> with helium, a gas almost as buoyant as hydrogen yet completely inert.



Since “filled” appears in what precedes “not,” “filled” is understood to carry over to what follows “but.” Therefore, for “filled” to appear again after “but,” as it does in this version, does not make sense.


> <b>(B)</b> Modern airships, such as blimps, are filled not, as many assume, with hydrogen, the inflammable gas associated with the <em>Hindenburg</em> <b>disaster; they are filled instead
</b> with helium, a gas almost as buoyant as hydrogen yet completely inert.



The clause preceding the semicolon in this version does not work, as it includes the first part of the “not x but y” structure, “are filled not … with hydrogen,” but not the second part, and as a result, is awkward and somewhat illogical.


> <b>(C)</b> Modern airships, such as blimps, are filled not, as many assume, with hydrogen, the inflammable gas associated with the <em>Hindenburg</em> <b>disaster; instead, filled
</b> with helium, a gas almost as buoyant as hydrogen yet completely inert.



The clause preceding the semicolon in this version does not work, as it includes the first part of the “not x but y” structure, “are filled not … with hydrogen,” but not the second part, and as a result is awkward and somewhat illogical.


Also, the portion of the sentence following the semicolon is a fragment, rather than a complete clause. A semicolon should connect parallel structures, such as a clause and a clause, not a clause and a fragment.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D)</b> Modern airships, such as blimps, are filled not, as many assume, with hydrogen, the inflammable gas associated with the <em>Hindenburg</em> <b>disaster, but
</b> with helium, a gas almost as buoyant as hydrogen yet completely inert.



The version created via the use of this choice makes sense, as we can see by considering the following:


<strong>Modern airships … are filled</strong> (not … with hydrogen, the inflammable gas associated with the <em>Hindenburg</em> disaster, but with helium …).


Not <strong>modern airships … are filled</strong> with hydrogen, the inflammable gas associated with the <em>Hindenburg</em> disaster, but <strong>modern airships … are filled</strong> with helium ….


> <b>(E)</b> Modern airships, such as blimps, are filled not, as many assume, with hydrogen, the inflammable gas associated with the <em>Hindenburg</em> <b>disaster, but are filled instead
</b> with helium, a gas almost as buoyant as hydrogen yet completely inert.



Since “are filled” appears in what precedes “not,” “are filled” is understood to carry over to what follows “but.” Therefore, for “are filled” to appear again after “but,” as it does in this version, does not make sense.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Sentence Correction Example 18  

Some of the oldest surviving examples of recorded sound reside in the archives of the Edison <u>laboratory, having either been recorded on wax cylinder or</u> on tin plate.

1. [ ] (A) laboratory, having either been recorded on wax cylinder or
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) laboratory, and this was recorded either on wax cylinder or recorded
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) laboratory and were recorded either on wax cylinder or
4. [ ] (D) laboratory, which were recorded on either wax cylinder or
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) laboratory, being either recorded on wax cylinder or recorded
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



In answering this question, we have to be careful to select a choice the use of which results in an “either … or” structure that is logically constructed and in a version that expresses a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(A)</b> Some of the oldest surviving examples of recorded sound reside in the archives of the Edison <b>laboratory, having either been recorded on wax cylinder or
</b> on tin plate.



Because “recorded” comes after “either,” “recorded” is not understood to carry over to the part of the “either … or” structure that follows “or.” Therefore, the “either … or” structure is not logically constructed, as we can see by considering the following:


<strong>having </strong>(either been recorded on wax cylinder or on tin plate)


either <strong>having </strong>been recorded or <strong>having </strong>on tin plate


We can see that “having on tin plate” does not make sense.


Also, this version has a meaning issue, because it does not quite make sense to say that the examples of recorded sound “reside in the archives … having … been recorded.” The examples of recorded sound reside in the archives because someone put them there, not because of their having been recorded on wax cylinder or tin plate, but the use of the closing participial phrase in this version results it’s conveying that the examples’ residing in the archives is somehow a result of their “having … been recorded.”


> <b>(B)</b> Some of the oldest surviving examples of recorded sound reside in the archives of the Edison <b>laboratory, and this was recorded either on wax cylinder or recorded
</b> on tin plate.



Since there is nothing specific to which the demonstrative pronoun “this” would refer, the use of “this” does not make sense. While conceivably “this” could refer to “sound,” the sentence does not mention a particular “sound.” So, the use of a demonstrative pronoun, which is meant to refer to something specific, does not make sense.


Also, since “recorded” precedes “either,” “recorded” is understood to carry over to what appears after “or.” However, “recorded” appears also after “or.” So, the “either … or” structure illogically repeats “recorded,” as we can see by considering the following:


this was <strong>recorded</strong> (either on wax cylinder or recorded on tin plate)


this was either <strong>recorded</strong> on wax cylinder or <strong>recorded</strong> recorded on tin plate


We can see that “recorded recorded on tin plate” does not make sense.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(C)</b> Some of the oldest surviving examples of recorded sound reside in the archives of the Edison <b>laboratory and were recorded either on wax cylinder or
</b> on tin plate.



This version conveys the logical meaning that some of the earliest examples of recorded sound reside in the archives and were recorded in two ways.


Also, the “either … or” structure is logically constructed, as we can see by considering the following:


were<strong> recorded</strong> (either on wax cylinder or on tin plate)


were either <strong>recorded </strong>on wax cylinder or <strong>recorded</strong> on tin plate


> <b>(D)</b> Some of the oldest surviving examples of recorded sound reside in the archives of the Edison <b>laboratory, which were recorded on either wax cylinder or
</b> on tin plate.



Because of the way in which “which were recorded …” is placed, it can modify only “archives.” The modifier is too far from “examples” to modify “examples.” However, “archives” would not be recorded. So, the modifier “which were recorded …” is misplaced.


Also, since “on” precedes “either,” “on” is understood to carry over to what appears after “or.” However, “on” appears also after “or.” So, the “either … or” structure illogically repeats “on,” as we can see by considering the following:


recorded <strong>on</strong> (either wax cylinder or on tin plate).


recorded either <strong>on </strong>wax cylinder or <strong>on </strong>on tin plate.


We can see that “on on tin plate” does not make sense.


> <b>(E)</b> Some of the oldest surviving examples of recorded sound reside in the archives of the Edison <b>laboratory, being either recorded on wax cylinder or recorded
</b> on tin plate.



In this version, what appears after “either” matches what appears after “or,” and both what appears after “either” and what appears after “or” work logically with what precedes the structure, “being.” So, the “either … or” structure in this version is blatantly logical.


However, we must not allow ourselves to be tempted into choosing this choice by the presence of the blatantly correct parallel structure, because the meaning conveyed by this version does not make sense. This version says, essentially, “Some of the oldest surviving examples of recorded sound reside in the archives … being recorded,” as if, even as surviving examples of recorded sound reside in the archives, they are “being recorded.” Of course, this meaning does not make sense.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

Let’s now discuss some other important structures that demand parallelism.
