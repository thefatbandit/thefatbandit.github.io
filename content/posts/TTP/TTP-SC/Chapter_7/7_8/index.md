## Lesson Content
So far in this chapter, we’ve seen a number of situations in which parallelism must be maintained.

> X rather than Y

> mistake X for Y

> prefer X to Y

> range from X to Y

> more X than Y (in certain contexts)

Consider the following example sentence:

> The team <strong>traveled </strong>by rail rather than <strong>flew </strong>to the tournament.

Notice, what precedes “rather than,” “traveled,” is parallel to what follows “rather than,” “flew.”

The following is not correct:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The team <strong>traveled </strong>by rail rather than <strong>flying </strong>to the tournament.

While sentences like the above sentence are commonly used in speech and writing, the above sentence is not correct because the finite verb “traveled” is not parallel to the gerund “flying.”

> Please don’t mistake my <strong>silence</strong> for <strong>anger</strong>; I’m simply thinking of what to say.

In the expression “mistake X for Y,” the nouns “silence” and “anger” are parallel.

> Greg prefers <strong>cooking a meal for his family at home</strong> to <strong>eating in a crowded restaurant</strong>.

In the expression “prefer X to Y,” the two gerund phrases are parallel.

> The offerings at the eco-friendly products show range from <strong>a solar powered grill</strong> to <strong>reusable makeup removing pads</strong>.

In the expression “range from X to Y,” the two noun phrases are parallel.

> After Dr.

In the expression more X than Y, the nouns “beast” and “man” are parallel.

Of course, in some contexts, what follows “more” will not appear to be parallel to what follows “than.” Consider the following example:

> Raul was more <strong>skilled </strong>than <strong>Alonso</strong>.

So, if we see the expression, “more X than Y,” we have to consider context in determining whether what follows “more” has to be parallel to what follows “than.”

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Each of the following structures demands parallelism.
{{< /alert >}}

Now, a key thing to notice about the parallel structures in the above examples is that, unlike the other parallel structures we’ve seen in this chapter, most of these structures don’t involve carry over that we can use to determine whether they are properly parallel.

For instance, in “Please don’t mistake my <strong>silence</strong> for <strong>anger</strong>,” “mistake my silence for anger” uses proper parallelism because “silence” and “anger” are both nouns.

Notice also that the elements don’t have to be exactly the same.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In order for the structures “X rather than Y,” “mistake X for Y,” “prefer X to Y,” “range from X to Y,” or “more X than Y” to be parallel, X and Y must be grammatically similar.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 19  

While many office workers reported that they preferred working at home <u>to they were commuting</u> to work each day, many others reported that they missed the camaraderie they experienced in an office setting.

1. [ ] (A) to they were commuting
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) to they commute
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) more than they like to commute
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) to commuting
5. [ ] (E) instead of if they commute
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



> <b>(A)</b> While many office workers reported that they preferred working at home <b>to they were commuting
</b> to work each day, many others reported that they missed the camaraderie they experienced in an office setting.



This version does not contain a parallel “prefer X to Y” construction.


What appears after “preferred” should be parallel to what appears after “to.” However, the gerund “working” is not parallel to the independent clause “they were commuting.”


Notice that the meaning conveyed is not quite logical because “they were commuting” conveys an action that “they” were doing, not a preference.


The sentence created with this version is neither parallel nor logical.


> <b>(B)</b> While many office workers reported that they preferred working at home <b>to they commute
</b> to work each day, many others reported that they missed the camaraderie they experienced in an office setting.



This version does not contain a parallel “prefer X to Y” construction.


What appears after “preferred” should be parallel to what appears after “to.” However, the gerund “working” is not parallel to the independent clause “they commute.”


Notice that the meaning conveyed is not quite logical because “they commute” conveys an action that “they” were doing, not a preference.


The sentence created with this version is neither parallel nor logical.


> <b>(C)</b> While many office workers reported that they preferred working at home <b>more than they like to commute
</b> to work each day, many others reported that they missed the camaraderie they experienced in an office setting.



This version does not contain a parallel “prefer X to Y” construction.


What appears after “preferred” should be parallel to the second element of the construction. However, the gerund “working” is not parallel to the infinitive verb “to commute.”


Additionally, “preferred X <em>more than they liked </em>Y” is redundant, since “preferred” and “more than they liked” convey essentially the same meaning in this context. The correct idiom is “preferred X <em>to</em> Y.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D)</b> While many office workers reported that they preferred working at home <b>to commuting
</b> to work each day, many others reported that they missed the camaraderie they experienced in an office setting.



 This is the only version of the sentence that contains parallel elements in the “prefer X to Y” construction.


The gerund “working,” which appears after “preferred,” is parallel to the gerund “commuting,” which appears after “to.”


The sentence is grammatically correct, and the meaning makes sense.


> <b>(E)</b> While many office workers reported that they preferred working at home <b>instead of if they commute
</b> to work each day, many others reported that they missed the camaraderie they experienced in an office setting.



This version does not contain a parallel “prefer X to Y” construction.


What appears after “preferred” should be parallel to the second element of the construction. However, the gerund “working” is not parallel to the clause “if they commute ….”


Additionally, “preferred X <em>instead of</em> Y” is somewhat redundant and unnecessarily wordy, since “preferred” and “instead of” convey essentially the same meaning in this context. The correct idiom is “preferred X <em>to</em> Y.”


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Sentence Correction Example 20  

The TV critic suggested that the singing competition owed its high ratings more <u>to viewers’ fear of missing out than their actually being interested</u> in the outcome.

1. [ ] (A) to viewers’ fear of missing out than their actually being interested
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) because viewers are afraid of missing out than because they are actually interested
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) because of viewers’ fear of missing out than that they are actually interested
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) due to viewers being afraid of missing out than to their actual interest
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) to viewers’ fear of missing out than to their actual interest

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



In the context of a sentence like the one in this question, “more … than” is used in a way similar to the way in which a correlative conjunction such as “either … or” is used. So, what follows “more” must be parallel to what follows “than.”


> <b>(A)</b> The TV critic suggested that the singing competition owed its high ratings more <b>to viewers’ fear of missing out than their actually being interested
</b> in the outcome.



In this version, “more” and “than” are followed by two non-parallel elements, “to viewers’ fear of missing out” and “their actually being interested ….”


Also, the element that follows “than” does not work logically with what precedes the “more … than” structure, as we can see by considering the following:


the singing competition owed its high ratings … their actually being interested in the outcome


> <b>(B)</b> The TV critic suggested that the singing competition owed its high ratings more <b>because viewers are afraid of missing out than because they are actually interested
</b> in the outcome.



In this version, “more” and “than” are followed by two parallel elements, “because viewers are afraid …” and “because they are actually interested ….”


However, neither of the elements that follow “more” and “than” works logically with what precedes the “more … than” structure, as we can see by considering the following:


the singing competition owed its high ratings … because viewers are afraid of missing out


the singing competition owed its high ratings … because they are actually interested in the outcome


> <b>(C)</b> The TV critic suggested that the singing competition owed its high ratings more <b>because of viewers’ fear of missing out than that they are actually interested
</b> in the outcome.



In this version, “more” and “than” are followed by two non-parallel elements, “because of viewers’ fear …” and “that they are actually interested ….”


Also, neither of the elements that follow “more” and “than” works logically with what precedes the “more … than” structure, as we can see by considering the following:


the singing competition owed its high ratings … because of viewers’ fear of missing out


the singing competition owed its high ratings … than that they are actually interested in the outcome


> <b>(D)</b> The TV critic suggested that the singing competition owed its high ratings more <b>due to viewers being afraid of missing out than to their actual interest
</b> in the outcome.



In this version, “more” and “than” are followed by two non-parallel elements, “due to viewers’ being afraid …” and “to their actual interest …,” since “due to” is not parallel to “to.”


Also, the element that follows “more” does not work logically with what precedes “more,” as we can see by considering the following:


the singing competition owed its high ratings … due to viewers’ being afraid of missing out


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E)</b> The TV critic suggested that the singing competition owed its high ratings more <b>to viewers’ fear of missing out than to their actual interest
</b> in the outcome.



In this version, “more” and “than” are followed by two parallel elements, “to viewers’ fear …” and “to their actual interest ….”


Also, both of the elements that follow “more” and “than” work logically with what precedes the “more … than” structure, as we can see by considering the following:


the singing competition owed its high ratings … to viewers’ fear of missing out


the singing competition owed its high ratings … to their actual interest in the outcome


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Sentence Correction Example 21  

<u>Almost universally acknowledged is that the most successful teachers are those who, rather than just convey the basic facts and figures to their students, inspire them to think on their own. </u>

1. [x] (A) Almost universally acknowledged is that the most successful teachers are those who, rather than just convey the basic facts and figures to their students, inspire them to think on their own.
2. [ ] (B) Rather than just conveying the basic facts and figures, the most successful teachers are the ones who inspire students to think on their own, which is almost universally acknowledged.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) Almost universally acknowledged is the fact that the teachers who are the most successful are not only conveying the basic facts and figures to their students but can inspire them also to think on their own.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) The most successful teachers are the ones who inspire students, a fact almost acknowledged universally, rather than the ones who just convey the basic facts and figures.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) It is almost universally acknowledged that the most successful teachers are those who inspire students to think on their own, rather than just conveying the basic facts and figures to them.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



Two sentence elements connected by the expression “rather than” should be parallel. Only the correct version uses parallel elements with “rather than” and conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(A)</b> <b>Almost universally acknowledged is that the most successful teachers are those who, rather than just convey the basic facts and figures to their students, inspire them to think on their own.
</b>


In this version, the expression “rather than” connects parallel elements “convey” and “inspire,” and this version effectively conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>Rather than just conveying the basic facts and figures, the most successful teachers are the ones who inspire students to think on their own, which is almost universally acknowledged.
</b>


In this version, the expression “rather than” connects elements that are not parallel: “conveying,” a gerund, and “are,” a finite verb.


Also, the ideas connected by “rather than” do not work logically together, as it does not make sense to contrast teachers’ conveying facts and figures with teachers are the ones.


Also, the use of the relative clause “which is almost universally acknowledged” is not ideal, since the relative clause could be perceived as modifying “their own,” with the result that the sentence could be read as conveying the nonsensical meaning that “their own” is almost universally acknowledged. Furthermore, even if we were to decide that the relative clause modifies the clauses that precede it, that type of modification is not optimal and is not preferred by the GMAT.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>Almost universally acknowledged is the fact that the teachers who are the most successful are not only conveying the basic facts and figures to their students but can inspire them also to think on their own.
</b>


 The first sign that this choice is incorrect is its use of the present continuous “are … conveying.” By using that verb form, this choice suggests that the teachers mentioned are at this moment continuously “conveying” facts and figures. What makes more sense is to say that the teachers “convey” facts and figures, communicating that they do so in general rather than that they are doing so continuously at this moment.


Also, the “not only … but also” construction in this version is not properly parallel, as we can see by considering the following:


the teachers who are the most successful <strong>are</strong> (not only conveying the basic facts and figures to their students but can inspire them also to think on their own)


the teachers who are the most successful not only <strong>are</strong> conveying the basic facts and figures to their students but <strong>are</strong> can inspire them also to think on their own


We can see that “but are can inspire them also” does not make sense.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>The most successful teachers are the ones who inspire students, a fact almost acknowledged universally, rather than the ones who just convey the basic facts and figures.
</b>


In this version, the modifier “a fact almost acknowledged universally” is poorly placed, with the result that the modifier modifies only the first clause of the sentence, rather than the entire sentence, even though the sentence expresses one coherent idea that would be better modified as a whole.


Also, the meaning conveyed by “a fact almost acknowledged universally” doesn’t really make sense. The point to be made is that the fact is almost universally acknowledged, but the modifier expresses that the fact is universally almost acknowledged, as if everyone everywhere almost, but not quite, acknowledges the fact.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>It is almost universally acknowledged that the most successful teachers are those who inspire students to think on their own, rather than just conveying the basic facts and figures to them.
</b>


In this version “rather than” connects two elements that are not parallel: “inspire,” a finite verb, and “conveying,” a gerund.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

Now that we have covered various types of parallel structures, let’s talk about an important general concept of parallel structures: parallel does not always mean exactly the same.
