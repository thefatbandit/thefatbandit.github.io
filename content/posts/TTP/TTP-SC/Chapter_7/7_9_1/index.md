## Lesson Content
Some people believe that verbs that are in different tenses are not parallel to each other.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Verbs that are in different tenses can be parallel.
{{< /alert >}}

For example, consider the following sentence that has different tenses, past perfect and past continuous, in the same list:

> The ecologist said that genetically modified fish <strong>had found </strong>their way into the lake, <strong>had grown</strong> quickly, and <strong>were eating</strong> the other fish.

Does the sentence work? It does, right? Let’s break it down:

> The ecologist said <strong>that genetically modified fish</strong> (had found their way into the lake, had grown quickly, and were eating the other fish).

> The ecologist said <strong>that genetically modified fish</strong> had found their way into the lake, <strong>that genetically modified fish</strong> had grown quickly, and <strong>that genetically modified fish</strong> were eating the other fish.

We can see that each element in the list works logically with “that genetically modified fish.”

Let’s look at another example:

> Storms such as these affect critical infrastructure, topple trees, and will continue to pose serious problems for commuters if the city does not devise a comprehensive preparation plan.

The three elements in the series presented in the sentence above are parallel even though the first two verbs are in the simple present tense and the third is in the future tense.

> <strong>Storms such as these</strong> (affect critical infrastructure, topple trees, and will continue to pose serious problems for commuters if the city does not devise a comprehensive preparation plan).

> <strong>Storms such as these </strong>affect critical infrastructure, <strong>storms such as these </strong>topple trees, and <strong>storms such as these </strong>will continue to pose serious problems for commuters if the city does not devise a comprehensive preparation plan.

We see that each element in the list works logically with “storms such as these.”

Let’s consider one more example:

> The school <strong>opened</strong> to voters at 7 a.m.

The simple past tense verb “opened” and the present perfect “has been” are elements of a parallel construction using the coordinating conjunction “and.”

> <strong>The school</strong> (opened to voters at 7 a.m.

> <strong>The school</strong> opened to voters at 7 a.m.

How can we use this knowledge to score points on test day? We can take care not to eliminate sentence versions just because they use different verb tenses in structures that must be parallel.

##### Question Level: hard



To arrive at the correct answer to this question, we can first eliminate the versions that have errors in construction and then use meaning to choose between the choices that remain.


> <b>(A)</b> The <b>organization helped to shape Brazilian ecological policy, promote rainforest restoration, is likely to restore thousands of hectares in the near future, and
</b> is made up of enterprises ranging from small farms to major NGOs.



 The list in this version is not logically constructed, as we can see by considering the following:


<strong>helped to</strong> (shape Brazilian ecological policy, promote rainforest restoration, is likely to restore thousands of hectares in the near future, and is made up of)


<strong>helped to</strong> shape Brazilian ecological policy, <strong>helped to</strong> promote rainforest restoration, <strong>helped to</strong> is likely to restore thousands of hectares in the near future, and <strong>helped to</strong> is made up of


We can see that what introduces the list, “helped to,” works logically with only the first two elements of the list and not with the last two.


> <b>(B)</b> The <b>organization, having helped to shape Brazilian ecological policy, promote rainforest restoration, and being likely to restore thousands of hectares in the near future,
</b> is made up of enterprises ranging from small farms to major NGOs.



 The list in the modifier in this version is not parallel, as we can see by considering the following:


<strong>having helped to</strong> (shape Brazilian ecological policy, promote rainforest restoration, and being likely to restore thousands of hectares in the near future)


<strong>having helped to</strong> shape Brazilian ecological policy, <strong>having helped to</strong> promote rainforest restoration, and <strong>having helped to</strong> being likely to restore thousands of hectares in the near future


We can see that what introduces the list, “having helped to,” doesn’t work with the third list element since “having helped to being likely to restore” does not make sense.


> <b>(C)</b> The <b>organization, which helped to shape Brazilian ecological policy, promote rainforest restoration, and likely restore thousands of hectares in the near future,
</b> is made up of enterprises ranging from small farms to major NGOs.



 Often people answering this question narrow the choices down to this one and choice (E). Thus, often, correctly answering the question requires carefully considering this choice and choice (E) to find a key reason to choose one or the other.


So, notice what we get if we distribute “helped to” throughout the list in this version.


<strong>helped to</strong> (shape Brazilian ecological policy, promote rainforest restoration, and likely restore thousands of hectares in the near future)


helped to shape Brazilian ecological policy, <strong>helped to</strong> promote rainforest restoration, and likely <strong>helped to</strong> restore thousands of hectares in the near future


The list is parallel, but there is a meaning issue since “helped to restore thousands of hectares in the near future” doesn’t make sense. After all, it doesn’t make sense to use the past tense “helped to” in discussing an event that will occur “in the near future.”


So, this choice is incorrect.


> <b>(D)</b> The <b>organization, which helps to shape Brazilian ecological policy, which promotes rainforest restoration, which is likely to restore thousands of hectares in the near future, and which
</b> is made up of enterprises ranging from small farms to major NGOs.



 We have to be careful not to be tempted into choosing this choice just because it is so obviously parallel.


Notice that this version is composed of only “the organization” and a list of relative clauses that begins with the first “which” and runs all the way to the end of the sentence.


So, this version has no independent clause and thus is not a complete sentence.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E)</b> The <b>organization, which helped to shape Brazilian ecological policy, is promoting rainforest restoration, and will likely restore thousands of hectares in the near future,
</b> is made up of enterprises ranging from small farms to major NGOs.



 While the verbs in the list in this version are in different tenses, the list is perfectly parallel, as we can see by considering the following:


<strong>which</strong> (helped to shape Brazilian ecological policy, is promoting rainforest restoration, and will likely restore thousands of hectares in the near future)


<strong>which</strong> helped to shape Brazilian ecological policy, <strong>which</strong> is promoting rainforest restoration, and <strong>which</strong> will likely restore thousands of hectares in the near future


This version is logically constructed and conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
