## Lesson Content
One might assume that it’s incorrect to have both active voice verbs and passive voice verbs in the same list.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

There is nothing wrong with having active voice verbs and passive voice verbs together in a list.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following example:

> Having hit her GMAT target, Aisha <strong>applied</strong> to her first-choice business school, <strong>was invited</strong> for an interview, and <strong>was accepted</strong>.

As we already know well, each component of a list must work logically with the essential element or elements that introduce the list.

> Having hit her GMAT target, <strong>Aisha</strong> (applied to her first-choice business school, was invited for an interview, and was accepted).

Does each element in the list work logically with “Aisha?” Definitely.

> Having hit her GMAT target, <strong>Aisha</strong> applied to her first-choice business school, <strong>Aisha</strong> was invited for an interview, and <strong>Aisha</strong> was accepted.

Notice that it’s OK that the verb “applied” is in the active voice, while the verbs “was interviewed” and “was accepted” are in the passive voice.

Consider our example sentence with a small change:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Having hit her GMAT target, Aisha applied to her first-choice business school, was invited for an interview, and Aisha was accepted.

Let’s see what this sentence communicates:

> Having hit her GMAT target, <strong>Aisha</strong> (applied to her first-choice business school, was invited for an interview, and Aisha was accepted).

> Having hit her GMAT target, <strong>Aisha</strong> applied to her first-choice business school, <strong>Aisha</strong> was invited for an interview, and <strong>Aisha</strong> Aisha was accepted.

Notice that the third element in the list does not work logically with what introduces the list.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When items appear in a series, verb voice can change from active to passive without violating the rules of parallelism, as long as each element in the list works logically with the essential element or elements that introduce the list.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at a couple more examples.

> On Friday, Spencer left the office at 2:30 p.m., picked up his daughter from school, took her to her ballet class, and was reprimanded by his boss for arriving ten minutes late to the 4:00 p.m.

Despite the switch from the active verbs “left,” “picked up,” and “took” to the passive “was reprimanded,” the sentence above is parallel and correct.

> On Friday, <strong>Spencer </strong>(left the office at 2:30 p.m., picked up his daughter from school, took her to her ballet class, and was reprimanded by his boss for arriving ten minutes late to the 4:00 p.m.

> On Friday, <strong>Spencer </strong>left the office at 2:30 p.m., <strong>Spencer</strong> picked up his daughter from school, <strong>Spencer</strong> took her to her ballet class, and <strong>Spencer</strong> was reprimanded by his boss for arriving ten minutes late to the 4:00 p.m.

Notice that each of the elements in the list work logically with “Spencer.”

However, if we were to change the fundamental structure of an element within the series, even without changing any of the verbs, the sentence would no longer be parallel.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: On Friday, Spencer left the office at 2:30 p.m., picked up his daughter from school, he took her to her ballet class, and was reprimanded by his boss for arriving ten minutes late to the 4:00 p.m.

By placing the pronoun “he” at the beginning of the element “took her to her ballet class,” we have created an independent clause, which is not parallel to the other elements.

> On Friday, <strong>Spencer</strong> (left the office at 2:30 p.m., picked up his daughter from school, he took her to her ballet class, and was reprimanded by his boss for arriving ten minutes late to the 4:00 p.m.

> On Friday, <strong>Spencer</strong> left the office at 2:30 p.m., <strong>Spencer</strong> picked up his daughter from school, <strong>Spencer</strong> he took her to her ballet class, and <strong>Spencer</strong> was reprimanded by his boss for arriving ten minutes late to the 4:00 p.m.

Of course, the third item in the list does not make sense.

So, how do we use this knowledge to score points on test day? Easy! We don’t eliminate sentence versions just because they utilize both the active and passive verb voices.

##  Sentence Correction Example 23  

The jewel thief is being pursued by Interpol, <u>was last spotted at an outdoor market in Berlin, and will likely be</u> caught when he attempts to cross the border into a neighboring country.

1. [x] (A) was last spotted at an outdoor market in Berlin, and will likely be
2. [ ] (B) is last spotted at an outdoor market in Berlin, and is likely being
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) is last spotted at an outdoor market in Berlin, and likely to be
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) being last spotted at an outdoor market in Berlin, and likely being
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) last spotted at an outdoor market in Berlin, and likely
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

##### Question Level: easy



> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(A)</b> The jewel thief is being pursued by Interpol, <b>was last spotted at an outdoor market in Berlin, and will likely be
</b> caught when he attempts to cross the border into a neighboring country.



Remember that verbs that are in different tenses can be perfectly parallel. In this case, there is a list of three elements describing actions related to “the jewel thief”: “is being pursued,” “was last spotted,” and “will likely be caught.”


We know that the final two elements must be parallel to the first element, since the first element appears in the non-underlined portion of the sentence and thus cannot be changed. Although the first element, “<em>is</em> being pursued,” is present progressive tense, “<em>was</em> last spotted” is past tense, and “<em>will</em> likely be caught” is future tense, the verbs are still parallel, and the timeline of events is logical.


This version is parallel, grammatically correct, and conveys a logical meaning.


> <b>(B)</b> The jewel thief is being pursued by Interpol, <b>is last spotted at an outdoor market in Berlin, and is likely being
</b> caught when he attempts to cross the border into a neighboring country.



 Although this version may appear more parallel than others because “<em>is</em> being pursued,” “<em>is</em> last spotted,” and “<em>is</em> likely being caught” all begin with “is,” the meaning conveyed does not make sense.


Firstly, “is,” a present tense verb, conflicts with “last spotted,” which describes a past action.


Secondly, “is likely being caught” indicates an action that is currently underway, but “when he attempts to cross the border” indicates an action that hasn’t happened yet. Furthermore, it does not make sense that “the jewel thief is being <em>pursued</em>” and “is likely being <em>caught</em>” simultaneously. If the thief currently is being pursued, then he is not also currently being caught.


Remember, versions of a sentence that achieve parallelism by repeating the exact same words are not automatically correct; we must be sure that the meaning conveyed is logical.


> <b>(C)</b> The jewel thief is being pursued by Interpol, <b>is last spotted at an outdoor market in Berlin, and likely to be
</b> caught when he attempts to cross the border into a neighboring country.



Saying thatthe jewel thief “is last spotted” does not make sense because “is” indicates a present action while “last spotted” indicates a past action.


Additionally, the final element in the list, “likely to be caught” is not parallel to the first two elements because the adjective “likely” is not parallel to the verb “is.”


This version is not parallel or logical.


> <b>(D)</b> The jewel thief is being pursued by Interpol, <b>being last spotted at an outdoor market in Berlin, and likely being
</b> caught when he attempts to cross the border into a neighboring country.



Although this version may appear more parallel than others because “<em>being</em> pursued,” “<em>being</em> last spotted,” and “likely <em>being</em> caught” all include “being,” the meaning conveyed does not make sense.


If the thief is currently “being pursued,” then it is not possible that he is also currently “being caught.” Furthermore, the statement that “The jewel thief is … being caught when he attempts to cross the border” does not make sense because “being caught” indicates an action that is currently underway, but “when he attempts to cross the border” indicates an action that hasn’t happened yet.


Remember, versions of a sentence that achieve parallelism by repeating the exact same words are not automatically correct; you must be sure that the meaning conveyed is logical.


> <b>(E)</b> The jewel thief is being pursued by Interpol, <b>last spotted at an outdoor market in Berlin, and likely
</b> caught when he attempts to cross the border into a neighboring country.



If we carry over what precedes the start of the list to the other elements of the list, we can see that the meaning conveyed is illogical:


<strong>The jewel thief is </strong>being pursued, <strong>the jewel thief is </strong>last spotted at an outdoor market in Berlin, and <strong>the jewel thief is</strong> likely caught when he attempts to cross the border


Although the sentence is parallel and grammatically correct, it does not make sense to say that the jewel thief “islast spotted,” because “is” indicates a present action while “last spotted” indicates a past action.


Furthermore, if the thief currently “is being pursued,” then it does not make sense that he also currently is “likely caught.” Additionally, “is likely caught” indicates a present state, but “when he attempts to cross the border” indicates an action that hasn’t happened yet.


Thus, this version does not convey a logical timeline of events.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

##  Sentence Correction Example 24  

<u>John Francis Cook, Jr., a prominent educator, politician, and businessperson, served as a Howard University trustee, was appointed a Washington D.C. Justice of the Peace and appointed Chief Tax Collector, and partnered</u> with his brother and a former legislator to found the manufacturing firm Cook, Cook, and White.

1. [x] (A) John Francis Cook, Jr.
2. [ ] (B) As a prominent educator, politician, and businessperson, John Francis Cook, Jr.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) John Francis Cook, Jr.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) Because of being a prominent educator, politician, and businessperson, John Francis Cook, Jr.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) John Francis Cook, Jr.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(A)</b> <b>John Francis Cook, Jr. , a prominent educator, politician, and businessperson, served as a Howard University trustee, was appointed a Washington D. C. Justice of the Peace and appointed Chief Tax Collector, and partnered

This version uses an outer list and a nested list to effectively convey a meaning that makes sense.


The outer list is:


John Francis Cook, Jr.…:


served …


was …


partnered …


The nested list is:


was:


appointed a Washington D.C. Justice of the Peace


appointed Chief Tax Collector

The use of the opening modifier “as a prominent educator, politician, and businessman” is not quite logical. John Francis Cook, Jr. would not have been engaging in the activities listed in the sentence “as a prominent educator, politician, and businessperson,” i.e., “in the capacity of a prominent educator, politician, and businessperson.” He simply was a prominent educator, politician, and businessman who engaged in the listed activities.


Also, the list of John Francis Cook, Jr.’s activities is not logically constructed, since the third item, “appointed …,” does not work logically with what introduces the list, “John Francis Cook, Jr. served,” as we can see by combining the two:


John Francis Cook, Jr. served appointed …


Also, the use of the closing participial phrase, “partnering …,” results in the sentence’s conveying the illogical meaning that, in the process of or as a result of serving as a trustee and as a justice of the peace and of being appointed Chief Tax Collector, John Francis Cook, Jr. partnered with his brother and a legislator to found a manufacturing business.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>John Francis Cook, Jr. , a prominent educator, politician, and businessperson, who, serving as a Howard University trustee, was appointed a Washington D. C. Justice of the Peace, was appointed Chief Tax Collector, and was partnered
</b> with his brother and a former legislator to found the manufacturing firm Cook, Cook, and White.



This version may seem to be correct since all three verbs, “was appointed,” “was appointed,” and “was partnered,” in the list of events that appears in the sentence are in the passive voice. However, by looking at aspects of the sentence other than that parallelism, we can see that the sentence is rather flawed.


While “was partnered” may match the other passive voice verbs in the list, saying that John Francis Cook, Jr. was partnered with others to found the manufacturing firm is not logical, as it seems to convey that some outside force caused him to be partnered with the others.


Also, since everything after “John Francis Cook, Jr.” modifies “John Francis Cook, Jr.,” the sentence has no main verb.


Also, the use of the modifier “serving as a Howard University Trustee” results in the sentence’s conveying the somewhat illogical meaning that the events mentioned were somehow related to John Francis Cook, Jr.’s serving as a Howard University Trustee.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>Because of being a prominent educator, politician, and businessperson, John Francis Cook, Jr. served as a Howard University trustee, was appointed a Washington D. C. Justice of the Peace, and was appointed Chief Tax Collector, partnering
</b> with his brother and a former legislator to found the manufacturing firm Cook, Cook, and White.



This flawed choice contains a perfectly constructed list and is basically grammatically correct. So, it is a rather tempting trap choice.


“Because of being a …” is not effective, because it does not make clear that John Francis Cook, Jr. was the one being all those things.


Also, the use of the closing participial phrase, “partnering …,” results in the sentence’s conveying the illogical meaning that, in the process of or as a result of serving as a trustee and being appointed a justice of the peace and Chief Tax Collector, John Francis Cook, Jr. partnered with his brother and a legislator to found a manufacturing business.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>John Francis Cook, Jr. , who was a prominent educator, politician, and businessperson, served as a Howard University trustee, was appointed a Washington D. C. Justice of the Peace, and Chief Tax Collector, to partner
</b> with his brother and a former legislator to found the manufacturing firm Cook, Cook, and White.



The list in this version is not logical, because the third element “Chief Tax Collector” has no verb and, thus, does not work logically with what introduces the list, “John Francis Cook, Jr.


Also, “to partner …” conveys the illogical meaning that John Francis Cook, Jr. served as a trustee of a university and was appointed to government offices in order to partner with people to found a manufacturing firm.

Let’s now discuss another idea that will help us to avoid eliminating correct answers in SC questions.
