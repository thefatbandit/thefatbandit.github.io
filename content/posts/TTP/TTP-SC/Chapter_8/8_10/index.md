## Lesson Content
The word “like” can be used in a comparison, but not to introduce an example.

To see the difference between the use of “like” and the use of “such as” demonstrated, consider the example sentences below:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: When traveling through the Everglades, one may see many animals, <strong>like </strong>snakes, mammals, and alligators.

Since “like” is strictly a comparison word on the GMAT, using it in this context would imply that one may see, not the animals mentioned, but rather animals similar to, or “like,” snakes, mammals, or alligators.

To correct the sentence, we can replace the word “like” with “such as,” as demonstrated below:

> When traveling through the Everglades, one may see many animals, <strong>such as </strong>snakes, mammals, and alligators.

We could also say:

> When traveling through the Everglades, one may see many animals, <strong>including </strong>snakes, mammals, and alligators

In the above two examples, “such as” and “including” introduce lists of examples of animals that one may actually see.

Note that when “such as” is used to introduce a set of examples, it is often preceded by a comma, as it is in the following example:

> The boy received for his birthday many toys, <strong>such as</strong> action figures, a soccer ball, and a board game, and his favorite among them was a tricky puzzle.

It would also be correct to say:

The boy received for his birthday many toys, <strong>including</strong> action figures, a soccer ball, and a board game, and his favorite among them was a tricky puzzle.

Also, a sentence can be correct with “such” and “as” separated, as they are in the following version:

> The boy received for his birthday <strong>such </strong>toys <strong>as </strong>action figures, a soccer ball, and a board game, and his favorite among them was a tricky puzzle.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The word “like” is strictly a comparison word and cannot be used to introduce examples. To introduce examples, we can use “such as.” We can also use “including” to introduce examples.
{{< /alert >}}

One thing that helps with understanding this difference is the following: we use “like” to compare two nouns that are similar, yet not the same.

> Raspberries are like blueberries.

> Wolves are like dogs.

> Tigers are like house cats.

> Diamonds are like cubic zirconias.

In other words, “like” is used in discussing a noun or nouns similar to another noun (the reference noun), but the reference noun is not in the set to which the other noun or nouns belong.

Of course, using “such as” in such contexts would not make sense, as it doesn’t in the following nonsensical examples.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Raspberries are such as blueberries.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Wolves are such as dogs.

In contrast, “such as” is used to introduce a set of examples that are in the set mentioned before the list of examples:

> Consumption of fruits high in antioxidants, such as raspberries and blueberries, can be good for one’s health.

The above sentence expresses that raspberries and blueberries are in the set “fruits high in antioxidants.

> Canines, such as wolves and dogs, are social animals.

The above sentence expresses that wolves and dogs are in the set “canines.”

##  Sentence Correction Example 13  

The young scouts, <u>such as many scouts</u> before them, built character and learned much via making the hundred mile trek along a section of the Appalachian Trail.

1. [ ] (A) such as many scouts
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) such as many scouts who were
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) like many scouts
4. [ ] (D) like many scouts, who had been
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) such as many scouts, who had been
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



The sentence is about scouts who are similar to or “like” scouts before them. So, in this case, using “like” is correct.


> <b>(A)</b> The young scouts, <b>such as many scouts
</b> before them, built character and learned much via making the hundred mile trek along a section of the Appalachian Trail.



The “many scouts before them” are not examples of “the young scouts.” The “many scouts before them” are different scouts, scouts before the young scouts. So, using “such as,” which is used for introducing examples, does not make sense.


> <b>(B)</b> The young scouts, <b>such as many scouts who were
</b> before them, built character and learned much via making the hundred mile trek along a section of the Appalachian Trail.



The “many scouts who were before them” are not examples of “the young scouts.” The “many scouts who were before them” are different scouts, scouts who were before the young scouts. So, using “such as,” which is used for introducing examples, does not make sense.


Also, since it would not be the case that the “many scouts” mentioned literally “were before” the young scouts when the young scouts were making the trek, the meaning conveyed by “the many scouts who were before them” does not make sense. Yes, “many scouts” had gone before the young scouts, but it is not really logically to say that the many scouts “were before” the young scouts. Were what before the young scouts?


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(C)</b> The young scouts, <b>like many scouts
</b> before them, built character and learned much via making the hundred mile trek along a section of the Appalachian Trail.



Since the point to be made is that the young scouts were like many scouts before them, the use of “like” makes sense in this case.


Also, the simple wording “like many scouts before them” effectively compares the young scouts with the other scouts without the complicating present of a verb, unlike the wording seen in other choices, which includes verbs such as “were” or “had been,” the use of which results in the versions expressing meanings that are not quite logical.


> <b>(D)</b> The young scouts, <b>like many scouts, who had been
</b> before them, built character and learned much via making the hundred mile trek along a section of the Appalachian Trail.



Since the point to be made is that the young scouts were like many scouts before them, the use of “like” makes sense in this case.


However, because “who had been” is set off from the sentence by commas, “who had been” is nonrestrictive and, therefore, does not narrow down the “many scouts” to a certain group of scouts. Rather, it adds some nonessential information about “many scouts.” As a result, the logic of what the sentence expresses is a bit off, because the sentence does not clearly indicate that the many scouts mentioned are scouts other than the young scouts. It does not say “like many <em>other</em> scouts.” It does not say “like many scouts who had been before the young scouts,” without a comma between “scouts” and “who.” It simply says, “the young scouts, like many scouts … built character and learned.” Since “the young scouts” could be included in “many scouts,” the sentence could be read as comparing the young scout with themselves.


Also, the meaning of “who had been before them” is not clear. Had been what before them?


> <b>(E)</b> The young scouts, <b>such as many scouts, who had been
</b> before them, built character and learned much via making the hundred mile trek along a section of the Appalachian Trail.



The “many scouts” mentioned are not examples of “the young scouts.” The “many scouts” mentioned are other scouts. So, using “such as,” which is used for introducing examples, does not make sense.


Also, the meaning of “who had been before them” is not clear. Had been what before them?


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)

##  Sentence Correction Example 14  

Wheat protein, often referred to as gluten, can <u>disrupt the sleep of those who are intolerant to it by triggering an immune response similar to pathogens, like</u> viruses, yeast, and bacteria, all of which have the ability to disrupt sleep.

1. [ ] (A) disrupt the sleep of those who are intolerant to it by triggering an immune response similar to pathogens, like
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) be disruptive to the sleep of intolerant people because it can trigger an immune response, which, like the responses triggered by pathogens, such as
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) disrupt the sleep of those intolerant to gluten by triggering an immune response like pathogens do, including
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) be disruptive for the sleep of those intolerant to it by triggering an immune response similar to that of the responses triggered by pathogens, including
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) disrupt the sleep of those who are intolerant to it by triggering an immune response like the one triggered by pathogens, such as

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



> <b>(A)</b> Wheat protein, often referred to as gluten, can <b>disrupt the sleep of those who are intolerant to it by triggering an immune response similar to pathogens, like
</b> viruses, yeast, and bacteria, all of which have the ability to disrupt sleep.



This version illogically compares an immune response with pathogens.


Also, it incorrectly uses “like” to introduce “viruses, yeast, and bacteria.” The sentence is not structured to state that pathogens are like viruses, yeast, and bacteria. Rather, it is set up to express that “viruses, yeast, and bacteria” are examples of pathogens. Therefore, “viruses, yeast, and bacteria” should be introduced by “such as,” not by “like.”


> <b>(B)</b> Wheat protein, often referred to as gluten, can <b>be disruptive to the sleep of intolerant people because it can trigger an immune response, which, like the responses triggered by pathogens, such as
</b> viruses, yeast, and bacteria, all of which have the ability to disrupt sleep.



The use of the expression “intolerant people” in this context does not make sense, because the sentence does not clearly indicate that the people mentioned do not tolerate gluten. Rather it merely describes them as “intolerant,” which adjective, when used in isolation, is generally understood to mean “unwilling to tolerate certain ideas, beliefs, or cultures.” So, this version expresses the illogical idea that gluten disrupts the sleep of people unwilling to tolerate certain ideas, beliefs, or cultures because gluten triggers an immune response.


Also, the relative clause that begins with “which” has no main verb and does not express a complete thought. Which do what?


> <b>(C)</b> Wheat protein, often referred to as gluten, can <b>disrupt the sleep of those intolerant to gluten by triggering an immune response like pathogens do, including
</b> viruses, yeast, and bacteria, all of which have the ability to disrupt sleep.



This version incorrectly uses “like” to connect two clauses “wheat protein can disrupt … by triggering an immune response” and “pathogens do.” As discussed in the next section of this chapter, “as” rather than “like” should be used in this type of situation.


Also, this version illogically jumps from “pathogens do” to introducing a list of examples of pathogens. Since “including viruses, yeast, and bacteria” provides a list of examples of pathogens, it should follow directly after “pathogens,” not after “pathogens do.”


> <b>(D)</b> Wheat protein, often referred to as gluten, can <b>be disruptive for the sleep of those intolerant to it by triggering an immune response similar to that of the responses triggered by pathogens, including
</b> viruses, yeast, and bacteria, all of which have the ability to disrupt sleep.



The preposition “for” is misused. Gluten is not disruptive “for” sleep. The idiomatically correct and relatively logical expression to use in this context is “disruptive to sleep.”


Also, since “that” could only refer to “the immune response,” this version conveys the illogical meaning that gluten triggers an immune response similar to the immune response of the responses triggers by pathogens.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E)</b> Wheat protein, often referred to as gluten, can <b>disrupt the sleep of those who are intolerant to it by triggering an immune response like the one triggered by pathogens, such as
</b> viruses, yeast, and bacteria, all of which have the ability to disrupt sleep.



This version clearly indicates that gluten triggers an immune response in people intolerant to gluten and correctly uses “such as” in introducing a list of examples of pathogens.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

Let’s now discuss “like” and “as.”
