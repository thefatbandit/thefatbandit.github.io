## Lesson Content
Just as people often misuse the words “like” and “such as,” they also often use the word “like” when the word “as” is needed.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The teacher acts like she were a drill sergeant.

While “as” is a versatile word that can be used as a preposition or a conjunction, the comparison word “like” can be used only as a preposition.

So, the above example does not make sense, since, in the above example, the object of “like” is a clause, “she were a drill sergeant.”

A corrected version is:

> The teacher acts <strong>as though</strong> she were a drill sergeant.

Now, we could change things a bit and use the preposition “like” along with a noun that serves as the object of “like”:

> The teacher acts <strong>like </strong>a drill sergeant.

Notice that, in the above example, the object of “like” is the noun phrase “a drill sergeant.” So, the use of “like” makes sense.

When “like” is used in the way in which it is used in the above example, “like” means, essentially, “in the manner of.” So, the above example expresses, “The teacher acts in the manner of a drill sergeant.”

Here’s another example in which “like” is used to convey “in the manner of”:

> The normally shy student was acting <strong>like </strong>an extrovert.

In the above example, “an extrovert” is the object of the preposition “like,” and “like” conveys the meaning “in the manner of.” So, the sentence conveys the following meaning:

> The normally shy student was acting <strong>in the manner of</strong> an extrovert.

We could also say:

> The normally shy student’s actions were <strong>like </strong>an extrovert’s.

Notice that, in the above example, “like” is appropriately used to compare “actions,” a noun, with “an extrovert’s” actions.

> The normally shy student’s <strong>actions </strong>were like <strong>those</strong> of an extrovert.

Here actions (of the shy student) are compared with actions (of an extrovert).

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

The word “like” can be used only in comparisons of nouns or pronouns. It cannot be used to connect verbs or clauses. In a comparison involving two clauses, “as” must be used.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s consider another example:

> The judge is acting <strong>as though</strong> she would prefer to be somewhere else today.

Notice that, in the sentence above, “as though” is used to connect the two clauses.

We also could follow “as” with “if,” as seen here:

> The judge is acting <strong>as if</strong> she would prefer to be somewhere else today.

What about the following?

> Samantha and Maria, like their sister Natalia, are away at college.

Notice that we are comparing nouns (“Samantha,” “Maria,” and “Natalia”), so “like” is the proper comparison word.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Just like eating a vegan diet is good for the body, drinking organic coffee regularly is good for the mind.

This sentence is incorrect because “like” is used in comparing two clauses.

> Just <strong>as </strong>eating a vegan diet is good for the body, drinking organic coffee regularly is good for the mind.

What about the following?

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Cosmologist Stephen Hawking revolutionized our understanding of time and space, like Albert Einstein and Max Plank had before him.

Notice that the comparison involves two clauses: “Stephen Hawking revolutionized our understanding of time and space” and “Albert Einstein and Max Plank had before him.” Because two clauses are compared, we need to use “as.” A correct version is:

> Cosmologist Stephen Hawking revolutionized our understanding of time and space, <strong>as </strong>Albert Einstein and Max Plank had before him.

A final thought for this section: it’s important to be aware that either of the following sentences would be considered correct on the GMAT:

> Like his father, Amaury drives race cars.

> As his father does, Amaury drives race cars.

We can directly compare Amaury with his father, or we can compare what they do.

##  Sentence Correction Example 15  

Having merely voted to repeal deleterious legislation that she had previously voted in favor of, the legislator was carrying on <u>as if she were</u> a savior of the people.

1. [x] (A) as if she were
2. [ ] (B) like she was
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) like she were
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) as though she was
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) as if she was
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



Since the sentence expresses a comparison of two ideas expressed by clauses, using “like” is incorrect and using “as though” or “as if” makes sense.


Also, since the sentence is meant to convey that the legislator’s being a savior of the people is not an event that may actually have occurred but rather a hypothetical situation, the use of the subjunctive “she were” makes sense. If you didn’t catch that the subjunctive “were” works better than the indicative “was,” you might want to review the discussion of the use of the subjunctive in the Verb Tense, Mood, and Voice chapter.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(A)</b> Having merely voted to repeal deleterious legislation that she had previously voted in favor of, the legislator was carrying on <b>as if she were
</b> a savior of the people.



This version correctly uses “as if” in expressing a comparison of two ideas expressed by two clauses.


Also, the use of the subjunctive “she were” to indicate that the legislator’s being a savior of the people is a hypothetical situation makes sense.


> <b>(B)</b> Having merely voted to repeal deleterious legislation that she had previously voted in favor of, the legislator was carrying on <b>like she was
</b> a savior of the people.



This version incorrectly uses “like” in expressing a comparison of two ideas expressed by two clauses, “she was carrying on” and “she was ….”


Also, the use of the indicative “she was” does not make sense, because the point to be made is not that the legislator may have actually been a savior of the people but, rather, that the legislator’s being a savior is a hypothetical situation, and so, the use of the subjunctive “were,” rather than the indicative “was,” is necessary.


> <b>(C)</b> Having merely voted to repeal deleterious legislation that she had previously voted in favor of, the legislator was carrying on <b>like she were
</b> a savior of the people.



This version incorrectly uses “like” in expressing a comparison of two ideas expressed by clauses, “she was carrying on” and “she was ….”


> <b>(D)</b> Having merely voted to repeal deleterious legislation that she had previously voted in favor of, the legislator was carrying on <b>as though she was
</b> a savior of the people.



While this version correctly uses “as though” in expressing a comparison of two ideas expressed by two clauses, the use of the indicative “she was” does not make sense, because the point to be made is not that the legislator may have actually been a savior of the people but, rather, that the legislator’s being a savior of the people is a hypothetical situation, and so, the use of the subjunctive “were,” rather than the indicative “was,” is necessary.


> <b>(E)</b> Having merely voted to repeal deleterious legislation that she had previously voted in favor of, the legislator was carrying on <b>as if she was
</b> a savior of the people.



While this version correctly uses “as if” in expressing a comparison of two ideas expressed by two clauses, the use of the indicative “she was” does not make sense, because the point to be made is not that the legislator may have actually been a savior of the people but, rather, that the legislator’s being a savior of the people is a hypothetical situation, and so, the use of the subjunctive “were,” rather than the indicative “was,” is necessary.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

##  Sentence Correction Example 16  

The fourteen-year-old girl playing bass with the band that was playing in the <u>park; she was slapping the strings as would a veteran with decades of training and experience</u>.

1. [ ] (A) park; she was slapping the strings as would a veteran with decades of training and experience
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) park was slapping the strings like a veteran with decades of training and experience
3. [ ] (C) park, slapping the strings as if she were a veteran with decades of training and experience
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) park was slapping the strings like a veteran with decades of training and experience would
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) park was slapping the strings as a veteran with decades of training and experience
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



> <b>(A)</b> The fourteen-year-old girl playing bass with the band that was playing in the <b>park; she was slapping the strings as would a veteran with decades of training and experience
</b>.



Using “as” to connect the clause “she was slapping …” with the inverted clause “would a veteran …” is logical. However, this version is incorrect because it uses a semicolon to connect a phrase, “the fourteen-year-old girl playing …,” with a clause, “she was slapping ….” A semicolon can be used to connect a clause with a clause, but not to connect a phrase with a clause.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B)</b> The fourteen-year-old girl playing bass with the band that was playing in the <b>park was slapping the strings like a veteran with decades of training and experience
</b>.



This version correctly uses “like” to convey that the fourteen-year-old girl was slapping the strings in the manner of a veteran.


> <b>(C)</b> The fourteen-year-old girl playing bass with the band that was playing in the <b>park, slapping the strings as if she were a veteran with decades of training and experience
</b>.



While using “as if” to connect a clause ending with “slapping the strings” with the clause “she were a veteran …” would make sense, since there is no independent clause before “as if,” this version is composed of merely “the fourteen-year-old girl” followed by an extended modifier, “playing the bass …,” and, therefore, is a fragment rather than a complete sentence.


> <b>(D)</b> The fourteen-year-old girl playing bass with the band that was playing in the <b>park was slapping the strings like a veteran with decades of training and experience would
</b>.



This version incorrectly uses “like” to connect a clause, “the fourteen-year-old girl … was slapping the strings,” with a clause, “a veteran … would.”


> <b>(E)</b> The fourteen-year-old girl playing bass with the band that was playing in the <b>park was slapping the strings as a veteran with decades of training and experience
</b>.



This version conveys the illogical meaning that, as a veteran with decades of training and experience, the fourteen-year-old girl was slapping the strings. A fourteen-year-old girl would not be doing something as a veteran with decades of experience, since she has not lived for decades.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
