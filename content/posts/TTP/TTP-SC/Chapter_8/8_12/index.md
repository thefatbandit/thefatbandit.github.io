## Lesson Content
Some nouns are countable, meaning that they have plural forms and that their plural forms name quantities made up of countable items.

Other nouns are uncountable, meaning that quantities named by these nouns are not made up of countable items.

One reason why understanding the distinction between countable and uncountable nouns is useful in GMAT Sentence Correction is that the words used in expressing comparisons involving quantities, called “quantity words,” vary along with the types of quantities compared.

So, let’s discuss the use of quantity words in comparisons, starting with the quantity words “fewer” and “less.”

<strong><u>“Fewer” versus “Less”</u></strong>

“Fewer” modifies a countable noun, whereas “less” modifies an uncountable noun.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: If you relax your face, you will have less wrinkles than you have now.

Notice that “wrinkles” is a countable noun.

> If you relax your face, you will have <strong>fewer</strong> wrinkles than you have now.

“Fewer” is correctly used with the countable noun, “wrinkles.”

> <u>Incorrect</u>: You have less apples than John has.

“Apples” is a countable noun, so we cannot use “less.”

> You have <strong>fewer</strong> apples than John has.

We use “fewer” with the countable noun “apples.”

> <u>Incorrect</u>: You have fewer juice than John has.

We see that “juice” is uncountable, so we cannot use “fewer.”

> You have <strong>less</strong> juice than John has.

Now the sentence makes sense, with “less” used with “juice.”

What about the following more complicated sentence?

> <u>Incorrect</u>: As she prepares for the marathon, Alessandra is consuming less pasta and cookies than she normally consumes.

Many people would see that sentence as correct.

A possible correction is:

> As she prepares for the marathon, Alessandra is consuming <strong>less pasta</strong> and <strong>fewer cookies</strong> than she normally consumes.

Now, “less” is used with the uncountable noun “pasta,” and “fewer” is used with the countable noun “cookies.”

We must always be on the lookout for such small but important mistakes in Sentence Correction questions.

What about the following?

> <u>Incorrect</u>: As a result of its waste reduction program, the restaurant now discards much less uneaten food and empty containers than it discarded in the past.

Notice that we have "much less" used with both "uneaten food" and "empty containers.” We see that “uneaten food” is uncountable, so using “much less” with “uneaten food” is perfect.

A corrected sentence is:

> As a result of its waste reduction program, the restaurant now discards <strong>much less</strong> uneaten food and <strong>far fewer</strong> empty containers than it discarded in the past.

Let’s look at one more example.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: With each passing year, the minimum wage provides families with less security and opportunity, a trend that may result in less educational and economic opportunities for their children later in life.

Pay close attention to the words in the sentence above.

> With each passing year, the minimum wage provides families with <strong>less security and opportunity</strong>, a trend that may result in <strong>fewer</strong> <strong>educational and economic opportunities</strong> for their children later in life.

##  Sentence Correction Example 17  

Because of changing tastes, in recent years, the bakery has sold fewer <u>pies and cakes than it sold in the past, a development negatively affecting profits</u>.

1. [x] (A) pies and cakes than it sold in the past, a development negatively affecting profits
2. [ ] (B) pies and cakes than they had sold in the past, negatively affecting profits as a development
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) pie and cake than it had sold in the past, which has been a development negatively affecting profits
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) pies and cakes than were sold in the past, and it has negatively affected profits as a development
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) pie and cake than was sold in the past, which had been a development negatively affecting profits
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

##### Question Level: easy



> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(A)</b> Because of changing tastes, in recent years, the bakery has sold fewer <b>pies and cakes than it sold in the past, a development negatively affecting profits
</b>.



“Fewer” is correctly used to modify the countable nouns “pies” and “cakes,” the singular “it” correctly refers to the singular “bakery,” and the closing abstract appositive “a development …” logically modifies the entire preceding clause.


> <b>(B)</b> Because of changing tastes, in recent years, the bakery has sold fewer <b>pies and cakes than they had sold in the past, negatively affecting profits as a development
</b>.



The plural “they” does not agree with what it seems to refer to, which is the singular “bakery.”


Also, the use of the past perfect “had sold,” while not 100 percent incorrect, does not make sense, since the phrase “in the past” clearly indicates the sequence of events, making the use of the past perfect unnecessary and somewhat illogical. The point to be made is not that bakery “had sold” more pies and cakes in the past. The point to be made is that the bakery “sold” more pies and cakes in the past.


Also, the expression “negatively affecting profits as a development” implies that either the bakery or the profits were a development, neither of which implications makes sense.


> <b>(C)</b> Because of changing tastes, in recent years, the bakery has sold fewer <b>pie and cake than it had sold in the past, which has been a development negatively affecting profits
</b>.



The combination of the non-underlined portion of the sentence with this answer choice creates “the bakery has sold fewer pie and cake.” Since “pie” and “cake” are uncountable nouns, the use of “fewer” to modify these nouns is incorrect.


Also, the use of the past perfect “had sold,” while not 100 percent incorrect, does not make sense, since the phrase “in the past” clearly indicates the sequence of events, making the use of the past perfect unnecessary and somewhat illogical. The point to be made is not that bakery “had sold” more pies and cakes in the past. The point to be made is that the bakery “sold” more pies and cakes in the past.


Also, the only possible noun to which “which” could refer is “past,” but the past would not be a development negatively affecting profits. Alternatively, “which” could refer to the entire idea expressed by the preceding clause, but the GMAT does not prefer this type of reference, especially if there is a noun that directly precedes “which” to which “which” could refer, as “past” does in this case.


> <b>(D)</b> Because of changing tastes, in recent years, the bakery has sold fewer <b>pies and cakes than were sold in the past, and it has negatively affected profits as a development
</b>.



The wording “fewer pies and cakes than were sold in the past” does not clearly connect the pies and cakes mentioned to the bakery mentioned. Pies and cakes that “were sold in the past” could have been sold by any bakery, not just the one that is the subject of the sentence.


Also, “it” has no logical referent. While “it” could refer to “bakery,” that the bakery would have “negatively affected profits” by selling fewer pies and cakes does not quite make sense. Also, if “it” refers to “bakery,” then the sentence conveys the nonsensical meaning that the bakery negatively affected profits “as a development,” meaning that the bakery was “a development.”


> <b>(E)</b> Because of changing tastes, in recent years, the bakery has sold fewer <b>pie and cake than was sold in the past, which had been a development negatively affecting profits
</b>.



The combination of the non-underlined portion of the sentence with this answer choice creates “the bakery has sold fewer pie and cake.” Since “pie” and “cake” are uncountable nouns, the use of “fewer” to modify these nouns is incorrect.


Also, the only possible noun to which “which” could refer is “past,” but the past would not be a development negatively affecting profits. Alternatively, “which” could refer to the entire idea expressed by the preceding clause, but the GMAT does not prefer this type of reference, especially if there is a noun that directly precedes “which” to which “which” could refer, as “past” does in this case.


Also, the use of the past perfect “had been a development” does not make sense, since the use of the past perfect indicates that the event described occurred before other past events mentioned in the sentence, a sequence of events that conflicts with what the rest of the sentence expresses, which is that the development described is ongoing in the present.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

Let’s now discuss “many” and “much.”

<u>“<strong>Many” versus “Much”</strong></u>

Although “many” and “much” both modify quantities, we use “much” with uncountable nouns and “many” with countable nouns.

For example:

> She has much love in her heart.

> How much time is remaining in the movie?

> Sam has much more interest in mountain climbing than Sandeep has.

Since “love,” “time,” and “interest” are uncountable nouns, we use “much” when modifying them.

> There are many ways to get to Moscow from Seoul.

> How many countries have you visited?

> Although elephants were once common in Africa, there aren’t many left.

Since, “ways,” “countries,” and “elephants” are countable nouns, we use “many” to modify or refer to them.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Use “much” with uncountable nouns and “many” with countable nouns.
{{< /alert >}}

Consider the following more difficult example sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The high waters caused by the storm had deposited so much sand and sea shells on the property that the house appeared to be standing in the middle of a beach.

Notice that we have “so much” used with “sand” and with “sea shells.” Using “so much” with “sand” is fine because “sand” is uncountable.

> The high waters caused by the storm had deposited <strong>so much</strong> sand and <strong>so many</strong> sea shells on the property that the house appeared to be standing in the middle of a beach.

##  Sentence Correction Example 18  

The campaign director estimated that over 3000 people were in attendance at the rally, <u>which was many more than they had</u> expected.

1. [ ] (A) which was many more than they had
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) much more than was
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) many more as
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) many more than had been
5. [ ] (E) much more people than had been
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: medium



Since the sentence is about 3000 people, which is a countable quantity, the correct word to use is “many.”


> <b>(A)</b> The campaign director estimated that over 3000 people were in attendance at the rally, <b>which was many more than they had
</b> expected.



While this version correctly uses “many,” “which” has no logical referent, since a rally would not be “many more than they had expected.”


Also, “they” has no referent within the sentence, though that it does not is not a strong reason to eliminate this choice, because “they” could refer to people mentioned in a sentence previous to this one, unlike “which,” which must refer to something mentioned within the sentence in which “which” appears.


> <b>(B)</b> The campaign director estimated that over 3000 people were in attendance at the rally, <b>much more than was
</b> expected.



“Much” is incorrectly used to refer to the countable “3000 people.”


Also, the simple past “was expected” is not as good as the past perfect “had been expected,” because, presumably, the people were expected to attend before the campaign director estimated how many attended. So, since the campaign director “estimated” in the past, it makes sense to use the past perfect “had been expected” to indicate that the people were expected to attend before the campaign director estimated how many attended.


> <b>(C)</b> The campaign director estimated that over 3000 people were in attendance at the rally, <b>many more as
</b> expected.



Because this version uses “as” rather than “than” with “many more,” the comparison expressed by this version is not complete, leaving the reader wondering “Many more than what?”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D)</b> The campaign director estimated that over 3000 people were in attendance at the rally, <b>many more than had been
</b> expected.



This version correctly uses “many” to refer to the countable “3000 people.”


Also, it uses the past perfect “had been expected” along with the simple past “estimated” to indicate that the people were expected before the campaign director estimated.


> <b>(E)</b> The campaign director estimated that over 3000 people were in attendance at the rally, <b>much more people than had been
</b> expected.



This choice could be tempting because the repetition of the word “people” in the final phrase of the sentence could appear to add clarity. However, the repetition of “people” is unnecessary, because “many more” is understood to mean “many more people,” and, meanwhile, the use of “much” with the countable “people” is incorrect.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
