## Lesson Content
Time, money, and weight may seem to be countable.

For instance, we know that we would use “less” with “water,” as does the following example:

> The tank currently contains less than two gallons of water.

Money works similarly, as we can see in the following example:

> The account currently holds less than two dollars.

Time works similarly, as we can see in the following example:

> The sun will set less than five hours from now.

One would not say:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The sun will set fewer than five hours from now.

One would say:

> Ten minutes is generally too much time to spend on one quant question when one is taking the GMAT.

One would not say:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Ten minutes are generally too many time to spend on one quant question when one is taking the GMAT.

Weight is also uncountable, and so, both of the following are correct:

> Even though Michael consumes over 4000 calories per day, because he is so active, he has gained less than five pounds.

> Eight kilograms is not much more than the weight of an average sized cat.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Nouns that name quantities of time, money, or weight are treated as uncountable nouns.
{{< /alert >}}

Now, if a sentence is about the units of time, money, or weight themselves, the nouns are countable.

> How many minutes are left?

The above sentence is correct because it’s about the number of minutes themselves.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: How much minutes is left?

Similarly if a sentence is about units of money themselves, the noun naming the units is a countable noun, as in the following example:

> Many Euros have been spent on the repairs of the classic cathedral.

The above sentence is correct because it’s about the number of Euros themselves.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Much Euros has been spent on the repairs of the classic cathedral.

On the other hand, the following, which is about a quantity of money rather than about the units of money themselves is correct:

> Some people felt that 2 billion Euros was too much money to spend on rebuilding a cathedral.

We can see that the above sentence is not about the Euros themselves, but about a quantity of money, which is considered uncountable.

The following is correct as well:

> Some people felt that 2 billion Euros was too much to spend on rebuilding a cathedral.

Even though the word “money” is not included in the above version, the sentence is understood to convey that 2 billion Euros is too much money to spend.

    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) less than 100 pounds but armed with confidence, Jerrie Mock became the first woman to fly around the world solo, returning to her hometown, Columbus Ohio, less than 30 days after she

##### Question Level: hard



Since weight and time are treated as if they are uncountable, any version of this sentence that uses “fewer” with “100 pounds,” which expresses Jerrie Mock’s weight, or uses “fewer” with “30 days,” which expresses a quantity of time, is probably incorrect. Further, and more importantly, any version that uses “less” with “100 pounds” or with “30 days” is certainly not incorrect because it uses “less.”


> <b>(A)</b> Weighing <b>fewer than 100 pounds though armed with confidence, Jerrie Mock became the first woman flying around the world solo and returning to her hometown, Columbus Ohio, fewer than 30 days after she had
</b> departed.



The use of “fewer” with “100 pounds” and with “30 days” does not make sense, because, in this sentence, “100 pounds” and “30 days” are used to express quantities of weight and time, both of which are considered uncountable. “Less” should be used instead.


Also, this version suggests the illogical idea that Jerrie Mock weighed less than 100 pounds even though she was armed with confidence, as if being armed with confidence would normally cause someone to weigh more than 100 pounds.


Also, the use of the continuous “flying” and “returning” results in the sentence’s conveying the illogical meaning that Jerry Mock became the first woman continuously flying around the world solo and returning to Jerry Mock’s hometown, as if she did so on an ongoing or nonstop basis.


Also, the use of the past perfect “had departed” is not necessary and somewhat illogical, because the sequence of events is indicated via the use of “after.” So, the use of the simple past “departed” would make more sense.


> <b>(B)</b> Weighing <b>less than 100 pounds, Jerrie Mock was armed with confidence and became the first woman flying around the world solo, returning to her hometown, Columbus Ohio, fewer than 30 days after she
</b> departed.



The use of “fewer” with “30 days” does not make sense, because, in this sentence, “30 days” is used to express a quantity of time, which is considered uncountable. “Less” should be used instead.


Also, this version seems to suggest that Jerrie Mock’s weighing less than 100 pounds is somehow connected to her being armed with confidence and becoming the first woman “flying around the world solo,” perhaps expressing that Jerry Mock was armed with confidence and flew around the world solo as a result of her weighing less than 100 pounds.


Also, the use of the continuous “flying” results in the sentence’s expressing the illogical meaning that Jerry Mock became the first woman continuously flying around the world solo, as if she did so on an ongoing or nonstop basis. This meaning is in conflict with the fact stated by the sentence that she returned to her hometown after 30 days, a fact that indicates that actually she flew around the world just once, not continuously.


> <b>(C)</b> Weighing <b>less than 100 pounds but armed with confidence, Jerrie Mock became the first woman to fly around the world solo, by returning to her hometown, Columbus Ohio, in less than 30 days after she had
</b> departed.



This version conveys the nonsensical meaning that by merely returning to her hometown, Jerrie Mock became the first woman to fly around the world solo.


Also, “in less than 30 days after she had departed” does not make sense. This modifier should be either “in less than 30 days,” which would express how long she took, or “less than 30 days after she had departed,” which would express when she returned. As written, the modifier seems to attempt to express both of those meanings at the same time, with the result that does not express any logical meaning.


Also, the use of the past perfect “had departed” is not necessary and somewhat illogical, because the sequence of events is indicated via the use of “after.” So, the use of the simple past “departed” would make more sense.


> <b>(D)</b> Weighing <b>fewer than 100 pounds though armed with confidence, Jerrie Mock became the first woman to fly around the world solo and return to her hometown, Columbus Ohio, by fewer than 30 days after she
</b> departed.



The use of “fewer” with “100 pounds” and with “30 days” does not make sense, because, in this sentence, “100 pounds” and “30 days” are used to express quantities of weight and time, both of which are considered uncountable. “Less” should be used instead.


Also, this version suggests the illogical idea that Jerrie Mock weighed less than 100 pounds even though she was armed with confidence, as if being armed with confidence would normally cause someone to weigh more than 100 pounds.


Also, this version suggests the illogical idea that the point to be made is that Jerrie Mock was not merely the first woman to fly around the world solo, but rather the first woman to fly around the world solo and return to Jerry Mock’s hometown “fewer than” 30 days after she departed. This meaning seems unlikely to be the one meant to be expressed by the author.


Also, the use of “by” is somewhat illogical. “By” is used to indicate a point in time, but the point to be made in this case is not that Jerry Mock returned to her hometown by a point in time. The point to be made is that she returned to her hometown within a period of time “fewer than” 30 days.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E)</b> Weighing <b>less than 100 pounds but armed with confidence, Jerrie Mock became the first woman to fly around the world solo, returning to her hometown, Columbus Ohio, less than 30 days after she
</b> departed.



This version correctly uses “less” with “100 pounds,” which expresses a weight, and with “30 days,” which expresses a quantity of time.


Also, “weighing less than 100 pounds but armed with confidence” expresses a logical contrast between Jerrie Mock’s weight and her level of confidence.


Also, this version clearly expresses that Jerrie Mock became the first woman to fly around the world solo, and that, in the process of doing so, she returned to her hometown less than 30 days after she left.


Notice that, even though this version uses the simple past “she departed” rather than the past perfect “she had departed,” the sequence of events is made clear via the use of “after.”


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Sentence Correction Example 22  

Unfortunately, a decline in the quality of people’s self care has resulted in <u>many more dollars spent by them on healthcare than were</u> spent in past years.

1. [ ] (A) many more dollars spent by them on healthcare than were
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) they spent much more dollars on healthcare than they
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) much more dollars that people spent on healthcare than were
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) people’s spending on healthcare many more dollars than they
5. [ ] (E) much more dollars being spent on healthcare than was
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



While “money” is an uncountable noun, in this case, the sentence is about dollars themselves, which are countable. So, the correct word to use is not “much” but “many.”


> <b>(A)</b> Unfortunately, a decline in the quality of people’s self care has resulted in <b>many more dollars spent by them on healthcare than were
</b> spent in past years.



While this version correctly uses “many” with the countable “dollars,” the meaning conveyed is not logical. The sentence conveys not that a decline in the quality of self care resulted in an increase in the number of dollars spent on healthcare but rather that the decline resulted in many more of the dollars spent on healthcare. Then, the dollars that somehow resulted from the decline are illogically compared with dollars that were spent in past years. It’s actually a bit challenging to say what this version conveys, because, while this version may “sound correct,” it’s actually a virtually meaningless jumble of words.


> <b>(B)</b> Unfortunately, a decline in the quality of people’s self care has resulted in <b>they spent much more dollars on healthcare than they
</b> spent in past years.



This version incorrectly uses “much” with the countable “dollars.”


Also, while “resulted in” should be followed by an object of the preposition “in,” which object has to be a noun or pronoun or something that acts as a noun or pronoun does, in this case “resulted in” is illogically followed by a clause, “they spent ….”


Of course, since unlike the virtually meaningless versions (A) and (C), this version does express a logical comparison, we have to be careful not to be tempted into choosing this version even though it has the flaws described above.


> <b>(C)</b> Unfortunately, a decline in the quality of people’s self care has resulted in <b>much more dollars that people spent on healthcare than were
</b> spent in past years.



This version incorrectly uses “much” with the countable “dollars.”


Also, like the version created via the use of (A), this version conveys the nonsensical meaning, that a decline in the quality of people’s self care resulted in dollars. Also the latter part of this version appears to express a comparison but, actually, is a virtually meaningless jumble of comparison words.


Notice also that the use of the plural “were,” the use of which implies that “dollars” is plural and countable, is in conflict with the use of “much,” the use of which implies that “dollars” is a single uncountable entity.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D)</b> Unfortunately, a decline in the quality of people’s self care has resulted in <b>people’s spending on healthcare many more dollars than they
</b> spent in past years.



This version correctly uses “many” with the countable “dollars.


Also, in this version, “resulted in” is followed by something that can logically serve as the object of “in”: “people’s spending.”


Also, unlike the versions created via the use of (A) and (C), this version expresses a logical comparison, “many more dollars than they spent in past years.”


Of course, for the purpose of making the question challenging, this correct version is written in a way such that this version may at first “sound” less correct than other versions.


(While many people feel that a personal pronoun, such as “they,” should not have as its referent a noun in the possessive form, such as “people's,” in GMAT Sentence Correction questions, such references have appeared in correct answers, as “they” referring to “people's” does in this correct answer.)


> <b>(E)</b> Unfortunately, a decline in the quality of people’s self care has resulted in <b>much more dollars being spent on healthcare than was
</b> spent in past years.



This version’s use of “much” and “was” with the countable and plural “dollars” is clearly incorrect.


Also, while, unlike the versions created via the use of (A) and (C), this version does a halfway decent job of expressing a logical meaning, it still could be read as conveying the illogical meaning that a decline in the quality of people’s self care resulted in dollars.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)
