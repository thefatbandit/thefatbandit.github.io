## Lesson Content
“Greater than” is used in comparing the sizes, levels, degrees, magnitudes, or extents of things.

“More … than” is not synonymous with “larger than” or “higher than.” “More” can be used in multiple ways, including as a determiner that directly modifies what follows it.

Consider the following example:

> Her dislike of gambling is greater than her dislike of exercise.

Notice that, in this case, the magnitudes of her dislikes are compared.

The following is incorrect:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Her dislike of gambling is more than her dislike of exercise.

We wouldn’t use “more than” in this context, because “more than” is not synonymous with “larger than.” So, the above sentence does not make sense.

On the other hand, the following example correctly uses “more” as a determiner that modifies “space.”

> Sara has more space than Olivia has.

Notice that, in this case, the sentence conveys that both Sara and Olivia have space and indicates that the quantity of space had by Sara is larger than the quantity had by Olivia by using the determiner “more” to modify the space that Sara has.

The following would be incorrect:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Sara has greater space than Olivia has.

Of course, the above sentence does not make sense.

Now, the following is correct:

> The amount of space that Sara has is <strong>greater than</strong> the amount that Olivia has.

Why is the above example correct? Because it correctly uses “greater than” in comparing the sizes of two amounts.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The amount of space that Sara has is <strong>more than</strong> the amount that Olivia has.

Since “more than” is not synonymous with “larger than,” the above sentence does not make sense.

Notice, we can substitute “larger than” for “greater than” and still have a logical sentence:

> The amount of space that Sara has is <strong>larger than</strong> the amount that Olivia has.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

“Greater than” is used in comparing the sizes, levels, degrees, magnitudes, or extents of things.
{{< /alert >}}

In Sentence Correction questions, we'll sometimes see statistics compared.

For example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The standard deviation of the test scores of the students in group A was more than that of the test scores of the students in group B.

Notice that the sentence is comparing the sizes of the standard deviations, which are statistics, so we cannot use “more than.” A correct version is:

> The standard deviation of the test scores of the students in group A was <strong>greater than</strong> that of the test scores of the students in group B.

Notice, we could use “larger than” where “greater than” is and have a logical sentence:

> The standard deviation of the test scores of the students in group A was <strong>larger than</strong> that of the test scores of the students in group B.

Here’s another example that expresses a comparison of statistics:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: In Tribeca, the average selling price for a new home is more than the average selling price in the Dumbo area of Brooklyn.

We see that the level of the “average selling price” in one area is compared with the level of “average selling price” in a different area.

The following is a possible correction:

> In Tribeca, the average selling price for a new home is <strong>greater than</strong> the average selling price in the Dumbo area of Brooklyn.

It is also correct to say the following:

> In Tribeca, the average selling price for a new home is <strong>higher than</strong> the average selling price in the Dumbo area of Brooklyn.

Of course, we need to be sure that whenever a comparison is made via the use of “greater than” or “more than,” that comparison is done properly.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The standard deviation of the test scores of the students in group A was greater than the students in group B.

We see that the comparison is broken.

What about the following?

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The standard deviation of the test scores of the students in group A was greater than those of group B.

We see that “the standard deviation of the test scores of the students in group A” is being compared with “those.” Presumably, “those” refers to the students in group B or the test scores of the students.

A corrected version of the sentence could be:

> The standard deviation of the test scores of the students in group A was greater than <strong>that </strong>of the test scores of the students in group B.

or

> The standard deviation of the test scores of the students in group A was greater than <strong>the standard deviation of the test scores</strong> of the students in group B.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When comparing statistics, use “greater than”; do not use “more than.”
{{< /alert >}}
