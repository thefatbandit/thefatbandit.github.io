## Lesson Content
It’s common for people to perceive “whereas” and “unlike” as somewhat synonymous or as functioning in similar ways in sentences.

Now, we’ve already learned that, when we see a comparison that employs “unlike,” the rules governing such a comparison are fairly strict and that the two elements related by “unlike” have to be rather similar.

> Unlike Stacy Ann, Jovan enjoys photography.

Notice that two people named by proper nouns, “Stacy Ann” and “Jovan” are compared.

> Unlike avocados, oranges do not contain significant quantities of omega-3 fatty acids.

In this case, two fruits named by nouns, “avocados” and “oranges,” are compared.

The above comparisons make sense because the items compared in each case are similar to each other.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Unlike computers made today, using a computer made fifty years ago required a fair amount of technical knowledge.

This last example illogically compares things that are quite different from each other: computers and using a computer.

So, we have been conditioned to look for similarity of items compared when we evaluate the logic of comparisons.

This conditioning can be problematic when we see words such as “whereas,” “while,” or “although” in sentences that express contrasts, because the way in which such words function is different from the way in which “unlike” functions, and, as a result, the type of similarity required when “unlike” is used is not required when words such as “whereas,” “while,” or “although” are used.

Let’s consider “whereas.” “Whereas” means “in contrast with the fact that” and is used to connect clauses, and clauses connected by “whereas” don’t have to be particularly similar to each other.

> Whereas you will go to Venice this fall, I will go to Rio de Janeiro.

The clauses in the above example are similar to each other.

> Whereas some people enjoy jewelry, jewelry is considered superfluous by others.

> Whereas Lucas loves cookies, the likelihood that cookies will be popular with the other guests is low.

Notice that the clauses connected by “whereas” in the above two example sentences are quite different from each other.

So, we can see that, unlike sentence elements connected by “unlike,” clauses connected by “whereas” don’t have to be particularly similar to each other.

💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Unlike sentence elements connected by “unlike,” clauses connected by “whereas” don’t have to be particularly similar to each other.
{{< /alert >}}

We see a similar phenomenon with the words “while” and “although.” Consider the following examples:

> While roses are red, violets are blue.

> While Edgar spends his time writing novels, Elizabeth’s days are spent writing software.

> While we love the mountains, swimming in the ocean is a great way to spend a summer afternoon.

> Although you love ice cream, I love cake.

> Although she was running on the beach, no sand got into her hair.

> Although the Earth is clearly round, some people believe it to be flat.

As is the case with clauses connected by “whereas,” while, in some sentences, clauses connected by “while” or “although” are similar to each other, in some well-constructed sentences, clauses connected by “while” or “although” are quite different from each other.

##  Sentence Correction Example 27  

<u>Even though toothed whales have conical teeth and use them for catching fish or squid, baleen whales have fringe-like structures that they use for retaining the krill and plankton upon which they feed called plates of baleen.</u>

1. [ ] (A) Even though toothed whales have conical teeth and use them for catching fish or squid, baleen whales have fringe-like structures that they use for retaining the krill and plankton upon which they feed called plates of baleen.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)

    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)

    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) Whereas toothed whales have conical teeth that they use to catch fish or squid, plates of baleen, a fringe-like structure, are used by baleen whales to retain the krill and plankton upon which they feed.

##### Question Level: medium



Correctly answering this question requires analyzing the logic of the comparisons and contrasts expressed by the versions and avoiding being tempted into choosing an illogical choice by wording that is structured in a parallel fashion that “sounds right.”


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Even though toothed whales have conical teeth and use them for catching fish or squid, baleen whales have fringe-like structures that they use for retaining the krill and plankton upon which they feed called plates of baleen.
</b>


This choice may be tempting, because the first clause of this version begins with “toothed whales have” and the second clause begins with “baleen whales have.” So, this choice may appear to be correct at first.


However, careful analysis reveals that the use of “even though” results in the sentence’s suggesting the illogical idea that baleen whales have fringe-like structures even though toothed whales have teeth, as if toothed whales’ having teeth would somehow be expected to keep baleen whales from having fringe-like structures.


Also, the placement of “plates of baleen” at the end of the sentence after “krill and plankton upon which they feed” results in the sentence’s seeming to convey the nonsensical meaning that krill and plankton are called plates of baleen.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>Toothed whales have conical teeth, and they are used for catching fish or squid, while, at the same time, baleen whales have plates of baleen, a fringe-like structure, and they are used for retaining the krill and plankton upon which they feed.
</b>


This choice may be tempting, because the clauses in this version are structured in a rather parallel fashion. So, this choice may appear to be correct at first. However, this version does not effectively convey a logical meaning.


“And they are used for catching fish or squid” does not clearly connect the use of the teeth to the toothed whales. It merely describes what the teeth are used for without indicating that it’s the toothed whales that use them in that way.


“They” in “they are used for catching” does not clearly refer to “teeth,” and could refer to “toothed whales.”


The use of the conjunction “while” would be sufficient. The use of “at the same time” is redundant and results in the sentence’s seeming to suggest that the point to be made is that toothed whales have teeth and baleen whales have plates of baleen at the same time, as if the timing of the possession of these features matters, an idea unlikely to be the author’s point.


“They are used for retaining the krill and plankton upon which they feed” does not clearly connect the use of the plates of baleen to the baleen whales. It merely describes what the plates of baleen are used for without indicating that it’s the baleen whales that use them in that way.


“They” in “they are used for retaining” does not clearly refer to “plates of baleen,” and could refer to “baleen whales.”


“They” in “upon which they feed” does not clearly refer to “baleen whales” and could refer to “plates of baleen.”


> <b>(C)</b> <b>Unlike toothed whales, which have conical teeth that they use to catch fish or squid, plates of a fringe-like structure called baleen are used by baleen whales to retain the krill and plankton upon which they feed.
</b>


This version presents an illogical contrast between toothed whales and plates of a fringe-like structure called baleen.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>When compared with the conical teeth that toothed whales use to catch fish and squid, plates of baleen are a fringe-like structure used by baleen whales to retain the krill and plankton upon which they feed.
</b>


This version may seem to present a logical comparison of conical teeth and plates of baleen. However, what it actually expresses is not a comparison but, rather, the illogical meaning that plates of baleen are a fringe-like structure used by baleen whales when plates of baleen are compared with conical teeth, as if plates of baleen are not a fringe-like structure when they are not compared with conical teeth.


Also, saying that the plural “plates of baleen are” the singular “a fringe-like structure” does not quite make sense.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E)</b> <b>Whereas toothed whales have conical teeth that they use to catch fish or squid, plates of baleen, a fringe-like structure, are used by baleen whales to retain the krill and plankton upon which they feed.
</b>


This version uses two clauses connected by “whereas” to logically express a contrast. Even though the two clauses of the sentence are structured differently from each other, in that the subject of the first is “toothed whales,” while the subject of the second is “plates of baleen,” the meaning conveyed makes sense.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##### Question Level: hard



This question includes multiple choices that are worded to seem correct but include illogical comparisons or convey illogical or nonsensical meanings. So, correctly answering the question takes using careful analysis of the logic of the comparisons expressed by the choices and of the meanings conveyed by the choices to eliminate the incorrect choices and arrive at the correct answer, which, though worded to sound a little off, uses two clauses connected by “while” to express a logical comparison and conveys meanings that make sense.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Windsurfing is considered physically challenging, involves standing on a board and holding a sail, and is in contrast to kiteboarding, which involves standing on a board and being attached to a kite and can be done in ways to make it
</b> not very physically challenging, though still somewhat dangerous.



Saying simply “windsurfing … is in contrast to kiteboarding,” does not make sense. “In contrast to” is meant to be used as a modifier that modifies an entire idea, as in, “In contrast to windsurfing, kiteboarding is X.” This version instead lists “is in contrast to kiteboarding” as one of the attributes of windsurfing, as if being in contrast to something is an attribute that stands on its own.


Also, the wording “being attached to a kite” seems to convey the illogical meaning that the activity kiteboarding centers on the process of “being attached to a kite.”


Also, the wording “can be done in ways to make it” illogically mixes two concepts. The “ways” mentioned are either ways in which kiteboarding “can be done” or ways “to make it not very physically challenging. However, by using the wording “can be done in ways to make it,” this version seems to say both things at once and conveys neither. Conveying a logical meaning requires wording along the lines of the following, which don’t illogically mix the two ideas:


… kiteboarding can be done in ways such that it is not very physically challenging.


or


… there are ways to make kiteboarding not very physically challenging.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>Whereas windsurfing, which involves standing on a board and holding a sail, is considered to be physically challenging, kiteboarding, which involves standing on a board and attached to a kite, can be done in ways in which it is
</b> not very physically challenging, though still somewhat dangerous.



Since the first clause of this sentence begins with “whereas windsurfing, which involves …” and the second clause begins with “kiteboarding, which involves …,” this version may appear to be correct. However, this version is flawed in multiple ways.


“Considered to be” is not idiomatically correct and not quite logical. The point to be made is that windsurfing is considered X, not that windsurfing is considered to be X.


Also, “which involves standing on a board and attached to a kite” does not make sense no matter how it is read. It could be read as the following list:


which involves:


standing on a board


attached to a kite


“Which involves … attached to a kite” does not make sense.


Alternatively, the clause could be read as the following list:


which involves standing:


on a board


attached to a kite


“Standing … attached to a kite” is not quite logical.


Also, since “it” in “can be done in ways in which <strong>it </strong>is not very physically challenging” refers to “kiteboarding,” the sentence conveys the nonsensical meaning that kiteboarding is not very physically challenging “in” the ways in which it can be done.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>Unlike the way in which windsurfing, which involves standing on a board and holding a sail, is considered physically challenging, there are ways in which kiteboarding, which involves standing on a board while attached to a kite, can be done so that it is
</b> not very physically challenging, though still somewhat dangerous.



Since this version uses “unlike” to connect “the way in which windsurfing” with “there are ways in which kiteboarding,” it may appear to be correct at first. However, the comparison expressed is not logical.


One issue is that “the way” cannot be logically compared with “there are ways.”


Another is that the sentence is about two different types of ways, one of them “the way in which windsurfing is considered” something, and the other “ways in which kiteboarding … can be done.” So, even though both sides of the comparison involve “ways,” they don’t actually involve the same type of items.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D)</b> <b>Windsurfing, which involves standing on a board and holding a sail, is considered physically challenging, while there are ways in which kiteboarding, which involves standing on a board while attached to a kite, can be done such that it is
</b> not very physically challenging, though still somewhat dangerous.



This version may appear to be incorrect, because the two clauses in this version are different from each other, in that the first is about windsurfing, while the second begins with “there are ways”. All the same, the two clauses effectively convey contrasting ideas, and connecting the two clauses via the use of “while” makes sense.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>Windsurfing, involving standing on board while holding a sail, is considered to be physically challenging, unlike ways in which kiteboarding, which involves standing on a board while being attached to a kite, can be done so that it is
</b> not very physically challenging, though still somewhat dangerous.



This version illogically contrasts “windsurfing” with “ways in which kiteboarding … can be done.”


Also, “considered to be” is not idiomatically correct and not quite logical. The point to be made is that windsurfing is considered X, not that windsurfing is considered to be X.


Also, the wording “which involves standing on a board while being attached to a kite” conveys the illogical meaning that the activity kiteboarding centers on the process of “being attached to a kite.”


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

Let’s now discuss avoiding being trapped by incorrect answers that include well written comparisons.
