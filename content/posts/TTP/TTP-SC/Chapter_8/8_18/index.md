## Lesson Content
As we have discussed previously, in order to force test-takers to use logic in choosing among Sentence Correction choices rather than simply choose a choice that “sounds correct,” Sentence Correction question writers often seek to make incorrect choices seem correct and correct choices seem incorrect.

Consider the following two sentence versions:

> <u>Version 1</u>: Having been hunted to near extinction in the wild, there are now more tigers living in captivity in zoos, sanctuaries, and private hands than there are living in the wild.

> <u>Version 2</u>: Wild tigers having been hunted to near extinction, there are now more tigers living in captivity, sanctuaries, and private hands than living in the wild.

A test-taker focused on the comparison part of the sentence may decide that Version 1 is the better version, because Version 1 words the comparison very clearly with its use of “there are more tigers living in … than there are living in,” while Version 2 uses more elliptical, minimal wording “there are now more tigers living in … than living in.”

However, the truth is that both ways of expressing the comparison are fine.

Meanwhile, Version 1 is clearly incorrect, because the opening modifier, “having been hunted …,” is a noun modifier, but there is no noun for it to logically modify.

The takeaway from this example is that we have to be careful not to be fooled into choosing an incorrect choice by the fact that, in expressing a comparison, that incorrect choice uses wording that seems a bit better than the, also acceptable, wording used by the correct answer to the question.

##  Sentence Correction Example 29  

<u>Whereas the paintings of Jackson Pollock are completely non-representational, elements that represent real world objects are included among those of Henry Moore’s sculptures.</u>

1. [x] (A) Whereas the paintings of Jackson Pollock are completely non-representational, elements that represent real world objects are included among those of Henry Moore’s sculptures.
2. [ ] (B) Unlike Jackson Pollock’s completely non-representational paintings, Henry Moore included in his sculptures some elements that represent real world objects.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) In contrast to the elements of Jackson Pollock’s paintings, which are completely non-representational, the elements of Henry Moore’s sculptures include some, which represent real-world objects.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) In comparison with Jackson Pollock’s completely non-representational paintings are the elements of Henry Moore’s sculptures, among which are some that represent real-world objects.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) Unlike the paintings of Jackson Pollock, being completely non-representational, the sculptures of Henry Moore include some elements, representing real world objects.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

##### Question Level: hard



Correctly answering this question requires avoiding being tempted into choosing an incorrect choice by the fact that the elements of the comparison expressed by that incorrect choice match well. The correct answer to this question may not appear to be correct at first, but, via careful analysis of the meanings expressed by the choices, we can eliminate incorrect choices that may initially seem correct and arrive at the correct answer, which is the only choice that conveys a meaning that makes sense.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(A)</b> <b>Whereas the paintings of Jackson Pollock are completely non-representational, elements that represent real world objects are included among those of Henry Moore’s sculptures.
</b>


Because the subject of the first clause of this choice is “paintings,” while the subject of the second clause is “elements,” this choice may at first seem to be incorrect. However, we have to remember that clauses of a sentence that uses “whereas” to express a comparison don’t have to match in the way that elements of a comparison that uses “unlike” do. As long as the clauses express contrasting ideas, the sentence can work.


In this case, the two clauses connected by “whereas” do effectively express a contrast between Jackson Pollock’s paintings and Henry Moore’s sculptures.


Another aspect of this version that may seem incorrect is the use of the pronoun “those,” which seemingly could refer to “paintings,” “elements,” or “objects.” However, since it does not make sense that Henry Moore’s sculptures would have paintings, and since it’s clear that the sentence is not expressing that Henry Moore’s sculptures have objects, the only logical referent of “those” is “elements.”


> <b>(B)</b> <b>Unlike Jackson Pollock’s completely non-representational paintings, Henry Moore included in his sculptures some elements that represent real world objects.
</b>


This choice illogically compares Jackson Pollock’s paintings with Henry Moore.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>In contrast to the elements of Jackson Pollock’s paintings, which are completely non-representational, the elements of Henry Moore’s sculptures include some, which represent real-world objects.
</b>


This choice may seem correct, because it contrasts elements with elements, and because this choice’s use of two relative clauses beginning with “which” may make this choice seem well laid out and balanced.


However, since “which represent real-world objects” is a nonrestrictive modifier, the clause “the elements of Henry Moore’s sculptures include some, which represent real-world objects” conveys the illogical meaning that the elements include some elements. Well, there’s a surprise! Then the nonrestrictive modifier “which represent real-world objects” merely adds nonessential information that the some elements included in the elements represent real-world objects.


This version would work if, instead of the nonrestrictive “which represent …,” a restrictive modifier, such as “that represent …,” were used, so that “some” would be extended to, for instance, “some (elements) that represent real world objects,” as we can see by considering the following:


… the elements of Henry Moore’s sculptures include some that represent real-world objects.


The above version of the clause clearly conveys that the elements include some elements that represent real-world objects.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>In comparison with Jackson Pollock’s completely non-representational paintings are the elements of Henry Moore’s sculptures, among which are some that represent real-world objects.
</b>


The most blatant flaw in this version is that it seems to express an illogical comparison between Jackson Pollock’s paintings and elements of Henry Moore’s sculptures.


Another issue is that the version does not actually express a comparison, but rather expresses the illogical idea that the paintings are “in comparison” with the elements.


Additionally, “which” seems to refer to “sculptures” rather than to “elements,” though we know from the other versions that it is the elements, rather than the sculptures, that represent “real-world objects. This last issue does not, however, constitute a clear reason for eliminating this choice, since the idea that sculptures represent real-world objects does make sense.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>Unlike the paintings of Jackson Pollock, being completely non-representational, the sculptures of Henry Moore include some elements, representing real world objects.
</b>


This choice may seem correct, because it logically compares the paintings of Jackson Pollock with the sculptures of Henry Moore, and because this choice’s use of two non-restrictive present participial modifiers, “being …” and “representing …,” may make this choice seem well laid out and balanced.


However, “paintings” is not the subject of a clause and is not the actor of any action mentioned by the sentence. Therefore, the present participial phrase “being non-representational” would most logically take as its agent, not “paintings,” but “sculptures,” which, unlike “paintings,” is the subject of a clause. As a result of the foregoing, the sentence conveys the meaning that, as a result of being non-representational, the sculptures include elements, which meaning is of course nonsensical, because all sculptures have elements, and being non-representational would not result in sculptures’ including elements.


Further, “the sculptures of Henry Moore include some elements, representing real-world objects” conveys the nonsensical idea that, by or in the course of including elements, the sculptures represent real-world objects.


So, we have to be careful not to be lured by the fact that the comparison expressed by this choice is obviously logical into choosing this choice, whose overall meaning is nonsensical.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)
