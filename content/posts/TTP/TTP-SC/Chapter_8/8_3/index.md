## Lesson Content
Whenever we make a comparison, that comparison must be logical.

Let’s discuss how these illogical comparisons typically occur.

<u>When an Item Is Illogically Compared With a Different Type of Item</u>

Some illogical comparisons involve an item compared with a different type of item.

Consider the following sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The cost of summer camp is much higher than summer school.

Let’s identify the two items being compared.

We can’t logically compare summer school with the cost of summer camp.

To correct this error, we must revise the sentence so that two similar items are compared.

> <strong>The cost of summer camp</strong> is much higher than <strong>summer school’s</strong>.

> <strong>The cost of summer camp</strong> is much higher than <strong>that of summer school</strong>.

Note that we could repeat “the cost” to clarify the comparison, but the GMAT typically finds this approach a little wordy.

> <strong>The cost of summer camp</strong> is much higher than <strong>summer school’s cost</strong>.

Or

> <strong>The cost of summer camp</strong> is much higher than <strong>the cost of summer school</strong>.

Although the above sentences are significantly better than sentences with illogical comparisons, keep in mind that the GMAT prefers concise answers, all else equal.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When we make a comparison, the items compared must be logically comparable. If a comparison is made in a sentence, locate the items compared and make sure that it is logical to compare those items.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s look at some more examples:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: In comparison with the president’s views, the vice president is conservative.

By noting the words used, “in comparison," and the meaning that they convey, we can tell that the sentence is expressing a comparison of two things, “the president’s views” and “the vice president.” Is this comparison a logical one? Certainly not.

> In comparison with <strong>the president’s views</strong>, <strong>the vice president’s views</strong> are conservative.

> In comparison with <strong>the president</strong>, <strong>the vice president</strong> is conservative.

The following more concise sentence is also a correct version of the sentence.

> In comparison with <strong>the president’s views</strong>, <strong>those of the</strong> <strong>vice president </strong>are conservative.

Now, the two items are logically comparable.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Last year, the cost of an MBA was greater than a house.

Notice that the sentence is illogically comparing the “cost of an MBA” to a “house.” We can re-write the sentence:

> Last year, the <strong>cost of an MBA</strong> was greater than <strong>the cost of a house</strong>.

> Last year, the <strong>cost of an MBA</strong> was greater than <strong>that of a house</strong>.

Now, each sentence conveys a logical meaning.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The maximum acceleration of the F-22 is much greater than the F-16.

We can’t compare the “acceleration of the F-22” with the “the F-16.” We can re-write the sentence:

> <strong>The maximum acceleration</strong> of the F-22 is much greater than <strong>the maximum acceleration</strong> of the F-16.

> <strong>The maximum acceleration</strong> of the F-22 is much greater than <strong>that </strong>of the F-16.

> The <strong>F-22’s</strong> maximum acceleration is much greater than the <strong>F-16’s</strong>.

Now, each sentence conveys a logical meaning.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The colors used in Picasso’s lithographs are quite similar to those by Van Gogh.

Are certain colors “by Van Gogh?” Not really.

We can re-write the sentence:

> <strong>The colors used in Picasso’s lithographs</strong> are quite similar to <strong>those used by Van Gogh. </strong>

> The colors used in Picasso’s lithographs are <strong>like </strong>those used in Van Gogh’s lithographs.

Now the comparison is logical.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Therapeutic treatment with antibiotic A produced fewer secondary sinus infections than antibiotic B.

The above sentence illogically compares therapeutic treatment with antibiotic A with antibiotic B.

> <strong>Therapeutic treatment with antibiotic A</strong> produced fewer secondary sinus infections than <strong>were produced by therapeutic treatment with antibiotic B</strong>.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The students in this chemistry course are unlike other chemistry classes.

We cannot compare students to classes.

> The <strong>students</strong> in this chemistry course are unlike <strong>those </strong>in other chemistry classes.

> The <strong>students</strong> in this chemistry course are unlike the <strong>students</strong> in other chemistry classes.

Now the sentence is logically written.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Like many CEOs, Jack’s budget appeared well reasoned; however it was badly flawed.

Can we compare CEOs with budgets? For sure, no.

> Like the <strong>budgets</strong> of many CEOs, Jack’s <strong>budget</strong> appeared well reasoned; however it was badly flawed.

> Like many <strong>CEOs</strong>, <strong>Jack</strong> had a budget that appeared well reasoned; however it was badly flawed.

In this last sentence, we’ve changed the meaning slightly.

Also, notice that, in the correct versions, plural entities and singular entities are compared.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Comparing singular and plural entities with each other is fine as long as doing so results in a logical comparison.
{{< /alert >}}

In the United States, even <u>after only the people who spend time at the beach are considered, the odds of being attacked by a shark are about 1 in 12 million, while being struck by lightning is</u> much higher – about 1 in 700,000.

1. [ ] (A) after only the people who spend time at the beach are considered, the odds of being attacked by a shark are about 1 in 12 million, while being struck by lightning is
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) after the people only spending time at the beach are considered, the odds of being attacked by a shark are about 1 in 12 million, while that of being struck by lightning is
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) if the people only spending time at the beach are considered, the odds of being attacked by a shark are about 1 in 12 million, while that of lightning is
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) if only the people who spend time at the beach are considered, the odds of being attacked by a shark are about 1 in 12 million, while those of being struck by lightning are
5. [ ] (E) if the people who only spend time at the beach are considered, the odds of being attacked by a shark are about 1 in 12 million, while those of lightning are
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

##### Question Level: easy



Each version of the sentence in this question expresses a comparison between the odds of being attacked by a shark and something else. Only in the correct version does the comparison expressed compare the odds of being attacked by a shark with something with which the odds of being attacked by a shark can logically be compared.


> <b>(A)</b> In the United States, even <b>after only the people who spend time at the beach are considered, the odds of being attacked by a shark are about 1 in 12 million, while being struck by lightning is
</b> much higher – about 1 in 700,000.



The use of the preposition “after” does not make sense. The odds mentioned are not calculated “after” the people mentioned are considered. They are calculated via considering those people.


Also, this version illogically compares the odds of being attacked by a shark with “being struck by lightning.”


> <b>(B)</b> In the United States, even <b>after the people only spending time at the beach are considered, the odds of being attacked by a shark are about 1 in 12 million, while that of being struck by lightning is
</b> much higher – about 1 in 700,000.



The use of the preposition “after” does not make sense. The odds mentioned are not calculated “after” the people mentioned are considered. They are calculated via considering those people.


Also, “only” is misplaced. The point to be made is that the odds are calculated via considering only people who spend time at the beach, not via considering “people only spending time at the beach,” i.e., people who do nothing in life other than spend time at the beach.


Also, the use of the singular pronoun “that” does not make sense, because “that” is understood to be standing in for the plural “odds,” and can’t stand in for the singular “odd,” because the singular “odd” is never used in this type of context, one in which “odds” is used synonymously with “probability.”


> <b>(C)</b> In the United States, even <b>if the people only spending time at the beach are considered, the odds of being attacked by a shark are about 1 in 12 million, while that of lightning is
</b> much higher – about 1 in 700,000.



“Only” is misplaced. The point to be made is that the odds are calculated via considering only people who spend time at the beach, not via considering “people only spending time at the beach,” i.e., people who do nothing in life other than spend time at the beach.


Also, the use of the singular pronoun “that” does not make sense, because “that” is understood to be standing in for the plural “odds,” and can’t stand in for the singular “odd,” because the singular “odd” is never used in this type of context, one in which “odds” is used synonymously with “probability.”


Also, even if the use of “that” did make sense, this version would illogically compare the odds of being attacked by a shark with the “odds of lightning,” which comparison does not make sense because, while there are odds of a person’s being struck by lightning and odds that lightning will strike at a certain time or in a certain place, lightning itself does not have odds.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D)</b> In the United States, even <b>if only the people who spend time at the beach are considered, the odds of being attacked by a shark are about 1 in 12 million, while those of being struck by lightning are
</b> much higher – about 1 in 700,000.



In this version “only” is placed so as to logically restrict the people considered to only those in the category “people who spend time at the beach.”


Also, this version logically compares the odds of being attacked by a shark with those, i.e., the odds of, being struck by lightning.


> <b>(E)</b> In the United States, even <b>if the people who only spend time at the beach are considered, the odds of being attacked by a shark are about 1 in 12 million, while those of lightning are
</b> much higher – about 1 in 700,000.



“Only” is misplaced. The point to be made is that the odds are calculated via considering only people who spend time at the beach, not via considering “people who only spend time at the beach,” i.e., people who do nothing in life other than spend time at the beach.


Also, this version illogically compares the odds of being attacked by a shark with the “odds of lightning,” which comparison does not make sense because, while there are odds of a person’s being struck by lightning and odds that lightning will strike at a certain time or in a certain place, lightning itself does not have odds.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Sentence Correction Example 2  

With the average yearly temperature increasing in the state, meteorologists predict that severe forest fires will damage <u>this year a number of California’s state parks greater than</u> last year.

1. [ ] (A) this year a number of California’s state parks greater than
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) this year a greater number of California’s state parks than such fires damaged
3. [ ] (C) a number of California’s state parks this year greater in comparison with such fires
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) a greater number of California’s state parks this year than the parks
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) California’s state parks this year in a number that will be greater than the parks damaged
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



Correctly answering this question takes being careful not to unconsciously mentally fix the flaws in the logic of the comparisons expressed by the incorrect versions.


> <b>(A)</b> With the average yearly temperature increasing in the state, meteorologists predict that severe forest fires will damage <b>this year a number of California’s state parks greater than
</b> last year.



This version is tricky, because it could seem to logically compare the number of parks predicted to be damaged this year with the number of parks damaged last year.


However, the truth is that, in saying “a number of California’s state parks greater than last year,” this version expresses an illogical comparison of “a number of … parks” with “last year.”


Of course, a number of parks would not be greater than “last year.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B)</b> With the average yearly temperature increasing in the state, meteorologists predict that severe forest fires will damage <b>this year a greater number of California’s state parks than such fires damaged
</b> last year.



This version logically compares the number of parks forest fires are predicted to damage this year with the number such fires damaged last year.


> <b>(C)</b> With the average yearly temperature increasing in the state, meteorologists predict that severe forest fires will damage <b>a number of California’s state parks this year greater in comparison with such fires
</b> last year.



In saying, “a number of California’s state parks this year greater in comparison with such fires last year,” this version suggests the illogical idea that “a number of … parks” is “greater” than “such fires.”


Of course, a number of parks would be greater than another number of parks, not greater than fires.


> <b>(D)</b> With the average yearly temperature increasing in the state, meteorologists predict that severe forest fires will damage <b>a greater number of California’s state parks this year than the parks
</b> last year.



In saying “a greater number of California’s state parks this year than the parks last year,” this version suggests the illogical idea that “a … number of parks” is greater than “the parks.”


What we have to notice is that a number would not be greater than “the parks.” A number would be greater than another number.


Also, “the parks last year” is not quite logical. Parks would not be in a year. “The parks damaged last year” would make sense, but “the parks last year” does not.


> <b>(E)</b> With the average yearly temperature increasing in the state, meteorologists predict that severe forest fires will damage <b>California’s state parks this year in a number that will be greater than the parks damaged
</b> last year.



In saying “a number that will be greater than the parks damaged,” this version illogically compares “a number” with “the parks.”


Although the comparison may seem to make sense since “a number” refers to a number of parks, it does not make sense to say that a number will be greater than parks.


Also, the wording of this version, “forest fires will damage California’s state parks this year in a number,” does not convey a meaning that makes sense. How exactly would forest fires damage parks “in a number”?


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

Let’s now consider another type of illogical comparison – one in which something is mistakenly compared with itself.
