## Lesson Content
Another type of illogical comparison involves the comparison of something with itself.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Julie is more skilled at singing than any singer I’ve encountered.

This sentence expresses a comparison of Julie with any singer that the speaker has ever encountered.

Since Julie cannot be more skilled than herself, we need to distinguish between Julie and “any singer.” We can do this by inserting the word “other,” as demonstrated below:

> Julie is more skilled at singing than any <strong>other</strong> singer I’ve encountered.

This change seems like a small one, but it is not.

The word “other” is not the only word we can use to make a distinction between items or people compared.

> Julie is more skilled at singing than anyone <strong>else</strong> I have encountered.

Now, the comparison is clear.

Let’s look at another example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Regardless of whether they love or hate the Italian soccer club Juventus, many soccer fans would agree that Cristiano Ronaldo is better at soccer than any player in history.

Although we may be comfortable speaking that sentence, it’s illogical.

> Regardless of whether they love or hate the Italian soccer club Juventus, many soccer fans would agree that Cristiano Ronaldo is better at soccer than any <strong>other</strong> player in history.

Now the logic of the sentence works.

Sometimes the fact that something is illogically compared with itself is harder to see.

Consider the following:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The bears that live in Pennsylvania survive longer than they do in Texas.

What’s wrong with that sentence? What does “they” stand for? “They” takes the place of “bears that live in Pennsylvania.”

So, let’s replace the pronoun with the noun phrase:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The <strong>bears that live in Pennsylvania</strong> survive longer than <strong>bears that live in Pennsylvania</strong> do in Texas.

What!? There is some madness for you, right? The sentence makes an extremely illogical comparison.

At this point, many people say, ‘Hey.

Possible corrections are:

> The bears that live in Pennsylvania survive longer than <strong>the bears that live in Texas</strong> do.

> The bears that live in Pennsylvania survive longer than <strong>those that live in Texas</strong> do.

> Bears survive longer in Pennsylvania than they do in Texas

These sentences now make logical comparisons.

In general, we must be very vigilant for errors and ambiguities in meaning and logic when pronouns refer to nouns that are immediately followed by modifiers, such as “dogs that live in the house,” “women who attend Stanford University,” or “elephants that live in Africa.”

Let’s examine another example sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Trees that are planted in rich soil grow faster than they do in poor soil.

What’s wrong with that sentence? What does the pronoun “they” represent? “They” represents “Trees that are planted in rich soil.” Let’s replace the pronoun with the noun that it represents:

> Trees that are planted in rich soil grow faster than <strong>trees that are planted in rich soil</strong> do in poor soil.

The sentence is illogical.

> Trees that are planted in rich soil grow faster than <strong>those planted in poor soil</strong> do.

> Trees that are planted in rich soil grow faster than <strong>trees planted in poor soil</strong> do.

Now the sentences make comparisons that are logical.

> <strong>Trees</strong> grow faster in rich soil than <strong>they</strong> do in poor soil.

In this last case, the prepositional phrase “in rich soil” no longer modifies the noun “trees,” and thus the pronoun “they” refers to trees in general, with the result that the comparison makes sense.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Although students have long considered Mr.

What is the meaning of the above sentence? Does the same group of kids behave better when they are in Ms.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Although students have long considered Mr.

The comparison is illogical.

So, in order for the sentence to express a logical comparison, it has to be written in a different way.

> Although students have long considered Mr.

> Although students have long considered Mr.

Both sentences express a clear meaning and a logical comparison.

Let’s look at one more example.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: While home values in many rural and suburban areas of the country plummeted during the 2008 economic crisis now known as the Great Recession, the real estate market in New York City proved far more resilient than it did even in the country’s second-largest city, Los Angeles.

Did you catch the error in the sentence above? The use of the pronoun “it” does not result in the sentence’s expressing a logical comparison between the real estate market in New York City and the real estate market in Los Angeles.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: While home values in many rural and suburban areas of the country plummeted during the 2008 economic crisis now known as the Great Recession, <strong>the real estate market in New York City </strong>proved far more resilient than <strong>the real estate market in New York City</strong> did even in the country’s second-largest city, Los Angeles.

The real estate market in New York City can’t possibly be in Los Angeles.

> While home values in many rural and suburban areas of the country plummeted during the 2008 economic crisis now known as the Great Recession, the real estate market in New York City proved far more resilient than <strong>the real estate market in the country’s second-largest city</strong>, Los Angeles.

##  Sentence Correction Example 3  

<u>Although a hardy houseplant, aloe vera plants that are in the wild grow faster than when indoors.</u>

1. [ ] (A) Although a hardy houseplant, aloe vera plants that are in the wild grow faster than when indoors.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) Aloe vera plants grow faster in the wild than they do indoors, though they are hardy houseplants.
3. [ ] (C) Aloe vera plants that are in the wild grow faster than when they are indoors, although they are a hardy houseplant.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) Aloe vera plants that are in the wild grow faster than they do when indoors, though they are hardy houseplants.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) Even though they are hardy houseplants, aloe vera plants grow faster in the wild than what they do indoors.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

##### Question Level: medium



Correctly answering this question takes understanding the following.


Because the modifier “that are in the wild” restricts what it modifies, the expression “aloe vera plants that are in the wild” restricts the aloe vera plants mentioned to a certain category, ones that are in the wild.


So, when a sentence uses the expression “aloe vera plants that are in the wild,” it is naming only certain aloe vera plants, the ones that are in the wild.


Thus, if a pronoun, such as “they,” refers to “aloe verb plants that are in the wild,” that pronoun does not refer to “aloe vera plants.” It refers to the entire expression “aloe vera plants that are in the wild.”


By applying the above information, we can see that the comparisons expressed by some choices in this question illogically compare “aloe vera plants that are in the wild” with themselves.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Although a hardy houseplant, aloe vera plants that are in the wild grow faster than when indoors.
</b>


Since the opening modifier “although a hardy houseplant” is placed so as to modify “aloe vera plants that are in the wild,” this version has an agreement issue, because the singular “a hardy houseplant” does not match the plural “aloe vera plants.” Multiple aloe vera “plants” would not be “a hardy houseplant.”


Furthermore, “aloe vera plants <em>that are in the wild</em>” would not be “houseplants.” So, saying, “although a hardy houseplant, aloe vera plants that are in the wild” does not make sense.


Also, the comparison expressed by this version does not make sense. In saying, “<em>aloe vera plants that are in the wild</em> grow faster than <em>when indoors</em>,” this version seems to express a nonsensical comparison of aloe vera plants that are in the wild with a time, “when indoors.”


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B)</b> <b>Aloe vera plants grow faster in the wild than they do indoors, though they are hardy houseplants.
</b>


Notice that, unlike in some other versions of the sentence, in this version, the logical referent of “they” is “aloe vera plants.”


As a result, this version logically compares how fast aloe vera plants grow in the wild with how fast aloe vera plants grow indoors, as we can see by substituting “aloe vera plants” for “they.”


<strong>aloe vera plants</strong> grow faster in the wild than <strong>they </strong>do indoors, though <strong>they </strong>are hardy houseplants


<strong>aloe vera plants</strong> grow faster in the wild than <strong>aloe vera plants</strong> do indoors, though <strong>aloe vera plants</strong> are hardy houseplants


> <b>(C)</b> <b>Aloe vera plants that are in the wild grow faster than when they are indoors, although they are a hardy houseplant.
</b>


In saying, “<em>Aloe vera plants that are in the wild</em> grow faster than <em>when they are indoors</em>,” this version expresses a nonsensical comparison of aloe vera plants in the wild and a time, “when they are indoors.”


Also, <em>s</em>ince the logical referent of “they” is “aloe vera plants that are in the wild,” this version suggests the illogical ideas that aloe vera plants that are in the wild sometimes “are indoors” and that aloe vera plants that are in the wild “are hardy houseplants,” as we can see by substituting “aloe vera plants that are in the wild” for “they.”


<strong>aloe vera plants that are in the wild</strong> grow faster than when <strong>they </strong>are indoors, though <strong>they </strong>are hardy houseplants


<strong>aloe vera plants that are in the wild</strong> grow faster than when <strong>aloe vera plants that are in the wild</strong> are indoors, although <strong>aloe vera plants that are in the wild</strong> are hardy houseplants


Aloe vera plants in the wild would not be indoors or houseplants.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>Aloe vera plants that are in the wild grow faster than they do when indoors, though they are hardy houseplants.
</b>


Since the logical referent of “they” is “aloe vera plants that are in the wild,” this version illogically compares “aloe vera plants that are in the wild” with themselves, as we can see by substituting “aloe vera plants that are in the wild” for “they.”


<strong>aloe vera plants that are in the wild</strong> grow faster than <strong>they </strong>do when indoors


<strong>aloe vera plants that are in the wild</strong> grow faster than <strong>aloe vera plants that are in the wild</strong> do when indoors


Notice also that this version suggests the illogical idea that aloe vera plants that are in the wild sometimes are indoors. Aloe vera plants that are in the wild would not be indoors.


Also,<em> “</em><strong>Aloe vera plants that are in the wild</strong> grow faster …, though <strong>they</strong> are hardy houseplants,” conveys the nonsensical meaning that aloe vera plants that are in the wild are houseplants. Of course, aloe vera plants in the wild would not be houseplants.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>Even though they are hardy houseplants, aloe vera plants grow faster in the wild than what they do indoors.
</b>


This version expresses a nonsensical comparison between aloe vera plants and “what they do indoors.”


Also, the use of “even though” in this context does not quite make sense. “Even though” is used to express that something occurs despite something else.


So, in this case, “Even though they are hardy houseplants, aloe vera plants grow faster in the wild” suggests the illogical idea that aloe vera plants grow faster in the wild despite the fact that they are hardy houseplants, as if aloe vera plants’ being hardy houseplants might be expected to keep aloe vera plants from growing faster in the wild. Aloe vera plants’ being hardy, in other words surviving well, as houseplants would not keep aloe vera plants from growing faster in the wild.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)
