## Lesson Content
So far, whether examples that we have considered have been logical has depended primarily on whether comparing the things compared, which are named by nouns, makes sense.

One thing that we can be sure of is that repeating in the second part of a comparison the verb or verb phrase that appears in the first part of a comparison is generally going to work.

> Jim <strong>attends the club meetings</strong> less regularly than Andrew <strong>attends the club meetings</strong>.

> Catherine Ndereba <strong>likes running</strong> more than most people <strong>like running</strong>.

> Andrea <strong>worked </strong>more carefully than Shashank <strong>worked</strong>.

> Franklin Delano Roosevelt <strong>held the office of President</strong> for longer than Teddy Roosevelt <strong>held the office</strong>.

Because the comparisons in the above examples basically repeat in the second parts of the comparisons the verbs or verb phrases from the first parts of the comparisons, the sentences rather obviously make sense.

Another way to create logical comparisons is to substitute “does” or “do” for present tense action verbs and “did” for past tense action verbs.

> Jim <strong>attends the club meetings</strong> less regularly than Andrew <strong>does</strong>.

In the above example, “does” is substituted for “attends the club meetings.”

> Catherine Ndereba <strong>likes running</strong> more than most people <strong>do</strong>.

In the above example, “do” is substituted for “like running”

> Andrea <strong>worked </strong>more carefully than Shashank <strong>did</strong>.

In the above example, “did” is substituted for “worked.”

> Franklin Delano Roosevelt <strong>held the office of President</strong> for longer than Teddy Roosevelt <strong>did</strong>.

In the above example, “did” is substituted for “held the office.”

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Logical comparisons can be created via the substitution of “does” or “do” for repeated present tense action verbs or verb phrases and “did” for repeated past tense action verbs or verb phrases.
{{< /alert >}}

We can also use multiple tenses in comparisons as long as the resulting comparisons make sense.

> Michael Phelps <strong>swims </strong>faster than Mark Spitz <strong>swam</strong>.

> Early space exploration <strong>excited people </strong>more than space exploration <strong>excites people</strong> currently.

> Some of today’s healthcare practitioners <strong>are </strong>more aware of the mind-body connection than many of their predecessors <strong>were</strong>.

The first example expresses a logical comparison of how Michael Phelps swims in the present and how Mark Spitz swam in the past.

Multiple tenses can work in comparisons also when “did” is substituted for a past tense verb or “does” is substituted for a present tense verb, as in the following examples:

> Michael Phelps <strong>swims </strong>faster than Mark Spitz <strong>did</strong>.

> Early space exploration <strong>excited people</strong> more than space exploration <strong>does </strong>currently.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Verbs of multiple tenses can appear in comparisons as long as the resulting comparisons make sense.
{{< /alert >}}

Now, here’s an example that doesn’t work:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Elisa is just as patient with the students as Colleen does.

The above example is incorrect because it compares how Elisa is with what Colleen does.

> Elisa <strong>is </strong>just as patient with the students as Colleen <strong>is</strong>.

Here’s another example that erroneously compares what one person did with what another person was:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Yesterday, Alexander, who really wanted to win, played harder than Giulio was.

The above example almost sounds correct, but if we read it carefully, we see that it illogically compares what Alexander did with how Giulio was.

> Yesterday, Alexander, who really wanted to win, <strong>played </strong>harder than Giulio <strong>did</strong>.

The above example logically compares what Alexander did yesterday with what Giulio did yesterday.

Here’s another example that doesn’t make sense:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Katina has less experience in managing projects than Sondra has been.

It doesn’t make sense to compare something that Katina has with “Sondra has been,” because, even though both parts of the comparison use the word “has,” “Katina has” expresses something that Katina does – she has – whereas “Sondra has been” would logically be used to express, not something that Sondra does, but Sondra’s state of being, how Sondra has been.

A correct version is:

> Katina <strong>has </strong>less experience in managing projects than Sondra <strong>has</strong>.

Now, the sentence expresses a logical comparison of what Katina has with what Sondra has.

Now, the incorrect examples above are pretty blatantly incorrect.

To see how such obscuring can work, consider the following example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: A bright light flashing in the darkness is more likely to attract the attention of passing drivers than a similarly bright light shining steadily would.

The above example is illogical because it compares how likely a bright light flashing “is” to attract attention with what a bright light shining steadily “would.” However, because of the length and complexity of the sentence, the sentence could seem fine if read casually.

The issue becomes more obvious if we simplify the sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: A light flashing <strong>is </strong>more likely to attract attention than a light shining <strong>would</strong>.

It doesn’t make sense to say what that sentence is understood to convey, which is “a light shining would likely to attract attention.”

To correct the sentence, we have to make the comparison logical by replacing “would” with “is,” as in:

> A bright light flashing in the darkness <strong>is </strong>more likely to attract the attention of passing drivers than a similarly bright light shining steadily <strong>is</strong>.

Now the sentence logically compares how likely a bright light flashing is to attract attention with how likely a bright light shining steadily is to attract attention.

Let’s consider another example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The study found that professional athletes have greater ability to handle mental fatigue of the type experienced during long matches or endurance events than amateurs can.

While “have ability” and “can” are similar in meaning, this example is illogical because it compares what professional athletes have, “greater ability,” with what amateurs can.

> The study found that professional athletes <strong>have </strong>greater ability to handle mental fatigue of the type experienced during long matches or endurance events than amateurs <strong>have</strong>.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

It’s important to pay attention to the verbs used in comparisons, because the use of the wrong verb in a sentence that expresses a comparison can render the sentence illogical.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 5  

Because of the new compensation scheme recently put into place, the people working on the assembly line are <u>paid much better than they did</u> in the past.

1. [ ] (A) paid much better than they did
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) much better paid than they could
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) being paid much better than it was
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) much better paid than they were
5. [ ] (E) paid much better than those had
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: easy



Correctly answering this question takes finding a choice the use of which results in a version that expresses a logical comparison between how well paid the people mentioned are in the present and something “in the past.” Some of the choices are rather similar to each other, and choice of verb turns out to be what defines the difference between incorrect choices and the correct answer.


> <b>(A)</b> Because of the new compensation scheme recently put into place, the people working on the assembly line are <b>paid much better than they did
</b> in the past.



The most logical referent for “they” in this version is “people working on the assembly line.” Since presumably those people didn’t pay themselves, this version illogically compares how well those people “are paid” with something unnamed that the same people “did” in the past. So, the use of the verb “did” is what makes this choice incorrect.


> <b>(B)</b> Because of the new compensation scheme recently put into place, the people working on the assembly line are <b>much better paid than they could
</b> in the past.



The most logical referent for “they” in this version is “people working on the assembly line.” So, this version illogically compares how well those people “are paid” with something unnamed that the same people “could” in the past. So, the use of the verb “could” is what makes this choice incorrect.


> <b>(C)</b> Because of the new compensation scheme recently put into place, the people working on the assembly line are <b>being paid much better than it was
</b> in the past.



The word “being” is unnecessary. It is more concise and more direct to say simply “are paid.” While the use of “being” in this version is not a strong reason to eliminate this choice, it is a flaw in this version.


Also, “it” has no clear referent. While “it” may seem to refer to the compensation of the people mentioned, their compensation is not mentioned in the sentence. Further, even if we were to decide that “it” refers to their compensation, the comparison would be illogical, because the sentence mentions nothing that would be logically compared with their compensation. Read literally, with “it” understood to mean “their compensation,” this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that the workers are better paid than their compensation was paid in the past.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(D)</b> Because of the new compensation scheme recently put into place, the people working on the assembly line are <b>much better paid than they were
</b> in the past.



This version expresses a logical comparison between how well paid the workers “are” now with how well paid they “were in the past.” The use of the verb “were” rather than verbs used in some other choices is what makes this choice correct.


> <b>(E)</b> Because of the new compensation scheme recently put into place, the people working on the assembly line are <b>paid much better than those had
</b> in the past.



The most logical referent for “those” in this version is “people working on the assembly line.” So, this version illogically compares how well people working on the assembly line “are paid” with something unnamed that the same people, i.e., “those,” “had” in the past. So, the use of the verb “had” makes this choice incorrect.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Sentence Correction Example 6  

Analysts predict that, this year, the company’s revenue will grow 12 percent, <u>a number much greater than it was</u> in any of the previous five years.

1. [ ] (A) a number much greater than it was
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
2. [ ] (B) which is much more than it was
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
3. [x] (C) much more than it did
4. [ ] (D) much more than they had
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)
5. [ ] (E) much more than it was
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (C)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



> <b>(A)</b> Analysts predict that, this year, the company’s revenue will grow 12 percent, <b>a number much greater than it was
</b> in any of the previous five years.



Since “it” would logically refer to “a number,” and since “a number” refers to “12 percent,” this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that 12 percent is much greater than 12 percent was in any of the previous five years.


> <b>(B)</b> Analysts predict that, this year, the company’s revenue will grow 12 percent, <b>which is much more than it was
</b> in any of the previous five years.



Since “which” would most logically refer to “12 percent,” this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that 12 percent is much greater than 12 percent was in any of the previous five years.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(C)</b> Analysts predict that, this year, the company’s revenue will grow 12 percent, <b>much more than it did
</b> in any of the previous five years.



In this version “much more than it did in any of the previous five years” is an appositive renaming “12 percent.”


Also, “it” would most logically refer to “revenue,” since “did” is a logical substitute for “grew.”


So, this version conveys the logical meaning that analysts predict that revenue will grow 12 percent and that 12 percent is much more than revenue grew in any of the previous five years.


Notice, key to this choice’s being correct is the use of the verb “did” as a replacement for “grew,” which use results in the sentence’s expressing a logical comparison between how much revenue is predicted to grow this year and how much it “did,” i.e., grew, in the past.


> <b>(D)</b> Analysts predict that, this year, the company’s revenue will grow 12 percent, <b>much more than they had
</b> in any of the previous five years.



In this version, the logical referent for “they” is “analysts.” So, “they had” means “analysts had.” Thus, key to understanding this version is determining what is it that is “much more than analysts had in the past.”


We might be tempted to decide that the sentence is meant to convey that “12 percent” is much more than analysts had in the past. However, the sentence does not say that the analysts currently “have” 12 percent. Also, the sentence does not indicate what the analysts had in the past that 12 percent would be more than. So, it doesn’t make sense to say that 12 percent is what is “much more than they (analysts) had in the past.”


An alternative interpretation is that the sentence is meant to convey that analysts now predict that revenue will grow 12 percent much more than analysts had in the past predicted that revenue will grow 12 percent. Of course this meaning is ridiculous, and the truth is that, while the wording of this version may sound as if it means something that makes sense, there is no possible interpretation of the wording of this version that makes sense.


> <b>(E)</b> Analysts predict that, this year, the company’s revenue will grow 12 percent, <b>much more than it was
</b> in any of the previous five years.



If we decide that “it” refers to “revenue,” then this version illogically compares how much revenue is predicted to “grow” this year with how much revenue “was” in any of the past five years.


If we decide that “it” refers to “12 percent,” then this version conveys the nonsensical meaning that 12 percent is much more than 12 percent was in any of the past five years.


> ##### Correct Answer: (C)
