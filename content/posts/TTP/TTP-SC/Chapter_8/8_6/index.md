## Lesson Content
While the word “ellipsis” may not be familiar to you, ellipsis is not a complex concept.

Consider the following example:

> The head of sales attends webinars more often than other team members.

When we read the above sentence, we understand it to mean the following:

> The head of sales attends webinars more often than other team members <strong>attend webinars</strong>.

In the first version, the wording “attend webinars” is omitted after “other team members.” This omission, or “ellipsis,” is considered correct because, even though the sentence does not include “attend webinars” after “other team members,” a reader can readily tell what the sentence is meant to express.

In the previous section of this chapter, we discussed that “does” or “do” can be substituted for a verb phrase in a comparison, as “do” is in the following version of the sentence:

> The head of sales <strong>attends webinars</strong> more often than other team members <strong>do</strong>.

So, what we are seeing now is that, in addition to substitution, in which words such as “does” or “do” are substituted for words that would otherwise be repeated, there is another option, complete omission of words that would otherwise be repeated.

Let’s consider another set of examples:

> A racing sailboat <strong>can round the course</strong> faster than a cruising sailboat <strong>can round the course</strong>.

> A racing sailboat can <strong>round the course</strong> faster than a cruising sailboat <strong>can</strong>.

> A racing sailboat <strong>can round the course</strong> faster than a cruising sailboat.

Notice, we can omit some or all of the repeated words “can round the course” from the second part of the comparison without eroding the clarity of the comparison made.

To lock in this concept, let’s consider some more examples in which repeated words are omitted:

> She <strong>is quoted in the news </strong>less often than her sister <strong>is quoted in the news</strong>.

> She <strong>is quoted in the news </strong>less often than her sister <strong>is</strong>.

> She <strong>is quoted in the news </strong>less often than her sister.

The second and third versions of sentence omit “quoted in the news” and “is” without sacrificing clarity.

> Surprisingly, research has shown that highly plugged-in teens <strong>are just as likely to socialize in person</strong> as less plugged-in teens <strong>are to socialize in person</strong>.

> Surprisingly, research has shown that highly plugged-in teens <strong>are just as likely to socialize in person</strong> as less plugged-in teens.

The second version of the sentence omits “are to socialize in person” without losing clarity.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Words that would otherwise be repeated in a sentence that expresses a comparison can be omitted as long as the sentence effectively expresses its meaning without the omitted words.
{{< /alert >}}

Let’s now discuss ambiguous comparisons.
