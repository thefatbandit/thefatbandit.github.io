## Lesson Content
As we have seen, omitting repeated words in a sentence that expresses a comparison is OK as long as the sentence still expresses its meaning effectively.

Consider the following sentence:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Alex is much nicer to me than Alfredo.

This sentence could be interpreted in two different ways.

In order for the sentence to be effective, the sentence has to include words that help clarify the meaning.

To convey the first meaning unambiguously, we can say:

> Alex is much nicer to me than Alfredo <strong>is</strong>.

Notice, even though “is” is repeated in this case, it can’t be omitted, because without the second “is” the sentence does not effectively convey one meaning.

To convey the second meaning unambiguously, we can say

> Alex is much nicer to me than <strong>to</strong> Alfredo.

In this case, the second “to” is necessary, because, without the second “to,” the sentence does not effectively convey one meaning.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Correcting ambiguous comparisons conveying two or more possible meanings can be accomplished via the addition of words that help clarify the meaning.
{{< /alert >}}

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Sara loves shark diving more than Scott.

This meaning of the sentence is unclear.

With the addition of the substitute verb “does” to the sentence, the meaning conveyed becomes clear:

> Sara loves shark diving more than Scott<strong> does</strong>.

Now, it’s clear that Sara loves Shark diving more than Scott loves Shark diving.

But, maybe the speaker meant:

> Sara loves shark diving more than<strong> she </strong>loves Scott.

Ok, now not so great for Scott, right?

Sentences must always be precise in their meanings, and we must be ever watchful for sentences that convey ambiguous meanings.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Joanna knows less about the Chilean culture than Rudy.

What is being compared? Does Joanna know less about the Chilean culture than she knows about Rudy, or does Joanna know less about the Chilean culture than Rudy knows? As written, the sentence does not clearly convey one or the other.

> Joanna knows less about the Chilean culture than <strong>she knows about </strong>Rudy.

The above sentence expresses a comparison between Joanna’s knowledge of Chilean culture and her knowledge of Rudy.

> Joanna knows less about the Chilean culture than Rudy <strong>knows</strong>.

Now, we are comparing Joanna’s knowledge of Chilean culture with Rudy’s knowledge of Chilean culture.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The foundation donated more money to the Gentle Barn Animal Sanctuary than other animal sanctuaries.

Notice the ambiguity? Did the foundation donate more money to the Gentle Barn Animal Sanctuary than to other animal sanctuaries? Or, did the foundation donate more money to the Gentle Barn Animal Sanctuary than did other animal sanctuaries? The sentence does not clearly indicate which it is meant to convey.

> The foundation donated more money to the Gentle Barn Animal Sanctuary than <strong>to</strong> other animal sanctuaries.

Now it’s clear that the foundation gave the Gentle Barn more money than the foundation gave other animal sanctuaries.

> The foundation donated more money to the Gentle Barn Animal Sanctuary than <strong>did</strong> other animal sanctuaries

Now it’s clear that the foundation gave money to the Gentle Barn, and so did other animal sanctuaries, but the foundation gave more than the other animal sanctuaries gave.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The current chief economist has been more focused on the company’s investment portfolio than the junior economists.

Notice that it’s hard to determine what is being compared.

> The current chief economist has been more focused on the company’s investment portfolio than the junior economists <strong>have been</strong>.

Now, the sentence makes clear that what’s being compared is the degree of focus of the chief economist and the degree of focus of the junior economists.

> The current chief economist has been focused <strong>more on</strong> the company’s investment portfolio than <strong>on </strong>the junior economists.

Now, the sentence makes clear that it’s expressing a comparison of the degree to which the chief economist is focused on the investment portfolio and the degree to which the chief economist is focused on the junior economists.

##  Sentence Correction Example 7  

So extensive is the critic’s <u>knowledge, so thorough is his research, and his reviews often delve as deeply into the subjects of the books he reviews as</u> the books themselves.

1. [ ] (A) knowledge, so thorough is his research, and his reviews often delve as deeply into the subjects of the books he reviews as
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
2. [x] (B) knowledge and so thorough his research that his reviews often delve as deeply into the subjects of the books he reviews as do
3. [ ] (C) knowledge, his research is so thorough that his reviews often delve as deeply into the subjects of the books he reviews as
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
4. [ ] (D) knowledge and so thorough is his research, and so, his reviews often delve as deeply into the subjects of the books he reviews as are
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)
5. [ ] (E) knowledge, and his research is so thorough, thus his reviews often delve deeply into the subjects of the books he reviews as
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (B)

##### Question Level: medium



In order for the sentence to be effective, it has to clearly indicate with what how deeply the reviews delve into the subjects is being compared. Only one version clearly indicates that how deeply the reviews delve into the subjects is being compared with how deeply the books themselves delve into the subjects.


> <b>(A)</b> So extensive is the critic’s <b>knowledge, so thorough is his research, and his reviews often delve as deeply into the subjects of the books he reviews as
</b> the books themselves.



The wording of this version is such that the version could be interpreted as expressing either of the following comparisons:


His reviews often delve as deeply into the subjects of the books he reviews as the books themselves <strong>delve</strong>.


His reviews often delve as deeply into the subjects of the books he reviews as <strong>his reviews delve into</strong> the books themselves.


Neither “delve” nor “reviews delve into” is repeated in this version, and either could be understood to be repeated. So, the meaning conveyed is ambiguous.


Also, a clause like “so extensive is the critic’s knowledge” has to be followed by a clause that begins with “that …,” as in, “so extensive is the critic’s knowledge that x is the case,” but this version illogically puts “so extensive is the critic’s knowledge” and “so thorough is his research” into a list with a third clause, “his reviews often delve …,” rather than use “that” to logically connect the first two clauses to the third.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(B)</b> So extensive is the critic’s <b>knowledge and so thorough his research that his reviews often delve as deeply into the subjects of the books he reviews as do
</b> the books themselves.



By adding the verb “do,” which substitutes for “delve,” this version makes clear that the comparison made is between how deeply the reviews delve into the subjects and how deeply the books themselves “do.”


This version may sound a bit off because “is” is not used in “so thorough his research.” However, “is,” having appeared in “so extensive is his knowledge” is understood without being written to be repeated in “so thorough (is) his research.”


> <b>(C)</b> So extensive is the critic’s <b>knowledge, his research is so thorough that his reviews often delve as deeply into the subjects of the books he reviews as
</b> the books themselves.



The wording of this version is such that the version could be interpreted as expressing either of the following comparisons:


His reviews often delve as deeply into the subjects of the books he reviews as the books themselves <strong>delve</strong>.


His reviews often delve as deeply into the subjects of the books he reviews as <strong>his reviews delve into</strong> the books themselves.


Neither “delve” nor “reviews delve into” is repeated in this version, and either could be understood to be repeated. So, the meaning conveyed is ambiguous.


Also, the way “so extensive is the critic’s knowledge” is merely placed at the opening of the sentence and set off from the rest of the sentence by a comma without any connecting word leaves that clause unconnected to the rest of the sentence and without a clear purpose in the sentence.


> <b>(D)</b> So extensive is the critic’s <b>knowledge and so thorough is his research, and so, his reviews often delve as deeply into the subjects of the books he reviews as are
</b> the books themselves.



The use of “are” results in this version’s illogically comparing how deeply the reviews “delve” with how the books themselves “are.”


Also, a clause like “so extensive is the critic’s knowledge” has to be followed by a clause that begins with “that …,” as in, “so extensive is the critic’s knowledge that x is the case,” but this version illogically uses “and so” to connect “so extensive is the critic’s knowledge and so thorough is his research” to an independent third clause, “his reviews often delve …,” rather than use “that” to logically connect the first two clauses to the third.


> <b>(E)</b> So extensive is the critic’s <b>knowledge, and his research is so thorough, thus his reviews often delve deeply into the subjects of the books he reviews as
</b> the books themselves.



If we read this version carefully, we see that there is no “as” before “deeply.” Thus, this version does not express a comparison but, rather, conveys that “his reviews delve deeply … as the books,” as if to say something along the lines of that the reviews are acting “as the books.”


Also, a clause like “so extensive is the critic’s knowledge” or “his research is so thorough” has to be followed by a clause that begins with “that …,” as in, “so extensive is the critic’s knowledge that x is the case,” but this version illogically uses “thus” to connect “so extensive is the critic’s knowledge, and his research is so thorough” to an independent third clause, “his reviews often delve …,” rather than use “that” to logically connect the first two clauses to the third.


Also, since “thus” cannot alone serve as a conjunction that connects two clauses, the sentence is incorrectly composed of clauses connected by a comma without a conjunction and, thus, is a comma splice.


Also, ideally, “so extensive is the critic’s knowledge” and “his research is so thorough” would be structured in the same way, rather than slightly differently, as these two clauses are in this sentence, though the fact that they are not structured in the same way is not a very clear reason to eliminate this choice.


> ##### Correct Answer: (B)

##  Sentence Correction Example 8  

<u>A major reason for the loss of urban owls, hawks, and even herons is people’s using advanced rodenticides, which spread throughout the food chain and are just as lethal to predators as to rats and mice.</u>

1. [x] (A) A major reason for the loss of urban owls, hawks, and even herons is people’s using advanced rodenticides, which spread throughout the food chain and are just as lethal to predators as to rats and mice.
2. [ ] (B) People using advanced rodenticides, which spread throughout the food chain and are not only lethal to rats and mice but also to predators, are major reasons for the loss of urban owls, hawks, and even herons.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) Spreading throughout the food chain and just as lethal to predators as rats and mice, a major reason for the loss of urban owls, hawks, and even herons is people’s use of advanced rodenticides.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) People’s use of advanced rodenticides that have been spread throughout the food chain and are just as lethal to predators as rats and mice is a major reason for the loss of urban owls, hawks, and even herons.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) A major reason for the loss of urban owls, hawks, and even herons, people’s use of advanced rodenticides spreads throughout the food chain and is lethal, not just to rats and mice, but predators as well.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>

##### Question Level: medium



Some versions of the sentence, including the correct version, include comparisons of how lethal the rodenticides are to predators with something else, but only the correct version clearly indicates what that something else is and expresses an unambiguous meaning. The other versions express comparisons that could be interpreted in either of the following two ways:


The rodenticides are just as lethal to predators as the rodenticides are to rats and mice.


The rodenticides are just as lethal to predators as rats and mice are to predators.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(A)</b> <b>A major reason for the loss of urban owls, hawks, and even herons is people’s using advanced rodenticides, which spread throughout the food chain and are just as lethal to predators as to rats and mice.
</b>


Because this version uses “to” twice in “just as lethal <strong>to </strong>predators as <strong>to </strong>rats and mice,” this version clearly indicates that the comparison it expresses is between how lethal the rodenticides are “to predators” and how lethal the rodenticides are “to rats and mice.”


While the use of the possessive + gerund structure “people’s using” may sound a little funny, this version is grammatically correct and effectively expresses one logical meaning.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>People using advanced rodenticides, which spread throughout the food chain and are not only lethal to rats and mice but also to predators, are major reasons for the loss of urban owls, hawks, and even herons.
</b>


The clearest issue in this version is that the “not only … but also” structure it contains is not parallel, as we can see by considering the following:


<strong>are </strong>(not only lethal to rats and mice but also to predators)


not only <strong>are </strong>lethal to rats and mice but also <strong>are </strong>to predators


We can see that “lethal” which appears after “not only” and, therefore, is not carried over to after “but also” is missing after “but also,” and, as a result the sentence says the illogical “are to predators,” leaving the reader to ask, “Are what to predators?”


Also, the meaning conveyed by the wording “people using advanced rodenticides … are the reasons” does not make complete sense. It’s really the people’s use of or people’s using the pesticides that is the reason for the loss of owls, etc., not the people themselves.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>Spreading throughout the food chain and just as lethal to predators as rats and mice, a major reason for the loss of urban owls, hawks, and even herons is people’s use of advanced rodenticides.
</b>


The meaning conveyed by “just as lethal to predators as rats and mice” is not clear, as that wording could be interpreted as expressing either of the following:


just as lethal <strong>to </strong>predators as <strong>to </strong>rats and mice


just as lethal <strong>to predators</strong> as rats and mice <strong>are to predators</strong>


Also, since the opening modifier “spreading throughout the food chain …” is a noun modifier, the target of the modifier is the subject of the clause that follows the modifier, which subject is “a reason.” Since a reason would not be spreading throughout the food chain, this modification does not make sense.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>People’s use of advanced rodenticides that have been spread throughout the food chain and are just as lethal to predators as rats and mice is a major reason for the loss of urban owls, hawks, and even herons.
</b>


The meaning conveyed by “just as lethal to predators as rats and mice” is not clear, as that wording could be interpreted as expressing either of the following:


just as lethal <strong>to </strong>predators as <strong>to </strong>rats and mice


just as lethal <strong>to predators</strong> as rats and mice <strong>are to predators</strong>


Also, “people’s use of advanced rodenticides that have been spread throughout the food chain” seems to convey the illogical meaning that people use the rodenticides after the rodenticides have been spread throughout the food chain by some unnamed entity.


> <b>(E)</b> <b>A major reason for the loss of urban owls, hawks, and even herons, people’s use of advanced rodenticides spreads throughout the food chain and is lethal, not just to rats and mice, but predators as well.
</b>


This version conveys the illogical meaning that people’s use of rodenticides, rather than the rodenticides themselves, spreads throughout the food chain and is lethal.


Also, the “not just … but … as well” structure is not parallel, as we can see by considering the following:


<strong>lethal</strong>, (not just to rats and mice, but predators as well)


not just <strong>lethal </strong>to rats and mice, but <strong>lethal </strong>predators as well


We can see that, because “to” follows “not just,” “to” is not carried over to what follows “but,” and so, the sentence is understood to say “lethal predators,” which does not make sense in this context. “Lethal predators” is missing “to.’


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

Let’s now discuss incomplete comparisons.
