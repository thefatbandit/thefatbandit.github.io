## Lesson Content
Sometimes a sentence leaves a comparison incomplete.

Let’s look at an example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: This tent is just as waterproof.

The meaning of this sentence is ambiguous because of an incomplete comparison.

> This tent is just as waterproof <strong>as more expensive models</strong>.

The comparison becomes more complete via the addition of a noun to which the existing noun is compared.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: This project will cost less.

Cost less than what? Cookies? A new swimming pool? A trip to Honolulu? This comparison is incomplete.

> This project will cost less <strong>than the prior project</strong>.

Notice that now the comparison is clear; the sentence is comparing the cost of this project with the cost of the prior project.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

When a sentence expresses a comparison, if the sentence does not mention at least two items that are compared, the comparison expressed is not complete.
{{< /alert >}}

Now, probably, the GMAT won’t simply use a sentence like “This tent is just as waterproof,” and call it incorrect, because a sentence like that one is not strictly incorrect.

> My brother prefers more expensive tents over this tent, because he believes the others to be more waterproof.

In the above context, the meaning of “this tent is just as waterproof,” is fairly clear, because of what the previous sentence says.

So, rather than call a simple incomplete comparison incorrect, what the GMAT would probably do is make a sentence that expresses an incomplete comparison rather clearly incorrect by having the expression of an incomplete comparison result in the sentence’s being clearly illogical or ineffective.

Consider the following example:

> The researchers believed that they had finally developed a method for determining the composition of faraway planets, but the method turned out not to be as effective.

The above sentence clearly does not make sense, because it indicates that the method developed was the first of its kind, so there was no other such method with which to compare the method developed by the researchers.

One possible correct version is the following:

> The researchers believed that they had finally developed a method for determining the composition of faraway planets, but the method turned out not to be as effective <strong>as they had hoped</strong>.

OK, now we have a complete comparison.

An alternative corrected version would change the comparison to a description, as in:

> The researchers believed that they had finally developed a method for determining the composition of faraway planets, but the method turned out to be <strong>not very </strong>effective.

Now, the sentence is not expressing an incomplete comparison because it’s not expressing a comparison at all.

Let’s consider another example:

> The amateur astronomers had expected to easily find Alpha Centauri in the night sky, but, because it is not very different, they didn’t find it until they used a star chart.

The sentence says that Alpha Centauri is “not very different,” but doesn’t say what Alpha Centauri is not very different from.

> The amateur astronomers had expected to easily find Alpha Centauri in the night sky, but, because it is not very different <strong>from other stars</strong>, they didn’t find it until they used a star chart.

Let’s consider one more example:

> While commuting by bike can be enjoyable, if it takes much longer, people may be resistant to traveling that way.

The sentence does not indicate what commuting by bike may take much longer than.

> While commuting by bike can be enjoyable, if it takes much longer <strong>than commuting by train</strong>, people may be resistant to traveling that way.

##  Sentence Correction Example 9  

<u>Unlike the weather in the Hawaiian Islands, which tends to be significantly cooler during the winter months than the summer, the weather in Panama is consistently warmer.</u>

1. [ ] (A) Unlike the weather in the Hawaiian Islands, which tends to be significantly cooler during the winter months than the summer, the weather in Panama is consistently warmer.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) The weather in Panama is consistently warmer than that of the Hawaiian Islands, which tend to be significantly cooler during the winter months than the summer months are.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) The weather in Panama is consistently warmer than it is in the Hawaiian Islands, where it tends to be significantly cooler during the winter months than it is during the summer months.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) Whereas, during the winter months, the weather in the Hawaiian Islands becomes significantly cooler than they are during the summer, the weather in Panama is consistently warmer.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) In Panama, the weather is consistently warmer than that of the Hawaiian Islands, which tends to be significantly cooler during the winter months than in summer.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



Correctly answering this question requires finding a version that expresses comparisons that are both complete and logical. One has also to avoid choosing a choice in which well matched sentence elements are used for the purpose of distracting the test-taker from the fact that the version does not effectively express a meaning that make sense.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Unlike the weather in the Hawaiian Islands, which tends to be significantly cooler during the winter months than the summer, the weather in Panama is consistently warmer.
</b>


The relative clause “which tends to be significantly cooler during the winter months than the summer” does not effectively express a comparison that makes sense. Since “during” is not repeated before “summer,” the clause seems to express that the weather, which “which” refers to, is cooler than the summer, a meaning that obviously does not make sense.


Also the comparison “unlike the weather in the Hawaiian islands … the weather in Panama is consistently warmer” does not make sense, because “the weather in Panama is consistently warmer” is an incomplete comparison, since the sentence does not indicate what the weather in Panama is warmer than.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>The weather in Panama is consistently warmer than that of the Hawaiian Islands, which tend to be significantly cooler during the winter months than the summer months are.
</b>


The relative clause “which tend to be significantly cooler during the winter months than the summer months are” illogically compares the Hawaiian Islands, which “which” refers to, with the summer months.


> <b>(C)</b> <b>The weather in Panama is consistently warmer than it is in the Hawaiian Islands, where it tends to be significantly cooler during the winter months than it is during the summer months.
</b>


“It” appears three times in this version and seems to refer to the weather of the Hawaiian Islands. However, this version never actually mentions the weather of the Hawaiian Islands, and the only possible referent of “it” is “the weather in Panama.” As a result, this version conveys the following nonsensical meaning:


The weather in Panama is consistently warmer than <strong>the weather in Panama</strong> is in the Hawaiian Islands, where <strong>the weather in Panama</strong> tends to be significantly cooler during the winter months than <strong>the weather in Panama</strong> is during the summer months.


Notice “it” is repeated in this incorrect version to make this version appear coherent and logical even though it isn’t. Similarly, “during the winter months” is paired with the matching “during the summer months” to add further reason for test-takers to misperceive this version to be well-constructed and logical.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>Whereas, during the winter months, the weather in the Hawaiian Islands becomes significantly cooler than they are during the summer, the weather in Panama is consistently warmer.
</b>


Since the only logical referent of “they” is “the Hawaiian Islands,” this version illogically compares how the weather in the Hawaiian Islands becomes in winter with the Hawaiian Islands themselves, as we can see by substituting “Hawaiian Islands” for “they.”


the weather in the Hawaiian Islands becomes significantly cooler than <strong>the Hawaiian Islands </strong>are during the summer


Also, “the weather in Panama is consistently warmer” does not express a complete comparison, since it does not indicate what the weather in Panama is consistently warmer than.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E)</b> <b>In Panama, the weather is consistently warmer than that of the Hawaiian Islands, which tends to be significantly cooler during the winter months than in summer.
</b>


This version expresses a logical comparison of the weather in Panama and “that,” i.e., “the weather,” of the Hawaiian Islands. It also effectively describes the weather of the Hawaiian Islands as cooler during the winter months than it is in summer.


Notice that the wording of this version is chosen with the intent of raising doubt in the mind of test-takers that this version is correct. To that end, weather is connected with Panama via the use of “in,” while weather is connected with the Hawaiian Islands via the use of “of.” Also, “during the winter months” and “in summer,” while logically similar, are not perfectly matched.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

##  Sentence Correction Example 10  

The upstart battery company asserts that, although its batteries use a technology never <u>before applied by a battery company, its batteries last longer than others</u>.

1. [x] (A) before applied by a battery company, its batteries last longer than others
2. [ ] (B) applied in batteries made by other companies, its batteries last longer
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) applied by any battery company, its batteries last longer than others do
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) before applied by battery companies, its batteries last longer
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) applied by another battery company, its batteries last longer than theirs do
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



Correctly answering this question requires not only noticing whether comparisons are complete and logical but also avoiding being distracted by the differences in comparisons into choosing a choice that is flawed in a way that does not involve a comparison.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(A)</b> The upstart battery company asserts that, although its batteries use a technology never <b>before applied by a battery company, its batteries last longer than others
</b>.



The comparison expressed by this version is complete, because it compares the upstart company’s batteries with others, i.e., other batteries.


Also, the technology is logically described as “technology never before applied by a battery company.”


(Note: The use of the singular “battery company” with the plural “others” is meant to give the test-taker the impression that this correct version of the sentence may not be logically constructed.”)


> <b>(B)</b> The upstart battery company asserts that, although its batteries use a technology never <b>applied in batteries made by other companies, its batteries last longer
</b>.



The comparison expressed by this version is incomplete, because this version does not indicate what the upstart company’s batteries “last longer” than.


> <b>(C)</b> The upstart battery company asserts that, although its batteries use a technology never <b>applied by any battery company, its batteries last longer than others do
</b>.



While the comparison expressed by this version is blatantly complete, we have to be careful not to be distracted by the obvious correctness of the comparison into choosing this choice, because this version illogically describes the technology used by the upstart company’s batteries as “never applied by any battery company.” If the upstart company’s batteries use this technology, then it has been applied by one company, and so, saying that it has never been applied does not make sense.


> <b>(D)</b> The upstart battery company asserts that, although its batteries use a technology never <b>before applied by battery companies, its batteries last longer
</b>.



The comparison expressed by this version is incomplete, because this version does not indicate what the upstart company’s batteries “last longer” than.


> <b>(E)</b> The upstart battery company asserts that, although its batteries use a technology never <b>applied by another battery company, its batteries last longer than theirs do
</b>.



In this version, since “another battery company” is singular, there is no logical referent for “theirs.”


Also, “never applied by another battery company” seems meant to express that there is a a specific other battery company that has not used the technology. “Never applied by any other battery company” more effectively expresses that no other battery company of all battery companies has used the technology.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

Let’s now discuss sentences that express comparisons that are incomplete even though the items meant to be compared are all mentioned.
