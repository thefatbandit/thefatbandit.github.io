## Lesson Content
There is another move involving incomplete comparisons that the GMAT can make.

> <u>Incorrect</u>: When it is compared with eating butter, eating olive oil is healthier.

Notice what’s going on in the above example.

Yes, the sentence says “compared with eating butter,” but “compared with eating butter” is part of a modifying clause, “when it is compared with eating butter,” that modifies “eating olive oil is healthier.”

So, read literally, the sentence conveys that eating olive oil is healthier than something else – the sentence does not say what else – at certain times.

So, even though the sentence mentions both olive oil and butter, strictly speaking, the comparison is not complete.

> Eating olive oil is healthier than eating butter.

Here’s another example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: Although tigers can run fast, when compared with tigers, cheetahs run much faster.

In this case, we have a sentence that may seem to express a comparison of how fast cheetahs run with how fast tigers run, but what it actually conveys is that, at certain times, those times when cheetahs are compared with tigers, cheetahs run faster than something not mentioned by the sentence.

A correct version would directly and clearly compare how fast cheetahs run with how fast tigers run, as does the following:

> Although tigers can run fast, cheetahs run much faster than tigers.

Let’s consider one more example:

> <u>Incorrect</u>: The bones of birds, if compared with those of other animals, are much different.

The above example expresses the illogical meaning that the bones of birds are much different from something unmentioned, but only if the bones of birds are compared with those of other animals.

> The bones of birds are much different from those of other animals.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

In analyzing sentences that involve comparisons, we have to be careful not to be fooled by sentences that mention the items meant to be compared yet don’t actually express complete comparisons of those items.
{{< /alert >}}

Of course, not every sentence that uses wording like “when compared” or “if compared” expresses an incomplete comparison.

> The Torre Eurosky, the tallest building in Rome, towers over buildings around it, but <strong>when the Torre Eurosky is compared with the Freedom Tower in New York City</strong>, the Torre Eurosky may seem rather small.

In the above example, the use of the modifying clause “when the Torre Eurosky is compared with the Freedom Tower in New York City” makes sense, because the point of the sentence is that, at certain times, the times when the Torre Eurosky is compared with the Freedom Tower, the Torre Eurosky may seem rather small.

{{< alert type="warning" >}}
💡<b>MUST KNOW:</b>

Not every sentence that uses wording like “when compared” or “if compared” expresses an incomplete comparison. In some cases, sentences that use such wording are perfectly logical.
{{< /alert >}}

##  Sentence Correction Example 11  

Some people claim that <u>healthcare executives are not as concerned about patients as are nurses</u>.

1. [x] (A) healthcare executives are not as concerned about patients as are nurses
2. [ ] (B) when comparing healthcare executives with nurses, they’re not as concerned about patients
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
3. [ ] (C) healthcare executives are not as concerned about patients as nurses
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
4. [ ] (D) nurses are more concerned about patients than healthcare executives
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)
5. [ ] (E) nurses are more concerned about patients when they are compared with healthcare executives
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (A)

##### Question Level: medium



> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(A)</b> Some people claim that <b>healthcare executives are not as concerned about patients as are nurses
</b>.



 Although this version may sound a little awkward because of the way in which the second “are” is placed, the use of the second “are” results in the sentence’s clearly expressing a comparison of how concerned about patients healthcare executives are with how concerned nurses are.


> <b>(B)</b> Some people claim that <b>when comparing healthcare executives with nurses, they’re not as concerned about patients
</b>.



 Whether “they” refers to “healthcare executives,” to “nurses,” or to some other people who are “comparing healthcare executives with nurses” is not clear.


Also, regardless of whom “they” refers to, the sentence conveys the illogical meaning that “they,” whoever they are, are not as concerned about patients at times when “they” are comparing healthcare executives with nurses.


Also, the comparison expressed by this version is incomplete, as this version does not indicate whom or what “they” are not as concerned as.


> <b>(C)</b> Some people claim that <b>healthcare executives are not as concerned about patients as nurses
</b>.



 Because neither “are” nor “about” is repeated in this version, the meaning conveyed by this version is ambiguous, and could be either of the following:


Healthcare executives are not as concerned about patients as nurses <strong>are about patients</strong>.


Healthcare executives are not as concerned about patients as <strong>healthcare executives are about</strong> nurses<strong>.</strong>


> <b>(D)</b> Some people claim that <b>nurses are more concerned about patients than healthcare executives
</b>.



 Because neither “are” nor “about” is repeated in this version, the meaning conveyed by this version is ambiguous, and could be either of the following:


Nurses are more concerned about patients than healthcare executives <strong>are</strong>.


Nurses are more concerned about patients than <strong>about </strong>healthcare executives<strong>.</strong>


(Note: This choice is not incorrect because it expresses a meaning slightly different from that expressed by the other choices. It’s incorrect solely because it does not effectively express one clear meaning.)


> <b>(E)</b> Some people claim that <b>nurses are more concerned about patients when they are compared with healthcare executives
</b>.



 The comparison expressed by this version is incomplete, as, although both nurses and healthcare executives are mentioned, the sentence does not express a comparison of the people in the two categories. Rather it expresses the illogical meaning that nurses are more concerned about patients than someone or something not mentioned at times when “they,” whoever “they” may be, are compared with healthcare executives.


Of course, the fact that “they” could refer to “nurses” or “patients” is another issue had by this version.


> ##### Correct Answer: (A)

##  Sentence Correction Example 12  

<u>Similar to a cat, a fox’s tail helps them to balance when they are</u> running, jumping or pouncing.

1. [ ] (A) Similar to a cat, a fox’s tail helps them to balance when they are
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) When compared with that of a cat, a fox’s tail is similar, helping the fox to balance when
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) A fox’s tail is similar to that of a cat, which helps the fox to balance when
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) Like a cat’s tail, a fox’s tail is similar, and helps it to balance when
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) A fox’s tail, similar to that of a cat, helps it to balance when

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

##### Question Level: hard



In answering this question, we have to be careful not to choose a choice that seems to express a complete comparison but doesn’t really, even though it uses comparison type wording and mentions both items meant to be compared.


> <b>(A)</b> <b>Similar to a cat, a fox’s tail helps them to balance when they are
</b> running, jumping or pouncing.



This version illogically describes a fox’s tail as “similar to a cat.”


Also, the plural pronouns “them” and “they” have no logical referent, since the fox’s tail would not help both a cat and a fox to balance, and so, there is no plural noun or combination of nouns to which “them” and “they” would refer. “Them” and “they” seem to stand in for “foxes,” but the sentence is about the tail of a single fox, not the tails of multiple foxes.


> <b>(B)</b> <b>When compared with that of a cat, a fox’s tail is similar, helping the fox to balance when
</b> running, jumping or pouncing.



While this version mentions both “a fox’s tail” and “that of a cat,” it does not actually compare the two. Rather it conveys the illogical meaning that a fox’s tail is similar to something not mentioned, at a time when a fox’s tail is compared with the tail of a cat.


Also, while the use of the participial phrase “helping the fox to balance” may seem correct, especially since it uses “fox” rather than “it,” the pronoun that the correct answer, (E), uses, the truth is that, via the use of that participial phrase, the sentence conveys the nonsensical meaning that, by being similar (to something not mentioned), the fox’s tail helps the fox to balance, as we can see more easily by considering that part of the sentence in isolation:


a fox’s tail is similar, helping the fox to balance


> <b>(C)</b> <b>A fox’s tail is similar to that of a cat, which helps the fox to balance when
</b> running, jumping or pouncing.



The comparison expressed by this version makes sense, and so, this version is perfect up to the comma. However, the modifier after the comma has no logical target, and seems to modify “cat,” with the result that the sentence seems to convey the nonsensical meaning that a cat helps the fox to balance.


Even if we were to decide that “which helps the fox” modifies the entire preceding clause, a type of modification that the GMAT does not prefer, it would not really make sense to say that the fact that a fox’s tail is similar to that of a cat helps the fox to balance.


> <b>(D)</b> <b>Like a cat’s tail, a fox’s tail is similar, and helps it to balance when
</b> running, jumping or pouncing.



This version mentions both a cat’s tail and a fox’s tail, but is illogical, because it says that “a fox’s tail is similar” without indicating what a fox’s tail is similar to. It may seem to convey that a fox’s tail is similar to a cat’s tail, but actually conveys that a fox’s tail is like a cat’s tail in that both a cat’s tail and a fox’s tail are similar to something not mentioned. So, the comparison expressed by “a fox’s tail is similar” is incomplete.


> <span class="correct-answer"><i class="fal fa-check-circle"></i><span class="ms-1 fw-semibold correctness-text">CORRECT ANSWER</span></span><b>(E)</b> <b>A fox’s tail, similar to that of a cat, helps it to balance when
</b> running, jumping or pouncing.



This version clearly conveys that a fox’s tail helps a fox to balance.


The use of the modifier “similar to that of a cat” is perhaps slightly awkward, but “that” clearly refers to “tail” and the modifier does clearly modify “a fox’s tail,” in a manner similar to the manner in which “red and shiny” modifies “cherry” in the following example:


A cherry, red and shiny, sat on the plate.


Also, it may seem that which noun or pronoun the pronoun “it” refers to is ambiguous, since “fox’s,” “tail,” “that,” and “cat” all precede “it” in the sentence. However, since “fox’s tail” is the clearly the subject of “helps,” and since the fox’s tail would help the fox, we can tell that “it” refers to “fox.”


Also, although, in this version, the referent for the personal pronoun “it” is the possessive “fox’s,” having a personal pronoun refer to the possessive form of a noun is considered acceptable in GMAT Sentence Correction.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)
