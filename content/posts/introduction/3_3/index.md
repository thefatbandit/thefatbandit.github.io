## Lesson Content
Most of the time, recognizing a Weaken the Argument question is easy, because these questions utilize predictable language.

> <em>Which of the following, if true, most seriously weakens the argument above?</em>

> <em>Which of the following, if true, casts the most doubt on the conclusion drawn above?</em>

> <em>Which of the following, if true, does the most to call into question the claim made?</em>

> <em>Which of the following, if discovered, would be evidence against the speculation above?</em>

> <em>Which of the following, if true, most seriously undermines the support for the above conclusion?</em>

> <em>Which of the following, if true, would indicate a vulnerability of the speaker’s argument?</em>

> <em>Which of the following, if true, most seriously weakens the force of the speaker’s evidence?</em>

It’s pretty easy to see that all of these stems ask us to weaken, in some way, an argument.

💡Learn to recognize the common and predictable language used by Weaken the Argument questions.

##  Example 3 

Although campfires are illegal in Aurora Falls National Park, campfires often start forest fires in the park.

Which of the following, if true, most seriously weakens the argument above?

1. [ ] (A) Aurora Falls National Park has provided its rangers with extensive training in the safe operation of aerial drones as well as in the best practices in campfire extinguishment.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
2. [ ] (B) A significant percentage of the forest fires that occur in Aurora Falls National park are not started by campfires.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
3. [ ] (C) Statistics compiled by Aurora Falls National Park rangers show that forest fires started by campfires have been responsible for the destruction of thousands of acres of park property.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)
4. [x] (D) Because of Aurora Falls National Park’s large size, park rangers require 30 minutes on average to reach most park locations, during which time campfire sparks can easily start forest fires.
5. [ ] (E) The aerial drones utilize advanced heat sensors that some militaries deploy on satellites used to detect the launch of surface-based missiles.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (D)

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

<u>Background</u>: Although campfires are illegal in Aurora Falls National Park, campfires often start forest fires in the park.

<u>Premise</u>: Tomorrow, park rangers will begin using aerial drones that detect campfires and relay coordinates of campfire locations to park rangers, who will go to and extinguish the campfires.

<u>Conclusion</u>: Surely, going forward, only rarely will campfires start forest fires in the park.

Notice that this question is clearly a Weaken the Argument question, because the question stem asks, “Which of the following, if true, most seriously weakens the argument above?”

The author has presented a plan. Any time an author presents a plan, she assumes that the plan will work. In other words, she assumes that there are no variables that will spoil the plan. In the TTP Course, we refer to such variables as “malicious variables.” When an author presents a plan, she assumes that there are no malicious variables.

What has the author failed to consider? What malicious variable has gone unnoticed?


> (A) Aurora Falls National Park has provided its rangers with extensive training in the safe operation of aerial drones as well as in the best practices in campfire extinguishment.

What this answer choice says strengthens the argument.

If Aurora Falls National Park has provided its rangers with extensive training in the safe operation of aerial drones as well as in the best practices in campfire extinguishment, then the rangers have some important training related to the plan presented in the argument. That fact makes it likely that the plan will be successful.

Of course, we’re not seeking a strengthener. We’re seeking an answer choice that weakens the argument.


> (B) A significant percentage of the forest fires that occur in Aurora Falls National park are not started by campfires.

Notice that the conclusion of the argument is not that, in general, forest fires will rarely occur in the park. It’s that, going forward, only rarely *will campfires start* forest fires in the park.

So, the fact that a significant percentage of the forest fires that occur in Aurora Falls National park are not started by campfires does not affect the support for the conclusion at all, because even if things other than campfires start forest fires in the park, the evidence presented still supports the conclusion, which is specifically about forest fires started by campfires.

So, this choice has no effect on the support for the conclusion.


> (C) Statistics compiled by Aurora Falls National Park rangers show that forest fires started by campfires have been responsible for the destruction of thousands of acres of park property.

The conclusion is simply that, going forward, only rarely will campfires start forest fires in the park.

Our job in answering the question is to weaken the support for that conclusion.

This choice tells us how much destruction forest fires caused by campfires have caused in the past. Does that information indicate that putting out campfires won’t prevent them from starting forest fires “going forward”? Of course not. No matter how much destruction campfires have caused in the past, putting them out could prevent them from causing forest fires in the future.

So, what this answer choice says does not affect the support for the conclusion or weaken the argument at all.


> (D) Because of Aurora Falls National Park’s large size, park rangers require 30 minutes on average to reach most park locations, during which time campfire sparks can easily start forest fires.

What this answer choice says certainly weakens the argument.

The argument’s conclusion is that, going forward, only rarely will campfires start forest fires in the park.

So, imagine that one of the drones spots a campfire, and a park ranger mobilizes to get to that location. If, as this choice says, park rangers require 30 minutes on average to reach most park locations, during which time campfire sparks can easily start forest fires, then a campfire could easily start a forest fire prior to the ranger’s reaching the campfire.

So, this choice provides a good reason to question whether the plan will work. Notice also that this answer choice presents a malicious variable that the author has failed to consider.


> (E) The aerial drones utilize advanced heat sensors that some militaries deploy on satellites used to detect the launch of surface-based missiles.

If anything, what this answer choice says strengthens the argument.

After all, if the thermal cameras on the aerial drones utilize advanced heat sensors that some militaries deploy on satellites used to detect the launch of surface-based missiles, then these heat sensors must be pretty sophisticated. If the heat sensors are sophisticated, then the aerial drones equipped with these thermal cameras would likely do a good job of detecting campfires in the park. Therefore, given what this choice says, we have more conviction that the plan will work.

Of course, in answering this question, we’re not seeking a strengthener; we need to weaken the argument.


> ##### Correct Answer: (D)

##  Example 4 

It is much easier to cultivate, i.e., plough and otherwise prepare for planting, fields from which any rocks near the surface have been removed.

Which of the following, if true, most seriously undermines the above conclusion?

1. [ ] (A) The people of Rapa Nui were dependent on farming, as there were on the island few trees from which could be built boats for fishing.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
2. [ ] (B) When Europeans first arrived on Rapa Nui, they found a thriving population.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
3. [ ] (C) Many of the pieces of rock found in the fields used for farming on Rapa Nui were small enough that they could have easily been removed by an individual working alone.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
4. [ ] (D) The people of Rapa Nui carved from volcanic rock large statues, which they placed at regular intervals on the island.
    > <br> <b>Correct Answer:</b> (E)
5. [x] (E) Volcanic rock gives off small amounts of phosphorus, a plant nutrient the levels of which are generally quite low in the soil found on Rapa Nui.

{{< /quizdown >}}

   <h5><details><summary><b><u>View Solution</u></b></summary>
   {{< alert type="secondary" >}}

The question stem asks us to find the answer that “most seriously undermines the conclusion.” So, this question is a Weaken question, and we have to find a way to weaken the argument.

The conclusion that we have to undermine is:

<u>Conclusion</u>: It must be the case that the people who lived on Rapa Nui did not develop expertise in agriculture.

The conclusion is supported by that fact that fields on Rapa Nui used for centuries for farming were found to be full of rocks, even though it’s much easier to cultivate fields from which any surface rocks have been removed.

There is a gap in this argument, in that it jumps from the fact that the fields were found to be full of rocks to the conclusion that the people of Rapa Nui did not develop expertise in agriculture.

Notice that the author assumes that the only reason why there would be rocks in fields used for farming is that the people farming had not developed expertise in agriculture. However, if there was an alternative explanation for why there would be rocks in fields, then the argument would be weakened.


> (A) The people of Rapa Nui were dependent on farming, as there were on the island few trees from which could be built boats for fishing.

This choice could be very tempting, as one might be tempted to believe that, if the people of Rapa Nui were dependent on farming, it’s likely that they developed expertise in agriculture.

However, the truth is that what this choice says does not contradict what the passage says or attack the connection between the fact that there were rocks in the fields and the conclusion.

After all, the people of Rapa Nui could have been dependent on farming and yet not developed expertise in agriculture.

So, regardless of how dependent on farming the people of Rapa Nui were, the premise, that the fields were full of rocks, still indicates that they didn’t develop expertise in agriculture.


> (B) When Europeans first arrived on Rapa Nui, they found a thriving population.

This choice could be tempting, as one might be tempted to believe that, if the people of Rapa Nui were thriving, it’s likely that they developed expertise in agriculture.

However, the truth is that what this choice says does not contradict what the passage says or attack the connection between the fact that there were rocks in the fields and the conclusion.

After all, the people could have been thriving without developing expertise in agriculture.

So, regardless of whether the people of Rapa Nui were thriving, the premise, that the fields were full of rocks, still indicates that they didn’t develop expertise in agriculture.

> (C) Many of the pieces of rock found in the fields used for farming on Rapa Nui were small enough that they could have easily been removed by an individual working alone.

This choice strengthens the support for the conclusion, since it eliminates an alternative reason for the rocks’ being in the fields: they would have been difficult to remove.

By eliminating the possibility that the rocks were in the fields, not because people left them there out of lack of agricultural expertise, but because they would have been difficult to move, this choice strengthens the case for the conclusion that the people of Rapa Nui did not develop expertise in agriculture.


> (D) The people of Rapa Nui carved from volcanic rock large statues, which they placed at regular intervals on the island.

This choice could be tempting, as one might be tempted to believe that the presence of the rocks in the fields was some consequence of the creation of the volcanic rock statues, and that, therefore, it somehow makes sense that the rocks would be in the fields even if the people of Rapa Nui had developed expertise in agriculture.

The truth is, however, that it is not clear that the presence of the rocks in the fields has anything to do with the creation of the statues.

Furthermore, even if the presence of the rocks in the fields was a consequence of the creation of the statues, the presence of the rocks in fields used for farming could still support the conclusion that the people of Rapa Nui did not develop expertise in agriculture. After all, regardless of how the rocks got there, the fields use for agriculture were full of rocks even though cultivating the fields would presumably have been much easier if the rocks had been removed.


> (E) Volcanic rock gives off small amounts of phosphorus, a plant nutrient the levels of which are generally quite low in the soil found on Rapa Nui.

This choice severely undermines the support for the conclusion, as it very effectively attacks the assumption that the only reason why there would be rocks in fields used for farming is that the people farming had not developed expertise in agriculture.

The choice attacks that assumption by providing reason to believe that, actually, the rocks were in the fields because the people of Rapa Nui had developed expertise in agriculture.

By stating that the levels of the plant nutrient phosphorus are generally quite low in the soil found on Rapa Nui, and that volcanic rocks give off phosphorus, this choice provides a reason to believe that the rocks were purposely left in or placed in the fields to provide plants growing in the fields with phosphorus, an action that would be taken by farmers, not out of ignorance, but rather because they had expertise.

So, by showing that the fact that there were volcanic rocks in the fields could indicate that the people of Rapa Nui had expertise in farming, this choice seriously undermines the support for the conclusion.


> ##### Correct Answer: (E)

Let’s now discuss a strategy for answering Weaken the Argument questions.
