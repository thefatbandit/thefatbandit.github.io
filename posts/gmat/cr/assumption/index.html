CR-9 Assumption

  • MBA
  • GMAT
  • CR

An assumption is simply an unstated premise that must be true in order for the argument to be true. It is an integral component of the argument that the author takes for granted and leaves unsaid.

💡 The answer to an assumption question must be something that the author must believe in order for the conclusion to be properly drawn.

The assumptions are minimal & can’t contain extraneous statements

Diff. b/w Strengthen & Assumption Questions

Strengthen questions ask us to support the argument in any way possible. The support can be anything from 1% - 100%.

On the other hand, assumption questions ask you to identify a statement that the argument assumes or supposes.

  • Example

    • Argument: That teenager is an outstanding golfer.

    • Assumption: The teenager almost always hits the ball. The teenager almost never swings and misses the ball.

    • Strengthen: The teenager won a local club tournament.

Assumption Vs. Must Be True

The difference can be described as Before Vs. After. In an assumption question, we are required to find the statements that were used to make the conclusion. Whereas, in a Must Be True question we select the answers that follow from the argument made in the stimulus.

Question Stems

  • “The argument in the passage depends on which of the following assumptions?”
  • “The argument above assumes that”
  • “The conclusion above is based on which of the following assumptions?”
  • “Which of the following is an assumption made in drawing the conclusion above?”
  • “The conclusion of the argument above cannot be true unless which of the following is true?”

The Supporter/Defender Model

Supporter/Defender are the two roles that assumptions play in GMAT.

  1. Supporter Assumptions (Easier to identify): It is the traditionally linking role where an assumption connects the pieces of the argument. They often connect “new”/”rogue” pieces of information in the argument that appears only in the conclusion or the premise.
Example

“All male citizens of Athens had the right to vote. Therefore, Socrates had the right to vote in Athens.”

The linking assumption here is that Socrates was a male citizen of Athens. It connects the premise element of male citizens have Right to vote and the conclusion that Socrates had the same right.

💡 Should you ever see a gap/new element in the conclusion, a Supporter assumption answer will close the gap/connect the new element.
  1. Defender Assumptions: They protect the argument by eliminating ideas that could weaken the argument.
Example

“People who read a lot are more intelligent than other people. Thus, reading must cause a person to be intelligent.”

There are a lot of ideas that can undermine the author’s argument:

  • Sleeping more than eight hours causes a person to be intelligent.
  • Regular exercise causes a person to be intelligent.

But they are assumed to be not possible by the authors. They, therefore, make the following assumptions in the original argument:

  • Sleeping more than eight hours does not cause a person to be intelligent.
  • Regular exercise does not cause a person to be intelligent.

These statements “defend” the argument by showing that a possible avenue of attack has been eliminated.

💡 If there are no discernable weakness in the argument, expect to see a defender answer choice.
🚨 NOTE: You can pre-phase on the supporter answer choices. But for the defender answers, there are too many possibilities to consider.

The Assumption Negation Technique

Through this, we can decide between Contenders or confirm our answer choice. It changes an assumption question to a Weaken question type. To apply the technique:

  1. Logically negate the answer choices under consideration
Example

Statement: The congressman always votes for gun control

Negation: The congressman does not always vote for gun control

  1. The negated answer choice that attacks the argument is the correct answer
🚨 The Assumption Negation Technique is a tedious process & should only be applied on contenders & NOT every answer choice.

Logical Opposites

Logical opposites are different from antonyms (or as they are called “polar” opposites)

Eg. What is the logical opposite of sweet? - Not sweet.

We might be tempted to give “sour” as an answer but that doesn’t cover everything (bland, bitter) which are not under the sweet category.

  • Logical Opposites
    AllNot all
    SomeNone
    AlwaysNot always
    SometimesNever
    EverywhereNot everywhere
    SomewhereNowhere
    willmight
  • Practice: Find the logical negations of the following statements

  • The tax increase will result in more revenue for the government

    • Solution
      The tax increase might result in more revenue for the government
  • All of the missions succeeded
    • Solution

      Polar: None of the missions succeeded

      Logical: Not all of the missions succeeded

  • The positive effects of the US Immigration policy are everywhere
    • Solution

      The positive effects of the US Immigration policy are not everywhere

      NOTE: “The negative effects of the US Immigration policy are everywhere” might seem like a negation of the original statement but isn’t

Quirks of Assumption Question Answer Choices

  1. Watch for “at least one” or “at least some” answers

    The chances of such answers being correct is unusually high.

🚨 But don’t just blindly assume it to be the answer. Use the proper negation to check the answer.

  1. Avoid “the primary purpose”, “top priority”, or “the main factor” answers claiming most important consideration for the author

    These answers have always been wrong. And unless the author discusses prioritization of ideas in the stimulus, these answers will continue to be wrong since an author can always claim that the idea under discussion was not necessarily the most important.

  2. Watch the use of “not” or -ves in assumption answer choices

    Do not rule out a -ve answer choice just because you are used to seeing assumptions as a +ve part of the argument. Defender choices generally contain -ve terms like “no”, “not”, and “never” which make them easy to negation technique.

Correct Answer Choices (Assumptions & Causality)

Because the author always assumes that the stated cause is the only cause, assumption answer choices tend to work exactly like Strengthen answer choices in arguments with causal reasoning.

So the correct answer types in Assumptions are (Same as Strengthen):

  1. Cause_2 -/> Effect Eliminate alternate causes for the stated effect
  2. Cause → Effect Show that when the cause occurs, the effect does occur
  3. Effect -/> Cause Show that the stated relationship can not be reversed
  4. Statistical Accuracy: Show that the data used to make the causal statement is accurate

Fill in the blanks

Fill in the Blanks are almost always assumption questions in GMAT. And when they are not assumption type, they are often Must Be True/Main Point questions.

The stimulus is preceded by the question stem which basically tells you to fill the missing blank. The blank is placed at the last. There is a premise indicator at the start of the final sentence or just before the blank that helps us in recognizing the missing premise to fill.

In rare cases, GMAT makers will on occasion use to turn certain FIB questions into Weaken-FIB questions.

“However, this is a poor approach, because ________”

“Hence, the proposal should be discarded since _________”

Practice

    (B)- The ease of learning has no context here.

    (C)- The author’s argument concerns what tourists can do to assure themselves that they have done the most they can in order to assist in communicating with Zorba’s locals. Whether tourists are committed to taking those steps is not part of the argument.
