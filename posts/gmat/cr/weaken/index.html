CR-6 Weaken

  • MBA
  • GMAT
  • CR

Weaken type question require you to select the answer choice that undermines the author’s argument as decisively as possible.

💡 MOST FREQUENT CR QUESTION TYPE

Rules to Solve

  1. The stimulus will always contain an argument since you are asked to weaken the author’s reasoning.
  2. Focus on the conclusion. The more we know about its specifics, the better armed we will be to deciding the correct answer choice.
  3. Information in the stimulus is suspect. Read the arguments carefully
  4. 🚨Pre-phase possible answers🚨 before proceeding to the answer choices
  5. 💡Answer choices are accepted as given even if they include “new information”.

Identifying Question Type

  1. The stem uses words synonymous to “weaken”.
  • “Weaken” synonyms: Weaken, Attack, Undermine, Refute, Call into question, cast doubt, challenge, damage, counter
  1. The stem usually indicates that you should accept the answer choices as true

Examples

  • “Which of the following if true, most seriously weakens the argument above?”
  • “Which of the following, if true, casts the most doubt on the conclusion drawn above?”
  • “Which of the following, if true, most calls into question the claim above?”
  • Which of the following, if true, is most damaging to the conclusion above?
  • “Which of the following, if known, is evidence that contradicts the hypothesis above?”
  • “Which of the following, if discovered, would be evidence against the speculation above?”

How to weaken an argument

The key is to weaken not attack the conclusion

🚨 Attack is not the same as destroy (Attack ≠ Destroy). The answer choices most likely hurt the argument.

When evaluating an answer ask “Would this answer choice make the author reconsider his/her position or force the author to respond?”

The Premises

Attacking the premises on which the conclusion rests. Rare occurrence since it makes easy spotting of answer choice.

💡 One of the few rare times a premise is attacked, it is a sub-conclusion that is being used to drive in the main conclusion

🚨The Conclusion🚨

Most likely. But the correct answer choice doesn’t simply contradict the conclusion. But rather, it often does so by showing that the conclusion fails to account for some element or possibility.

How to attack a causal conclusion

There are a few ways you can prepare to weaken/strengthen a causal relationship in GMAT:

  1. Cause_2 → Effect Find an alternate cause for the stated effect
  2. Cause → Effect Show that even when the cause occurs, the effect does not occur
  3. Cause → Effect Show that though the effect occurred, the cause did not
  4. Effect → Cause Show that the stated relationship is reversed
  5. Stat Problem: Show that a statistical problem exists with the data used to make the causal statement

Common Weakening Scenarios

The most common logical fallacies that are to be attacked in GMAT Weaken question stimuli are:

  1. Incomplete Information: When author fails to consider all possibilities or relies upon incomplete evidence. This flaw is attacked by bringing up new possibilities or information.
  2. Improper Comparison: Author attempts to compare items that are essentially different
  3. Overly Broad Comparison: The author draws a conclusion that is broader than the premises support.

Answer Traps

  1. Opposite Answers: These answer choices are presenting information that relates perfectly to the arguments, but instead of weakening it, they are strengthening it.
  2. Shell Game Answers: The idea/concept raised is similar to the ones present in the stimulus but changed enough to be incorrect yet attractive.
  3. Out of Scope Answers: They simply miss the point of the argument & raise unrelated/tangential issues to the argument

Practice

Solution

(D)

The stimulus presents the somewhat unusual payment system proposed by Axis Auto. The question stem asks us to attack the proposed payment system.

(A)- Nothing to do with the amount of money drivers of starlight are likely to spend on gas

(B)- Irrelevant since the question stem asks us to attack the Axis Auto proposal. Other manufacturers’ plan in coming years has nothing to do with the proposal

(C)- This actually benefits Axis since greater miles in the fuel-efficient car implies greater savings on gas & thus increasing their profits.

(D)- This is the correct answer. If the price of gasoline increases over 3 years, the overall difference in cost will be reduced.

(E)- No real affect on the proposal.

Solution

(D)

For the statement to be appealing to state schools, the legislators have assumed that they find greater financial benefits in getting the tax incentives

(A)- The fact doesn’t attack the presumed tax benefits in any way

(B)- A comparison of private & public school’s tuition costs does not undermine the proposed benefits

(C)- This actually supports the legislators’ beliefs by showing the difficulty applicants face in attending out-of-state universities.

(D)- This is the correct choice since it questions upon the supposed tax benefits being more financially viable than taking in other state applicants.

(E)- Supports the argument by presenting facts in favor of increasing in-state applicants.
