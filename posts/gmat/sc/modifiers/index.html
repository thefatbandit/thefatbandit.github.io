SC-4 Modifiers

  • MBA
  • GMAT
  • SC

A modifier describes/provides extra information about something else in the sentence. Incorrectly used modifiers lead to ambiguity or illogical meaning.

💡 Many modifiers answer the questions who, what, when, where, or why

Adjectives & Adverbs

These are one-word modifiers.

  • Adjective → ONLY Noun/Pronoun

  • Adverb → Almost anything EXCEPT Noun/Pronoun

    • Verbs, Adjectives
    • Prepositional Phrases
    • Entire Clauses

Many adverbs are formed by adding “-ly” to the adjective

💡 Often Adjective ↔ Adverb use will be interchanged as the sentence error.

These errors are often grammatically correct but can be found out on logical basis.

Eg: Max's grandmother is his supposed Irish ancestor. ☑️ | Max's grandmother is his supposedly Irish ancestor. ✅✅

  1. Max’s grandmother is not actually his ancestor. (Doesn’t make sense)

  2. Max’s grandmother is not actually Irish (plausible).

Some Common Adjective ↔ Adverb Words

correspondingfrequentindependent

Noun Modifiers

Apart from adjectives, there are some phrases that act as noun modifiers too.

TypeExampleMeaning
PrepositionSue placed the CAT on the counch.Where if the cat? (On the couch)
Past ParticipleThe CAT owned by Sue is playful.Whose cat? (the one owned by Sue)
Present Participle without CommasThe CAT sleeping on the rug belongs to Sue.Which cat? (the one sleeping on the rug)
💡 Noun modifiers answer: WHO, WHAT, WHICH, OR WHERE

Essential Vs. Non-Essential Modifiers

  • Removing essential modifiers from a sentence may compromise its meaning.
  • The meaning is still retained on removing non-essential modifiers.
    • “comma-which” is a non-essential modifier.

Position of Noun Modifiers

The reader must know exactly which noun is the modifier modifying.

🚨 Noun Modifiers & Nouns should be very close to each other.

Eg:

 ❌ A hard worker and loyal team player, the new project was managed by Sue.

 ✅ A hard worker and loyal team player, Sue managed the new project.

💡 In case of multiple modifiers, the essential modifier is kept closer to the noun.

 ☑️ Jim biked along an old dirt road to get to his house, which cut through the woods.
 ✅✅ To get to his house, Jim biked along an old dirt road, which cut through the woods.

Possessive Nouns ≠ Nouns

💡 Possessive Nouns can never be the subject of a modifier.

Possessive Nouns = Adjectives

Eg:
❌ Happy about his raise, Bill’s celebration included taking his friends out to dinner.
 ✅ Happy about his raise, Bill celebrated by taking his friends to dinner.

In the above sentence the modifier “happy about his raise” should be referring to Bill. But it seems like it’s modifying “Bill’s Celebration”.

Noun Modifier Markers

Relative Pronouns

WhichThatWho
WhoseWhomWhere
When

Except for that, these words almost always signal noun modifiers.

  • Who/Whom: Only people.
  • That/Whose: People or Things.
    • The town whose water supply was contaminated
    • The [son of a gun] that stole my lunch deserves death.
  • Which/Whom: Prepositions. “The canal through which water flows” & “the senator for whom we worked”
🚨 Which ≠ “people” (Only use it for prepositions)
  • Where: Noun Place.

🚨 “Where” ≠ metaphorical place: condition, situation, case, circumstances, or arrangement

Use “Which” in these situations.

 ❌ We had an arrangement WHERE he cooked and I cleaned.
 ✅ We had an arrangement IN WHICH he cooked and I cleaned.

  • When/in which: Noun event/time.

Prepositions and Participles

Can be noun/adverbial modifiers.

  • 💡A preposition after a noun modifies the noun.

Participles

Participle Forms

VerbPresent Participle (-ing)Past Participle
to playplayingplayed
to managemanagingmanaged
to beginbeginningbegun
  • -ing word functioning as part of the verb form will have another verb immediately before it.
    • -ing words that are NOT verbs & not separated from the rest of the sentence by a comma will either be a noun/noun modifier.
  • Past participles, or - ed words, can be verb by themselves. But CANNOT function as a noun.
Eg:
Exhausted from her job, she bought a lottery ticket with hopes of winning big. (exhausted…job = adverbial modifier)

Adverbial Modifiers

These modify anything that isn’t just a plain noun. (Everything ≠ Noun)

💡 They answer questions like how, where, when, or why an action occurred.

In general adverbial modifiers can be placed anywhere in the sentence and don’t have the positional bound like noun modifiers.

It is possible to place adverbial modifiers poorly, though.

 ❌ He walked and caught up with his sister more rapidly.
 ✅ He walked more rapidly and caught with his sister.

Sequence in Participle Modifiers

Adverbial modifiers that use participles. So the the cause-effect relationship of these should be clearly visible.

 ✅ Slipping on the ice, she broke her ankle.
 ❌ Breaking her ankle, she slipped on the ice.
 ✅ She slipped on the ice, breaking her ankle.

Here Slipping on Ice→Breaking her ankle & not the other way round.

Subordinators

These clauses modify the main clause to which they are attached.

Eg: Although the economy is strong, the RETAIL INDUSTRY IS STRUGGLING.

It is almost like a complete sentence but has a subordinator (although) at the beginning.

althoughbeforeunlessbecausethatso that
ifyetafterwhilesincewhen

💡 The subordinator and the clause-subbordinator relationship should be consistent.

Although the economy is strong, the retail industry is doing well. ❌

Errors

  • Multiple Connecting words

    ALTHOUGH I need to relax, YET I have so many things to do! ❌

  • Connecting with the right meaning

    If the sentence parts are an opposition we should expect contrasting connecting words between them.

     She is not interested in playing sports, AND she likes watching them on TV. ❌
     She is not interested in playing sports, BUT she likes watching them on TV. ✅
     ALTHOUGH she is not interested in playing sports, she likes watching them on TV.✅

  • 💡 Main Clause + (Not a Clause)

    Citizens of many countries are expressing concern about the environmental damage cause by the widespread release of greenhouse gases may be impossible to reverse. ❌

    Here there is no subject for the clause “may be impossible to reverse”. You may want to think that environmental damage is the subject. But its part of a prepositional phrase & can never be the subject of a clause.

    So we use either remove the prepositional phrase:

    Citizens of many countries are expressing concern THAT the environmental damage cause by the widespread release of greenhouse gases may be impossible to reverse

    Or make the 2nd clause an additional clause:

    Citizens of many countries are expressing concern about the environmental damage cause by the widespread release of greenhouse gases, DAMAGE THAT may be impossible to reverse.

🚨Which🚨 (Difficult to trap)

WHICH always refers to the closest preceding noun.

Eg:

 ❌ Crime has recently decreased in our neighborhood, WHICH has led to a rise in property values.

But here it is being used to refer to an entire clause.

🚨 Use WHICH only to refer to nouns — never to refer to an entire clause.

Corrections

  1. Turn the first thought into a noun phrase and make this phrase the subject of the verb, eliminating WHICH altogether:

    The recent decrease in crime in our neighbourhood has led to a rise in property values.

  2. Use an adverbial modifier to refer to the whole clause:

    Crime has recently decreased in our neighbourhood, leading to a rise in property values.

Modifiers Vs. Core

2 long non-essential modifiers in a row.

Eg:

 ❌ George Carlin, both shocking and entertaining audiences across the nation, who also struggled publicly with drug abuse, influenced and inspired a generation of comedians.

We can place 1 modifier before & 1 after the noun: (Modifier ↔ Noun ↔ Modifier)

 ✅ Both shocking and entertaining audiences across the nation, George Carlin, who also struggled publicly with drug abuse, influenced and inspired a generation of comedians.

On the tougher questions, one modifier can be made part of the sentence:

 ✅ Both shocking and entertaining audiences across the nation, George Carlin influenced and inspired a generation of comedians yet struggled publicly with drug abuse.

💡 **LONG UNDERLINED PORTIONS** ⇒ **Sentence portions moving about/ Change complete roles**

Quantity

Countable Vs. Uncountable Things

💡 Try counting on the object to check if it’s Un/countable
 1 Hat, 2 hat, 3 hat ✅
 1 Patience, 2 Patience, 3 Patience ❌

Countable Modifiers
MANY hatsFEW stores
FEWER childrenFEWEST shoes
NUMEROUS booksNUMBER of chairs
Uncountable Modifiers
MUCH patienceLITTLE mechandise
LESS moneyLEAST greed
AMOUNT of furnitureGREAT courage

💡 “MORE”, “MOST”, & “ALL“ work for both countable & uncountable.

🚨 LESS is being used too frequently for countable things. Instead FEWER must be used.

 ❌ There were less Numidian KINGS than Roman emperors.
 ✅ There were fewer Numidian KINGS than Roman emperors.

Money & Volume

Dollars and gallons (unit nouns) by nature, are countable. But these nouns represent uncountable quantities like money and volume: one money, two moneys,… (?)

Comparative Vs. Superlative (Words Relating 2 Things Vs. > 2 Things)

  • Comparative form of adjectives/adverbs (better, worse, more, less) for 2 things.
  • Superlative form of adjectives/adverbs (best, worst, most, least) for > 2 things.

💡 2 Things ⇒ BETWEEN | >2 Things ⇒ AMONG

 ✅ I mediated a dispute BETWEEN Maya and Kalen.
 ✅ I mediated a dispute AMONG Maya, Logan, and Kalen.

The Word “Numbers” (COUNTABLE)

Use Greater Than Vs. More than

 ✅ The rare Montauk beaked griffin is not extinct; its NUMBERS are now suspected to be much GREATER than before.

Increase/Decrease Vs. Greater/Less

  • Increase/Decrease - Signal change of a thing over time.
  • Greater/Less - Signals comparison between 2 things.

Watch out for redundancies with increase/decrease:

 ❌ The price of silver FELL by a more than 35% DECREASE.
 ✅ The price of silver DECREASED by more than 35%.
 ✅ The price of silver FELL by more than 35%.

“THAT” in a sentence

“That” is a modifier

🚨 “That”, “Which”, “Who”, & “Where” modify the immediately preceding noun.

“That of” is used for comparison

 ❌ The debt-ratio of Greece is greater than Japan. (Greece’s Debt Ratio ↔ Japan)
 ✅ The debt-ratio of Greece is greater than THAT OF Japan. (Debt-Ratios of 2 countries compared)

Parallelism in “that”

Care must be taken to use that in the parallel clauses.

❌ I believe that Santa Clause is real and the Knicks will win the NBA title.
 ✅ I believe that Santa Clause is real and THAT the Knicks will win the NBA title.
Reason

Here in the 1st portion, the “Santa Clause is real” is part of the belief but that Knicks will be treated as an absolute truth. While in the 2nd sentence, the “that” creates a correct parallel and the Knicks winning is also part of the belief now.

Exception of “that” modifier rule (Idiomatic Phrases)

Although the general rule to “that” as a modifier holds, there are cases where it should not be blindly applied.

Eg: Here we can’t simply use “gun” as the subject of our modifier.

 ❌ The son of a gun THAT stole my lunch deserves to die.
 ✅ The son of a gun THAT stole my lunch deserves to die.

Practice-1

Each of the following sentences contains one or more underlined modifiers. For each of these modifiers, (a) identify the word or words, if any, that it modifies, and (b) indicate whether the modifier is correct. If the modifier is incorrect, suggest a way to correct the error.

  1. Angola, which was ravaged by civil war for many years after it gained independence from Portugal, which is now one of Africa’s success stories, has an economy that grew by 21% last year, where parliamentary elections are to be held later this week.

    Solution
    1. Which was ravaged .. from Portugal: CORRECT. This relative clause modifies the noun Angola.
    2. which is now one of Africa’s success stories: INCORRECT. This relative clause illogically modifies Portugal (which is in Europe).
    3. where parliamentary … this week: INCORRECT. A relative clause that begins with where must modify a noun that names a physical place, so this clause cannot modify year. The clause is too far away from Angola, however, to perform its intended role of modifying Angola.

  1. People, who talk loudly on their cell phones in crowded trains, show little respect for other passengers.

    Solution
    who talk … crowded trains: INCORRECT. This clause is wrong because the commas that enclose it make it a nonessential clause. So if we remove it & see the sentence, we get “People show little respect for other passengers” which falsely claims for every human being. To correct remove the commas.

  1. Of all the earthquakes in European history, the earthquake, which destroyed Lisbon in 1755, is perhaps the most famous.

    Solution
    which destroyed Lisbon in 1755: INCORRECT. The 2 commas enclosing “the earthquake” make it a nonessential clause. But because of the which we require a noun for it to modify

  1. Unaccustomed to the rigours of college life, James’s grades dropped.

    Solution

    Unaccustomed here is being used for James himself & not James’s grades.

    ✅ Unaccustomed to the rigours of college life, James allowed his grades to drop.

Practice-2

The following four sentences contain highlighted sections. Use the rules in this chapter to correct any errors that you can find in them. Do not change anything that is not highlighted.

  1. Jim is trying to reduce the number of soda that he drinks , at last night’s party, however, his resolve to drink fewer soda was sorely tested , he found himself quaffing many of the sodas.

    Solution

    Jim is trying to reduce the AMOUNT of soda that he drinks ; at last night’s party, however, his resolve to drink LESS soda was sorely tested , ANDhe found himself quaffing A NUMBER of the sodas.

    1. Number should be amount or quantity. Here soda is an uncountable substance-otherwise, soda would be sodas.
    2. The comma after drinks should be a semicolon, which would appropriately separate two independent clauses: Jim is trying … that he drinks and at last night’s party … sorely tested.
    3. Fewer should be Jess. Once again, regard soda as an uncountable substance.
    4. An and should be inserted after the comma after tested. This placement of and appropriately separates two main clauses: his resolve … was sorely tested and he found … sodas.
    5. Many should be a number. Since there is an -son the end of sodas, you know that sodas are now thought of as countable things-presumably servings of soda. A number of is an appropriate modifier for countable things.

  1. Between 1998 and 2003, there was heavy fighting in Parthia between numerous armed factions yet this conflict, so much more complicated than a conventional war between two states, involved no less that eight countries and twenty-five militias.

    Solution

    Between 1998 and 2003, there was heavy fighting in Parthia AMONG numerous armed factions ; this conflict, so much more complicated than a conventional war between two states, involved no FEWER that eight countries and twenty-five militias.

    1. The first between is correct, since only two dates are mentioned. The second between should be among, since the fighting involved more than two factions.
    2. Yet should be a semicolon. Yet is illogical because the action in the second clause (this conflict … militias) did not happen despite the action in the first clause (Between .. factions), as a word such as yet suggests.
    3. The third between is correct, since only two states are mentioned.
    4. Less should be fewer, because countries and militias are countable entities.

  1. Most legislators—including MUCH in the governor’s own party—realise that the governor’s budget would imperil the state’s finances , nonetheless, the budget is likely to be approved , because few legislators want to anger voters by cutting spending or raising taxes.

    Solution

    Most legislators—including MANY in the governor’s own party—realise that the governor’s budget would imperil the state’s finances ; nonetheless, the budget is likely to be approved, because few legislators want to anger voters by cutting spending or raising taxes.

    1. Much should be many, because legislators are countable.
    2. Nonetheless should be preceded by a semicolon. The clauses it connects are Most legislators … state’s finances and the budget is likely to be approved.
    3. The comma before because is correct. Because is a subordinating conjunction; therefore, it can be separated from a main clause by a comma.

Q1-Solution
Correct Answer: (E)

  2. Mark Vs. Marks: Plant and Animal Extinctions (Plural) ⇒ mark (D)
  3. (C) → No parallelism to use here with the “and”
Q2-Solution
(B)
  1. The “that of” Comparison doesn’t add up. (A) (D), & (E)
  2. “that it” comparison is not making a whole lot of sense. (C).
Q3-Solution
(A)
  1. Parallelism: We need a “that” at the end to complete the parallelism. (B), (C), & (D)
  2. (E) → Has no contrasting words in the sentence. We can also use meaning to eliminate these answer choices.

(E)
