3.2 Focus on Assumptions to Effectively Weaken an Argument.

  • MBA
  • GMAT
  • TTP
  • SC

Lesson Content

As we’ve discussed, an argument is composed of a conclusion, the evidence that supports the conclusion, any background information, and the assumptions that connect the evidence with the conclusion.

  1. Conclusion
  1. Evidence
  1. Background
  1. Assumptions

On the GMAT, we’ll never find an answer choice that weakens an argument by attacking the premises (that is, the evidence) upon which the conclusion rests, because while in real life premises may turn out to be invalid, on the GMAT the premises are taken to be true.

💡On the GMAT, never try to weaken an argument by attacking the premises (that is, the evidence) upon which the conclusion rests. The premises are always taken to be true.

So, for example, if an argument’s premise says,

Last year, 50 percent of all of Company Z’s sales came from product P.

we must assume that premise to be factual.

Because background information is also considered factual, we’ll never be able to weaken an argument by attacking background information.

💡Because background information is also considered factual, we’ll never be able to weaken an argument by attacking background information.

Since the answer to a Weaken question can’t undermine the argument by attacking the evidence (or the background information), the answer has to undermine the support for the conclusion in another way, which is to attack the reasoning that links the evidence to the conclusion.

💡The correct answer to a Weaken the Argument question will show that the conclusion does not necessarily follow from the premises, even though we take those premises to be true.

How do we show that the conclusion does not necessarily follow from the premises? We target the assumptions.

💡In answering Weaken questions, we must be aware of assumptions made by authors because, in general, we weaken GMAT arguments by attacking the assumptions upon which the arguments rest.

In other words, correct answers to Weaken questions say things that indicate that assumptions authors have made are not valid.

💡Correct answers to Weaken questions say things that indicate that assumptions authors have made are not valid.

From the Identify the Assumption chapter, you already know that an assumption is an unstated piece of information that must be true in order for the logic of the argument to work.

Let’s revisit the disease x argument from the chapter on Assumption questions:

All people in houses in which communicable diseases are present should be quarantined. Two people in the yellow house on Maple Street came down with disease x yesterday. Therefore, all people in the yellow house on Maple Street should be quarantined.

From the Assumption questions chapter, we already know that the author assumes that disease x is a communicable disease.

The only way this argument works is if it’s true that disease x is a communicable disease.

The structure of the argument is:

Premise 1: All people in houses in which communicable diseases are present should be quarantined.

Premise 2: Two people in the yellow house on Maple Street came down with disease x yesterday.

Assumption: Disease x is a communicable disease.

Conclusion: People in the yellow house should be quarantined.

How can we weaken this argument? Easy.

For instance, what if we were to provide a new fact, the fact that it is not the case that disease x is a communicable disease?

In that case, the assumption that holds the argument together would not be true, and the argument would fall apart.

So, an assumption functions as the unwritten information that connects the evidence to the conclusion.

Thus, if we can attack the author’s assumption, we can weaken the argument.

💡To weaken an argument, we can attack the assumption(s) made by the author. By breaking this critical glue that connects the evidence to the conclusion, we weaken the argument.

Let’s practice a bit.

Targeted Practice Solutions

Let’s now consider an example similar to one that we saw in the chapter on Assumption questions.

Example Qs

--- primary_color: green secondary_color: lightgray text_color: black shuffle_questions: false shuffle_answers: false --- ## Example 1 MowGreen, a company that uses sustainable methods to maintain lawns, has been expanding quickly, and its services are now available in many areas of the country. Which of the following, if true, most seriously weakens the argument above? 1. [x] (A) MowGreen’s management team does not consider profitability in determining whether to expand into an area. 2. [ ] (B) People in the Drawnville area are not interested in using sustainable methods for maintaining their lawns. >
Correct Answer: (A) 3. [ ] (C) There remain in the country areas in which MowGreen is not currently operating into which it could profitably expand its operations. >
Correct Answer: (A) 4. [ ] (D) MowGreen operates profitably in all of the areas in which it currently operates. >
Correct Answer: (A) 5. [ ] (E) Sustainable methods for maintaining lawns are generally more expensive to use than are less environmentally friendly methods. >
Correct Answer: (A)
View Solution

Breakdown of the Argument:

Background: MowGreen has been expanding, and its services are available in many areas.

Premise: MowGreen would not be able to operate profitably in the Drawnville area.

Conclusion: One can be sure that MowGreen will not expand into the Drawnville area any time soon.

Notice that the author is making the assumption that MowGreen’s management team considers profitability in determining whether to expand into an area. After all, if this assumption were found to be false, the argument would fall apart.

Which of the answer choices effectively attacks this assumption?

Thus, the correct version will have a singular verb that agrees with the singular subject, “proliferation.”

(A) MowGreen’s management team does not consider profitability in determining whether to expand into an area.

If MowGreen’s management team does not consider profitability in determining whether to expand into an area, this argument falls apart.

After all, if MowGreen’s management team does not consider profitability in determining whether to expand into an area, then the premise that MowGreen would not be able operate profitably in the Drawnville area doesn’t really support the conclusion that MowGreen will not expand into the Drawnville area.

So, this choice weakens the argument by effectively attacking an assumption integral to the argument.

(B) People in the Drawnville area are not interested in using sustainable methods for maintaining their lawns.

Given that MowGreen uses sustainable methods to maintain lawns, the fact that people in the area around Drawnville are not interested in using sustainable lawn care methods is in line with the conclusion that MowGreen will not start operating in that area.

(C) There remain in the country areas in which MowGreen is not currently operating into which it could profitably expand its operations.

The fact that there are areas of the country into which MowGreen can profitably expand its operations does not change the fact that MowGreen would not be able to operate profitably in the Drawnville area.

(D) MowGreen operates profitably in all of the areas in which it currently operates.

This choice could be temping if a test-taker were to take the fact that MowGreen operates profitably in all of the areas in which it operates as an indication that actually MowGreen could somehow operate profitably in the Drawnville area.

Remember, though, in GMAT Critical Reasoning, the premises are taken to be true. So, we can’t weaken the argument by attacking the premise that MowGreen would not be able to operate profitably in the Drawnville area. That premise is a simple fact.

(E) Sustainable methods for maintaining lawns are generally more expensive to use than are less environmentally friendly methods.

The fact that the methods MowGreen uses are more expensive to use than are other methods has no effect on the argument, as it neither changes the fact that MowGreen would not be able to operate profitably in the Drawnville area nor provides reason to believe that MowGreen would operate in Drawnville unprofitably.

Correct Answer: (A)

Ok – so we’ve seen that, if an argument’s assumptions are shown to be false, the argument will fall apart.

Instead, these correct answers simply cast some doubt on the validity of the assumptions made by authors, thereby casting some doubt on the efficacy of the arguments.

Notice how the correct answer in the following example simply casts a bit of doubt on the validity of the conclusion.

--- primary_color: green secondary_color: lightgray text_color: black shuffle_questions: false shuffle_answers: false --- ## Example 2 The Games Plus franchise stocks the game TigerTrail at all of its stores. Which of the following, if true, most seriously weakens the argument? 1. [ ] (A) Preceding TigerTrail’s release, the Games Plus franchise mounted an extensive online advertising campaign announcing the game. >
Correct Answer: (C) 2. [ ] (B) Many game buyers associate above eye-level shelf placement of a game with high popularity. >
Correct Answer: (C) 3. [x] (C) Most people who have bought TigerTrail from Store A have called in the order and picked up at the register. 4. [ ] (D) Every year since it opened, Store A has reported higher annual profits than any other Games Plus franchise store. >
Correct Answer: (C) 5. [ ] (E) Patrons of Store A have no more discretionary income to spend on video games than do patrons of other stores in the Games Plus franchise. >
Correct Answer: (C)
View Solution

Here’s a breakdown the argument:

Premise 1: Store A always displays its copies of TigerTrail on shelves above eye-level.

Premise 2: The other stores in the franchise rotate their copies of TigerTrail between eye-level and above eye-level shelves.

Premise 3: Store A sells more copies of TigerTrail per year than does any other store in the franchise.

Assumption: The author makes the unstated logical leap that the comparatively higher sales at Store A are the result of shelf placement rather than of some other factor. In other words, she assumes that there is no other factor that was responsible for the comparatively higher sales at Store A.

Conclusion: The shelf placement of TigerTrail at Store A is responsible for the higher sales of TigerTrail at Store A.

Notice that the author is making a cause-and-effect claim. The author argues that the cause of Store A’s selling more copies of TigerTrail per year than any other store is the shelf placement of TigerTrail at Store A. As we already know, a cause-and-effect claim is a bold one, and whenever an author makes a cause-and-effect claim, she assumes that there will not be any malicious variables that enter the equation and ruin her argument.

Often, that assumption turns out to be a bad one. Let’s see …

(A) Preceding TigerTrail’s release, the Games Plus franchise mounted an extensive online advertising campaign announcing the game.

Since there is no indication that this franchise-wide ad campaign would have had a special impact on Store A buyers in particular, this choice doesn’t affect an argument about the difference between Store A and other stores in the franchise.

In other words, this answer choice does not provide an alternate explanation for the difference between sales of TigerTrail at Store A and sales of TigerTrail at other stores.

So, this choice does not cast any doubt on the conclusion that the shelf placement is responsible for the difference in sales of TigerTrail.

(B) Many game buyers associate above eye-level shelf placement of a game with high popularity.

Notice that what this answer choice says provides an explanation for why above eye-level shelf placement works: perhaps buyers are fooled into thinking that TigerTrail must be really popular.

We are not, however, seeking an explanation for why shelving above eye-level results in increased sales; we’re seeking an alternate explanation for why Store A sold so many copies of TigerTrail.

This choice does not weaken the connection between above eye-level shelf placement and high sales of TigerTrail in Store A – if anything, it strengthens it by having us believe even more strongly in the power of such shelf placement.

(C) Most people who have bought TigerTrail from Store A have called in the order and picked up at the register.

This choice weakens the link between shelf placement of TigerTrail and sales of TigerTrail, because, if most of the buyers of TigerTrail at Store A have picked up the game at the register, it is quite possible that most of the buyers did not ever see the shelf TigerTrail was on.

Thus, this choice suggests that the buyers may not have been influenced by the shelf placement and that there is another reason for the difference in levels of sales.

Notice that this answer attacks the author’s assumption that the customers were ever in position to be affected by the shelf placement of TigerTrail. Notice too that this answer choice presents a malicious variable: the people did not even see the shelf-placement!

(D) Every year since it opened, Store A has reported higher annual profits than any other Games Plus franchise store.

What this answer choice says is not directly connected to sales of TigerTrail or even clearly connected to sales at all. The higher profits at Store A could be the result of factors other than sales, factors such as greater efficiency and keeping costs low.

Besides, the higher profits at Store A could also be the result of higher sales of TigerTrail. In such a case, this choice just presents a result of the higher sales of TigerTrail.

(E) Patrons of Store A have no more discretionary income to spend on video games than do patrons of other stores in the Games Plus franchise.

The fact that the methods MowGreen uses are more expensive to use than are other methods has no effect on the argument, as it neither changes the fact that MowGreen would not be able to operate profitably in the Drawnville area nor provides reason to believe that MowGreen would operate in Drawnville unprofitably.

If anything, this choice strengthens rather than a weakens the argument that shelf placement is responsible for the higher sales of TigerTrail at Store A, as it eliminates one alternative explanation for the higher sales, that there is a difference in the discretionary income of the customers.

In other words, if customers who shop at Store A have higher discretionary incomes than customers who shop at other stores, then that fact could provide an alternative explanation for why Store A sells so many copies of TigerTrail (its customer have more money to spend). What this answer choice says removes that alternative explanation as a possibility.

As we will see in greater detail in the Strengthen the Argument chapter, when a cause-and-effect claim is made, we can strengthen the support for that cause-and-effect claim by ruling out any alternative explanations for the observed events.

Correct Answer: (C)
