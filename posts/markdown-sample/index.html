3.8 Additive Phrases Don’t Create Plural Subjects

  • MBA
  • GMAT
  • TTP
  • SC

Lesson Content

In the last section, we saw that the use of the word “and” can result in the creation of a compound plural subject that requires a plural verb.

In this section, we’re going to discuss additive phrases.

What we’ll see as we discuss additive phrases is that, even though expressions such as “along with” or “as well as” resemble “and,” additive phrases don’t work the way “and” works with sentence subjects.

For example, the following sentence contains an additive phrase that begins with “in addition to.”

Estella, in addition to Frisky and Molly, was sleeping on the soft couch.

Some of you are thinking that that sentence contains an error, that the main verb should be “were sleeping.”

However, the key thing to understand about additive phrases is that, unlike the word “and,” additive phrases do not create plural subjects.

Incorrect: Estella, in addition to Frisky and Molly, were sleeping on the soft couch.

Even though “Estella” is followed by “in addition to Frisky and Molly,” “Estella” is a singular subject that requires the singular verb “was,” not the plural verb “were.

Let’s look at another:

Incorrect: The mayor, together with all of the members of the city council, are out to lunch at the moment.

The singular subject “mayor” does not become a plural subject when used in conjunction with the additive phrase “together with all of the members of the city council.” The correct sentence is:

The mayor, together with all of the members of the city council, is out to lunch at the moment.

💡Additive phrases, which are phrases that begin with structures such as “along with,” “as well as,” “in addition to,” “accompanied by,” or “together with,” do not create plural subjects. A singular subject followed by an additive phrase remains singular.

Let’s look at an another example:

Sally, along with Joe, Bobby, and Sue, (is/are) going to the fair tonight.

This sentence lets us know that there is more than one person going to the fair, and so, we may feel that a plural verb should be used.

Let’s see what we have when we remove everything between the two commas that set the additive phrase off from the rest of the sentence:

Sally, along with Joe, Bobby, and Sue, (is/are) going to the fair tonight.

Once we eliminate the intervening phrase, we can see that the subject, Sally, requires the singular verb “is,” as in:

Sally, along with Joe, Bobby, and Sue, is going to the fair tonight.

Let’s look at another example of how additive phrases fit into sentences.

Ringo, as well as the other members of the band, (has/have) requested that there be no flash photography at the press conference.

The subject, “Ringo,” is one person.

Ringo, as well as the other members of the band, (has/have) requested that there be no flash photography at the press conference.

As we can see when we remove the additive phrase, we need the singular verb “has” with our singular subject, “Ringo.”

Ringo, as well as the other members of the band, has requested that there be no flash photography at the press conference.

It’s also helpful to remember that the subject of a sentence will never be found within an additive phrase.

💡The subject of a sentence will never be found within an additive phrase.

For example:

Together with her coworkers, Lily (has/have) been planning a surprise birthday party for her boss all week.

In this case, an additive phrase comes at the beginning of the sentence and contains a plural noun, “coworkers.” Regardless of the placement of the additive phrase, it does not transform a singular subject into a plural subject.

The subject of the sentence will not appear within the additive phrase.

Together with her coworkers, Lily (has/have) been planning a surprise birthday party for her boss all week.

So, the correct sentence must use the singular verb “has” with “Lily”:

Together with her coworkers, Lily has been planning a surprise birthday party for her boss all week.

Targeted Practice Solutions

Targeted Practice: 1

Explanation:

The subject of the sentence is “prime minister.” “Accompanied by her assistant and several advisors” is an additive phrase and, thus, does not make the subject plural. So, the singular verb “is expected” is correct.

Upon arrival at the summit, the prime minister, accompanied by her assistant and several advisors, is expected to make a brief appearance to address the media.

Correct Answer: (A)
Targeted Practice: 2

Explanation:

This sentence has a compound subject, “The tyrannosaurus and the other large reptiles.” Therefore, the singular verb “was” is not correct, and we need the plural “were” instead.

The tyrannosaurus and the other large reptiles of the cretaceous period, such as the brontosaurus and triceratops, were unable to survive in the conditions created on Earth by the impact of a large asteroid.

Remember that “and” is not an additive phrase, and “and” does have the ability to create a compound subject, which must be paired with a plural finite verb.

Correct Answer: (B)
Targeted Practice: 3

Explanation:

The subject of the dependent clause is “dog,” which requires a singular subject. “together with a cat and a rat” is an additive phrase, which modifies the subject, “dog,” but does not make it plural. So, “come” has to be changed to the singular “comes.”

If a dog, together with a cat and a rat, comes this way, please tell the man who drove into town on a tractor.

Correct Answer: (B)
Targeted Practice: 4

Explanation:

The subject is “real estate and its associated services,” which is a compound subject that requires a plural verb. So, “is” is not correct and we need “are” instead. We have to be careful not to be fooled into thinking that the subject is singular by the presence of “renovation” in the phrase “including building renovation” just before the verb.

Now that the boom is over, real estate and its associated services, including building renovation, are expected to become less expensive.

Correct Answer: (B)
Targeted Practice: 5

Explanation:

The subject is the singular “production,” which requires, not the plural verb “have,” but rather the singular “has.” The additive phrase “along with that of most of the company’s professional hand tools” modifies the subject but does not make it plural. To clearly see the relationship between the subject and the verb, we can ignore both that additive phrase and the prepositional phrase “of the company’s kitchen appliances.”

Production of the company’s kitchen appliances, along with that of most of the company’s professional hand tools, has been moved out of the country.

Correct Answer: (B)
Targeted Practice: 6

Explanation:

The subject is the singular “foundation,” and so, the singular verb “has” is correct. The additive phrase “in association with several other nongovernmental organizations” does not make the subject plural.

The foundation, in association with several other nongovernmental organizations, has supported efforts to significantly reduce the prison population.

Correct Answer: (A)
Targeted Practice: 7

Explanation:

The subject is the singular “fruit.” Neither the additive phrase “in addition to vegetables” nor the prepositional phrase “of various types” makes the subject plural. So, the singular verb “is” is correct.

In addition to vegetables, local fruit of various types is generally available at farmers markets.

Correct Answer: (A)

Concept Mastery

--- primary_color: green secondary_color: lightgray text_color: black shuffle_questions: false shuffle_answers: false --- ## Concept Mastery 10 The proliferation of internet-related platforms, along with smartphones, digital cameras, and photo-editing software, mean that many journalists must be not only reporter but also photographer, marketer, and pundit in order to thrive in a more dynamic, multifaceted news environment. 1. [ ] (A) mean that many journalists must be not only reporter but also photographer, marketer, and pundit >
Correct Answer: (D) 2. [ ] (B) mean that many journalists must be not only reporters but also photographers, marketers, and pundits >
Correct Answer: (D) 3. [ ] (C) means that many journalists must be not only reporter but also photographer, marketer, and pundit >
Correct Answer: (D) 4. [x] (D) means that many journalists must be not only reporters but also photographers, marketers, and pundits 5. [ ] (E) meaning that many journalists must be not only reporters but also photographers, marketers, and pundits >
Correct Answer: (D)
View Solution

The subject of this rather busy sentence is the singular “proliferation.”

The presence of the additive phrase “along with smartphones, digital cameras, and photo editing software” does not make the singular subject plural.

Notice also that “internet-related platforms” appears within a prepositional phrase, “of internet-related platforms,” and therefore is not the sentence subject.

Thus, the correct version will have a singular verb that agrees with the singular subject, “proliferation.”

(A) The proliferation of internet-related platforms, along with smartphones, digital cameras, and photo-editing software, mean that many journalists must be not only reporter but also photographer, marketer, and pundit in order to thrive in a more dynamic, multifaceted news environment.

The plural verb “mean” does not agree with the singular subject “proliferation.”

(B) The proliferation of internet-related platforms, along with smartphones, digital cameras, and photo-editing software, mean that many journalists must be not only reporters but also photographers, marketers, and pundits in order to thrive in a more dynamic, multifaceted news environment.

The plural verb “mean” does not agree with the singular subject “proliferation.”

(C) The proliferation of internet-related platforms, along with smartphones, digital cameras, and photo-editing software, means that many journalists must be not only reporter but also photographer, marketer, and pundit in order to thrive in a more dynamic, multifaceted news environment.

This version correctly uses the singular verb “means” with the singular subject “proliferation.”

However, the wording of this choice, “many journalists must be not only reporter but also photographer …,” does not make as much sense as the wording of choice (D) which uses plural nouns “reporters,” “photographers,” “marketers,” and “pundits” to refer to the multiple roles of journalists. So, (D) is the better choice.

YOUR CHOICE(D) The proliferation of internet-related platforms, along with smartphones, digital cameras, and photo-editing software, means that many journalists must be not only reporters but also photographers, marketers, and pundits in order to thrive in a more dynamic, multifaceted news environment.

This version correctly uses the singular verb “means” with the singular subject “proliferation.”

(E) The proliferation of internet-related platforms, along with smartphones, digital cameras, and photo-editing software, meaning that many journalists must be not only reporters but also photographers, marketers, and pundits in order to thrive in a more dynamic, multifaceted news environment.

The participle “meaning” cannot serve as the main verb of a sentence. So, this version has no main verb and is not a complete sentence. In other words, this version lacks an independent clause; without an independent clause, there can be no sentence.

Correct Answer: (D)

[ ] (C) touting the proposed cross-country high-speed rail system, colloquially known as a “bullet train,” as the future of transportation, but facing >
Correct Answer: (B) 4. [ ] (D) tout the proposed cross-country high-speed rail system, colloquially known as a “bullet train,” as the future of transportation, but face >
Correct Answer: (B) 5. [ ] (E) tout the proposed cross-country high-speed rail system, colloquially known as a “bullet train,” as the future of transportation, but faces >
Correct Answer: (B)

The subject of the sentence is “the railroad lobby,” which is singular.

The presence of the additive phrase beginning with “in addition to …” does not make the singular subject plural.

So, in order to be correct, the sentence needs to use singular verbs with the singular “the railroad lobby.”

(A) The railroad lobby, in addition to environmental advocates and intercity commuters, touts the proposed cross-country high-speed rail system, colloquially known as a “bullet train,” as the future of transportation, but face an uphill battle against airline companies and automakers, two groups with an outsized influence in Washington.

The singular verb “touts” agrees with the singular subject “the railroad lobby,” but the plural “face” does not agree with the subject.

YOUR CHOICE(B) The railroad lobby, in addition to environmental advocates and intercity commuters, touts the proposed cross-country high-speed rail system, colloquially known as a “bullet train,” as the future of transportation but faces an uphill battle against airline companies and automakers, two groups with an outsized influence in Washington.

The singular verbs “touts” and “faces” agree with the singular subject “the railroad lobby.”

(C) The railroad lobby, in addition to environmental advocates and intercity commuters, touting the proposed cross-country high-speed rail system, colloquially known as a “bullet train,” as the future of transportation, but facing an uphill battle against airline companies and automakers, two groups with an outsized influence in Washington.

Since neither “touting” nor “facing” can on its own serve as a main verb, this version has no main verb.

(D) The railroad lobby, in addition to environmental advocates and intercity commuters, tout the proposed cross-country high-speed rail system, colloquially known as a “bullet train,” as the future of transportation, but face an uphill battle against airline companies and automakers, two groups with an outsized influence in Washington.

The plural verbs “tout” and “face” do not agree with the singular subject “the railroad lobby.”

(E) The railroad lobby, in addition to environmental advocates and intercity commuters, tout the proposed cross-country high-speed rail system, colloquially known as a “bullet train,” as the future of transportation, but faces an uphill battle against airline companies and automakers, two groups with an outsized influence in Washington.

The plural verb “tout” does not agree with the singular subject “the railroad lobby.”

Correct Answer: (B)
