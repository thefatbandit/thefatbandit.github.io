2.12 Assumptions and Weakening an Argument

  • MBA
  • GMAT
  • TTP
  • CR

Lesson Content

Remember, when an author makes an argument, she assumes that that argument is correct.

In the next chapter, we’ll discuss ways to weaken an argument.

Before we get there, we want you to see that an argument’s assumptions are closely tied to statements that could weaken that argument.

Consider the following mini-example:

It tends to be the case that the higher the quality of the food a person eats, the better that person’s health will be. However, higher quality food generally costs more than lower quality food costs. Clearly then, people are forced to make a choice between lower expenses and better health.

OK, now notice that the following statement weakens the above argument.

In general, the added expense of a person’s eating higher quality food is completely offset by a reduction in that person’s medical expenses.

The statement that higher food expenses are completely offset by lower medical expenses is contrary to and weakens the support for the conclusion that people are forced to make a choice between lower expenses and better health.

Therefore, someone making the argument that people are forced to make a choice between lower expenses and better health must be assuming that the above statement that weakens the argument is not true.

Since arguing that people are forced to make a choice between lower expenses and better health requires assuming that the above statement that weakens the argument is not true, the negated version of that statement, which follows, must be an assumption upon which the argument depends.

In general, the added expense of a person’s eating higher quality food is NOT completely offset by a reduction in that person’s medical expenses.

Notice that this assumption is simply a statement that would weaken the argument combined with the word “not.” In other words, the “not” in the assumption neutralizes a statement that would otherwise weaken the argument.

Interestingly, many Assumption question correct answer choices include the word “not” and work in the following way:

  • The answer choice mentions something that would conflict with, or weaken, the argument.
  • The word “not” appears in the answer choice in a way that negates the information that would conflict with, or weaken, the argument.
  • The combination of the information that would weaken the argument with the word “not” makes the choice an assumption upon which the argument relies.

So, many Assumption question correct answer choices are composed of information that would weaken the argument combined with the word “not.”

Of course, not every choice that includes “not” is an Assumption correct answer.

To determine which of the employees was responsible for the accounting fraud, the investigators plan to interview each employee. Since people tend to perspire when they lie, the investigators will be able to tell who is lying by noticing which employee perspires during the interview.

Which of the following is an assumption upon which the expectation that the plan will work relies?

(Incorrect Choice that Includes “not”) The employees who will be interviewed will not have time to prepare their stories.

(Correct Answer that Includes “not”) The location where the interviews will be held will not be so hot that everyone there will perspire regardless of whether they are lying.

The point of the investigation in this example is to determine who is responsible for the accounting fraud.

The incorrect choice that includes “not” is not required in order for the plan to work, as the plan is based entirely on the idea that, when people lie, they perspire.

On the other hand, the conclusion that the plan will work clearly relies on the correct answer that includes “not”.

💡MUST KNOW:

The correct answer to an Assumption question can be composed of information that would weaken the argument combined with the word “not.”

Let’s now practice noticing how “not” functions in correct answers to Assumption questions.

--- primary_color: green secondary_color: lightgray text_color: black shuffle_questions: false shuffle_answers: false --- ## Example 37 Factory Floor Supervisor: I have heard from my counterparts at other factories that government inspectors are cracking down on factories violating safety codes. Which of the following is an assumption that the supervisor is making in choosing to prescribe the outlined course of action? 1. [ ] (A) Government inspectors will make an unannounced visit to the factory sometime in the near future. >
Correct Answer: (B) 2. [x] (B) People working at the factory will not violate safety codes by acting in accordance with the factory’s standard operating procedures. 3. [ ] (C) All of the department managers of the factory are well versed in the factory’s standard operating procedures. >
Correct Answer: (B) 4. [ ] (D) Adhering exactly to the factory’s standard operating procedures is the best way to make the factory safe for those who work there. >
Correct Answer: (B) 5. [ ] (E) If inspectors find a violation of safety codes, they will recommend relatively light consequences if they see that factory personnel are following standard operating procedures. >
Correct Answer: (B)
View Solution
Question Level: medium

The factory floor supervisor starts with some background information:

  • Government inspectors are cracking down on factories violating safety codes.

The factory floor supervisor states a goal that is a response to the background information:

  • Ensure that we are not violating any safety codes.

Finally, the factory floor supervisor prescribes a course of action for achieving the stated goal:

  • Make sure that everyone is following our standard operating procedures exactly.

The question asks for an assumption that the factory floor supervisor is making in prescribing the outlined course of action. The purpose of the course of action that he outlines is to ensure that he and whomever he is speaking to are not violating any safety codes. So, in order to answer the question correctly, we need an answer that expresses an assumption that the factory floor supervisor must be making in concluding that following standard operating procedures exactly will serve the purpose of ensuring that they are not violating any safety codes.

(A) Government inspectors will make an unannounced visit to the factory sometime in the near future.

While the factory floor supervisor does seem to be concerned that government officials may visit the factory floor, concluding that, by following standard operating procedures, they will achieve the goal of ensuring that they are not violating any safety codes does not require assuming that government officials will show up unannounced.

YOUR CHOICE(B) People working at the factory will not violate safety codes by acting in accordance with the factory’s standard operating procedures.

This choice is interesting and includes the word “not”. So, let’s see whether the supervisor must be assuming what this choice says.

The purpose of following standard operating procedures exactly is to ensure that factory personnel are not violating any safety codes.

Now, notice what we get if we remove “not” from this choice:

People working at the factory will not violate safety codes by acting in accordance with the factory’s standard operating procedures.

Of course, if people working at the factory WILL violate safety codes by acting in accordance with the factory’s standard operating procedures, then following the factory’s standard operating procedures will cause what it is meant to prevent. So, without “not” this choice would weaken the argument.

Thus, this choice is a weakener + “not.”

Therefore, the factory supervisor must be assuming what this choice says, that people working at the factory will not violate safety codes by acting in accordance with the factory’s standard operating procedures.

(C) All of the department managers of the factory are well versed in the factory’s standard operating procedures.

This choice could be tempting, in that a test-taker could feel that the plan will not work unless the managers are well versed in the standard operating procedures.

However, we are not looking for an assumption necessary for concluding that factory personnel are ready to make the plan work. We are looking for an assumption necessary for concluding that following standard operating procedures would serve the purpose of ensuring that factory personnel are not violating any safety codes.

In prescribing a course of action that he wants the factory personnel to follow, the factory supervisor does not have to assume that everyone is fully ready to follow that course of action. In other words, he can decide what he wants them to do and say what he wants them to do without assuming that they all already know how to do it.

(D) Adhering exactly to the factory’s standard operating procedures is the best way to make the factory safe for those who work there.

The stated goal is not to make the factory safe for everyone who works there. So, prescribing the course of action of adhering exactly to standard operating procedures does not require assuming that adhering exactly to the factory’s standard operating procedures is the best way to make the factory safe.

(E) If inspectors find a violation of safety codes, they will recommend relatively light consequences if they see that factory personnel are following standard operating procedures.

What this choice says sounds reasonable.

However, the purpose of the course of action is not to ensure that inspectors will recommend relatively light consequences if they find any violations of safety codes. The purpose of the course of action is to ensure that factory personnel are not violating any safety codes

So, this choice does not express an assumption integral to choosing to prescribe the course of action.

Correct Answer: (B)
--- primary_color: green secondary_color: lightgray text_color: black shuffle_questions: false shuffle_answers: false --- ## Example 38 New Owner of Joe’s Snack Shack: Any who are concerned about the future of Joe’s Snack Shack under our ownership might consider the following. Which of the following is an assumption upon which the new owner’s conclusion depends? 1. [ ] (A) Updating Joe’s Snack Shack will result in an increase in profits that will justify the costs of updating the restaurant. >
Correct Answer: (D) 2. [ ] (B) Joe’s Snack Shack will not become more popular unless its exterior and interior are updated significantly. >
Correct Answer: (D) 3. [ ] (C) Many of the restaurants that compete most directly with Joe’s Snack Shack will be updated extensively in the near future. >
Correct Answer: (D) 4. [x] (D) Joe’s Snack Shack’s antique décor and vintage look are not the most significant reasons for the restaurant’s popularity. 5. [ ] (E) Unless the exterior and interior of a restaurant are updated regularly, the restaurant will go out of business. >
Correct Answer: (D)
View Solution
Question Level: medium

Here’s a breakdown of the argument.

Premise: Neither the interior nor the exterior of Joe’s Snack Shack, which has been operating for 70 years, has been significantly updated since the restaurant first opened.

Premise: Over the next year, we plan to modernize virtually every aspect of the restaurant.

Conclusion: There is no reason to doubt that the restaurant will become more popular than ever.

There is a noticeable gap in the argument. The premises state that the restaurant has not been updated and will be modernized. The conclusion says that there is no reason to doubt that the restaurant will become more popular. More modern and more popular are not the same thing. So, there is a gap between modernizing the restaurant and the restaurant’s becoming more popular.

What piece of information is missing that must be true in order for this argument to work?

(A) Updating Joe’s Snack Shack will result in an increase in profits that will justify the costs of updating the restaurant.

Notice that the conclusion of the argument is that there is no reason to doubt that the restaurant will become more popular. This answer choice, on the other hand, is about an increase in profits.

The fact that the restaurant will be modernized could support the conclusion that the restaurant will become more popular regardless of whether modernizing the restaurant will result in an increase in profits.

So, the argument could work even if, contrary to what this choice says, updating Joe’s Snack Shack will NOT result in an increase in profits. Thus, this choice is not an assumption upon which the argument depends.

YOUR CHOICE(B) Joe’s Snack Shack will not become more popular unless its exterior and interior are updated significantly.

Notice that, even though this choice includes the word “not,” this choice is not an assumption upon which the argument depends.

After all, even if what this choice says were not true, and Joe’s Snack Shack would become more popular even if it were not updated, it could still be true that the Joe’s Snack Shack will become more popular than ever after it is modernized.

Since the argument’s conclusion could follow from the premises even if what this choice says were not true, this choice is not an assumption upon which the argument depends.

(C) Many of the restaurants that compete most directly with Joe’s Snack Shack will be updated extensively in the near future.

The fact that competing restaurants will be updated in the near future could be seen as a reason for modernizing Joe’s Snack Shack.

However, we are not looking for a reason for updating the restaurant. We are looking for an assumption that connects the premise that the restaurant will be modernized to the conclusion that it will become more popular.

In fact, if anything, the fact that other restaurants will be updated might serve to call into question the conclusion that modernizing Joe’s Snack Shack will result in its becoming more popular. After all, if other restaurants will be updated, then maybe modernizing Joe’s Snack Shack will just help Joe’s Snack Shack keep up with other restaurants rather than result in its becoming more popular.

So, if anything, this choice weakens the argument, rather than states an assumption integral to the argument.

CORRECT ANSWER(D) Joe’s Snack Shack’s antique décor and vintage look are not the most significant reasons for the restaurant’s popularity.

This choice is a weakener combined with “not.”

Without “not,” this choice would say the following:

Joe’s Snack Shack’s antique décor and vintage look are not the most significant reasons for the restaurant’s popularity.

If that statement were true, then, contrary to the conclusion of the argument, we would have reason to doubt that the restaurant will become more popular after it is modernized. After all, if Joe’s Snack Shack’s antique décor and vintage look ARE the most significant reasons for the restaurant’s popularity, then modernizing the restaurant is likely to result in the restaurant’s becoming less popular, rather than more popular.

So, without “not,” this choice is a weakener.

Thus, in order for the argument to work, it has to be true that Joe’s Snack Shack’s antique décor and vintage look are NOT the most significant reasons for the restaurant’s popularity.

So, this choice states an assumption upon which the argument depends.

(E) Unless the exterior and interior of a restaurant are updated regularly, the restaurant will go out of business.

It could very well be that the new owner believes that, unless a restaurant is updated, it will go out of business.

However, it is not necessary to assume what this choice says in order to conclude that Joe’s Snack Shack will be more popular after it is modernized.

After all, even if it is NOT true that, unless a restaurant that is updated, it will go out of business, the fact that Joe’s Snack Shack will be modernized could support the conclusion that it will become more popular.

Correct Answer: (D)
