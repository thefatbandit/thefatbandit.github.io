3.8 The Difference Between Trap Choices and Correct Answers in Weaken Questions

  • MBA
  • GMAT
  • TTP
  • CR

Lesson Content

One key to correctly answering Weaken questions is being able to see the difference between trap choices and correct answers.

Trap Choice: In general, a trap choice in a Weaken the Argument question says something that seems detrimental to the conclusion and that is somehow related to the argument, and at the same time, what the trap choice says is not logically connected to the argument in a way such that the choice actually weakens the argument.

Correct Answer: Often, at first glance, a correct answer may not seem to be directly related to the conclusion of the argument.

Consider the following example:

A survey has found that parents who smoke are more likely than parents who don’t smoke to have children who are involved in juvenile delinquency. Therefore, it’s clear that parental smoking causes children to engage in destructive behavior.

Which of the following, if true, would most seriously weaken the argument?

(Trap Choice) Some of the children who most actively engage in destructive behavior have parents who do not smoke.

(Correct Answer) Living in stressful circumstances can lead parents to smoke and children to engage in destructive behavior.

Notice the difference between the trap choice and the correct answer.

The trap choice says something about children who “most actively engage in destructive behavior,” and the wording of the trap choice makes it sound important.

Notice, however, that the fact that some children whose parents do not smoke engage in destructive behavior does not conflict with the conclusion that parental smoking causes children to engage in destructive behavior.

The correct answer, on the other hand, indicates that it could be that the true cause-and-effect relationship is not that parental smoking causes children’s destructive behavior but, rather, that a third variable, living in stressful circumstances, underlies both the parents’ smoking and the children’s destructive behavior.

Notice this choice attacks the assumption that a parent’s smoking is the cause of a child’s juvenile delinquency.

So, the correct answer says something that clearly serves to call the conclusion into question.

Now that we better understand the difference between a trap choice and a correct answer to a Weaken question, here is some Targeted Practice that includes some tricky trap choices.

Targeted Practice Solutions

Targeted Practice: 1

Explanation:

(A) Trap. This choice seems to provide a reason to doubt the value of the data used in the premise. However, unless we have a valid reason to believe that the data from police reports is biased, this choice doesn’t impact the argument.

CORRECT ANSWERCORRECT ANSWER

This choice clearly undermines the support for the conclusion.

If it is often the case that people who wear seatbelts drive faster and less carefully than they would were they not wearing seatbelts, then some of the protection offered by seatbelts would be offset by the less safe driving habits of those wearing seatbelts. So, the results of a law requiring seatbelt use would not likely match the results that wearing seatbelts achieved for people who were all driving at the same speed, 60 miles per hour.

Thus, what this choice says indicates that the fact that using car seatbelts reduced the incidence of serious injury in crashes that occurred at a speed limit of 60 miles per hour by 48 percent does not necessarily support the conclusion of the argument.

Notice that this choice attacks the author’s assumption that all people wearing seat belts would experience results the same as those achieved by a specific set of people, people who were wearing seatbelts of their own volition while driving at a certain speed.

Correct Answer: (B)
Targeted Practice: 2

Explanation:

CORRECT ANSWERCORRECT ANSWER

While this answer choice says nothing directly about profitability, it indicates that, while providing insulation services is not as profitable for the firm as providing other services is, customers who interact with the firm because of its insulation services often end up making purchases that are more profitable for the firm. Thus, it is as a result of offering insulation services that the firm makes some its more profitable sales.

So, this choice casts doubt on the conclusion that the firm would be better off ceasing to provide insulation services.

Notice that this choice attacks the assumption that providing insulation services doesn’t somehow increase the overall profitability of the company.

(B) Trap. This choice is tempting, since it can be construed as indicating that providing insulation services can be a great way to make money. However, the argument has established that, for the firm, providing insulation services is less profitable than other lines of business in which the firm engages. The fact that individuals can make above average incomes by providing insulation services does not change the relative level of profitability experienced by the firm as a result of its providing insulation services. So, this choice does not affect the argument.

Correct Answer: (A)
Targeted Practice: 3

Explanation:

CORRECT ANSWERCORRECT ANSWER

At first glance, an answer choice that discusses what people do when the weather is cold and rainy may seem totally unconnected to a discussion of food and petty crime. However, with a little thinking, we can find that actually there is a connection.

It’s common knowledge that people eat cold snacks more when the weather is hot and sunny and less when the weather is cold and rainy. Meanwhile, this choice indicates that teenagers, some of whom perpetrate petty crime, are out more when it is hot and sunny and less when it is cold or rainy.

With those facts in mind, we can clearly see that both sales of frozen fruit snacks and rates of petty crime are affected by, a third variable, the weather.

Thus, by indicating that it is quite likely that the reason why there is a correlation between frozen fruit sales and petty crime is not that eating frozen fruit products can result in a tendency to engage in criminal behavior but, rather that, the weather affects both frozen fruit sales and the rate of petty crime, this choice undermines the support for the health official’s conclusion.

(B) Trap. This is a pseudo-weakener, one that seems to disprove the conclusion while actually making a very weak statement.

Notice that it says that “some people report that … they feel happy and peaceful.” “Some people” is any number of people more than one. So, this answer choice could be true if only two people feel happy and peaceful after eating frozen fruit products and millions do not.

Further, people who feel happy and peaceful could commit petty crimes.

Thus, this choice does not clearly weaken the argument.

Correct Answer: (A)
Targeted Practice: 4

Explanation:

(A) Trap. The conclusion of the argument is that implementing the safety measures will be hurtful to the profitability of the business, and, at first glance, this answer choice may seem to indicate otherwise.

Let’s look, though, at what this pseudo-weakener answer choice actually says.

It says that implementing the safety measures will not keep the company from following through on some plans that the company already had. The fact that the company can follow through on some plans that it already had does not mean that implementing the safety measures will not cost the company time and money and, thus, hurt profitability.

So, this choice, which may seem to weaken the argument, actually changes nothing.

CORRECT ANSWERCORRECT ANSWER

This answer, which is about consumer preferences, may not seem related to safety measures and the costs of implementing them. However, consumer preferences are related to sales, and sales affect profitability, which is what the conclusion is about. So, with a little analysis, we can see that this answer choice says something that offsets what the argument says.

Notice that what this choice says indicates that, even though implementing the safety measures may be costly, there could be a payoff to implementing them: if they actually serve to reduce industrial accidents, consumers may do more business with the company. Of course, consumers’ doing more business with the company could completely offset the costs of implementing the safety measures or even result in the company’s becoming more profitable than it would have been.

So this answer choice calls into question the conclusion that implementing the safety measures will hurt profitability.

Correct Answer: (B)
Targeted Practice: 5

Explanation:

(A) Trap. The appeal of this answer lies in the fact that it seems to weaken the argument by attacking one of the premises, the premise that people are going to reduce the percentage of their leisure time that they spend playing tennis. One could think, “Wait, playing tennis is going to cost less. So, actually people will play more.”

However, the truth of the matter is that the argument is based on the projections, and regardless of any expected reduction in the cost of playing tennis, the projections are what they are.

Notice that the conclusion starts with, “If these projections are correct.”

So, this answer choice that seems to weaken the argument actually changes nothing, and is therefore a trap.

CORRECT ANSWERCORRECT ANSWER

Using some simple math, we can see that this answer choice destroys the argument.

If the percentage of leisure time spent playing tennis goes from 5% to 3%, but leisure time doubles, then 3% of future leisure time will be 2 x 3% of today’s leisure time.

Thus, given what this choice says, in the future, people will be spending 2 x 3% = 6% of today’s leisure time playing tennis.

So, if the projections are correct, the amount of time people spend playing tennis will increase rather than decrease, going from 5% of today’s leisure time to 6%.

So, this choice completely destroys the argument.

Correct Answer: (B)

Now, consider the following example, and watch out for trap choices.

--- primary_color: green secondary_color: lightgray text_color: black shuffle_questions: false shuffle_answers: false --- ## Example 24 Marketing Executive: We use independently administered customer satisfaction surveys to rate the performance of our company, and we recently switched from using one survey provider to using another. Which of the following, if true, would most undermine the executive’s interpretation of the survey results? 1. [ ] (A) The provider of the new survey has only half as many company clients as has the provider of the previous survey. >
Correct Answer: (C) 2. [ ] (B) The new survey has been in use for only five months, while the previous one was used for three years. >
Correct Answer: (C) 3. [x] (C) The more positive a person’s relationship with a company the more likely it is that the person will take the time to respond to a long, detailed survey for the company. 4. [ ] (D) Designing a survey that accurately captures multiple aspects of the satisfaction of a company’s customers requires detailed analysis of what to ask. >
Correct Answer: (C) 5. [ ] (E) The first few questions in the new survey are nearly identical to the first few questions that were in the previous survey. >
Correct Answer: (C)
View Solution
Question Level: medium

The marketing executive’s conclusion, that the ratings generated by the new survey are more accurate than those generated by the previous survey, is based on the premise that the new survey is longer and more detailed.

Notice that the marketing executive is essentially making a cause-and-effect claim in saying that it was the accuracy of the longer survey that produced the higher ratings. The author makes the assumption that there could be no other explanation for the more favorable ratings. He believes that the only explanation is that the longer survey generates more accurate ratings.

Was it accuracy that was responsible? Could some other factor have caused the increase? The correct answer will somehow call into question the idea that the longer and more detailed survey has generated more accurate ratings.

(A) The provider of the new survey has only half as many company clients as has the provider of the previous survey.

This choice is tempting, as one could take it to be indicating that the provider of the new survey is somehow not as good as the provide or the previous survey and that, therefore, the new survey is likely not as accurate as the previous survey.

However, the fact that one provider has more clients than the other has does not clearly indicate anything about the quality of either provider or about the quality of the survey provided by each.

So, this choice does not have a clear effect on the argument.

(B) The new survey has been in use for only five months, while the previous one was used for three years.

This answer could be tempting since the fact that the new survey has been used for only five months could be taken as indicating that the new survey is somehow lacking something had by the previous survey, which had been used for three years. Any such lack could result in the new survey’s generating ratings that are less accurate than those generated by the previous survey.

However, the truth is that the fact that the new survey has been used for only five months does not provide any solid reason to doubt the accuracy of the ratings generated by the new survey. If the survey is well designed, it could have generated accurate ratings on the first day it was used.

YOUR CHOICE(C) The more positive a person’s relationship with a company the more likely it is that the person will take the time to respond to a long, detailed survey for the company.

This choice casts doubt on the conclusion that the higher ratings generated by the longer, more detailed survey are more accurate than the ratings generated by the previous survey

After all, if people with more positive relationships with the company are more likely to respond to the longer, more detailed new survey, then the ratings generated by this new survey are not likely to include a representative cross section of customer opinions of the company’s performance. Instead, the ratings generated by the new survey are likely to be disproportionately positive.

So, this choice indicates that it may be that bias, rather than accuracy, is responsible for the fact that the ratings generated by the new survey are higher than those generated by the previous survey.

Notice how clearly this answer choice weakens the argument. No conjecture is necessary for linking this choice to the conclusion. Rather, this choice provides a clear reason to doubt that the conclusion follows from the premise.

In general, whenever an author uses survey evidence to support his conclusion, he assumes that that survey has produced evidence that can in fact support the conclusion; he assumes that there are no malicious variables lurking in the survey evidence.

This answer choice underscores that that assumption was a bad one.

(D) Designing a survey that accurately captures multiple aspects of the satisfaction of a company’s customers requires detailed analysis of what to ask.

The fact that designing a survey requires detailed analysis of what to ask does not serve to call into question the accuracy of the new survey, as we are not given reason to believe that detailed analysis was not included in the process of creating the new survey.

Don’t be fooled into thinking that a general statement like this one casts doubt on the validity of a conclusion.

(E) The first few questions in the new survey are nearly identical to the first few questions that were in the previous survey.

The fact that the first few questions of the two surveys are similar could be construed as indicating that the two surveys are similar and, therefore, that concluding that the second is more accurate does not make sense.

However, the fact remains that the second survey is different from the first in that it is longer and more detailed. So, this choice does not affect the argument at all.

Correct Answer: (C)
