5.10.1 How Absolute Phrases Can Appear in Sentence Correction Questions

  • MBA
  • GMAT
  • TTP
  • SC

Lesson Content

In some cases, absolute phrases are used in ways that make correct answers clearly better than the other options.

Let’s begin by discussing how an absolute phrase can make an answer choice correct by being the best means of expressing a thought.

Consider the following two sentence versions.

Incorrect: Having spirits, which had been lifted by the joyful music, the crowd sang along with the band.

Their spirits having been lifted by the joyful music, the crowd sang along with the band.

Notice that, since “which had been lifted by the joyful music” is a nonessential modifier, the first version says, essentially, “Having spirits, … the crowd sang along with the band.” So, the first version does not effectively express the cause-and-effect connection between the crowd’s spirits’ having been lifted by the music and the crowd’s singing along with the band.

On the other hand, the second version uses an explanatory absolute phrase “their spirits having been lifted by the joyful music” to concisely and effectively express that the crowd sang along with the band because their spirits had been lifted by the joyful music.

So, the version that uses an absolute phrase is the better version.

Here’s another pair in which the version that uses an absolute phrase is more effective:

Incorrect: Increases in global temperatures are leaving polar bears hungry, along with the system via which they feed themselves having been disrupted.

Increases in global temperatures are leaving polar bears hungry, the system via which they feed themselves having been disrupted.

Notice the difference between the effectiveness of the first version and the effectiveness of the second version.

In the first, the wording “hungry, along with the system via which they feed themselves having been disrupted” describes two aspects of what polar bears are experiencing, but connecting the two via the use of the expression “along with” does not really make sense.

The second version, on the other hand, employs the absolute phrase “the system via which they feed themselves having been disrupted” to concisely convey that the disruption of their system of feeding themselves is the cause of the bears’ being hungry.

OK, now that we have seen some ways in which the use of absolute phrases can make sentences effective, let’s look at some examples of flawed use of absolute phrases.

Absolute Phrase Where a Clause Should Be:

Incorrect: The members of the committee having arrived at a decision, the chairperson declaring the end of the meeting.

This example is essentially made up of two absolute phrases, “the members of the committee having arrived at a decision” and “the chairperson declaring the end of the meeting.” Of course, this structure doesn’t constitute a sentence, as it lacks an independent clause.

The members of the committee having arrived at a decision, the chairperson declared the end of the meeting.

Absolute Phrase with an Illogical Relationship with the Clause that it Modifies:

Incorrect: Its foot caught in a trap, the environmentalist freed the bear.

We can tell from the use of the pronoun “its,” and from a common-sense interpretation of the sentence, that the foot is the bear’s.

Also, notice the following.

Incorrect: The environmentalist freed the bear, its foot caught in a trap.

In order for this sentence to make sense, it has to have a different kind of modifier.

The environmentalist freed the bear, whose foot had been caught in a trap.

Now the sentence makes sense.

Targeted Practice: 1

Explanation:

We see at the beginning of the sentence an absolute phrase composed of one noun, “remarks,” and some modifiers that modify “remarks,” and the absolute phrase logically modifies the clause that follows it.

Targeted Practice: 2

Explanation:

We must have a main verb somewhere in the sentence; neither “working” nor “to complete” qualifies as such. “The deadline nearing” is an absolute phrase, which, of course, does not contain the main verb. Notice that the conclusion of the sentence, “a pot of coffee by his side,” is also an absolute phrase. A complete sentence is:

The deadline nearing, Everett worked through the weekend to complete his application, a pot of coffee by his side.

Notice that the absolute phrase “the deadline nearing” explains why Everett worked through the weekend.

Targeted Practice: 3

Explanation:

We cannot join the two independent clauses “the children gobbled up the apple crisp” and “their enjoyment was obvious” with only a comma.

We can correct the sentence by changing the second independent clause to an absolute phrase (by removing the main verb from that clause).

Although they already were full from the roasted corn, sweet potatoes, and green beans, the children gobbled up the apple crisp, their enjoyment obvious.

Targeted Practice: 4

Explanation:

The absolute phrase “the mandatory recount underway” correctly modifies and adds descriptive information to the independent clause “voters waited eagerly to learn the result of the close Senate race.”

Also, the relative clause “which had been rife…” clearly refers to the noun that directly precedes it, “the close Senate race.”

Correct Answer: (A)
Targeted Practice: 5

Explanation:

The sentence is missing an independent clause. As currently written, what we have is merely two phrases. We cannot have a complete sentence made up of only phrases. A complete sentence is:

Floor swept, bunks made, and soldiers standing at attention, the unit was ready for the morning inspection by the commanding officer.

Notice that the absolute phrase “Floor swept, bunks made, and soldiers standing at attention” adds descriptive information.

Correct Answer: (B)
Targeted Practice: 6

Explanation:

The subordinating conjunction “while” should not introduce the phrase “his start-up’s financial records having been in disarray ….” We can make the sentence logical by removing the conjunction, making the phrase an absolute phrase, as in:

Noah was completely unprepared for the tax audit, his start-up’s financial records having been in disarray ever since his partner quit.

Notice that the absolute phrase “his start-up’s financial records having been in disarray ever since his partner quit” provides explanatory information.

Correct Answer: (B)
Targeted Practice: 7

Explanation:

“Its bustling factories shuttered…” is an absolute phrase that would logically describe “Josh’s hometown.” However, since the subject of the sentence is “the recession,” the opening modifier, while modifying the entire clause, focuses on “the recession.” Thus, the sentence conveys that the factories and mansions belong to the recession. Rephrasing the sentence will fix this modification issue. A more effective sentence is:

Its bustling factories shuttered and its grand old mansions crumbling and condemned, Josh’s hometown looked like a different universe after the recession, and the dwindling of the local workforce meant that any economic recovery would be slow.

Targeted Practice: 8

Explanation:

The phrase “troubled overseas markets … stifling their recovery” should not be connected to the independent clause that follows it by the conjunction “so.” In this type of context “so” should connect two clauses, and in no situation would “so” correctly connect a phrase and a clause. A correct version of the sentence uses an absolute phrase connected to a clause by a comma only.

Troubled overseas markets and contentious trade relations with China stifling their recovery, prices of commodities such as copper, oil, and gas have yet to bounce back fully from last month’s downturn, though traders are, for the moment, no longer in a selling frenzy.

Notice that the absolute phrase “Troubled overseas markets and contentious trade relations with China stifling their recovery” provides explanatory information.

Correct Answer: (B)

Now, let’s answer some Sentence Correction questions that involve absolute phrases.

--- primary_color: green secondary_color: lightgray text_color: black shuffle_questions: false shuffle_answers: false --- ## Sentence Correction Example 42 The old mall sat empty and abandoned, as well as its stores that were casualties of internet shopping. 1. [ ] (A) as well as its stores that were >
Correct Answer: (D) 2. [ ] (B) and with its stores that were >
Correct Answer: (D) 3. [ ] (C) and its stores >
Correct Answer: (D) 4. [x] (D) its stores 5. [ ] (E) having as its stores >
Correct Answer: (D)
View Solution
Question Level: medium

In every version, the part of the sentence that follows the comma adds information to what the main clause has stated. However, in only one case, the case in which an absolute phrase is used, does the information that follows the comma logically complement what has already been stated.

(A) The old mall sat empty and abandoned, as well as its stores that were casualties of internet shopping.

While this version may seem to make sense, what it says does not really make sense. If we simplify the sentence, we can see that it says essentially the following:

The mall sat empty and abandoned, as well as its stores.

The above sentence does not clearly convey that the stores were empty and abandoned or convey much of anything. The part about the stores is merely tacked onto the first part with no clear purpose and, thus, does not convey any particular meaning.

(B) The old mall sat empty and abandoned, and with its stores that were casualties of internet shopping.

This version conveys the nonsensical meaning that the mall sat empty and abandoned and sat with its stores.

(C) The old mall sat empty and abandoned, and its stores casualties of internet shopping.

This version almost seems to work. However, what follows the conjunction “and” has to match what comes before “and.” In this case, a clause precedes “and” but the second part of the sentence is not a clause as it has no main verb, and there is nothing before “and” that could be logically paired with “its stores casualties of internet shopping.” As a result, the structure works before the comma, but what comes after the comma does not work and is not clearly connected logically to what precedes the comma, as we can see by combining the answer choice with the non-underlined portions.

The old mall sat empty and abandoned, and its stores casualties of internet shopping.

YOUR CHOICE(D) The old mall sat empty and abandoned, its stores casualties of internet shopping.

In this version, the absolute phrase “its stores casualties of internet shopping” clearly and concisely modifies the first part of the sentence, providing information that explains why the mall is empty and abandoned.

(E) The old mall sat empty and abandoned, having as its stores casualties of internet shopping.

While this version is grammatically correct, what it conveys, that the mall had casualties of internet shopping as its stores, is, while perhaps poetic, not the type of clear meaning that is normally conveyed by the correct answer to a Sentence Correction question.

Correct Answer: (D)
--- primary_color: green secondary_color: lightgray text_color: black shuffle_questions: false shuffle_answers: false --- ## Sentence Correction Example 43 Having feet that were pounding the track, the runners ran by where the coach stood watching. 1. [ ] (A) Having feet that were pounding the track, the runners ran by where the coach stood watching. >
Correct Answer: (C) 2. [ ] (B) The runners ran by where the coach stood watching along with their feet pounding the track. >
Correct Answer: (C) 3. [x] (C) The runners ran by where the coach stood watching, their feet pounding the track. 4. [ ] (D) Having their feet pounding the track, the coach watched the runners run by where she stood. >
Correct Answer: (C) 5. [ ] (E) The coach stood watching the runners, their feet pounding the track running by. >
Correct Answer: (C)
View Solution
Question Level: medium

The main clause of this original version of this sentence expresses that the runners ran by where the coach stood watching. While the opening modifier “having feet that were pounding the track” adds descriptive information, what it conveys is illogically focused on the fact that the runners had feet. The best version of the sentence uses a more concise modifier that more effectively describes what occurred.

(A) Having feet that were pounding the track, the runners ran by where the coach stood watching.

The opening modifier illogically focuses on the fact that the runners had feet, as if the reason why they ran by is that they had feet.

(B) The runners ran by where the coach stood watching along with their feet pounding the track.

This version conveys an illogical meaning, that the coach was watching along with the runners’ feet.

YOUR CHOICE(C) The runners ran by where the coach stood watching, their feet pounding the track.

In this version, the closing absolute phrase “their feet pounding the track” concisely and effectively adds information describing an aspect of how the runners were when they ran by.

(D) Having their feet pounding the track, the coach watched the runners run by where she stood.

The opening modifier “having their feet pounding the track” is placed in a way such that the modifier modifies “coach,” and thus, this version conveys the illogical meaning that the coach had the runners’ feet pounding the track.

(E) The coach stood watching the runners, their feet pounding the track running by.

In this version, the absolute phrase “their feet pounding the track running by” nonsensically conveys either that the runners’ feet were running by or that the track was running by.

Correct Answer: (C)
