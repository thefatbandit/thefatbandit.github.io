5.3.4 Misplaced Prepositional Phrases

  • MBA
  • GMAT
  • TTP
  • SC

Lesson Content

A prepositional phrase is a modifier composed of a preposition, such as “in,” “under,” “by,” or “for,” followed by a noun, and possibly some modifiers that modify that noun.

in boxes

under rocks

by the lake

for illiterate individuals

Prepositional phrases can serve as adjectives or adverbs.

When prepositional phrases serve as adjectives, they modify nouns, as in the following examples:

On the shelf was chocolate in boxes.

The prepositional phrase “in boxes” modifies the noun “chocolate.”

Most of the houses by the lake were built decades ago.

The prepositional phrase “by the lake” modifies the noun “houses.”

When prepositional phrases serve as adverbs, they generally modify verbs.

Some species of insects live under rocks.

The prepositional phrase “under rocks” modifies “live” and indicates where the insects live.

The hikers took a break at noon.

The prepositional phrase “at noon” modifies “took” and indicates when the hikers took a break.

In the waves, some dolphins were playing.

The prepositional phrase “in the waves” modifies “were playing” and indicates where the dolphins were playing.

Writers can mistakenly position a prepositional phrase, rendering a sentence illogical.

💡MUST KNOW:

A modifying prepositional phrase must always be as close as possible to the word that it modifies.

When a modifying prepositional phrase is misplaced, odd – and sometimes humorous – things occur.

Incorrect: A man was walking a dog in a Speedo.

Unless the dog was wearing a Speedo, the prepositional phrase “in a Speedo” is a misplaced modifier; it should be modifying the noun “man.”

The sentence could be fixed in various ways that eliminate the misplaced modifier, including:

A man in a Speedo was walking a dog.

Notice that, in the revised version of the sentence, the prepositional phrase “in a Speedo” is close to the noun that it modifies, “man.”

Spot the problem in the following sentence:

Incorrect: The craftsman worked to develop a guitar that would allow a player to perfectly express himself when he played a song for twenty years.

Playing a song for twenty years? Humorous, right? The prepositional phrase “for twenty years” is misplaced, because it should be modifying “worked.” A corrected sentence could read:

The craftsman worked for twenty years to develop a guitar that would allow a player to perfectly express himself when he played a song.

The following is also correct, because a prepositional phrase that modifies a finite verb in a clause can be placed before the clause:

For twenty years, the craftsman worked to develop a guitar that would allow a player to perfectly express himself when he played a song.

Let’s try another example.

Incorrect: Every Saturday afternoon, the men who lived in the senior home on Kimball Avenue played cards in the park under a tree.

Pay close attention when you encounter a sentence that contains more than one prepositional phrase, because the order of the phrases matters.

Every Saturday afternoon, the men who lived in the senior home on Kimball Avenue played cards under a tree in the park.

Let’s look at one more.

Incorrect: Justine still refuses to create an online profile, even though several of her friends met people whom they ended up marrying on dating websites.

Unless Justine’s friends were live streaming their weddings on dating websites, the above sentence contains a misplaced prepositional phrase.

Justine still refuses to create an online profile, even though several of her friends ended up marrying people whom they met on dating websites.

Notice we must rearrange the sentence a bit more in this case to ensure that it clearly conveys that the couples met on dating websites and did not marry on dating websites.

Incorrect: Justine still refuses to create an online profile, even though several of her friends met people on dating websites whom they ended up marrying.

Although the above sentence places the prepositional phrase fairly close to the verb, “met,” that the phrase is meant to modify, the sentence does not clearly express the idea that the people met on dating websites.

💡MUST KNOW:

In answering Sentence Correction questions, be on the lookout for prepositional phrases that are improperly placed and, thus, modify the wrong things. Such sentences will depart from the meanings that their authors were seeking to express.

Targeted Practice Solutions

Targeted Practice: 1

Explanation:

This sentence contains a misplaced modifier. As written, “served samples of rare French wines to the people attending the event in fine crystal stemware” makes it appear that the people or, perhaps, the event was in fine crystal stemware. Of course, we want the sentence to convey that the samples of wines were served in fine crystal stemware. The following correctly written sentence does so.

The sommelier, wearing the colorful pin given only to master sommeliers, served to the people attending the event samples of rare French wines in fine crystal stemware.

Correct Answer: (B)
Targeted Practice: 2

Explanation:

The sentence contains three prepositional phrases, “in red, “at his side,” and “in the shape of the car….” All three are correctly placed, with the result that the sentence effectively conveys a logical meaning.

Correct Answer: (A)
Targeted Practice: 3

Explanation:

This sentence contains a misplaced modifier, “on the way to work,” and therefore conveys that the ducks were lost on their way to work. Of course, we want the sentence to convey that Jonathan stopped on his way to work. The following sentence does just that.

On the way to work, Jonathan, a man of style and grace, stopped to help a family of ducks that seemed lost.

Correct Answer: (B)
Targeted Practice: 4

Explanation:

Remember, a prepositional phrase that modifies a finite verb in a clause can be placed before the clause.

So, “on the way to school” is correctly placed to modify the finite verb “stopped.”

Correct Answer: (A)
Targeted Practice: 5

Explanation:

Was the soccer game happening in the stands? Probably not, right? So, the prepositional phrase “in the stands” is misplaced. The people were bantering in the stands, so we need the prepositional phrase to be placed in a way such that that meaning is the one conveyed. A corrected sentence is:

In the stands, we bantered about the soccer game.

Correct Answer: (B)
Targeted Practice: 6

Explanation:

Was Jacques in the oven? We hope not. The prepositional phrase “in the oven” is misplaced. It should be modifying the vegetables, not Jacques. A corrected sentence is:

Jacques checked the roasted vegetables in the oven.

or

Jacques checked the roasted vegetables, which were in the oven.

Correct Answer: (B)
Targeted Practice: 7

Explanation:

Can anyone explain how a lost dog could be rescued on Facebook? This sentence is illogical, right? The author meant to say that people read on Facebook that the dog was rescued. A corrected sentence could read:

On Facebook, we read about the lost dog that was rescued.

Correct Answer: (B)
Targeted Practice: 8

Explanation:

Can you imagine the train leaving with a large, blue suitcase? That sight would be interesting, right? The prepositional phrase “with a large, blue suitcase” is misplaced, since it should be modifying “man” not “train.” A corrected sentence is:

Some people standing on the platform said that they saw a man with a large, blue suitcase boarding the train that left for Prague at 9:02.

Now, some of you will be tempted to claim that the sentence now says that a “blue suitcase is boarding the train.” However, as we will discuss later, a noun and its modifiers can make a new, unique noun. So, “a man with a large, blue suitcase” is the noun, and that man was “boarding the train.”

Correct Answer: (B)
Targeted Practice: 9

Explanation:

Can you imagine an assistant pastry chef covered with pink icing? The prepositional phrase “with pink icing” is misplaced. The phrase should be modifying the cake, not the assistant pastry chef. An effective sentence is:

The assistant pastry chef frosted the cake with pink icing.

Correct Answer: (B)
Targeted Practice: 10

Explanation:

Can you picture the dinosaurs living in the Jurassic period in the new book? That meaning is not the meaning that the author intended, right? The prepositional phrase “in his new book” is improperly modifying “lived during the Jurassic period.” An effective sentence could read:

In his new book, the paleontologist discusses newfound information on the many types of dinosaurs that lived during the Jurassic period.

Correct Answer: (B)

--- primary_color: green secondary_color: lightgray text_color: black shuffle_questions: false shuffle_answers: false --- ## Sentence Correction Example 4 It was demonstrated by a study of genes conducted at a university that deactivation of genes associated with development of illness results from adoption of a healthy lifestyle. 1. [x] (A) It was demonstrated by a study of genes conducted at a university that deactivation of genes associated with development of illness results from adoption of 2. [ ] (B) A study of genes conducted at a university demonstrating deactivation of genes associated with the development of illness resulting from adopting >
Correct Answer: (A) 3. [ ] (C) When conducted as a study, genes at a university demonstrated resulting deactivation of genes associated with development of illness from adoption of >
Correct Answer: (A) 4. [ ] (D) At a university, genes associated with development of illness in a conducted study demonstrated that deactivation of genes results from adopting >
Correct Answer: (A) 5. [ ] (E) A study conducted of genes at a university demonstrated resulting deactivation of genes associated with development of illness from adoption of >
Correct Answer: (A)
View Solution
Question Level: medium

We can eliminate most of the incorrect choices in this question through noticing issues with the placement of prepositional phrases.

YOUR CHOICE(A) It was demonstrated by a study of genes conducted at a university that deactivation of genes associated with development of illness results from adoption of a healthy lifestyle.

It’s a little tricky to tell that this choice is the correct answer, because this version uses an expletive “it” and delayed subject construction “it was demonstrated … that.”

All the same, by reading carefully, we can see that this version has no modifier issues: “a study of genes conducted at a university” makes sense, and “deactivation of genes … results from adoption of a healthy lifestyle” makes sense.

So, this version has no errors and conveys a meaning that makes sense.

(B) A study of genes conducted at a university demonstrating deactivation of genes associated with the development of illness resulting from adopting a healthy lifestyle.

This version has no main verb and, thus, is not a complete sentence.

(C) When conducted as a study, genes at a university demonstrated resulting deactivation of genes associated with development of illness from adoption of a healthy lifestyle.

As a result of the placement of the prepositional phrase “at a university” directly after “genes,” this version suggests the illogical idea that genes were “at a university.”

Similarly, as a result of the placement of the prepositional phrase “from adoption of a healthy lifestyle” directly after “development of illness,” this version suggests the illogical idea that development of illness is “from adoption of a healthy lifestyle.”

Notice also that “when conducted as a study, genes … demonstrated” suggests the nonsensical idea that genes demonstrated something when genes were “conducted as a study.” A study of genes would be “conducted.” Genes themselves would not be “conducted as a study.”

(D) At a university, genes associated with development of illness in a conducted study demonstrated that deactivation of genes results from adopting a healthy lifestyle.

Because the prepositional phrase “at a university” is placed before the clause “genes … demonstrated …,” this version conveys the illogical idea that genes demonstrated something at a university.

Also, since the prepositional phrase “in a conducted study” is placed directly after “development of illness,” this version seems to suggest the illogical idea that illness would develop in a conducted study. The GMAT would prefer a version that more clearly conveys a logical meaning when read literally.

(E) A study conducted of genes at a university demonstrated resulting deactivation of genes associated with development of illness from adoption of a healthy lifestyle.

As a result of the placement of the prepositional phrase “of genes” after “a study conducted,” this version does not effectively convey a logical meaning. After all, there is no way for a study to be “conducted of genes.” A study of genes could be conducted, but this version does not effectively convey the meaning that a study of genes was conducted.

Also, as a result of the placement of the prepositional phrase “from adoption of a healthy lifestyle” directly after “development of illness,” this version suggests the illogical idea that development of illness is “from adoption of a healthy lifestyle.”

Correct Answer: (A)
--- primary_color: green secondary_color: lightgray text_color: black shuffle_questions: false shuffle_answers: false --- ## Sentence Correction Example 5 Sea and air travel are much safer now than they were in the past because computers used to make accurate predictions based in part on detailed information on how storms are formed by weather forecasters. 1. [ ] (A) because computers used to make accurate predictions based in part on detailed information on how storms are formed by weather forecasters >
Correct Answer: (E) 2. [ ] (B) due to the use of computers that accurately make predictions based in part on detailed information on the formation of storms by weather forecasters >
Correct Answer: (E) 3. [ ] (C) because weather forecasters’ use of computers makes predictions that are accurately based in part on detailed information on storm formation >
Correct Answer: (E) 4. [ ] (D) due to computers used to accurately make predictions based in part on detailed information on the formation of storms by weather forecasters >
Correct Answer: (E) 5. [x] (E) because of weather forecasters’ using computers to make accurate predictions based in part on detailed information on how storms are formed
View Solution
Question Level: hard

In order to correctly answer this question, we have to notice placement of modifiers and meanings conveyed.

For instance, the placement of the prepositional phrase “by weather forecasters” after “storms are formed” or after “the formation of storms” results in sentences that convey the nonsensical meaning that storms are formed by weather forecasters.

(A) Sea and air travel are much safer now than they were in the past because computers used to make accurate predictions based in part on detailed information on how storms are formed by weather forecasters .

Rather than say “because computers are used to,” this version says merely “computers used to.” Therefore, it conveys the illogical meaning that sea and air travel are safer now than they were in the past because computers used to (meaning did in the past but no longer do), make accurate predictions.

Also, since the prepositional phrase “by weather forecasters” directly follows “storms are formed,” this version seems to convey that storms are formed by weather forecasters.

(B) Sea and air travel are much safer now than they were in the past due to the use of computers that accurately make predictions based in part on detailed information on the formation of storms by weather forecasters .

The expression “due to” is incorrectly used. On the GMAT, and in most formal writing, “due to” is correctly used to begin a phrase that modifies a noun. In this case, “due to” incorrectly begins a phrase that modifies a verb, “are.”

Also, the adverb “accurately” is misused. The point to be made is not that computers “accurately make” predictions. The act of making is not one that can be accurate or inaccurate. It is what is made, in this case “predictions,” that would be accurate or inaccurate.

Also, since the prepositional phrase “by weather forecasters” directly follows “the formation of storms,” this version seems to convey that storms are formed by weather forecasters.

(C) Sea and air travel are much safer now than they were in the past because weather forecasters’ use of computers makes predictions that are accurately based in part on detailed information on storm formation .

This version suggests the illogical idea that the forecasters’ use of computers makes predictions. The forecasters themselves use computers to make predictions. Their “use of computers” does not make predictions.

Also, the adverb “accurately” is misused. The point to be made is that the predictions are accurate, not that the predictions are accurately based on information. One would not accurately base a prediction on information.

(D) Sea and air travel are much safer now than they were in the past due to computers used to accurately make predictions based in part on detailed information on the formation of storms by weather forecasters .

The expression “due to” is incorrectly used. On the GMAT, and in most formal writing, “due to” is correctly used to begin a phrase that modifies a noun. In this case, “due to” incorrectly begins a phrase that modifies a verb, “are.”

Also, the adverb “accurately” is misused. The point to be made is not that computers “accurately make” predictions. The act of making is not one that can be accurate or inaccurate. It is what is made, in this case “predictions” that would be accurate or inaccurate.

Also, since the prepositional phrase “by weather forecasters” directly follows “the formation of storms,” this version seems to convey that storms are formed by weather forecasters.

YOUR CHOICE(E) Sea and air travel are much safer now than they were in the past because of weather forecasters’ using computers to make accurate predictions based in part on detailed information on how storms are formed .

This version conveys the logical meaning that sea and air travel are safer because of weather forecasters’ using computer to make accurate predictions.

The use of the possessive “forecasters’” with the gerund “using” is correct, but may not sound correct to many test-takers. In fact, the structure, possessive + gerund, is included in this choice for the specific purpose of making this correct answer sound incorrect.

Correct Answer: (E)
