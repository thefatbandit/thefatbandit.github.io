5.7.3 When Relative Clauses Can Modify Their Antecedents “Remotely”

  • MBA
  • GMAT
  • TTP
  • SC

Lesson Content

In most cases in which we see a relative clause in a correct answer to a Sentence Correction question, that clause will directly follow the noun that the clause modifies.

💡MUST KNOW:

There are structures that are considered acceptable or correct in which a relative clause is separated from the noun that it modifies. In such a structure, the relative clause is called a “remote relative clause.”

There are two ways in which a relative clause may be separated from the noun that it modifies in a correct answer.

A relative clause may be separated from the noun that it modifies by another modifier.

A relative clause may be separated from the noun that it modifies by another modifier, such as a prepositional phrase or an appositive.

Consider the following example:

The town on the island, which is a sleepy seaside village, attracts many tourists each summer.

Is the relative clause “which is a sleepy seaside village” properly placed? The answer is yes, it is.

Students often wonder whether “which is a sleepy seaside village” should modify “town” or “island.” The answer is that, essentially, “town on the island” is one large noun, and the relative clause “which is a sleepy seaside village” is modifying the entire noun.

Let’s look at that sentence again:

The town on the island, which is a sleepy seaside village, attracts many tourists each summer.

Notice that the prepositional phrase, “on the island” acts as a restrictive modifier.

Let’s examine this idea further.

The tree grows taller every year.

The tree by the river grows taller every year.

The tree by the river that hangs over the bank grows taller every year.

The tree by the river that hangs over the bank below the ford grows taller every year.

Notice that each new sentence contains a greater number of modifiers describing the noun “tree.”

Let’s look at another sentence:

Marty has a fruit stand in his town’s farmers market that he uses not only to sell fruits and vegetables but also to spread his wisdom of logic and reasoning.

Presented with a sentence like this one, students often wonder whether the restrictive relative clause “that he uses to not only sell fruits and vegetables but also to spread his wisdom of logic and reasoning” should be next to “fruit stand” instead of next to “market.” The answer is that this sentence is correct as written.

Notice that the prepositional phrase “in his town’s farmers market” is essential to our understanding of what fruit stand is being discussed.

Thus, even though the modifier “that he uses to sell …” is not placed directly after the noun “stand,” the placement of the modifier makes sense.

Let’s consider another example:

Employees organized the walkout to show support for their colleagues in corporate offices abroad who had been denied an annual pay increase in favor of record bonuses for top executives.

The relative clause beginning with “who had been denied …” correctly modifies “colleagues in corporate offices abroad.”

💡MUST KNOW:

A relative clause may be separated from the noun that it modifies by another modifier, such as a prepositional phrase or an appositive.

Now, you might be wondering how we decide which noun a relative clause is meant to modify if the clause is placed after a structure that contains multiple nouns, such as “town on the island” or “tree by the river.” The answer is that the relative clause is understood to modify the shortest logical target.

Let’s explore this concept by considering an example:

The most beautiful house in the valley, which was built last summer, is not overly large.

The relative clause “which was built last summer” is placed after “the most beautiful house in the valley, which contains two nouns, “house” and “valley.” To determine what the clause is meant to modify, we can look for the shortest logical target.

The shortest possible target of the clause is “valley,” but “valley” is not a logical target of the clause, because a valley would not be “built last summer.”

The next target to consider is “house in the valley.” We can see that “house in the valley” is a logical target of the clause, because a house in the valley would be “built last summer.” Thus, “which was built last summer” is understood to modify “house in the valley.”

OK, what about this example?

The most beautiful house in the valley, which runs between two mountains, is not overly large.

In this case, the relative clause “which runs between two mountains” logically modifies “valley.” So, in this case we don’t have to check any further to find the target of the clause, because “valley” is the shortest logical target of the modifier and, thus, must be what it modifies.

Let’s consider one more example:

The F22 Raptor, a modern military fighter jet, has two engines with movable parts that direct their exhaust, each of which produces 35,000 pounds of thrust.

“Each of which produces 35,000 pounds of thrust” is a relative clause.

The shortest possible target is “exhaust,” but exhaust would not produce 35,000 pounds of thrust, nor would the next shortest possible target, “movable parts that direct their exhaust.”

The shortest logical target of the relative clause turns out to be “two engines with movable parts that direct their exhaust,” since it is logical that each of two engines “produces 35,000 pounds of thrust.” So, we can tell that the clause modifies “two engines with movable parts that direct their exhaust.”

💡MUST KNOW:

A relative clause placed after a structure that contains multiple nouns is understood to modify the shortest logical target.

Let’s now discuss the second way in which a relative clause may be separated from the noun that it modifies.

A relative clause may be separated from the noun that it modifies by the main verb of the sentence.

We have seen that a relative clause can be separated from the noun it modifies by another modifier.

In some cases, relative clauses that modify the subjects of sentences are rather long.

Even as we speak, solar panels that will generate power much more efficiently than any panels in use today are being developed.

We can see that the subject “solar panels” and the main verb “are being developed” are quite far apart because they are separated by the long relative clause “that will generate power much more efficiently than any panels in use today.” As a result, when we read that sentence, after we see the subject, we have to read for quite a while before arriving at the main verb.

To avoid separating a sentence’s subject and main verb that way, we can write the sentence differently, putting the main verb of the sentence directly after the subject and before the relative clause that modifies the subject.

Even as we speak, solar panels are being developed that will generate power much more efficiently than any panels in use today.

Now, the subject and main verb are not separated by the long relative clause.

Of course, to the untrained eye, the relative clause may seem to be misplaced because a verb separates the clause from the noun it modifies.

That said, such a structure won’t work in every sentence.

Of course, the main verb in the example, “are being developed,” is a verb that indicates that a development is occurring.

Let’s look at another example:

An expert is coming who will help the design team determine the best way to use cutting edge technology in some of our new products.

The subject is “expert.” The main verb is “is coming.” Notice the long relative clause “who will help the design team to determine the best way to use cutting edge technology in some of our new products.” Can you image how the sentence would read if that long relative clause were placed between the subject and main verb? Wouldn’t such a sentence be challenging to read? Take a look:

An expert who will help the design team determine the best way to use cutting edge technology in some of our new products is coming.

We wait a long time to get to the action of the sentence, right? Notice that the original version of the sentence avoids having such an issue by placing the subject close to the main verb.

So – what’s the takeaway?

Certain types of main verbs that indicate arrival, positioning, and coming into being can exist between a relative clause and the noun that it modifies in cases in which there is unlikely to be any confusion and the author would like to avoid putting the main verb after a long intervening relative clause.

💡MUST KNOW:

Certain types of main verbs that indicate arrival, positioning, and coming into being can exist between a relative clause and the noun that it modifies in cases in which there is unlikely to be any confusion and the author would like to avoid putting the main verb after a long intervening relative clause.

(A) A holistic method for solving cancer is being developed at the university’s wellness clinic, which has few negative side effects but, at this stage, has only been working for a fraction of the people who have used it.

In this version, because the relative clause “which has few negative side effects” is placed after “wellness clinic,” the clause seems to illogically modify “wellness clinic.”

Also, to confirm that this choice is not the correct answer, we can notice the placement of “only” before “been.” The point to be made is not that the method has only been working. The point to be made is that it has been working for only a small fraction of the people who use it.

(B) Under development at the university’s wellness clinic is a holistic method for solving cancer that has few negative side effects, which, at this stage, are working for only a fraction of the people who have used it.

This version works up to the first comma. After the first comma, the modifier “which, at this stage, are working …” seems to illogically modify “side effects.” Since side effects would not be “working for a fraction of the people,” that modification does not make sense.

YOUR CHOICE(C) At the university’s wellness clinic, a holistic method for solving cancer is being developed that has few negative side effects but, at this stage, has been working for only a fraction of the people who have used it.

While the relative clause “that has few negative side effects” is separated from what it modifies, “method for solving cancer,” by the main verb of the sentence, the GMAT would find this sentence acceptable, since what the modifier is meant to modify is clear, and since placing the main verb after the modifier would result in the main verb’s being placed far from the subject.

Also, the modifier itself is well structured and, thus, clearly conveys that the method has few negative side effects but has been working for only a small fraction of the people who have used it.

(D) While in development at the university’s wellness clinic, a holistic method for solving cancer at this stage has few negative side effects, which has been working for only a fraction of the people who have used it.

The beginning of this version conveys the somewhat illogical meaning that the method has few negative side effects while in development. So, when it is not in development it has many negative side effects?

Also, what “at this stage” is meant to modify is not clear. Is “at this stage” meant to indicate that the method is at a stage, or to modify “cancer?” If “at this stage” is meant to modify “cancer,” a further issue that exists is that the sentence does not mention any particular stage of cancer. So, what “this stage” would refer to is not clear.

Also, the relative clause “which has been working …” has no clear target. It would make sense for this clause to modify “method,” but “method” is far removed from this clause.

(E) Being developed at the university’s wellness clinic, a holistic method for solving cancer that has few negative side effects but that, at this stage, only works for a fraction of the people who have used it.

This version would make sense if it had a main verb. However, it does not have a main verb and, therefore, is not a complete sentence.

Correct Answer: (C)

Correct Answer: (A)
View Solution
Question Level: hard

Correctly answering this question requires noticing meaning issues and issues involving subject-verb agreement.

It also requires noticing that, in the correct answer, the modifier “which … results” refers not to the noun that directly precedes it, “levels,” but rather to the noun phrase “elevation of levels of stress hormones.”

YOUR CHOICE(A) The experience of chronic stress generally results in elevation of levels of stress hormones, which, in turn, results in reduced immune function and increased weight gain.

The relative clause “which, in turn, results …” clearly and logically modifies the entire preceding noun phrase, “elevation of levels of stress hormones.”

Notice also that the singular “elevation” agrees with the singular verb “results” in the modifier.

Thus, this version is grammatically correct and conveys a meaning that makes sense.

(B) The experience of chronic stress generally results in levels of stress hormones that are elevated that, in turn, results in reduced immune function and increased weight gain.

The relative clause “that, in turn, results …” has to modify one of the preceding nouns, “levels” and “stress hormones.” Since neither noun is singular, the singular “results” in the modifier cannot agree with any noun that the clause could modify. So, there is a subject-verb agreement issue.

(C) The experience of chronic stress generally results in elevating levels of stress hormones and, in turn, result in reduced immune function and increased weight gain.

The expression “elevating levels” is nonsensical. The levels would be elevated; levels would not themselves elevate.

Also, since the core of the sentence is “The experience results … and result …,” there is a subject-verb agreement issue, as the singular “experience” does not agree with the plural “result.”

Also, the experience, which is just one thing, would not result and, in turn, result. There has to be a second thing that would result “in turn.”

(D) The experience of chronic stress generally results in levels of stress hormones that elevate and that, in turn, result in reduced immune function and increased weight gain.

This version conveys the nonsensical meaning that the experience of chronic stress results in “levels of stress hormones that elevate.” In other words, this version does not convey that the experience of chronic stress causes levels of stress hormones to elevate. Rather it conveys that the experience of chronic stress results in levels of stress hormones – not high or low levels but levels in general, as if no levels of stress hormones would exist without stress – and that the levels then elevate themselves, as if levels can elevate themselves.

(E) The experience of chronic stress generally results in an elevated level of stress hormones, which, in turn, result in reduced immune function and increased weight gain.

This version expresses the illogical idea that stress hormones result in reduced immune function and increased weight gain.

That idea is illogical because tangible things, such as stress hormones, cannot “result in” something. Only a process or event, such as elevation of stress hormones, can result in something.

So, stress hormones may cause reduced immune function and increased weight gain, but stress hormones would not result in those effects.

Correct Answer: (A)
