8.4.4 Wrong Answer Type Four: An Answer that Conflicts in Some Way with Information that Appears in the Passage

Lesson Content One aspect of the GMAT is that it tests a test-taker’s degree of attention to detail, and one way in which the GMAT tests attention to detail is by including in a Find the Conclusion question choices that may seem to follow from what the passage says but actually conflict with some detail of what the passage says. Consider the following example: The city of Danterton gets tax revenue by adding a sales tax based on a percentage of the price of items sold to the sale of all retail merchandise sold in Danterton.

9.2 The Yes/No Test for Evaluate the Argument Questions

Lesson Content Many Evaluate the Argument questions can be solved via the use of what’s called the Yes/No test. In many cases, the answer choices in Evaluate the Argument questions pose questions that can be answered in two ways, either “Yes” or “No.” In such a case, the correct answer to an Evaluate the Argument Question must be a question such that, if it is answered “Yes”, the argument is affected in one way (either strengthened or weakened), and, if it is answered “No,” the argument is affected in the reverse way.

9.4 Strategy for Attacking Evaluate the Argument Questions

Lesson Content To solve Evaluate the Argument Questions, follow this strategy: Step 1 – Carefully Read, Break Down, and Completely Understand the Argument In an Evaluate the Argument question, the passage will present a plan, proposal, or argument. Lesson Content Many Evaluate the Argument answer choices allow us to apply the Yes/No test, because the choices pose questions that can be answered “Yes” or “No.” However, other answer choices can be structured in ways such that the Yes/No test doesn’t work if we apply the test literally.
