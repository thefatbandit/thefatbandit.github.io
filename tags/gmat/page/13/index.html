6.10.4 The Perfect Continuous Tenses

Lesson Content Just as the simple present, simple past, and simple future tenses have continuous tense counterparts, the perfect tenses – the present perfect, past perfect, and future perfect – have continuous counterparts. To compare the present perfect tense and the present perfect continuous tense, consider the following examples: That eagle has flown overhead many times. That eagle has been flying overhead for the last twenty minutes. In the first example, the present perfect is used to convey that, over a period of time extending from the past to the present, the eagle has flown overhead on multiple occasions.

6.13 The Subjunctive Mood: Hypothetical Situations

Lesson Content The subjunctive mood is used for statements that lack certainty, such as those that describe hypothetical or conditional situations, dreams, wishes, desires, or doubts. In a sentence that describes a hypothetical situation, the form of “to be” that should be used is the verb “were,” never the verb “was.” At first, this usage will probably sound incorrect to you. 💡MUST KNOW: Sentences that describe hypothetical situations must use the subjunctive mood.
