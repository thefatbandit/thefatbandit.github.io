8.8 Incomplete Comparisons

Lesson Content Sometimes a sentence leaves a comparison incomplete. Let’s look at an example: Incorrect: This tent is just as waterproof. The meaning of this sentence is ambiguous because of an incomplete comparison. This tent is just as waterproof as more expensive models. The comparison becomes more complete via the addition of a noun to which the existing noun is compared. Incorrect: This project will cost less. Cost less than what?

8.9 Sentences That Express Comparisons That Are Incomplete Even Though the Items Meant to Be Compared Are All Mentioned

Lesson Content There is another move involving incomplete comparisons that the GMAT can make. Incorrect: When it is compared with eating butter, eating olive oil is healthier. Notice what’s going on in the above example. Yes, the sentence says “compared with eating butter,” but “compared with eating butter” is part of a modifying clause, “when it is compared with eating butter,” that modifies “eating olive oil is healthier.” So, read literally, the sentence conveys that eating olive oil is healthier than something else – the sentence does not say what else – at certain times.

8.10 “Like” versus “Such As” and “Including”

Lesson Content The word “like” can be used in a comparison, but not to introduce an example. To see the difference between the use of “like” and the use of “such as” demonstrated, consider the example sentences below: Incorrect: When traveling through the Everglades, one may see many animals, like snakes, mammals, and alligators. Since “like” is strictly a comparison word on the GMAT, using it in this context would imply that one may see, not the animals mentioned, but rather animals similar to, or “like,” snakes, mammals, or alligators.

8.11 “Like” versus “As”

Lesson Content Just as people often misuse the words “like” and “such as,” they also often use the word “like” when the word “as” is needed. Incorrect: The teacher acts like she were a drill sergeant. While “as” is a versatile word that can be used as a preposition or a conjunction, the comparison word “like” can be used only as a preposition. So, the above example does not make sense, since, in the above example, the object of “like” is a clause, “she were a drill sergeant.

8.12 Comparisons That Involve Quantity Words and Countable or Uncountable Nouns

Lesson Content Some nouns are countable, meaning that they have plural forms and that their plural forms name quantities made up of countable items. Other nouns are uncountable, meaning that quantities named by these nouns are not made up of countable items. One reason why understanding the distinction between countable and uncountable nouns is useful in GMAT Sentence Correction is that the words used in expressing comparisons involving quantities, called “quantity words,” vary along with the types of quantities compared.

8.14 Correctly Using Quantity Words When Discussing Quantities of Time, Money, or Weight

Money works similarly, as we can see in the following example: The account currently holds less than two dollars. 8.15 “Greater Than” versus “More Than”

Lesson Content “Greater than” is used in comparing the sizes, levels, degrees, magnitudes, or extents of things. “More … than” is not synonymous with “larger than” or “higher than.” “More” can be used in multiple ways, including as a determiner that directly modifies what follows it. Consider the following example: Her dislike of gambling is greater than her dislike of exercise. Notice that, in this case, the magnitudes of her dislikes are compared.
