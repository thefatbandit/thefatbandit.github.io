Lesson Content In the last section, we saw that the use of the word “and” can result in the creation of a compound plural subject that requires a plural verb. In this section, we’re going to discuss additive phrases. What we’ll see as we discuss additive phrases is that, even though expressions such as “along with” or “as well as” resemble “and,” additive phrases don’t work the way “and” works with sentence subjects.

3.9 Or, Either … Or, and Neither … Nor

Lesson Content Sentences and clauses containing compound subjects joined by the conjunctions “or,” “either … or,” or “neither … nor” abide by their own special subject-verb agreement rule. In these cases, the verb must agree with the subject noun that is closest to the verb. 💡MUST KNOW: The verb in a clause or sentence containing a compound subject joined by the conjunctions “or,” “either … or,” or “neither … nor” must agree with the subject noun to which it is closest.

Lesson Content Leave it to the GMAT test-makers to turn sentences inside out. 💡MUST KNOW: In an inverted sentence, the main verb precedes the subject. 3.11 Collective Nouns

Lesson Content Collective nouns are nouns that name groups of people or things. 💡MUST KNOW: Collective nouns are nouns that refer to groups of people or things. When collective nouns appear as subjects on GMAT Sentence Correction questions, they are almost always considered singular. Consider the following sentence: The team is at the lake this weekend, for a workshop on conflict resolution skills. Notice that the collective noun “team” is singular since in this context it refers to a single collection of people.

3.12 The Collective Nouns “Majority” and “Minority”

Lesson Content Like other collective nouns that we have discussed, the collective nouns “majority” and “minority” can be considered singular or plural depending on the contexts in which they appear. In the following sentences, “majority” and “minority” are plural: A majority of the students are already applying for jobs. In today’s world, in which most people have some knowledge of geography, only a small minority believe Earth to be flat.

3.13 Collective Nouns that are not Clearly Singular or Plural in Sentence Correction Questions

Lesson Content Of course, since a collective noun subject can be correctly paired with a singular verb or plural verb, we could see a Sentence Correction question in which it is not entirely clear whether a collective noun would most logically be considered a singular subject or plural subject. In such a case, our best move may be to choose an answer choice by looking for differences between choices other than agreement between that collective noun and its associated verb.

3.14 Subjects with Certain Preceding Elements

Lesson Content Some preceding elements in a subject can require that the subject take a singular verb even if the subject seems to be plural. For instance, a subject composed of a noun preceded by “every,” “each,” or “many a” always takes a singular verb. Consider the following examples: The guys at the prom look amazing. Every guy at the prom looks amazing. In the first sentence, the subject, “guys,” is plural and therefore requires a plural verb.

3.15 Pronoun-Verb Agreement

Lesson Content A pronoun is a word that takes the place of a noun. Robert said that he will be able to attend the party. In this sentence, the pronoun “he” refers to the antecedent “Robert.” Notice that, by using the pronoun, we don’t have to repeat the noun “Robert.” The zebra and the hippo decided that they were tired, so they looked for a comfortable place to sleep. In this sentence, the pronoun “they” refers to the antecedents “the zebra and the hippo.

3.16 Relative Pronouns

Lesson Content Relative pronouns, such as “which,” “that,” and “who,” can be singular or plural depending on their referents. The dresser that is on sale is still expensive. The curtains that are on sale are beautiful. As demonstrated in the sentences above, the relative pronoun “that” can take a singular or plural verb depending on whether the noun to which “that” refers is singular or plural. 💡MUST KNOW: When a relative pronoun refers to a singular noun, it too is singular and takes a singular verb.

3.19 Plural Indefinite Pronouns

Lesson Content Some indefinite pronouns are always plural and therefore always take a plural verb. These indefinite pronouns include the following: “both,” “few,” “many,” and “several.” Be sure to memorize these plural indefinite pronouns. 💡MUST KNOW: A plural indefinite pronoun always takes a plural verb.
