5.9.11 Use of a Restrictive or Nonrestrictive Past Participial Phrase where using a Past Participial Phrase of the Other Type, Nonrestrictive or Restrictive, Would Make More Sense

Lesson Content A second way in which Sentence Correction question writers can create issues involving past participial phrases is to use a restrictive or nonrestrictive past participial phrase where using a past participial phrase of the other type, nonrestrictive or restrictive, would make more sense. Incorrect: Only a tiny percentage of students pursue degrees in physics considered one of the most challenging college subjects. Since there is no comma between “physics” and the past participial phrase “considered one of the …,” the use of the phrase restricts “physics” to one particular type of physics, the physics considered one of the most challenging college subjects.

5.10 Absolute Phrases

Lesson Content An absolute phrase is a phrase that: • consists of only a noun and its modifiers, and • modifies an entire clause. To remember that absolute phrases modify entire clauses, people sometimes say, “Absolute phrases modify absolutely everything.” The following sentence contains an absolute phrase. His work completed, John headed home for the night. In this example, “his work completed” is an absolute phrase. Here’s another: Jennifer sat waiting in the dentist chair, her arms and legs crossed.

6.6 Present Perfect Tense

Lesson Content When an event began in the past and continues into the present, we use the present perfect tense. 💡MUST KNOW: When an action began in the past and continues into the present, we use the present perfect tense The present perfect tense is formed by combining “has” or “have” with the past participle of the main verb. I have lived here for five years. The above sentence conveys that the event “lived” began in the past but is still happening in the present moment.
