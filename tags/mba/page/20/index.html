2.5 In Critical Reasoning Questions, Authors Assume that their Arguments are Correct.

Lesson Content In Critical Reasoning, we use the term “author” to refer to whoever is setting forth an argument in a question. The “authors” of these arguments vary from question to question, but the one commonality that these authors all share is that they assume that they are correct, whatever their arguments may be. 💡MUST KNOW: In making the arguments found in Critical Reasoning questions, the authors of these arguments assume that they are correct; they assume that their arguments are sound and logical.

2.6.2 Assumption Correct Answer Type 2: States an Assumption that Must be True in order for a Cause-and-Effect Conclusion to be Correct

Lesson Content Many arguments that appear in the GMAT involve cause-and-effect reasoning, something that we’ll often mention in the chapters that follow, but we’ll begin building our expertise in cause-and-effect reasoning here. An author makes a cause-and-effect claim when she maintains that some factor x is the cause of some outcome y. When an author makes a cause-and-effect claim, she assumes that her cause-and-effect claim is correct; she assumes that there could not be an alternative explanation for the observed events.

2.6.3 Assumption Correct Answer Type 3: States an Assumption that There are No Other Paths to Success

Lesson Content In Assumption questions, we often encounter arguments that conclude that some plan will not work or that some outcome will not occur. However, the evidence that she provides will not be sufficient alone for the argument to work. 💡MUST KNOW: Whenever an author concludes that a plan will not work or that some outcome will not occur, she must assume that there is not some other way or some other path (that she has failed to consider) via which the plan could work or via which the outcome could occur.

2.6.4 Assumption Correct Answer Type 4: Highlights an Assumption that a Plan for Solving a Problem will not Result in the Same Problem or a Similar One

Lesson Content As we now know, many arguments in the GMAT focus on plans or courses of action. Another way of thinking about this is that, when an author sets forth a plan or course of action, she believes that she has correctly and completely analyzed whatever situation the plan or course of action centers on. So then, in Assumption questions, authors typically make the assumption that a plan or a course of action they suggest for solving a problem will not itself result in the same problem in a new way.

2.6.6 Assumption Correct Answer Type 6: Highlights an Assumption that a Change Occurring over Time does not Render the Conclusion Invalid

Lesson Content Arguments about topics such as plans, predictions, or explanations can include elements that are from different time periods. What all of these arguments have in common are assumptions that a conclusion about something occurring in one time period can be logically based on evidence derived from events in another time period. For instance, in arriving at a conclusion about events that occurred a thousand years ago by observing certain conditions that exist today, one may be assuming that the relevant conditions that exist today are similar to conditions that existed a thousand years ago.
