4.7 Explicitly State an Assumption Upon Which the Argument Depends

Lesson Content As you well know, an assumption is a piece of missing information that must be true in order for the logic of the argument to hold. Every argument relies upon many assumptions; it isn’t possible to include every single piece of relevant information in an argument. When an assumption is uncontroversial (it is something that “everyone would agree is true”), the presence in the argument of that unstated assumption has no impact on the strength of the argument.

4.8 Address an Issue That Could Affect the Validity of the Conclusion

Lesson Content Often, in a scenario described in a Strengthen the Argument passage, there is room for issues that could undermine the conclusion. For example, imagine if the CEO of an electric car company made the claim that his electric cars are much more environmentally friendly than their gas-powered counterparts because, while gas-powered cars emit greenhouse gases, electric cars do not. In making that argument, he makes many assumptions, one of which is that there is no part of the electric car production process that could cause enough harm to the environment that his argument would be unsound.

4.9 Rule Out an Alternate Cause of an Effect

Lesson Content In the arguments found in Strengthen the Argument questions, authors will often use cause-and-effect reasoning; in other words, they will link an effect to a cause. 💡MUST KNOW: Whenever an author makes a cause-and-effect claim, he assumes that there cannot be an alternative explanation for the effect observed. In other words, he assumes that his cause-and-effect claim is completely accurate. In other words, whenever we can rule out an alternative explanation for an observed effect, we have strengthened the argument.

4.10 Confirming a Conclusion by Changing, Adding, or Eliminating a Variable

Lesson Content You are probably familiar with how many scientific experiments work: a hypothesis is presented, and then experiments are done in which variables are changed to see how changes in variables affect results. For instance, a researcher who feels that children’s eating sugar makes learning difficult for them could confirm that hypothesis by having a control group and an experimental group. If the children in the control group were to learn more easily than the children in the experimental group, the researcher could say that the results support the hypothesis that sugar consumption was causing the children in the experimental group to have difficulty learning.

4.11 The Difference Between Trap Choices and Correct Answers in Strengthen Questions

Lesson Content In most Strengthen the Argument questions, along with the correct answer, there are one or more answer choices that can appear to strengthen the argument while not actually doing so. Noticing the difference between these so called “trap” choices and the correct answer is generally the key final move in choosing the correct answer to a Strengthen the Argument question. Trap Choice: A trap choice in a Strengthen the Argument question says something that is somehow related to the argument and that seems to strengthen the argument, and at the same time, what the trap choice says is not logically connected to the argument in a way such that it strengthens the argument.

4.13 Strengthen Incorrect Choice Type 1: Provides Information that Does Not Affect the Argument in any Way

Lesson Content The most blatantly wrong answer choices in Strengthen questions simply do not affect the arguments. In many cases, such choices are fairly easy to eliminate. Consider the following example: The government of Golado has recently increased the taxes imposed on the sale of all alcoholic beverages in Golado. While the tax increase is sure to result in a significant decrease in consumption of alcohol in Golado, government officials believe that increasing the taxes on sales of alcohol will result in a reduction in Golado’s government budget gap.

4.14 Strengthen Incorrect Choice Type 2: Information That Seems to Confirm the Validity of a Premise

Lesson Content In Strengthen questions, there can be choices that seem to strengthen arguments by providing information that seems to confirm the validity of premises. 💡MUST KNOW: Some incorrect answers to Strengthen questions are incorrect because their only function is to seem to confirm the validity of premises. Since the premises are already considered facts, these answer choices do not affect the strength of arguments. 4.16 Strengthen Incorrect Choice Type 4: Explains a Part of the Stimulus

Lesson Content We must always remember that the premises in Critical Reasoning arguments are considered factual. 💡MUST KNOW: The premises in Critical Reasoning arguments are considered factual. In other words, the premises are facts. Since there is no way to strengthen a fact, there is no way to strengthen a premise. Therefore, it’s important to know that answer choices that explain part of a passage do not strengthen an argument because, as we just said, premises are facts, and even if we were to provide a 100-page essay explaining a fact, we couldn’t strengthen it.
