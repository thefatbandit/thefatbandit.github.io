6.6.2 Situations in Which the Present Perfect Tense Can’t Be Used

Lesson Content When a sentence describes an event that has clearly ended in the past, the use of the present perfect is not correct. 💡MUST KNOW: When a sentence describes an event that clearly ended in the past, the present perfect is not correct. The reason is that, as we know, the present perfect tense describes an action that began in the past and continues to be happening in the present or continues to be affecting the present, so if the action ended in the past, using the present perfect is illogical.

6.7 The Past Perfect Tense

Lesson Content The past perfect tense is used in describing a past action that occurred before another past action. The past perfect tense = “had” + the past participle of a verb. For example, “had been,” ”had eaten,” and “had performed” are all in the past perfect. 💡MUST KNOW: The past perfect tense is used with the action that occurred deeper in the past, and the simple past tense is used with the action that occurred in the more recent past.

6.7.1 Eliminate Sentences that Reverse the Correct Position of the Past Perfect Tense

Lesson Content SC questions may try to trick us into choosing wrong answers that reverse the correct placement of the past perfect tense. 💡MUST KNOW: SC questions may try to trick us into choosing wrong answers that reverse the correct placement of the past perfect tense. These questions, which have two past actions mentioned, utilize the past perfect tense with the more recent past action. For example: Incorrect: She had skipped the popcorn at the movies because she ate a big supper.

6.7.2 Use the Past Perfect When Describing an Action/Event Completed by a Certain Time in the Past.

Lesson Content A sentence must use the past perfect tense when the sentence is about an action or event completed by a specified date or time period in the past. For example: By late evening on Election Day, it had become clear that the upstart candidate would soon be the president of the country. Late evening on Election Day is a time in the past, and we have an event, becoming, that was completed before that time.

6.7.3 The Past Perfect Tense Is Unnecessary When the Time Sequence Is Clear Without It.

Lesson Content At this point, you know that, when we have two past actions with one of the actions occurring deeper in the past, we can use the past perfect tense to make the time sequence clear. However, if a sentence makes the sequence of events clear without the use of the past perfect tense, there is no need to use the past perfect. 💡MUST KNOW: If a sentence makes clear the time sequence of past events without the use of the past perfect tense, the use of the past perfect tense is not necessary.

6.8 The Future Perfect Tense

Lesson Content The future perfect tense indicates that an event that has not yet happened will be completed before another event that has not yet happened or before a specific time in the future. 💡MUST KNOW: The future perfect tense indicates that an event that has not yet happened will be completed before another event that has not yet happened or before a specific time in the future. The future perfect tense is formed by combining “will,” the helping verb “have,” and the past participle of the main verb.

6.9 The Future in the Past

Lesson Content The future in the past is used when sentences about past events refer to events that were in the future when the past events occurred. Consider the following example: At the rally, the candidate said that he would take campaign donations only from individuals. The sentence conveys that, at a time in the past, the candidate said that he was going to do something in the future, “take campaign donations only from individuals.

6.10.1 The Present Continuous Tense

Lesson Content The present continuous tense is used for describing events that are temporarily ongoing in the present. The children are playing outside. The dog is running on the beach. The sun is setting over the Pacific Ocean. The members are taking their placement tests. Notice that, in each of the above sentences, the event is occurring “right now.” In addition, the events are continuous. 💡MUST KNOW: The present continuous tense is used to describe events that are temporarily ongoing in the present.

6.10.2 The Past Continuous Tense

Lesson Content The past continuous tense is used to describe an action or event that was ongoing but has ended. The children were playing outside yesterday. The plane was flying over Paris one hour ago. Last night, the sun was setting over the ocean. Notice that the events described by these examples have already occurred and that, when the events occurred, they were ongoing, or continuous. 💡MUST KNOW: The past continuous tense is used to describe an action or event that was ongoing but has ended.

6.10.3 The Future Continuous Tense

Lesson Content The future continuous tense is used to describe an action or event that will be ongoing but has not yet begun; the action or event will occur in the future. The children will be playing outside tomorrow. The plane will be flying over Madrid in about five hours. Astronomers will be studying the comet as it passes through the inner solar system. Notice that the events described by these examples will be ongoing at some point in the future.
