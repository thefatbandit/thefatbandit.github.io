2.6 What is a Sentence

Lesson Content In the simplest sense, a sentence is a collection of words that contains at least one independent clause. Let’s continue by talking about subjects. Most of you are familiar with the concept of the subject of a sentence. For example: Whales are highly intelligent animals. The blue ball was rolling down the hill. Doug will ask Martha to come to his house for dinner. In the sentences above, “whales,” “ball,” and “Doug” are the subjects performing the actions.

2.8 The Simple Sentence

Lesson Content A simple sentence consists of a single independent clause followed by a period. 💡MUST KNOW: A simple sentence consists of a single independent clause followed by a period. The following are examples of simple sentences: I do. You will. I was at the beach. John and Jeff were reading books. Spending time with animals is one of the best ways to enjoy your days. We’ll discuss simple sentences in more detail as we progress, but for now it’s important to understand that the simple sentence is the simplest collection of words that can constitute an English sentence.

2.9 The Compound Sentence

Lesson Content A compound sentence is composed of at least two independent clauses. 💡MUST KNOW: A compound sentence is composed of at least two independent clauses. We must connect these independent clauses in specific ways. One way is to use what’s referred to as a coordinating conjunction. There are seven coordinating conjunctions: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so We can use the mnemonic device “FANBOYS” to help us distinguish these conjunctions from other types of transition words.

2.10 A Semicolon Can be Used to Connect Two Independent Clauses

Lesson Content We have just discussed that one way to connect two independent clauses is by using a comma and a coordinating conjunction (FANBOYS). Consider the following example: IC IC My breakfast was cold; the door had been left open. The semicolon here acts in the same way as a comma and a coordinating conjunction would. When a semicolon is used as a substitute for a comma and coordinating conjunction, an independent clause must be found on each side of the semicolon.

2.11 Correct Use of a Colon in a Sentence

Lesson Content We’ll encounter the colon from time to time in Sentence Correction questions. For example: The board of directors made its final decision: the CEO will be replaced immediately.

2.12 Complex Sentences

Lesson Content A complex sentence is composed of one independent clause and at least one subordinate clause (SC). 💡MUST KNOW: A complex sentence is composed of one independent clause and at least one subordinate clause. The following are examples of complex sentences: SC IC Because Lucy loves wine, I brought her a pinot noir from Burgundy. IC SC I brought Lucy a pinot noir from Burgundy because she loves wine.

2.13 Compound-Complex Sentences

Lesson Content A compound-complex sentence is a sentence with more than one independent clause and at least one subordinate clause.For example: SC IC IC Because we went to the beach, I ran in the soft sand, so I could build my endurance. This example features one subordinate clause followed by two independent clauses, making this a compound-complex sentence. 💡MUST KNOW: A compound-complex sentence is a sentence with more than one independent clause and at least one subordinate clause.

2.14 The Relative Clause: Another Type of Dependent Clause

Lesson Content Earlier in the chapter, we said that there are three types of dependent clauses: subordinate clauses, relative clauses, and noun clauses. Relative clauses begin with relative pronouns. that, which, who, whom, whose, where, when 💡MUST KNOW: Memorize the relative pronouns: A relative clause is a type of dependent clause, and as such, a relative clause is unable to stand alone as a sentence. Relative clauses always appear embedded within other clauses.

2.15 Relative Clauses are Often Used to Create Sentence Fragments.

Lesson Content Remember that a relative clause is a dependent clause, and therefore it cannot stand alone as a sentence. 💡MUST KNOW: A relative clause is a dependent clause, and therefore it cannot stand alone as a sentence. In other words, a relative clause must be combined with an independent clause if we are to have a sentence. However, in Sentence Correction questions, we’ll often encounter sentence versions that are just relative clauses attached to nouns.

2.16 Noun Clauses

Lesson Content A noun clause is a clause that plays the role of a noun. Many noun clauses begin with the following words: that, which, how, who, whom, where, whether, what, why For example, in each of the following sentences the noun clauses are bolded: Marty feels that spiritual development has no limits. That holiday leave had been cancelled depressed the entire company. The crowd was unhappy with what happened next.

2.17 A Noun Clause Can Serve as the Subject of a Sentence.

Lesson Content Many people believe that the subject of a sentence must be a noun. That Napoleon’s ruse would fail in its intended objective had not occurred to the French generals. This is a perfectly acceptable sentence. Notice that each of the following sentences has a noun clause serving as the subject: That she had the nerve to attend the party is shocking. What few believed could exist was eventually created.
