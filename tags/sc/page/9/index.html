5.6 Squinting Modifiers

Lesson Content When a modifier is placed between two words or expressions, either of which it could modify, the conveyed meaning can be ambiguous. Incorrect: People who train consistently achieve score increases. Notice that the adverb “consistently” is a squinting modifier because it is placed between two verbs, “train” and “achieve,” either of which it could be modifying. For the sentence to have one clear meaning, the modifier must be placed differently, so that it becomes clear which word is being modified.

5.7.2 The Proper Placement of Relative Clauses

Lesson Content Like faulty placement of adjectives and adverbs, misplacement of relative clauses can result in the creation of illogical sentences. Incorrect: The salesman sold the car to the woman that had 15,000 miles. Notice that the restrictive relative clause “that had 15,000 miles” is incorrectly modifying “woman.” Of course, it’s not the woman who had 15,000 miles; it’s the car she purchased. The salesman sold to the woman the car that had 15,000 miles.

5.8 Appositives

Lesson Content An appositive is an element of a sentence that modifies or further describes another element of a sentence by renaming it. On the GMAT, appositives generally appear in the forms of nouns, pronouns, noun phrases, or noun clauses that are used to modify other nouns, pronouns, noun phrases, or noun clauses. 💡MUST KNOW: Appositives generally appear on the GMAT in the form of nouns, pronouns, noun phrases, or noun clauses that modify other nouns, pronouns, noun phrases, or noun clauses.

Lesson Content Appositives usually, though not always, appear beside what they modify. Incorrect: My brother, running through the park, Eduardo saw a rare California condor.
