5.9 Participles and Participial Phrases

Lesson Content Sentences containing participles or participial phrases appear quite often in Sentence Correction questions. Let’s begin by discussing present participles and past participles. Present Participles and Past Participles A participle is a verb form that functions as a modifier. Present Participle Examples: “jumping,” “running,” “singing,” and “setting” Past Participle Examples: “ruined,” “laminated,” “spoken,” and “swollen” 💡MUST KNOW: A participle is a verb form that functions as a modifier. Participles come in two varieties: present participles, which always end in -ing, and past participles, which usually end in –ed or -en.

5.9.1 A Participle on its Own Can Never Be the Main Verb in a Clause

Lesson Content A participle can never on its own function as the main verb in a clause. 💡MUST KNOW: A participle can never on its own function as the main verb (finite verb) in a clause. So, a sentence in which the only verb form is a participle will not be complete. Because participles look like verbs, sometimes a participle can be perceived to be the main verb in a sentence when, in fact, no main verb (finite verb) actually exists.

5.9.3 How Present Participial Phrases are Used in Sentences

Lesson Content For the purposes of learning what we need to know in order to correctly answer GMAT Sentence Correction questions, we can put most present participial phrases, which are phrases based on present participles, or “–ing” verbs, into three general categories: (1) Present participial phrases used to restrictively modify specific nouns. (2) Present participial phrases that appear at the beginning of or in the middle of clauses and modify multiple aspects of the clauses, are nonrestrictive, and can be treated as nonrestrictive noun modifiers for the purpose of placement.

5.9.4 Present Participial Phrases that Restrictively Modify Specific Nouns

Lesson Content A present participial phrase can be used to restrictively modify a specific noun. For example: The cat licking its paws is considering taking a nap. Notice that the present participial phrase “licking its paws” serves to restrictively modify the noun “cat,” making clear to us that the sentence is not speaking of all cats; it’s speaking of a specific cat – the one licking its paws. 5.9.5 Present Participial Phrases that Appear at the Beginning or in the Middle of Clauses, are Nonrestrictive, and Can be Treated as Noun Modifiers for the Purpose of Placement

Lesson Content A present participial phrase that appears at the beginning or in the middle of a clause and is set off from the rest of the clause by a comma or commas can be treated as a nonrestrictive noun modifier. Sailing with the wind, the clipper ship quickly reached its destination. The present participial phrase “sailing with the wind” modifies the noun “clipper ship.” It also explains how the clipper ship reached its destination.

5.9.6 Present Participial Phrases that Appear at the Ends of Clauses and Modify Entire Clauses

Lesson Content When a present participial phrase comes at the end of a clause and is set off from that clause by a comma, the participial phrase does not have to modify the noun closest to it. For example: The horses ran across the field, kicking up dirt with their hooves. Notice that the present participial phrase “kicking up dirt with their hooves” is separated from the clause by a comma.

5.9.7 The Meaning Conveyed by a Present Participial Phrase Must Fit with the Rest of the Sentence in which the Phrase Appears

Lesson Content Present participial phrases always indicate simultaneity. Consider the following sentence: Using fresh ingredients, the chef prepared the meal. Notice that these two things happen at the same time. However, notice what happens in the following two sentences: Incorrect: Using fresh ingredients, the chef looked for a recipe for the meal. Incorrect: Using fresh ingredients, the chef served the meal on white china. Neither of these sentences is logical because both of them use present participial phrases to describe events that would not possibly have occurred simultaneously with the events described by the main clauses.

5.9.8 Past Participial Phrases Used to Restrictively Modify Nouns

Lesson Content Past participial phrases, phrases based on past participles (usually “-ed” or “-en” verbs), can be used to restrictively modify nouns. 5.9.10 Issues Involving Past Participial Phrases

Lesson Content Sentence Correction question creators can create issues in sentences by misplacing past participial phrases. Incorrect: Awarded the Nobel Prize for his work in chemistry, research done by Linus Pauling was wide-ranging and hard-hitting. Notice the opening past participial phrase, “awarded the Nobel Prize for his work in chemistry.” What follows this phrase must be the noun that it is meant to modify: Linus Pauling. Awarded the Nobel Prize for his work in chemistry, Linus Pauling conducted research that was wide-ranging and hard-hitting.
