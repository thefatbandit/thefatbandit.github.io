10.2.2 Reviewing the Difference Between a Logical Flaw Question and a Weaken the Argument Question

Lesson Content Logical Flaw questions may seem to be rather similar to Weaken the Argument questions. As we’ve discussed, almost always, in order to weaken the conclusion in a Weaken the Argument question, the correct answer option brings in new, outside information. On the other hand, in answering a pure Logical Flaw question, we are not seeking to weaken the argument. 💡MUST KNOW: In answering a pure Logical Flaw question, we are not seeking to weaken the argument.

10.2.6 Logical Flaw Two: Overgeneralization and Sampling Flaws

Lesson Content As we have already discussed in another chapter, when making a generalization, we base a broad claim about a larger population upon observations of a small sample of a population. For example, let’s say that we know ten people who live in Dallas, and all vote Republican. In answering Logical Flaw questions, be on the lookout for broad generalizations made from small samples; such generalizations will be suspect.
