2.18 A Noun Clause Can Serve as a Direct Object

Lesson Content We already know that a noun clause can serve as the subject of a sentence. For example: Dayo picked apples. The noun “apples” is the direct object. For example: The deer ate the green grass. The noun phrase “the green grass” is the direct object. Direct objects don’t have to be single nouns though. I knew why the deer was eating my flowers. I felt that it was time to go swimming.

2.19 Noun Clause as Object of a Preposition

Lesson Content A noun clause can also serve as the object of a preposition. Incorrect: I was unhappy about. Incorrect: The bill was limited by. Those sets of words do not express complete thoughts. For example, a preposition can be followed by a single noun: I was unhappy about the weather. I was unhappy about the oranges. I was unhappy about market conditions. The bill was limited by greed. The bill was limited by time.

2.20 Distinguishing Between Relative Clauses and Noun Clauses

Lesson Content The proper use of relative clauses and noun clauses is important in GMAT Sentence Correction. Since relative clauses and noun clauses look similar, when we see a clause that begins with a word such as “that,” “which,” “who,” or “where,” we need to decide whether it is a relative clause or a noun clause. It will help to remember that a relative clause must follow the noun that the clause is modifying.

2.21 Items in a Series

Lesson Content When three or more items appear in a series, a comma and a coordinating conjunction must appear before the last item in the series. For breakfast, I had cereal, toast, and juice. Notice that, in this sentence, a comma and the coordinating conjunction “and” precede the final noun, “juice.” The comma just before the coordinating conjunction is referred to as the “Oxford Comma.” It must be present in such a list on the GMAT.

2.22 Using Semicolons to Clarify Lists

Lesson Content As we know, a semicolon can be used between two independent clauses. Consider the following example: When Monica is in Europe, she’ll visit London, England; Paris, France; Rome, Italy; and Vienna, Austria. Notice that the sentence uses semicolons to more clearly separate items in a list that could be confusing if commas were used instead. Imagine the sentence without the semicolons: Incorrect: When Monica is in Europe, she’ll visit London, England, Paris, France, Rome, Italy, and Vienna, Austria.

3.2 A Subject and Its Finite Verb in Any Clause Must Agree in Number

Lesson Content In addition to the rule that each sentence must have at least one subject and at least one main verb, when a sentence has more than one clause, each of the clauses must contain a subject that agrees in number with the finite verb in that clause. In other words, if the subject of a clause is singular, the subject’s finite verb in that clause must be singular, and if the subject of a clause is plural, the subject’s finite verb in that clause must be plural.

3.5 Placing an Appositive Between the Subject and Its Verb

Lesson Content An appositive is an element of a sentence that modifies or further describes another element of a sentence by renaming it. My brother John loves hiking in the Ozark mountains. In the above example, the noun “brother” is a general noun. Let’s consider another example that uses an appositive: The uninvited guest, a large spider, is creating a web above my television. In this example, “a large spider” is an appositive that further describes the noun “uninvited guest.

3.7 Compound Subjects

Lesson Content A compound subject is a subject composed of multiple nouns joined by the coordinating conjunction “and” or “or.” The following examples have compound subjects (bolded): The sidewalk and the street were damaged by the earthquake. The largest of the existing land animals, elephants and rhinoceroses are herbivores. 💡MUST KNOW: A compound subject is a subject composed of multiple nouns joined by the coordinating conjunction “and” or “or.” Sometimes compound subjects require plural verbs, but other times they do not.
